Bacteriophages must recognize host receptors and penetrate the host cell envelope to initiate infection. How the classic phage λ initiates infection is not yet understood. Here, we combine cryo–electron microscopy and tomography to visualize infection initiation by Ur-λ, the original λ isolate that uses side fibers to adsorb rapidly to Escherichia coli. We determine the structure of Ur-λ, resolving the full-length central and side fibers, thus providing a structural basis for host recognition. We show that Ur-λ contains six copies of its tape measure protein. We capture intermediates of the tail tip complex during infection initiation, revealing how extensive conformational changes enable adsorption, and visualize the trans-envelope channel required for genome ejection.

In situ structures reveal molecular insights into infection initiation of bacteriophage Ur-lambda.

INTRODUCTION

Bacteriophages specifically infect and replicate within bacteria. Understanding the structure, assembly, and infection process of phages is therefore important to realize their full potential in medicinal therapeutics, medicine, agriculture, environmental management, and biotechnology (1). Bacteriophages have developed mechanistically distinct genome delivery machinery to cross the complex barriers of host cell envelopes. Despite their extreme sequence diversity, the overall organization of siphophages is conserved, characterized by an icosahedral or prolate capsid containing genomic DNA and a long, flexible, noncontractile tail. The tip of the tail, a complex of several proteins, is responsible for host cell recognition, surface attachment, and facilitating the transmission of the viral genome into host bacteria.

Phage λ is the best studied siphophage. λ was discovered in 1949 as a viable phage spontaneously induced from the original lysogenic strain of Escherichia coli K-12 (2). λ became a paradigm in all aspects of phage genetics and molecular biology research and has been extensively used in biotechnology, initially as a cloning vector (3) and more recently in the development of engineered phages for research and therapeutic applications (4–6).

The wild-type λ (λWT) used in most laboratories differs from the original strain—Ur-λ—due to a frameshift mutation that prevents the synthesis and assembly of side fibers (7). These side fibers, encoded by Ur-λ stf, interact with E. coli OmpC and increase the adsorption rate (7–9). The structure of λWT has been extensively characterized by cryo–electron microscopy (cryo-EM) (10–13). The icosahedral capsid is encoded by genes E and D. One of the capsid vertices harbors the portal-neck complex formed by gpB, gpW, gpFII, gpU, and gpZ. The gpC protease modifies gpE, gpB, and gpFII and also autoproteolyzes (14), so the intact protein has not been visualized in mature particles. The tail tube is a stack of gpV rings whose lumen contains a cleaved form of gpH, called gpH*; cleavage occurs after tail assembly but before its attachment to a head (15). The tail abuts the tail tip complex that contains gpM, gpL, gpI, and gpJ. GpK is also required for tail assembly although its location is unknown. During infection, each monomer of the trimeric gpJ binds one monomer of the trimeric maltoporin (16).

The tape measure protein (TMP), encoded by gene H, consists of 853 amino acids and dictates tail tube length (17). Following tail assembly but before joining to the head, a ~14-kDa peptide from the glycine-rich region near the C terminus of gpH is cleaved, resulting in a shorter form known as gpH* (15, 18). After tail attachment, gpH* moves partway down the tail tube, providing space for the leading genome end (19, 20). As the tape measure function of gpH is completed before the formation of mature phage virions, we term the major protein gpH* and the C-terminal proteolytic fragment gpHC.

In early in vitro studies of λ infection using LamB-containing liposomes or cell membrane fragments, two distinct phage-cell complexes, type I and type II, were visualized (21–23). The tail fiber tip bound to the LamB receptor kept the tail tube about 17 nm above the cell surface in type I complexes. Type I complexes were shown to be precursors of type II complexes, in which the tail tube makes close contact with the cell surface. Associated with the transitions between free phage or type I complexes and type II complexes is a change in protease sensitivity of gpJ (24). GpJ is much more protease sensitive in type II than in type I complexes, suggesting that the transition between the two states involved a structural change of the tail fiber. Using LamB-containing liposomes that allowed λ to eject its genome, gpH* was converted into a fully protease-resistant state, suggesting that it formed a transmembrane pore in the liposome (21–24). Although recent reports on the λWT tail (11, 12) and its interaction with LamB (13) partially overcame the challenge of tail flexibility, it has remained difficult to characterize how λ penetrates the physical barriers of the cell during infection in vivo.

Here, we determine structures of intact Ur-λ, including a hexameric filament-like gpH* but trimeric gpHC, and both the central and side fibers. We use minicells, which can be infected with phages, including λ (25), to obtain higher-resolution structures than full-sized cells using cryo-electron tomography (cryo-ET). We also combine cryo-ET and cryo–focused ion beam (cryo-FIB) milling to visualize Ur-λ intermediates during infection initiation. We reveal the channel used by the phage genome to enter an infected cell. Collectively, this study offers insights into the mechanisms underlying infection initiation by Ur-λ, which have broad implications for the large family of siphophages.

RESULTS

Architecture of mature Ur-λ

Ur-λ adsorbs efficiently to host cells by its side fibers binding to the E. coli outer membrane receptor OmpC (7–9). To better understand the overall structure of Ur-λ and its side fibers, we leveraged cryo-ET, cryo-EM, and AlphaFold3 (26) to resolve a nearly complete structure of the free Ur-λ virion (Fig. 1A). Specifically, cryo-ET imaging of free virions reveals a ~180-nm-long tail extending from the capsid that terminates with a distinct central fiber; four side fibers also project orthogonally to the longitudinal axis of the tail (Fig. 1, B and C, and movie S1).

Cryo-EM single-particle analysis (figs. S1 to S3 and table S1) was then used to determine the free Ur-λ virion structure at near-atomic resolution (Fig. 1, D and E). The 3.38-Å resolution structure of the capsid with icosahedral symmetry (Fig. 1D) reveals 415 copies of the major capsid protein gpE and 420 copies of capsid decoration proteins gpD. The portal-neck complex, including gpB-gpW with C12 symmetry, was resolved at 3.05-Å resolution and gpFII-gpU with C6 symmetry at 3.18-Å resolution, yielding a structure essentially identical to that previously described (Fig. 1E) (11, 20). Like those previous studies, we did not resolve gpC, gpK, or gpZ.

The 132.7-nm tail tube is composed of 31 discs of gpV (Fig. 1C), consistent with a recent cryo-EM study of λWT (12). However, other studies (11, 27–29) reported 32 gpV discs; close inspection of the published figures suggested that the extra disc may be formed by gpM, a smaller protein that shares the same fold as the N-terminal 156 residues of gpV. Nevertheless, it is possible that a natural variation in the number of gpV discs exists for different phage isolates. The N-terminal domain of gpV (gpN) forms the stable core of the tail tube, while the C-terminal domain of gpV (gpVC), also known as the immunoglobulin (Ig)–like domain, distributes on the surface of the tail tube in three distinct arrangements (figs. S2 and S4). Last, by fitting cryo-EM structure of the tail tip complex into a low-resolution cryo-ET density map, the full-length side and central fibers were definitively positioned (Fig. 1F).

In mature particles, gpH* occupies much of the gpV tail tube (19, 20). Although most of gpH* remains unresolved, we resolved elongated polypeptide chains in the distal 29 nm of the tail tube at two regions close to the tail tip complex (Fig. 2, A to C). The chains exhibit sixfold symmetry, and together, the two regions occupy more than 20% of the entire length of the tail tube. Although other parts of gpH* were not resolved, our data suggest that the tail contains six copies of gpH*. This value agrees with the estimated copy number found by protein gel analysis (30) but is twice that reported in recent cryo-EM structures of λ (11, 12). However, the latter conclusion was actually based on the copy number of gpHC, not gpH*.

The tail tip complex serves as the host-recognition apparatus that binds LamB (31). The overall cryo-EM structure of the free Ur-λ tail tip complex, determined at overall resolution 3.37 Å using C3 symmetry (Fig. 2A), reveals the tail tube protein gpV, distal tail protein gpM, and central fiber protein gpJ (residues 1 to 807). We resolved three full-length copies of the tip attachment protein, gpL (232 residues), and three of the baseplate hub protein gpI (residues 134 to 222), which are clearly associated with three copies of residues 818 to 849 (from 853) of gpH, or in the mature phage, C-terminal residues of gpHC (Fig. 2D). We could not resolve the N-terminal region of gpHC (gpH residues ~690 to 817). Our cryo-EM structure of the gpI-gpHC complex agrees with recent cryo-EM structures of λWT attributed to gpI-gpH* (11, 12). However, it has been shown that the N-terminal sequences of gpH and gpH* are the same (18). We conclude, therefore, that there are six copies of gpH inside the tail tube when it serves as the tape measure and six copies of gpH* in the mature phage (30). Because we observe six copies of gpH* in the tail tube but resolve only three copies of gpHC, each of which is associated with one copy of the trimeric gpI in the tail tip complex, the other three copies of gpHC are either disordered and not resolved by cryo-EM or are lost from the mature virion. We explore these possibilities further in the Discussion.

The N-terminal domains of the side fibers (gpStfN) are well resolved in our 3.31-Å resolution cryo-EM structure (Fig. 3, A and B, and fig. S5, A and B). Residues 1 to 94 of gpStfN comprise an antiparallel six-strand β sheet followed by an α helix (fig. S5C). Twelve copies of gpStfN (gpStf-a, -b, etc.) form a collar that envelops the junction where a ring of six copies of gpV abuts a ring of six copies of gpM in the tail tip complex (Fig. 3, A to C). Through its β4-β5 loop, gpStf-a interacts with both gpV and gpM, but gpStf-b interacts only with gpM (Fig. 3C). Specifically, the β4-β5 loop of gpStf-a forms hydrogen bonds with gpV E63 and also interacts with gpM R80. The same β4-β5 loop of the adjacent gpStf-b does not interact with gpV but does with K2 and W6 from the adjacent gpM (fig. S5, D and E).

(A) Side, (B) tilt, and (C) top views showing the gpStf collar structure (with C12 symmetry, EMD-48256) around the gpV and gpM complex (with C3 symmetry, EMD-47686). Odd- and even-numbered subunits of gpStf are depicted, respectively, in gold and brown. (D) Two different views of the gpV-gpM-gpSTFN complex show that two gpStf β4-β5 loops interact with gpV and gpM. Three collar domains are connected to the proximal domain of the side fiber via flexible linkers (Fig. 3, D to F). The proximal domain is then connected to the distal domain through a ~124° hinge (Fig. 1B and fig. S6, A to E). The side fiber is somewhat flexible around its junction with the collar, allowing a limited degree of rotation as a rigid body (Fig. 3, D to F).

AlphaFold3 predicts a folded structure for full-length gpStf inconsistent with our cryo-ET observations (figs. S6, A to E, and S7, A and C, bottom), but separating the protein into smaller segments allowed us to build a model of an intact side fiber (fig. S7C, top). Each side fiber is formed by three copies of a protein composed of gpStfN (residues 1 to 91), a flexible linker (residues 92 to 101), a proximal coiled-coil domain (residues 102 to 393), a hinge region (residues 394 to 422), and a distal coiled-coil domain (residues 423 to 525) (fig. S7, B and C, top). The distal coiled coil contains six intertwined chains with the start and end points having a wider diameter than the rest of the fiber [fig. S7, C (top), D, and E], a feature visible in raw tomograms as two knobs in the distal domain (Fig. 1B and fig. S7A). The hexameric coiled coil in the Ur-λ side fiber is structurally homologous to the T4 long tail fiber protein gp37 (32). The Ur-λ side fibers extend away from the tail tip complex, while most long tail fibers of T4 and T7 are folded back onto the body of the virion (33, 34). The receptor-binding domain (RBD) of gpStf appears well positioned to interact with receptors on the host membrane (Fig. 1D). Although the interactions between the RBD and OmpC (35) are not readily resolvable by cryo-ET, they are predicted by AlphaFold3 with high confidence (fig. S7E).

Using a similar strategy of combining cryo-EM and cryo-ET data with AlphaFold3 predictions, we built a model of the full-length central fiber, including its RBD (Fig. 3, E and F, and fig. S7, F and G). Specifically, cryo-EM was used to solve a near-atomic structure of the tail tip complex, including the proximal part of the central fiber gpJ trimer (residues 1 to 807). AlphaFold3 was then used to predict a model of the intact gpJ trimer (fig. S7F). We fitted the predicted model into the cryo-ET density map of the free Ur-λ tail tip complex to validate the model (Fig. 3E and fig. S7G). Our combinatorial approach allowed us to resolve the complete central fiber and four side fibers, each of the latter a gpSTF trimer. The side fibers are the key difference between Ur-λ and λWT.

Both gpStf and gpJ facilitate initial adsorption

To determine the mechanism of infection initiation in vivo, we infected regular E. coli cells and minicells separately with Ur-λ. As regular E. coli cells are too large for direct cryo-ET imaging, cryo-FIB milling was used to prepare ~200-nm-thick lamellae. We then used cryo-ET to visualize infections of both minicells (movies S2 and S3) and cryo-FIB milled regular cells (movies S4 and S5 and fig. S6, F to J). Some phages are attached only through side fibers, but most have both central fiber and side fibers bound (Fig. 4A). Both central and side fibers maintain conformations similar to those in free virions (fig. S8, D to F). The four side fibers are notably longer than the central fiber, and one or more gpStf likely binds to OmpC before the central fiber binds to LamB. The interaction between a single side fiber and OmpC may therefore represent the earliest specific adsorption intermediate. However, if an infecting particle is even transiently tethered to the cell through OmpC, then the likelihood that the central fiber gpJ will interact with its LamB receptor increases.

To understand how the central fiber interacts with host receptor LamB, we performed subtomogram averaging on the tail tip complex, specifically selecting 1342 particles perpendicular to the minicell surface (Fig. 4B). The distance between the tail tip and collar is ~40 nm, the same as in free phage, indicating that no major structural changes had occurred in the tail tip complex (Fig. 4C). These structures likely correspond to type I complexes (22) and should probably be described as reversibly adsorbed phages.

Intimate interactions between the tail tip complex and host membrane

Beyond the described type I complexes, Ur-λ interacts with the host more intimately in other adsorption, although the capsid remains completely filled with DNA (Fig. 5A). Notably, a density likely corresponding to the central fiber is bent at the side of the tail (Fig. 5A; fig. S8, A to C; and movies S4 and S5), and the tail collar is closer to the cell surface, reducing the distance between them from 40 to 15 nm (fig. S8, G to I). To better understand the conformational changes of the tail tip complex at this stage of infection, we determined the in situ structure of the complex through subtomogram averaging and classification of 2338 phages adsorbed to minicells (Fig. 5B and fig. S8J). Our structure closely resembles the recent cryo-EM structure of the λWT tail complex with LamB receptor (13) and the in vitro type II complex with membranes (24). Crucially, however, the phages in our in vivo complex still contain DNA, directly showing that the type II complex is a precursor to, and not a product of, DNA ejection. For clarity, we now refer to this structure as a type IIa complex. In addition, our type IIa complex includes E. coli LamB; Shigella sonnei LamB is required to obtain a type II complex in vitro unless it is treated with CHCl3 (22). It is important to note that in vitro systems do not necessarily provide a complete or an accurate picture of what actually happens in vivo.

Note that there is no trans-envelope channel between the tail tip and the inner membrane.

We built a model of the infection intermediate by leveraging the cryo-EM structure of the λWT tail complex with LamB (13) and the in situ structure derived from cryo-ET and subtomogram averaging (Fig. 5C). It is evident that the central fiber undergoes a marked conformational change and becomes acutely bent as it transitions from a closed tail tube (mature phage and type I complex) to one where the distal end has an open conformation (type IIa complex infection intermediate) (Fig. 5, C and D). Our in situ structure of the type IIa complex likely represents an important infection intermediate and may be the earliest structure of irreversibly adsorbed Ur-λ. GpH* is still present in the tail tube, and the capsid appears full. The resolution is insufficient to visualize gpI and gpHC, but given the intimate interaction between the tail tip complex and outer membrane (Fig. 5, C and D), and as suggested from the in vitro complex (13), gpI, with or without gpHC, may be inserted in the outer membrane.

The trans-envelope channel

The previously described Ur-λ infection intermediates (Figs. 4 and 5) lack a trans-envelope channel, which is essential for phage genome translocation into the host cytoplasm. To capture this critical step, we analyzed 328 tomograms from both E. coli cells and minicells infected with Ur-λ. In some adsorbed phages, we observed a tail extension bridging the tail tip to the host inner membrane (Fig. 6A and movies S2, S4, and S5). Both central and side fibers exhibited conformational rearrangements: The hinge angle between the proximal and distal regions of the side fiber increased from 107° in free phages to 134°, while the central fiber, although visible in only a subset of particles, appeared even more sharply bent than in the previously defined type IIa complex (Fig. 6B). These conformational changes likely facilitate closer apposition of the tail tip complex to the cell surface. We designate this structure type IIb complex, which probably corresponds to the in vitro type II complex associated with membrane fragments (22).

To better visualize the extended tail, we manually selected 1556 tail tip complexes that appeared to contact the outer membrane and form a periplasmic channel. Subtomogram averaging and classification revealed the extended tail structure (Fig. 6C). Overall, the type IIb complex closely resembles the type IIa complex, except that the type IIb tail tube exhibits a loss of density. A key distinction, however, is the presence of a continuous channel spanning the entire periplasm (Fig. 6D). While the density outside the outer membrane could be fitted to the same model used for the type IIa complex (Fig. 6D), no structural model is now available to account for the periplasmic channel density.

As previously suggested (21, 24), gpH* is perhaps the prime candidate for the channel used by the phage genome to cross the cell periplasm. GpH* has been estimated to have a mass of ~78 kDa (15, 24), which corresponds approximately to gpH residues 1 to 710. To avoid overprecision, we used AlphaFold3 to predict structures of gpH*, varying its length between 691 and 730 residues as a monomer, trimer, and hexamer. However, none of these resulted in a channel or channel-like structure that resembles the density derived from cryo-ET and subtomogram averaging. We then separated gpH into three segments of residues: 1 to 319, 320 to 691, and 692 to the C terminus. Monomer and trimer predictions still did not result in any channel-like structure. By contrast, predicted hexamer models of the first two segments form tube-like structures and also fit well into the in situ trans-envelope channel density, suggesting that the gpH* hexamer is part of the conduit for DNA ejection (Fig. 6, G and H, and fig. S9). Much of the third segment corresponds to gpHC; modeled by AlphaFold3 as a trimer (corresponding to the portion that we show interacts with trimeric gpI), it is predicted to block the putative channel formed by segment 2. Segment 3, likely together with gpI, must therefore move out of the way to allow gpH* ejection. We tentatively positioned the N-terminal segment 1 of gpH* in the outer membrane, largely because the AlphaFold3-predicted structure of the hexamer made a slightly better fit into the cryo-ET density and the sequence includes a hydrophobic sequence that may potentially form a transmembrane domain (residues 116 to 140) (Fig. 6G). However, this positioning is by no means intended to be definitive; further study will be required to test both the potential membrane-spanning helices of gpH* and its orientation after ejection into the infected cell.

DISCUSSION

Side tail fibers

Most tailed phages display tail fibers or tailspikes as part of their adsorption apparatus. However, most laboratory strains of λ display only a central tail fiber, a trimer of gpJ that binds to the trimeric LamB receptor in the outer membrane of E. coli. λWT, also known as λPaPa, is actually a recombinant between strains of λ that were in use at Caltech and the Pasteur Institute (7, 36–38). The original strain of λ (2), named Ur-λ (7), differs from laboratory strains by displaying four side tail fibers. These accelerate λ adsorption to OmpC+ cells by ~10 to 30× (7–9, 39) but have no effect on the number of plaques. Stocks of Ur-λ or λPaPa have the same titer on ompC+ and ompC mutant cells. The nonessentiality of λ side fibers is similar in T5. T5 side tail fibers recognize the E. coli O9 antigen as their primary receptor, but common laboratory strains of E. coli are rough and lack any O antigen (40, 41). The second receptor for T5, which is essential for infection, is FhuA, an outer membrane porin for uptake of ferric hydroxamate (40, 42). Similar to λ, T5 adsorption to strains displaying the O9 antigen is 15 to 20× faster than to laboratory strains using the same titer (41, 43, 44). By contrast, the long tail fibers of T7 and T4 are normally considered essential.

The Stf protein of Ur-λ is broadly conserved in siphophages and myophages and shares some similarity to podophage tail fibers. In particular, λ gpStf shares extensive sequence and structural similarity with the tip domain of the long side tail fiber T4 gp37 (7, 45). In E. coli K-12, the T4 RBD of gp37 interacts with OmpC, similar to what we have found for Ur-λ gpSTf (fig. S7E). An atomic structure of the RBD portion of T4 gp37 has been determined (32) but is lacking for Ur-λ gpStf. Combining data from both cryo-EM and cryo-ET and comparing predicted structures by AlphaFold3 enabled us to determine the full-length structure of Ur-λ gpStf (figs. S5 to S7 and movie S1), providing a structural basis for reversible phage adsorption to OmpC. This structure suggests that each Ur-λ gpStf fits into the negatively charged cavity of an OmpC trimer, much like T4 gp37.

Each of the four Ur-λ side tail fibers is a trimer, and the N-terminal regions form a dodecameric collar around the tail at the junction between gpV and gpM. The collar is similar to that formed by T5-like phages except that Ur-λ dedicates a separate protein to support the attachment of side tail fibers. Twelve copies of T5 p132 and DT57C LtfC assemble around tail proteins to form an anchor for three copies of pb1 and LftAB, respectively (46, 47).

Role of the TMP in infection initiation

Our cryo-EM structural analysis of free Ur-λ reveals six copies of a filament-like structure occupying more than 20% of the tail tube lumen near its distal end (Fig. 2). This structure is gpH*, and its copy number is consistent with that estimated in mature particles by protein gel analysis (30). However, that copy number is twice that found for the C-terminal fragment of gpH and gpHC (Fig. 2) and also found in other cryo-EM reconstructions of λWT (11, 12). Threefold symmetry of the C-terminal portion has been noted for several TMPs, including the siphophage T5 and its relative DT57C (46, 47), staphylococcal phage 80α (48, 49), and the Rhodobacter capsulatus gene transfer agent RCGTA (50). More N-terminal regions of these TMPs were not clearly resolved, although protein gel analysis of T5 particles is in agreement by estimating three copies of the mature form of the TMP pb2* (51, 52). By contrast, the cyanophages Pam3 and A-1(L)—both myophages—have six copies of TMP (53, 54). A-1(L) is also thought to have three copies of the TMP C-terminal region. A third myophage, archaeal double-stranded DNA virus HFTV1, also has six copies of TMP, but only three interact with the tail tip (55). The observations of A-1(L) and HFTV1 are exactly the same as ours using Ur-λ, except that it is only known that λ TMP is cleaved during phage assembly into gpH* and gpHC (15). However, until recently, it had not been determined that gpHC remains with the particle. Although three gpHC copies are clearly associated with the baseplate hub protein gpI (Fig. 2D), the other three copies may either be disordered in the particle or lost during phage maturation. It is plausible that they are lost as gpH* in mature virions is partially accessible to external protease: About 3 kDa can be proteolytically removed from gpH* without loss of infectivity (24). Most gpH* is clearly inside the tail tube, and the protein must be ejected into the cell before genome ejection. This makes it unlikely that a portion of gpH* is exposed on the virion surface and suggests that mature particles have a “hole” that a protease can penetrate or where peptides can diffuse away. No actual hole is visible in our structures, but flexibility in structure near the tail tip may afford the protease limited access. A totally speculative idea is that the loss of three copies of gpHC provides space for gpH* to move down the tail tube, in turn providing space for the leading genome end to enter the tail tube, its location in mature virions (19, 20).

Only the TMPs of T5 and λ have been shown to be cleaved inside mature particles, but it is unclear whether cleavage has been carefully evaluated in other phages. It was proposed that the 92-residue T5 pb2C is ejected into the cell periplasm, where it can degrade the cell wall (47). The TMPs of several phages harbor a peptidoglycan hydrolase (56–60). However, λ virions do not contain such an enzyme (58), and there is no known enzymatic activity associated with gpH either before or after its cleavage. Although the function of gpH* in forming a trans-envelope structure has now been established, it is not known whether an intact gpH may serve the same purpose and thus whether cleavage is essential. It is also unknown how cleavage is catalyzed. Proposing a function of gpHC is more difficult because only three copies are complexed with gpI, but six copies were initially produced in the mature phage. Moreover, the precise site of cleavage in gpH is unknown. Some, but not all, programs predicting transmembrane helices suggest that parts of two stretches of hydrophobic residue in full-length gpH have some propensity for spanning a membrane. The more favored prediction involves the N-terminal portion of gpH*, residues 116 to 140; a much less confident prediction involves residues 691 to 712, which would position it at either the C terminus of gpH* or the N terminus of gpHC. Determining the precise cleavage site and experimentally locating those sequences in infected cells would be very informative.

More than 40 years ago, it was shown that gpH* penetrates proteoliposomes, allowing λ DNA ejection (24). These authors also predicted that during normal phage infection, gpH* would span the cell envelope to allow the λ genome to enter the infected cell cytoplasm. We have shown here that gpH* provides at least a major component of the periplasmic channel observed in λ-infected cells (Fig. 6). Furthermore, top views of the periplasmic structure show toroids with a 3- to 4-nm lumen, more than wide enough to allow DNA passage through this siphophage periplasmic channel.

We do not know whether proteins other than gpH* also constitute the channel, but recent in vitro studies (13) indicate that gpI, with its strongly predicted transmembrane domains, may remain in the outer membrane tail tube opening. Whether gpI remains bound to gpHC is not known, but by analogy with the structural gymnastics occurring during initiation of infection by T5, gpHC may be released into the periplasm. T5 pb2C dissociates from the baseplate hub protein pb3 during the opening of the tail tube lumen (47).

It also remains to be determined how the genome ejection channel crosses the cytoplasmic membrane. It has long been known that the inner membrane mannose transport proteins ManYZ affect λ infection (61–65). Mutations in the λ genes for the tail tube gpV and TMP gpH can overcome the block (62). To our knowledge, these mutants have not been further characterized, but it is hard to imagine simple mechanistic explanations for their phenotype. Several other phages also use other inner membrane transport proteins in facilitating infection (66–68), but in no case has it been shown that they play an active role. As also suggested (68), these inner membrane proteins may simply serve to stabilize the TMP and/or other ejected proteins as they form a trans-envelope channel. Much remains to be understood about infection initiation, even in λ, one of the best understood phages.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

Phage Ur-λ purification and cryo-EM sample preparation

Ur-λ cI857 was induced from a MC4100 lysogen. Phage in lysates was concentrated by polyethylene glycol 6000 precipitation, resuspended, and purified by centrifugation to equilibrium in CsCl. Immediately before use, phage was dialyzed against 20 mM tris-HCl (pH 7.6) and 10 mM MgCl2. Three microliters of the sample was applied to a Quantifoil R2/2 Cu300 grid previously glow-discharged negatively, blotted with filter paper for 4.5 s, rapidly frozen in liquid ethane that was cooled in liquid nitrogen, using a homemade gravity-driven plunger, and stored under liquid nitrogen.

Cryo-ET sample preparation for phage infection

For cryo-ET, E. coli minicells were isolated from E. coli K-12 WM4011 as described previously (33). Briefly, WM4011 was grown in 50 ml of LB containing 0.2% l-arabinose to late exponential phase. The culture was centrifuged at 13,000 rpm for 15 min. The supernatant containing the minicells was then centrifuged at 23,000 rpm for 30 min. The pellet was resuspended in 20 μl of LB containing rifampicin (400 μg/ml) to an optical density at 600 nm (OD600) of ~1.5. A total of 4 × 1011 Ur-λ plaque-forming units/ml corresponds to 1 OD260/ml, and it was assumed that this also corresponds to Ur-λ particles/ml. Preliminary experiments, counting the number of adsorbed phages per minicell, then determined the amount of Ur-λ needed to infect minicells at a multiplicity of infection (MOI) of ~20. Infected cells were incubated at 37°C for 15 min when a 5-μl sample was placed on a Quantifoil R2/2 Cu300 grid previously glow-discharged negatively, blotted with filter paper for 8 s, rapidly frozen in liquid ethane cooled by liquid nitrogen using a homemade gravity-driven plunger, and stored under liquid nitrogen.

For cryo-FIB milling, WM4011 was grown aerobically in LB broth at 37°C with shaking until an OD600 of ~1.0. Ur-λ was added at a MOI of ~50 and incubated at 37°C for 30 min; glycerol was then added to 10% (v/v). A 5-μl sample was placed on a freshly glow-discharged Quantifoil R2/2 Cu300 grid, blotted with filter paper for 14 s, and plunged into liquid ethane using a homemade gravity-driven plunger.

Thin lamella preparation with cryo-FIB milling

Aquilos2 (Thermo Fisher Scientific), a cryo-FIB system equipped with a rotatable cryo-stage cooled by a nitrogen circuit and an Autogrid specimen shuttle designed for FIB preparation under shallow milling angles, was used to prepare thin lamellae from Ur-λ-infected E. coli. Plunge-frozen grids were clipped into cryo-FIB Autogrids and mounted into the shuttle under liquid nitrogen. The shuttle was then loaded into the cryo-FIB system using a cryo–transfer system. Samples were sputter-coated with Pt (1 kV, 15 mA, 15 s) to improve overall sample conductivity. Lamellae were produced using a gallium ion beam at 30 kV with a 21° stage tilt angle, which results in a 14° angle between the lamella and the grid. The ion beam current was reduced according to lamella thickness during milling. The resulting lamellae are about 200-nm thick. A thin Pt layer was then sputter-coated (1 kV, 10 mA, 5 s) on the lamellae to minimize charging issues during cryo-ET data collection.

Single-particle cryo-EM data collection

Cryo-EM samples of free Ur-λ were loaded on a Titan Krios G2 electron microscope (Thermo Fisher Scientific) equipped with a field emission gun, a K3 Summit direct-detection device (Gatan), and an energy filter with a slit width of 20 eV. SerialEM (69) was used to collect cryo-EM image stacks with a defocus range of −1.0 to −3.0 μm and a total dose of 50 e−/Å2. The total exposure time for each image stack is 2 s, yielding 40 frames. With a nominal magnification of ×81,000, the calibrated pixel size at the specimen level is 0.549 Å.

Single-particle cryo-EM image processing

Flowcharts of the cryo-EM processing pipeline are presented in figs. S1 to S3. Cryo-EM data were processed using cryoSPARC v4.3.0 (table S1) (70). Dose-fractionated movies were subjected to Patch motion correction and Patch Contrast Transfer Function (CTF) estimation. A total of 1,131,847 capsid particles were initially selected using a template generated by manual picking. A total of 18,782 high-quality particles were selected from good two-dimensional (2D) classes for ab initio reconstruction (fig. S1C). The initial models served as starting references for rounds of heterogeneous refinement. Selected 3D classes then underwent additional homogeneous, local CTF refinement, and nonuniform refinements, yielding final maps at an overall resolution of 3.38 Å (fig. S2A).

Eight thousand sixty-nine portal particles were manually picked, with 7483 portal-neck particles from good 2D classes selected for ab initio reconstruction (fig. S1D). Initial models served as starting references for heterogeneous refinement. Good classes were then subjected to C1 nonuniform refinement to break symmetry. Local refinement on the 12 copies of gpB and gpW yielded final maps at an overall resolution of 3.05 Å. In addition, local refinement on the six copies of gpFII and gpU, after shifting to the refinement center, resulted in maps at an overall resolution of 3.18 Å (fig. S2B).

A total of 3,799,517 tail tube particles were identified using filament tracer. A total of 211,967 high-quality particles were selected from well-defined 2D classes for ab initio reconstruction with C1 symmetry (fig. S1E), followed by further screening of 174,317 particles through heterogeneous refinement, again using C1 symmetry. Subsequent 3D classification identified three distinct classes with different helical arrangements of the Ig-like domain: right-handed, left-handed, and mixed-handed classes. These structures were refined using local CTF refinement and nonuniform refinement with C3 symmetry to achieve resolutions of 3.33, 2.72, and 2.64 Å, respectively (fig. S2, C to F).

A total of 11,453 tail tip particles were manually picked. We selected 7508 particles from high-quality 2D classes for ab initio reconstruction (fig. S1F). The resulting initial models served as starting references for heterogeneous refinement, leading to the selection of classes containing 6517 particles. These particles underwent additional rounds of local CTF refinement, nonuniform refinement, and local refinement, resulting in final maps with an overall resolution of 3.37 Å (fig. S3). A subsequent round of local refinement, focusing on the side fiber collar domain, achieved a resolution of 3.31 Å. To capture the linker between the collar domain and the extended side fiber, C12 symmetry expansion and 3D classification using C4 were performed. A further round of local refinement identified the three linkers connecting the collar domain to the side fiber coiled coil.

To determine the oligomeric state of the TMP at the distal end of the tail tube, the particle box was shifted up along the tail axis. Ab initio reconstruction and heterogeneous refinement were performed to remove suboptimal particles, leaving 4613 particles for homogeneous refinement. These particles underwent 3D classification and local refinement to resolve a hexameric state of the TMPs.

Resolutions of cryo-EM maps were determined by gold-standard Fourier shell correlation (FSC) at 0.143 between two half-maps in cryoSPARC (70). Local resolution variation was also estimated from two half-maps (table S1).

Cryo-ET data collection and analyses

Tilt series of free phage and phage-infected cells were collected with SerialEM (69) on a Titan Krios G2 microscope (Thermo Fisher Scientific) equipped with a field emission gun, a K3 Summit direct-detection device (Gatan), and an energy filter with a slit width of 20 eV. The tilt series were collected at a magnification of ×42,000 and a pixel size of 2.148 Å/pixel, defocus ~5 μm from −48° to +48° with an increment of 3°. Tilt series were aligned and reconstructed using IMOD (71). In total, we got 171 tomograms from free phages, 251 from infected minicells, and 77 from thin lamellae of infected cells (table S2).

Subtomogram averaging and classification

For subtomogram averaging, 1470 free phages and 5236 adsorbed phages were manually picked and then extracted (640 by 640 by 640 voxels) for subtomogram analyses by using tomography package I3 as described previously (33, 34, 72). Briefly, the initial orientation of each tail tip complex was estimated by two coordinates manually selected, thereby providing two of the three Euler angles. To accelerate data analysis, 4× binned subtomograms (160 by 160 by 160 voxels) were used for initial alignment. Global averaging of the extracted subtomogram was performed after applying the two Euler angles. Multivariate statistical analysis and hierarchical ascendant classification (72) were used to analyze the subtomograms of the tail tip complex to distinguish different functional or conformational states of the particles. One thousand three hundred forty-two type I complexes, 2338 type IIa complexes, and 1556 type IIb complexes with a trans-envelope channel were resolved for further individual focused alignment and refinement by using 2× binned subtomograms (320 by 320 by 320 voxels). The resolutions of the cryo-ET maps were estimated by using FSC at 0.5 between two half-maps generated from randomly divided halves of the aligned subtomograms (table S2).
