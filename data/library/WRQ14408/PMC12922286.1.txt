Background

Escherichia coli is a diverse species, ranging from commensal strains that are part of the gut microbiome, to virulent strains that cause a variety of different infections. Much of this genomic diversity is attributable to horizontally-acquired elements, including bacteriophages. RNA sequencing has helped to reveal how pathogenic and laboratory-adapted strains respond to the gut environment, but relatively few studies have been conducted in commensal strains. Further, traditional transcriptomic data analysis techniques are not tailored to capture changes in horizontally-acquired elements, leaving them relatively understudied.

Results

Here we performed RNA sequencing on commensal E. coli MP1, which was recently isolated from mice. We analyzed the transcriptomes of MP1 grown in standard laboratory conditions or in simulated intestinal fluid (SIF), in strains that either carry or lack the vB_EcoP_Kapi1 prophage. We find that both lysogeny and growth media cause broad transcriptional changes in MP1, with growth media playing a larger role. Functional enrichment analysis revealed up-regulation of aerobic respiration, purine biosynthesis, and xenobiotic export, and down-regulation of flagellar motility and chemotaxis, acid stress response, fatty acid beta oxidation, and transport processes in SIF. In Kapi1 lysogens, transport processes were also down-regulated. Hypothesizing that horizontally-acquired genes may have been missed by traditional functional enrichment analyses, we next performed an in-depth analysis of these elements. Some genes on all identified elements were expressed, and many were differentially expressed; sugar uptake and contact-dependent inhibition systems were down-regulated in SIF, while the pathogenicity island PAI-X and multiple dynamin family proteins were strongly up-regulated in Kapi1 lysogens. Finally, we analyzed the Kapi1 transcriptome, revealing expression of λ-like regulatory genes and a putative novel accessory gene during lysogeny.

Conclusions

Our results demonstrate that growth media and lysogeny have profound impacts on the host transcriptome. Many of the processes differentially regulated in SIF have been previously shown to be differentially regulated in the gut. In Kapi1 lysogens, the only genes consistently up-regulated were encoded by horizontally-acquired elements, suggesting cross-talk. Importantly, these genes were completely missed in traditional functional enrichment analyses, highlighting a significant gap in our knowledge of the transcriptional landscape of horizontally-acquired elements.

Background

Escherichia coli is a normal member of the human gut microbiome, primarily colonizing the outer mucus layer of the small intestine where it metabolises simple sugars [1, 2]. However, E. coli strains are highly diverse, and can also cause a variety of intestinal and extraintestinal infections. Much of this diversity is attributable to horizontally-acquired elements like pathogenicity islands (PAIs) [3], plasmids [4], and temperate bacteriophages (phages) [5]. Temperate phages have two opposing lifecycles; the dormant lysogenic cycle where they integrate into the bacterial chromosome, and the lytic cycle where they replicate to high numbers within the cell, eventually bursting out into the surrounding environment. Many temperate phages encode accessory genes which are not required for the basic phage lifecycle, but improve fitness of the infected bacteria (the lysogen) during the dormant lysogenic cycle [6]. Because of this, temperate phages can have drastic impacts on bacterial communities, depending on which lifecycle they are in [7]. These phages are widespread in bacterial genomes (as prophages), and are further enriched in certain environments including the gastrointestinal tract [8]. Due to the high diversity of phages themselves [9], we do not yet have a complete understanding of how lysogeny impacts bacterial physiology, especially in natural environments like the gut.

We recently isolated a novel temperate bacteriophage, vB_EcoP_Kapi1 (Kapi1) [10] from a mouse commensal strain of E. coli (MP1) [11] as a model system to study lysogeny in the gut. MP1 is unique in that it was isolated from the gut relatively recently, and has not undergone extensive laboratory-adaptation like the more commonly studied E. coli K-12 strains [12]. Kapi1 is a 39 kb Lederbergvirus with podovirus morphology, and displays a relatively narrow host range, using the lipopolysaccharide O-antigen as its receptor [10]. Like many phages, Kapi1 is strongly induced into the lytic cycle by DNA-damaging agents like mitomycin C [10]. However, we were interested in understanding how temperate phage lifecycles are regulated in more physiologically-relevant conditions. Using simulated intestinal fluid (SIF; bile salts, KH2PO4, and pancreatin in half-strength LB) to mimic the gut environment in vitro, we found that Kapi1 maintained lysogeny at higher rates in SIF than in LB [10, 13].

SIF is most commonly used in pharmacokinetics research and has only been used in a handful of microbiology studies [13–16]. Accordingly, we aimed to gain a better understanding of how E. coli adapts to this media using transcriptomics. While transcriptomic studies of E. coli adaptation to gut [17, 18] or gut-mimicking conditions [19–21] have been published, all of these were performed in pathogenic strains, leading us to wonder what differences we might observe in a commensal strain. Our second aim was to characterize how lysogeny with Kapi1 impacts MP1, both in LB and in SIF. Transcriptomic studies have revealed that lysogeny significantly impacts expression of genes involved in a variety of processes including motility, acid resistance, transport and metabolism [22–24]. However, these studies were performed on laboratory-adapted E. coli K-12 strains under standard laboratory conditions. Altogether, transcriptomic profiling of a gut-adapted phage-host pair in physiologically-relevant media will contribute to our understanding of the fundamental biology of E. coli (including its resident prophages) in the gastrointestinal tract.

Methods

Bacterial strains and growth conditions

MP13 (denoted WT), and KP7 (denoted ∆Kapi1) were generous gifts from the Goulian Lab (University of Pennsylvania). MP13 and KP7 are gfpmut3.1 and mcherry fluorescently-tagged derivatives of the commensal mouse isolate Escherichia coli MP1 [11]. Besides the fluorescent markers, MP13 and KP7 only differ by the presence and absence of the Kapi1 prophage, respectively [10]. Strains were routinely cultured in LB (10 g/L tryptone, 5 g/L NaCl, 5 g/L yeast extract, 1.5% agar for plates) at 37 °C (with shaking at 225 rpm for liquid cultures). Simulated intestinal fluid (SIF [10, 13]) was prepared by adding 3.4 g/L KH2PO4, 0.63 g/L pancreatin, and 1.5 g/L bile salts to 50% LB (50% dH2O). The pH was adjusted to 7, and components were mixed for 5 min before pelleting any remaining debris from the pancreatin for 1 min at 4000 rpm. The solution was filter-sterilized (0.45 μm) and stored overnight at 4 °C for use the following day.

RNA extraction and sequencing

KP7 and MP13 were streaked from cryo-stocks and incubated overnight. The following day, MP13 was replica-plated onto a lawn of KP7 to identify colonies that were lysogenic for Kapi1 [10]. The following day, two colonies each of KP7 and MP13 were grown up overnight in LB. Each of these overnights were then sub-cultured 1:50 into either LB or SIF and grown until OD600 ~ 0.4, when RNA was extracted from 1.5 mL of culture using the Biosearch MasterPure Complete DNA and RNA Purification Kit. Cell pellets were washed twice with phosphate buffered saline (PBS) (137 mM NaCl, 2.7 mM KCl, 10 mM Na2HPO4, 1.8 mM KH2PO4, pH 7.4) and resuspended in 25 µL PBS before proceeding with RNA extraction as per the manufacturers protocol, except the DNase treatment step was performed twice, and RNA was resuspended in nuclease-free water.

RNA sample integrity was confirmed using an Agilent Bioanalyzer; all samples had an RNA integrity number (RIN) above 8.5. RNA was then express-shipped to SeqCenter (Pittsburgh, PA) on dry ice, where Illumina rRNA depletion sequencing was performed. Samples were further DNase treated with Invitrogen DNase (RNase free), and library preparation was performed using Illumina’s Stranded Total RNA Prep Ligation with Ribo-Zero Plus kit and 10 bp unique dual indices (UDI). Sequencing was done on a NovaSeq X Plus, producing paired end 150 bp reads. Demultiplexing, quality control, and adapter trimming was performed with bcl-convert (v4.1.5) [25].

Data analysis

After receiving.fastq files from SeqCenter, md5sums were checked, then trimming and read quality were assessed with fastp [26]. Trimmed reads were mapped to the MP1 reference genome (GenBank [27] accession number CP139039-40.1) [28] using Bowtie 2 [29], then converted to .bam format using SAMtools [30]. Bowtie 2 was run on default parameters, so reads that produced multiple alignments were first scored, and only the best alignment was kept for downstream analysis. Aligned reads were counted with featureCounts [31], using the MP1 reference .gtf annotations (GenBank [27] accession number GCA_034265375.1). edgeR [32] was used to filter out unexpressed genes, and calculate effective library sizes (using default TMM normalization) and counts per million (cpm). For between-sample comparisons, differential expression analysis was performed with edgeR [32], using a false discovery rate (FDR) cutoff of < 0.05 and log2 fold-change (log2FC) cutoff of > 1 or < −1 as determined by RNASeqPower [33]. For within-sample comparisons, fragments per kilobase million (FPKM) was calculated with edgeR [32].

Functional annotation of differentially expressed genes was performed using the DAVID webserver [34, 35], using the MG1655 GO Biological Process annotation database [36, 37], and the list of all expressed genes in MP1 as the background. Categories and clusters were filtered to only include results with a p-value of < 0.05.

Horizontally-acquired elements in MP1 were predicted with GIPSy [38], PHASTER [39, 40], and IslandViewer 4 [41]. After running all three programs, predicted islands were manually reviewed and compared against the MG1655 genome in EcoCyc [42, 43] to define boundaries of each element based on conserved chromosomal genes.

Results and discussion

Lysogeny and growth in simulated intestinal fluid induce broad transcriptional changes

Two colonies each of Escherichia coli MP1 either carrying (WT) or lacking (∆Kapi1) the vB_EcoP_Kapi1 prophage were grown to mid-log phase in LB or simulated intestinal fluid (SIF), and extracted RNA was sent to SeqCenter for Illumina rRNA depletion sequencing. We received an average of 24 million 150 bp paired-ends reads per sample, with > 94% bp having quality scores above Q30. Accordingly, > 96% of reads passed trimming, resulting in overall alignment rates of > 98%. More than 92% of these alignments were successfully assigned to a feature on the MP1 reference genome (GenBank [27] accession number GCA_034265375.1). Filtering excluded 319 poorly expressed genes from further analysis, indicating that > 93% of the MP1 genome was expressed under all conditions tested. Biological replicates correlated well with each other (Spearman’s correlation ≥ 0.99, biological coefficient of variation 0.17 [32]), and were treated as independent samples throughout (i.e., read values were not averaged). However, as only two replicates were sequenced, transcriptional changes reported here would benefit from verification by alternative methods like RT-qPCR. Detailed sequencing and alignment statistics for each sample are available in Additional File 1.

We next performed a differential expression analysis, making four different pair-wise comparisons to determine the impact of growth media and lysogeny in MP1 (Additional File 2). 603 genes are significantly differentially expressed when non-lysogens are grown in SIF, compared to LB (Fig. 1A). Similarly, 661 genes are differentially expressed when Kapi1 lysogens are grown in SIF, relative to LB (Fig. 1B). This reflects approximately 15% of the transcriptome being changed in SIF, with the majority (69%) of differentially expressed genes being down-regulated (Fig. 1AB). We noticed that the lacZYA operon is the most highly up-regulated operon in SIF, regardless of genotype (Fig. 1AB). While lactose is not a listed ingredient in SIF, we discovered that lactose is added to pancreatin as a stabilizer (MilliporeSigma P7545). The manufacturer informed us that the exact amount of lactose varies by batch, and that our bottle contains 21.1% lactose, resulting in a final concentration of 0.13 g/L lactose in our SIF. While diet-derived lactose may be occasionally present in the proximal small intestine, host-derived lactase should quickly deplete lactose levels. Accordingly, lacZ mutations in both commensal and pathogenic E. coli do not impact colonization of the streptomycin-treated mouse model [44]. We also noticed that yhjX was consistently the most down-regulated gene in SIF in both strains (Fig. 1AB). YhjX is part of an inducible pyruvate uptake system that is strongly induced by yeast extract [45], perhaps explaining why its expression is lower in SIF, since it contains only half as much yeast extract as LB.

Strikingly, 636 host genes are significantly differentially expressed in Kapi1 lysogens grown in LB, compared to non-lysogens (Fig. 1C). Similar analyses in E. coli [22–24], Bacteroides vulgatus [46], Clostridium difficile [47], and Staphylococcus aureus [48] have revealed more modest transcriptional changes during lysogeny, with differentially expressed genes ranging from 23 to 287. Interestingly however, the transcriptomes of Kapi1 lysogens and non-lysogens are much more similar in SIF than in LB, as only 155 genes are differentially expressed in SIF (Fig. 1D). Since there is lower spontaneous induction of Kapi1 in SIF [10], we hypothesize that the relatively stronger repression of Kapi1 in SIF results in a transcriptome that is more similar to a non-lysogen. Once again, we noticed some commonalities between the strongest up- and down-regulated genes in Kapi1 lysogens, regardless of growth media. A five-gene cluster (SID94_19700–19720) is the most strongly up-regulated, while the dpp dipeptide transport, cys sulfate assimilation, and ast L-arginine degradation operons are consistently down-regulated (Fig. 1CD) in Kapi1 lysogens, regardless of growth media. Overall, our data demonstrates that both growth media and lysogeny cause large-scale changes to the host transcriptome.

Functional enrichment analysis reveals changes in metabolism, respiration, transport, and motility

Due to the high number of differentially expressed genes in our dataset, we next performed functional annotation and enrichment analyses to understand more broadly what biological processes are altered by growth in SIF, or lysogeny with Kapi1. We observed little concordance between the most strongly-enriched gene ontology (GO) terms for each comparison (Fig. 2), suggesting that lysogeny and growth media impact diverse biological processes both individually and together. We identified a core set of 23 genes that were significantly down-regulated in SIF relative to LB and in lysogens compared to non-lysogens, no matter the pairwise comparison; acetyl-CoA synthase acs, acetate/glycolate permease actP, L-arginine degradation pathway astABCDE, sulfate assimilation pathway cysDHIJNP, dipeptide ABC transporter periplasmic binding protein dppA, fatty acid degradation genes fadAB, glutamate decarboxylase gadB, glutamate/aspartate ABC transporter ATP binding subunit gltL, L-lactate dehydrogenase lldD, nickel ABC transporter subunits nikCE, ATP-binding protein phoH, and hypothetical protein yhcO. The regulatory pattern of these genes suggest that they are repressed both by growth in SIF, as well as during lysogeny. Interestingly, sulfate assimilation genes were found to be down-regulated in a strain of neonatal meningitis-causing E. coli grown in simulated colonic fluid [19]. Acetyl-CoA synthase and sulfate assimilation genes are also differentially expressed in ΦPA8 and ΦMin27 lysogens, while acetate/glycolate permease and fatty acid degradation genes are down-regulated in ΦMin27 lysogens, and glutamate decarboxylase is down-regulated in Φ24B lysogens [22–24]. While ΦMin27, ΦPA8, and Φ24B are relatively similar Stx phages, they are very distinct from Kapi1, only sharing a few genes in common (Additional File 3). Given the commonalities in differential regulation of these host genes across diverse phages, they may be generally important during phage-host interactions, warranting further study.

To try and understand the impact of lysogeny or growth in SIF individually, we focused on gene sets that were commonly regulated in response to SIF regardless of genotype or commonly regulated in Kapi1 lysogens regardless of media. In both cases, commonly regulated gene sets were filtered to exclude the 23 core down-regulated genes mentioned above. In SIF, only 19% of differentially expressed genes were shared between both Kapi1 lysogens and non-lysogens (Fig. 3A) (Additional File 4). 82% of these genes were successfully mapped to a GO term, highlighting aerobic respiration (nuoGHJKLMN, ndh), purine biosynthesis (purFHKLM, guaAB) and xenobiotic export (ybhFSR) as commonly up-regulated in SIF. The observed up-regulation of aerobic genes in SIF highlights a major limitation of our culturing method, since the gut is known to be a microaerophilic environment [50]. However, purine and pyrimidine biosynthesis genes were previously found to be up-regulated in E. coli MG1655 colonizing germ-free mice, and are required for efficient intestinal colonization [51]. ybhFS was also found to be up-regulated in human enterotoxigenic E. coli infections [17]. Processes down-regulated in SIF include flagella-mediated motility (fliADEFGHILMNS, flgABC, flhB) and chemotaxis (mglB), diverse proton transporters (dtpC, lldP, hycCEG, fruA, frwC), D-ribose transport (alsABC, rbsAC), acid stress response (gadE, adiC, cadB, hdeAB), and fatty acid beta-oxidation (fadEHIJ). Flagella are commonly down-regulated in the gut [17, 52] or in gut-mimetic conditions [20], and nonmotile mutants are often selected for in the intestine [53, 54]. While these nonmotile mutants map to the master regulator of flagellar synthesis flhD, this mutation has pleiotropic effects on carbon and energy metabolism [55], perhaps partially explaining some of the other metabolic changes observed in our SIF dataset.

In Kapi1 lysogens, only 10% of differentially expressed genes were shared in both LB and SIF (Fig. 3B) (Additional File 5). 81% of the down-regulated genes were successfully mapped to a GO term, revealing primarily transport processes (nickel nikAB, dipeptides dppBDCF, 4-aminobutanoate gabP, D-serine/alanine/glycine cycA, glutamate/aspartate gltJK, glycine betaine proX, putrescine potG) to be down-regulated in Kapi1 lysogens. Dipeptide transport was also found to be down-regulated in MG1655::ΦMin27 lysogens [22]. However, none of the up-regulated genes in Kapi1 lysogens were successfully mapped to a GO term, highlighting an important drawback of functional enrichment analyses. Together, our data highlight metabolism, respiration, and motility as being the most strongly impacted biological processes in SIF, while transport processes are impacted by lysogeny with Kapi1.

Horizontally-acquired genes are responsive to changes in media type and lysogeny

We hypothesized that some of the genes that were unable to be functionally annotated in our previous analysis might be horizontally-acquired, as these genes are diverse [56], typically not well-annotated [57], and therefore are not well-represented in traditional databases like GO. Further, horizontally-acquired elements are often not annotated on the reference bacterial genomes available from public databases, making them easily overlooked. We first annotated all putative horizontally-acquired elements in MP1 using a consensus approach. Besides the already characterized functional prophage Kapi1 [10], cryptic prophage Questionable_1 [10], phage-inducible chromosomal island (PICI) EcCIMP1 [58], and colicin Y plasmid [11], we identified four additional pathogenicity islands (PAIs) (PAI-X [59], HPI [60], PAI-CFT073-aspV [3], PAI-CFT073-metV-like [3]) and 8 uncharacterized genomic islands (Additional File 6). Interestingly, most of the genes on each of these horizontally-acquired elements are expressed (Fig. 4A), which was somewhat unexpected given that H-NS is known to silence horizontally-acquired elements [61, 62]. This discrepancy may have to do with the sensitivity of RNAseq compared to more traditional methods, as we obtained ~ 24 million reads per sample (Additional File 1). Going back through our differential expression data, we noticed that some genes on nearly all the horizontally-acquired elements are differentially expressed in at least one condition (Fig. 4B), and indeed, many of these genes are not functionally annotated (i.e., there is no official gene name, only a locus tag) (Additional File 7). Looking at the expression pattern broadly, we noted that many genes were strongly up-regulated in Kapi1 lysogens (Fig. 4B). However, many of these same genes were also down-regulated when lysogens were grown in SIF, but not when non-lysogens were grown in SIF. Together, this pattern suggested to us that the regulation of some of these genes may follow the Kapi1 lifecycle, which we defined as Kapi1-responsive genes. Indeed, molecular cross-talk between bacteriophages and other horizontally-acquired elements has been previously observed [58, 63, 64].

We next focused our analysis on horizontally-acquired genes that were commonly regulated in SIF regardless of genotype, in Kapi1 lysogens regardless of media, or were Kapi1-responsive (repressed in SIF in Kapi1 lysogens, but not in non-lysogens). 23 Kapi1-responsive genes were found on six different horizontally-acquired elements (Fig. 5A). Among these, we identified several phosphotransferase (PTS) sugar import systems [65], contact-dependent inhibition (CDI) system inhibitors, and a regulator of cobalamin biosynthesis and propanediol utilization [66]. Regulation of CDI systems has only been characterized in a few species, and is varied [67, 68]. CDI systems are sometimes up-regulated at high cell densities, but since RNA was collected from cultures grown to approximately equal optical densities, we do not think that this is a factor in our study. While E. coli has a cobalamin biosynthesis operon, cob, Salmonella typhimurium additionally has a propanediol utilization operon, pdu [69], and has integrated the regulation of these two operons through a single positive regulator, pocR [66]. Interestingly, MP1 also has the S. typhimurium-like pdu operon and pocR regulator, encoded by island 3. Propanediol is a microbial metabolite produced through fucose fermentation, and is important for cross-feeding within the gut microbiome [70]. While cob and pdu are known to be induced by propanediol, our results suggest additional phage-derived regulatory factors may be involved.

Horizontally-acquired genes commonly regulated in SIF (regardless of genotype) were found on islands 4, 8, and PAI-CFT073-aspV (Fig. 5B). Once again, we noted down-regulation of CDI systems and PTS sugar transporters. Genes differentially expressed in Kapi1 lysogens (regardless of media type), were found on island 8, PAI-X, PAI-CFT073-aspV, the PICI EcCIMP1, as well as the colicin plasmid (Fig. 5C). In contrast to the Kapi1-responsive genes (Fig. 5A), these genes are strongly up-regulated in all conditions, still suggesting a connection to Kapi1, but the regulation of these elements does not appear to track with the lytic-lysogenic decision. Importantly, none of these 13 up-regulated genes were successfully mapped to a GO term in our previous analysis, highlighting the limitation of traditional functional enrichment analyses for horizontally-acquired genes. Of particular interest, the hyxRAB operon encoded on PAI-X has been implicated in nitrosative stress response and survival within macrophages [71], adherence and bladder colonization [72], and serum resistance and O-antigen chain length regulation [73]. We previously found that Kapi1 lysogens have altered lipopolysaccharide (LPS) structure compared to non-lysogens [10] but were unable to identify a phage-encoded gene responsible for this phenotype, leading us to wonder whether hyxRAB may be involved. We also noted that dynamin-family and RdcB-family proteins on both PAI-CFT073-aspV and island 8 were up-regulated in Kapi1 lysogens. In fact, these five genes on island 8 are the most strongly up-regulated genes in lysogens (Fig. 1CD), leading us to investigate this gene cluster in more detail.

We noticed that many of the horizontally-acquired genes that were differentially expressed in SIF and in Kapi lysogens were encoded on PAI-CFT073-aspV and uncharacterized island 8 (Fig. 5). PAI-CFT073-aspV is a large ~ 95 kb island encoding diverse functions (Fig. 6A), and is important for uropathogenic E. coli CFT073 colonization of the mouse bladder and kidneys [74]. While MP1 is not known to be uropathogenic, it is closely related to CFT073, as both strains are phylogroup B2, which includes both extraintestinal pathogenic strains and commensal strains [11]. Interestingly, island 8 encodes several similar features to PAI-CFT073-aspV, including a toxin/antitoxin system, phage-defense related genes, CDI locus, and sugar-related genes (Fig. 6A). Because of the high degree of homology between these islands, gene expression values may be impacted by multi-mapping reads (~ 2.6% of our dataset, Additional File 1), since by default only the best alignment for each read is kept [29]. To determine the impact of the read mapping strategy, we re-analyzed one biological replicate from each sample with an alternative fractional counting approach, where up to 10 alignments are kept for each multi-mapping read [29], and the resulting counts are then weighted accordingly [31]. Compared to the best-only approach, a fractional approach often preserves more data from highly repetitive genes but includes more lower-quality alignments. Comparing the counts from our initial best-only analysis and the fractional re-analysis, we found that 88 genes that were under-counted by the best-only approach: mainly rRNA’s, tRNA’s, transposases (n = 53), and some horizontally-acquired genes (n = 24) (Additional File 8). Only 5 genes that were excluded due to low expression in the best-only analysis were recovered by a fractional approach (a pseudogene and 4 tRNA’s), indicating that not many genes were lost in our initial analysis. However, the changes in counts of course have the potential to influence downstream differential expression analyses. 7 genes that we report as being differentially expressed in SIF (gadAB, tRNA-Gly, Island 8 CdiB transporter), or in Kapi1 lysogens (gadB, PAI-CFT073-aspV dynamin and RdcB family proteins, Island 8 hypothetical protein) were affected by multi-mapping and would benefit from independent verification via alternate methods.

In SIF, the cdiB transporters of both CDI loci are down-regulated (Fig. 5B). We noted the presence of homologous DNA-binding proteins upstream of cdiB on both islands (Fig. 6B), perhaps suggesting a novel CDI regulatory mechanism. The downstream PapB family transcriptional regulator on PAI-CFT073-aspV was also differentially expressed in SIF, and shares sequence homology with the regulator of the type I secretion system on PAI-CFT073-aspV [75], and the regulator of the P pilus on island 8 (Fig. 6A). Interestingly, overexpression of P pilus is protective against CDI in strains lacking an immunity protein [76], perhaps explaining the co-regulation of these genes.

In Kapi1 lysogens, homologous operons encoding a dynamin family protein, RdcB family regulator, and phospholipase on PAI-CFT073-aspV and island 8 are strongly up-regulated (Fig. 6C). Dynamins are a widespread family of GTPases found in both prokaryotes and eukaryotes with roles in membrane remodelling. The functions of bacterial dynamins have only recently been investigated, revealing roles in vesicle formation [77], membrane stabilization against pore-forming stressors [78], and phage defense [79, 80]. Interestingly, dynamin does not outright prevent phage infection, but instead reduces lysis of infected cells, contributing to population-level defense [79]. Further supporting a dedicated role for dynamins in phage defense, dynamins often co-localize with known defense systems [80]. This is also the case in MP1, as the dynamin operon on PAI-CFT073-aspV is preceded by the EcoKI restriction enzyme, and island 8 carries the Druantia defense system [81] (Fig. 6A). While regulation of defense-associated dynamins has not been described, our data suggest a dedicated up-regulation of this putative defense system in Kapi1 lysogens. The second gene in the up-regulated operon is a RdcB family regulator, which normally works in concert with RdcA to activate diguanylate cyclase DgcE, a crucial signalling cascade involved in biofilm formation [82]. Interestingly, RdcA is also a dynamin-like GTPase [82], but the core RdcAB proteins only share ~ 30% amino acid identity with the up-regulated dynamin-like and RdcB-like proteins on PAI-CFT073-aspV and island 8, suggesting that they may play different roles. We did not find a clear connection between phospholipases and bacterial dynamin-like proteins in the literature, however in eukaryotes, phospholipase D binds to and activates dynamin GTPase activity [83]. Together, this suggests that the E. coli RdcB-like and phospholipase-like proteins may regulate the activity of the dynamin-like protein, potentially mediating population-level phage defense. While crosstalk between horizontally acquired genetic elements is a relatively new area of study [63], our results lend further credence to the idea that within a single bacterial cell lies a complex ecosystem of interacting genetic elements [84].

The Kapi1 transcriptome shows signatures of λ-like regulation and possible lysogenic conversion

Finally, we focused on the Kapi1 transcriptome to identify what genes are expressed during lysogeny, and how the phage transcriptome changes in SIF where lysogeny is maintained at a higher rate [10]. Typically, the only phage genes expressed during lysogeny are those required for maintaining the lysogenic lifecycle. In the archetypal phage λ, this is achieved by the master repressor CI, which keeps all lytic genes turned off during lysogeny [86]. In addition to CI, some phages like P22 also require expression of another regulator, mnt [87]. Besides genes required for lysogeny, some phages also encode accessory genes which are highly expressed during the lysogenic cycle [88]. Several Kapi1 genes were not detected above the threshold in either LB or SIF and are considered to not be expressed in subsequent analyses; kil, CIII, putative anti-restriction protein Kapi1_017, mnt (Kapi1_063), and hypothetical proteins (ninE, ninF, ninH, Kapi1_002, 009, 014, 068, 070). Importantly, none of these genes were found to be impacted by multi-mapping reads in our previous analysis (Additional File 8), suggesting that the lack of expression is physiologic, and not a read-mapping artifact. We were surprised that mnt was not expressed since it plays a crucial role in maintaining lysogeny in the closely-related phage P22 by repressing synthesis of the antirepressor (Ant), which normally de-activates the phage repressor leading to lytic development [87]. Although Kapi1 has a similar mnt-arc-ant locus as P22, they are not identical, perhaps reflecting slight differences in regulatory strategies, and explaining the lack of mnt expression observed.

In our previous differential expression analysis comparing lysogens grown in SIF and LB (Fig. 1B), we did not identify any significantly differentially expressed genes on Kapi1 (log2FC > 1 or < −1 and FDR < 0.05). This was somewhat surprising to us given that we previously observed up-regulation of Kapi1 CI-lux reporter activity in SIF [10]. This discrepancy may be explained by post-transcriptional regulation of CI mRNA, or that our RNAseq samples were simply collected too early, since changes in Kapi1 CI-lux are more pronounced later in growth [10]. We did however note a general down-regulation of most of the Kapi1 transcriptome in SIF (Additional File 9), supporting our previous findings that lysogeny is maintained at a slightly higher rate in SIF, relative to LB [10]. Since there were no significantly differentially expressed genes, we calculated the fragments per kilobase million (FPKM) to conduct a within-sample comparison of Kapi1 gene expression levels in each media (Fig. 7). FPKM values are not comparable between different samples, but can be used to compare the expression levels of different genes within the same sample [89]. Using the median FPKM for all expressed phage genes, we defined genes with expression above the median as being highly expressed during lysogeny. Overall, which genes were expressed during lysogeny did not differ significantly between LB and SIF (Fig. 7). Surprisingly, many lytic genes were still expressed during lysogeny, perhaps reflecting spontaneous induction in a subset of the population [10]. However, we noted that the λ-like regulatory module (CI-cro-CII) was highly expressed, suggesting Kapi1 uses a similar regulatory strategy as λ. Interestingly, the ratio of CI (lysogenic) : cro (lytic) was much higher in SIF, reflecting that lysogeny is maintained at a higher rate in this media.

As a point of comparison, we also generated coverage plots of the raw read alignments to the Kapi1 genome, to allow visualization of any unannotated regulatory features like non-coding RNAs [90]. In general, the FPKM and coverage plots show similar trends, with hypothetical protein Kapi1_043, scaffold, capsid, and CI showing the highest expression (Fig. 8). Overall, we observed very few transcripts corresponding to unannotated regions of the genome. On the positive strand, there are a few transcripts anti-sense to Kapi1_001 through 003 in the LB samples, although we believe this is likely transcriptional read-through from the upstream chromosomal dsdC gene, since the strength of the anti-sense signal correlates well with the strength of dsdC transcription (Additional File 10). There is a short anti-sense signal observed near the 3’ end of Kapi1_005 dATP/dGTP pyrophosphohydrolase, but the expression level is extremely low under the conditions tested. In the last third of the Kapi1 genome, there appears to be a high level of anti-sense transcripts on the positive strand, but there are no clear spikes in expression, making it difficult to ascertain whether there are any true non-coding regulatory elements within this region (Fig. 8). On the negative strand, there is a clear signal anti-sense to the tRNAs, and hypothetical protein Kapi1_056. Interestingly, a similar transcriptional signal has been observed between the tRNAs and holin gene of Salmonella Typhimurium prophage BTP1 [88].

We were particularly intrigued by hypothetical protein Kapi1_043, which is the most highly-expressed gene on Kapi1 under all conditions (Figs. 7 and 8). Updated functional annotation efforts using BLASTp [91], CD-search [92], InterProScan [93], Foldseek [94], and HHpred [95] revealed homology to Thoeris anti-defense 2 (Tad2) like proteins. Thoeris is a widespread phage defense system found in bacteria and archaea [81] comprised of sensor ThsB and effector ThsA, which deplete cellular NAD upon phage infection and result in abortive phage infection [96]. More recently, phage-encoded Thoeris anti-defense proteins (Tad1, Tad2) have been identified, which act as molecular sponges and prevent activation of the Thoeris defense system [97, 98]. Using PADLOC [99] we determined that MP1 does not encode Thoeris, although it is found in many other strains of E. coli [81]. Due to its high expression during the lysogenic cycle [88] and homology with Tad2-like proteins, we hypothesize that Kapi1_043 is a Tad2-like anti-defense protein. Together, our transcriptomic analysis of Kapi1 suggests that it uses a λ-like mechanism to maintain lysogeny and identified a putative Tad2-like anti-defense protein, Kapi1_043.

Conclusions

Our analyses revealed very little overlap between biological processes that were differentially regulated in each of the four conditions tested, demonstrating a complex interplay between growth media and lysogeny, leading to a unique transcriptional signature in each condition. Because of the large number of changes detected, we narrowed our analyses to genes which were always differentially regulated in SIF (regardless of genotype), or in Kapi1 lysogens (regardless of media type), but further analysis of the genes that were unique to each condition will undoubtedly reveal more about the interplay between SIF and Kapi1. Many of the processes differentially regulated in SIF have previously been found to be differentially regulated in the gut or in other gut-mimetic media. In Kapi1 lysogens, we noted that host transport processes were down-regulated, while horizontally-acquired genes were strongly up-regulated, suggesting crosstalk between different horizontally-acquired elements in MP1. Across these elements, we noted up-regulation of the pathogenicity island PAI-X, putative dynamin phage defense systems, and a putative novel lysogenic conversion factor. Together, our transcriptomic analyses highlight a complex interplay between the environment, core genome, and horizontally-acquired elements in E. coli.
