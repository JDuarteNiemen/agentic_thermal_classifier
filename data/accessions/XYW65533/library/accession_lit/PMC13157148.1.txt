==============================
The ongoing evolution of the H9N2 avian influenza virus presents a growing threat to both the poultry industry and public health. In this context, vaccination is not only a vital preventive measure but also the primary strategy for controlling viral transmission and preventing outbreaks. To enhance vaccine efficacy, this study successfully established a novel self-amplified RNA vaccine platform utilizing the Semliki Forest virus (SFV) replicon. The core mechanism involves utilizing the CAG promoter to drive the expression of SFV RNA-dependent RNA polymerase (RdRp), which initiates the autonomous replication of mRNA encoding target antigens, thereby creating a self-propagating cascade amplification reaction that achieves sustained and elevated antigen expression in vivo.

Initially, we constructed the self-amplified RNA plasmid pYL345 (SFV-EF1α-RFP) and conventional DNA plasmid pYL347 (EF1α-RFP) as a control. In vivo chick imaging and quantitative reverse transcription polymerase chain reaction (qRT-PCR) experiments confirmed that pYL345 (SFV-EF1α-RFP) significantly enhances RFP expression, validating the effectiveness of self-replicating RNA system.

Afterward, we compared the self-amplified RNA plasmid pYL486 (SFV-EF1α-NA) with the conventional vaccine plasmid pYL423 (EF1α-NA). Quantitative analysis of NA protein and RdRp via qRT-PCR validated the successful construction of the novel self-amplified RNA system. To further boost the immune response, we integrated the HA1 antigen into pYL486 (SFV-EF1α-NA) to create the recombinant plasmid pYL615 (SFV-EF1α-NA-HA1). Immunofluorescence and Western blot assays confirmed the successful co-expression of NA and HA1 proteins across all constructed plasmids.

Utilizing an oral Salmonella delivery system, we conducted protection studies against avian influenza virus infection. Compared with other groups, oral immunization with the novel self-amplified RNA vaccine S615 (pYL615) delivered by Salmonella significantly increased humoral immunoglobulin G and mucosal secretory immunoglobulin. A levels, enhanced cellular immune responses, promoted T lymphocyte proliferation and differentiation, and upregulated cytokine expression (IL-4 and IFN-gamma). Challenge experiments demonstrated that dual-antigen co-expressing pYL615, conferred enhanced protection to chickens against the G57 subtype H9N2 viral challenge, alleviated infection-induced weight loss, reduced viral shedding in oropharyngeal and cloacal samples, decreased lung and tracheal virus titers, decreased pro-inflammatory cytokine production (IL-6 and IL-1β), and mitigated tracheal and pulmonary damage.

Overall, this study successfully developed a novel self-amplified mRNA vaccine platform based on the SFV replicon. Through oral Salmonella delivery, we demonstrated its capacity to elicit robust systemic and mucosal immune responses, offering a promising innovative strategy for controlling H9N2 avian influenza.

Introduction

The H9N2 subtype of avian influenza virus, a low-pathogenicity pathogen prevalent in global poultry farming, significantly impacts the livestock industry by causing economic losses owing to decreased production performance and secondary infections in poultry(Cáceres et al., 2021; Qi, et al., 2021). Additionally, it raises considerable public health concerns, as it can infect humans and serve as a genetic contributor to novel influenza viruses, including H5N1 and H7N9 (Horwood, et al., 2023; Yang, et al., 2025). Vaccination remains the cornerstone of avian influenza control, with large-scale immunization programs in China effectively mitigating the spread of highly pathogenic subtypes such as H5 and H7 (Liu, et al., 2020; Rehman, et al., 2022). However, challenges persist in controlling H9N2 transmission. Despite the use of inactivated vaccines for over 25 years, the virus maintains widespread endemicity—a situation compounded by its early classification as a low-pathogenicity virus and its capacity for continuous mutation and cross-species transmission(Chen, et al., 2024; Zhang, et al., 2023). Consequently, a deeper understanding of the epidemiology of H9N2 and the accelerated development of novel vaccines are urgent priorities in contemporary avian influenza prevention and control efforts.

Within the existing control framework, inactivated vaccines, including multivalent formulations, remain the most extensively employed immunization strategy. Their effectiveness is largely contingent upon the antigenic compatibility between the vaccine strain and circulating strains. The hemagglutinin (HA) protein on the viral surface is a critical target for eliciting protective immune responses, particularly neutralizing antibodies. The receptor-binding domain within the HA1 subunit is primarily responsible for inducing effective neutralizing antibodies (Hao, et al., 2025) . Moreover, the neuraminidase (NA) antigen, an essential component of conventional vaccines, influences viral transmissibility through its enzymatic activity(Senevirathne, et al., 2021). Antibodies generated against NA potentially enhance immune protection by inhibiting viral release, positioning NA as a core target in the design of broad-spectrum vaccines.

To address the limitations of traditional vaccines, nucleic acid-based vaccines including viral vectors, plasmid DNA, and mRNA vaccines have experienced rapid advancement since the 1990s(Yao, et al., 2024). These vaccines present several advantages, such as robust immunoprotective efficacy, the capacity to induce cross-protection and long-lasting immune responses, and relatively simplified production processes(Xiong, et al., 2023). Nonetheless, their clinical application continues to encounter challenges, including potential adverse effects stemming from the prolonged expression of foreign antigens, variable immunogenicity influenced by multiple factors, and stringent storage and transportation requirements.

Recently, particularly due to the global severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2) pandemic, mRNA vaccines especially self-amplifying mRNA (saRNA) vaccines have garnered significant attention for their brief development cycles, strong immunogenicity, and high translation efficiency. The saRNA vaccines incorporate a complete RNA replication system, including non-structural proteins and the 26S promoter, enabling autonomous intracellular replication and the generation of substantial quantities of subgenomic RNA for highly efficient foreign gene expression (Blakney, et al., 2021; Liu, et al., 2023; Pourseif, et al., 2022). These replicons can be derived from vectors such as the Sindbis, Semliki Forest (SFV), and Venezuelan equine encephalitis viruses(Jawalagatti, et al., 2022b; Kwon, et al., 2025). To data, a number of veterinary vaccine candidates have been reported using the saRNA vaccine platform, such as Pseudorabies virus(Ling, et al., 2025), H1N1 influenza virus(Pierce, et al., 2025), toxoplasmosis(Wu, et al., 2025) and Porcine reproductive and respiratory syndrome virus(Khan, et al., 2025), yielding very promising results for future application. However, it is worthy to mention that the lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) are still the current state-of-the-art delivery system for mRNA and saRNA, the cost, the stability and the injection immunization approach are still the main drawbacks for the real application of mRNA vaccine in veterinary field.

To overcome these issues, researchers have taken use of some other approaches for the delivery of mRNA and saRNA vaccine. Bacterial delivery appears to be one of the most promising choices, in particular, Salmonella, upon oral immunization, targets gut-associated lymphoid tissue and activates mucosal, humoral, and cellular immune responses (Gayet, et al., 2017; Kong, 2021). Jawalagatti et al. designed a DNA-launched RNA replicon platform delivered by Salmonella strain, after oral immunization, the vaccine candidate was safe and elicited robust antigen-specific antibody titers in mice, and a recall response from splenocytes revealed induction of strong cell-mediated immunity, providing important insight into development of an effective multivalent vaccine to combat SARS-CoV-2 and its variants(Jawalagatti, et al., 2022a, 2022c). After that, some other pathogens such as influenza virus were also selected as a model to evaluate its efficiency. A more recent paper indicated that the combination of hemagglutinin (HA) and neuraminidase (NA) using Salmonella-based eukaryotic expression system driven by RNA-dependent RNA polymerase could provide protection in vaccinated mice against lethal challenge with multiple H1N1 influenza strains, maintaining body weight and exhibiting significantly reduced viral loads in lung tissues(Kwon, Senevirathne and Lee, 2025). However, whether the Salmonella delivered saRNA vaccine still work in chicken H9N2 influenza model is still not clear.

In this study, we developed a self-amplified mRNA vaccine platform delivered by attenuated Salmonella for the co-delivery of NA and HA1 antigens. Our findings demonstrate that this platform effectively induces robust mucosal and systemic immune responses, providing a solid foundation for advancing a new generation of H9N2 vaccines.

Validation of NA -HA1 Protein and Its Adjuvant Expression by Immunofluorescence and Western Blotting

Immunofluorescence (IF) and Western blot analyses were performed as described previously. For IF, cells transfected with the indicated plasmids (pYL423, pYL486, pYL615) for 48 hours were fixed, permeabilized with 0.5% Triton X-100, and blocked with 2% BSA at 4°C overnight. The cells were then incubated with primary antibodies (anti-Flag for NA and anti-Myc for HA1, both at 1:1000) at 37°C for 1 hour, followed by a fluorescent secondary antibody (1:100) in the dark. Nuclei were counterstained with DAPI (1:5000) before the coverslips were mounted and imaged.

For Western blot analysis, cell lysates were denatured in SDS loading buffer, separated by SDS-PAGE, and transferred to a membrane. The membrane was probed with primary antibodies (anti-Flag and anti-Myc, both at 1:1000) in 2% BSA, followed by a fluorescent secondary antibody (1:100). Signals were detected using an imaging system.

Chicken Immunization and Protective Efficacy Evaluation

The chicken study design is shown in Fig. 4A. 1-day female chicken, purchased from the Changchun Agricultural Research Institute (Changchun, China), were randomly divided in to six groups (n = 16). The experimental groups included: χ11218(pYL423) (designated as S423), χ11218(pYL486) (S486), and χ11218(pYL615) (S615). The control groups consisted of: pYA4545 (empty vector), BSG (blank control), a naive group (Normal) and a commercial inactivated H9N2 vaccine (Vaccine) group (Yangling Green Bio, A/Chicken/Shanxi/SY/97)(n = 8). This vaccine was a trivalent oil-emulsion inactivated vaccine containing La Sota, M41 and A/Chicken/Shanxi/SY/97 strains, with mineral oil as the adjuvant. The official recommended vaccination schedule was a single intramuscular injection at 3-4 weeks of age.

The immunization schedule is illustrated in Fig. 4A. Chickens were immunized beginning at 3 days of age, designated as day 0. Prime-boost immunization was administered orally on days 0, 14, and 28. Both the empty vector group and the experimental groups received a dose of 10⁹ CFU per chicken per immunization. The blank control (BSG) group was orally administered 100 μL of BSG, while the commercial H9N2 avian influenza vaccine (Vaccine) group received a single 200 μL intramuscular injection on day 0 only. The Normal group remained unimmunized throughout the study. Prior to each oral immunization, food and water were withheld for 6 hours and restored 30 minutes post-administration.

On 14 days after the third immunization, splenic lymphocytes were collected again. Serum samples as well as tracheal, alveolar, and intestinal lavage fluids were first collected for antibody detection by enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA). Subsequently, T cell proliferation was assessed using the Cell Counting Kit-8 (CCK-8) assay. IFN-γ secretion at the single-cell level was then evaluated by ELISpot. Following this, the protein levels of IL-4 and IFN-γ secreted by splenic T lymphocytes were measured using specific ELISA kits. Finally, quantitative reverse transcription polymerase chain reaction (qRT-PCR) was employed to determine the mRNA expression levels of IL-4 and IFN-γ in cells stimulated with NA and HA1 proteins.

The remaining birds were exposed to the G57 subtype H9N2 strain (A/Chicken/Jilin/DH109/2012, kindly provided by Professor Yanlong Cong from Jilin University) on 28 dp3i via nasal drops at a dose of 107 EID50 in a 0.1-mL volume per bird. The weight changes of the chicks were recorded for 14 days post-challenge (dpc). Additionally, oropharyngeal and cloacal swabs were collected at 3, 5, 7, and 9 days post-infection (dpi). Viral isolation was conducted by inoculating the swabs into 9-to-11-day-old SPF embryonated chicken eggs, and viral replication was confirmed via hemagglutination (HA) assays using the harvested allantoic fluid. On 5 dpc, mRNA transcription levels of IL-6 and IL-1β in the lungs were evaluated using qRT-PCR assay and histopathological examination after hematoxylin and eosin (H/E) staining to examine pathological changes. At 5 days post-challenge (dpc), lung and trachea tissues were collected. IL-6 and IL-1β mRNA levels were analyzed by qRT-PCR with relative quantification (2–ΔΔCt method, normalized to reference genes). Viral load was determined by absolute qRT-PCR using a 10-fold serially diluted plasmid standard curve, normalized to total RNA input, and expressed as viral copies per mL eluate. Tissues were fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde, embedded in paraffin, and stained with hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) for histopathological examination.

ELISA to determine NA and HA1-specifc antibody titers

ELISA was conducted as described (Jia, et al., 2025) with modifications. Plates were coated with 1 µg/mL of in-house purified NA or HA1 protein. Serum HI antibody titers were determined using standard hemagglutination inhibition (HI) assays(Wang, et al., 2023). Samples, including serially diluted serum (1:50) and lavage fluids (tracheal, alveolar, intestinal; 1:10), were applied (100 µL/well). Following incubation and washing, plates were sequentially treated with biotinylated anti-mouse IgG/IgA (1:10,000; SouthernBiotech) and HRP-streptavidin (1:3,000; SouthernBiotech). Signal was developed with ABTS, and absorbance was read at 405 nm.

Analysis of splenic lymphocyte proliferation by CCK8 assay

Isolated splenic lymphocytes were seeded in 96-well plates at a density of 2 × 10⁵ cells/mL. Each sample was set up in triplicate, along with three additional wells containing RPMI 1640 medium as a blank control. The cells were stimulated with 10 μg/mL of ConA, HA1, or NA protein and incubated at 37 °C with 5% CO₂ for 44 hours. Afterwards, 10 μL of CCK-8 solution was added to each well, and the plates were incubated for another 4 hours under the same conditions. The OD450 values were read, and the stimulation index was calculated as SI = (experimental wells - blank wells)/ (control wells - blank wells).

ELIspot of IFN-γ secretion by splenic T lymphocytes

Splenic lymphocytes were adjusted to a concentration of 5 × 10⁶ cells/mL and stimulated with 10 μg/mL each of NA and HA1 proteins in complete RPMI-1640 medium for 36 hours. IFN-γ production was assessed using a commercial Chicken IFN-γ ELISpot kit (Mabtech, Sweden). After removal of the cells, the plates were washed five times with PBS. This was followed by sequential incubations with 100 μL/well of biotinylated MT7C10 antibody (1 μg/mL, 2 hours, room temperature) and streptavidin-HRP (1:1000 dilution, 1 hour, room temperature), with five PBS washes performed between each step. Spot development was carried out using TMB substrate, and the reaction was stopped by thorough washing with ultrapure water once clearly defined spots had formed. The dried plates were then subjected to spot counting.

Detection of of IFN-γ and IL-4 in spleen cells by quantitative reverse transcription PCR (qRT‒PCR) and ELISA

Isolated spleen lymphocytes were diluted to 6 × 106 cells/mL, and 500 μL of the cell suspension was added to each well of a six-well plate. Subsequently, an additional 500 μL of RPMI 1640 complete culture medium containing 10 μg/mL of NA or HA1 protein was introduced to each well, followed by 48 h incubation. Total cellular RNA was extracted using an RNA extraction kit (Biolegend Biotechnology, China), and 1 μg of the RNA was reverse transcribed into complementary DNA (cDNA) with the BeyoRT™ First Strand cDNA Synthesis Kit (Biolegend Biotechnology, China). Subsequently, 2 μL of the final cDNA product was utilized for quantitative PCR analysis on an ABI7500 Real-Time PCR system, using primers chIFN-γ-F/chIFN-γ-R and chIL4-F/chIL4-R (Table 2) to quantify the mRNA copies of IFN-γ and IL-4, respectively. Besides, the mRNA levels of GAPDH were evaluated using primers chGAPDH-F/chGAPDH-R as a normalization control (Bio-Rad, Richmond, CA).

The production of both cytokines in cell culture supernatants were also measured by commercial chicken IL-4 ELISA and IFN-γ ELISA Kits (Kete Biotech Co., Ltd., China) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. OD values were measured at 405 nm using an automated microplate reader.

Statistical Analysis

GraphPad Prism software (version 9) was used for plotting and statistical analysis of the data. One-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) was used to compare differences between groups (*P < 0.05; ** P < 0.01; *** P < 0.001; **** P < 0.0001).

Discussion

The experimental results of this study indicate that a vaccine platform utilizing a self-amplified RNA Salmonella vector, which co-expresses NA and HA1 antigens of the H9N2 virus, effectively elicits systemic humoral, mucosal, and cellular immune responses. Additionally, this approach significantly alleviates pathological damage and viral shedding following challenge. These findings provide substantial evidence for the innovation of novel vaccines against the H9N2 avian influenza virus.

First, the self-amplifying SFV replicon vector employed in this vaccine system markedly enhances the expression levels of antigen genes. RT-PCR results confirmed that transfection with the pYL486 plasmid containing the SFV replicon resulted in significantly higher mRNA expression levels of NA compared to those with the conventional pYL423 plasmid (P < 0.0001, Fig. 3B). This observation is consistent with the established mechanism of Alphavirus replicon vectors in vaccine design; through self-amplification, they generate abundant subgenomic RNA, enabling high-level expression of foreign antigens and effectively activating dendritic cell and T-cell responses (Butt, et al., 2010; Vijayan, et al., 2024). Our study further corroborates the efficient antigen presentation capabilities of this system in avian vaccines.

Second, the dual-antigen co-expression strategy for NA and HA1 demonstrated a synergistic effect in inducing comprehensive immune responses. Although serum NA-specific IgG levels did not reach statistical significance, NA-specific IgA levels in intestinal lavage fluid were significantly elevated (P < 0.01), underscoring the pivotal role of NA in mucosal immunity (Fig. 4B–I). Accordingly, a recent study reported that an epitope-optimized H9 HA nanoparticle vaccine elicited broad cross-protective immunity in chicken, with protective efficacy dependent on multivalent antigen presentation (Hao, Wang, Yang, Xu, Guan, Zhang and Chen, 2025; Shao, et al., 2022). In our study, the S615 dual-antigen group in our study showed superior protective effects compared with single-antigen groups, as evidenced by improved weight recovery, reduced viral loads in the lungs and trachea, and significantly lower pulmonary levels of IL-6 (P < 0.0001) and IL-1β (P < 0.01). (Fig. 7B-C). These data indicate that co-expression of HA1 and NA enhances immune coverage and helps alleviate inflammatory injury, leading to improved clinical outcomes.

Regarding cellular immunity, stimulation with NA and HA1 proteins in the S615 group resulted in significant increases in the protein and mRNA expression levels of IFN-γ and IL-4 in splenic lymphocytes (Fig. 6A–H). Moreover, ELISpot assays indicated a substantial rise in the number of IFN-γ-secreting cells (P < 0.01, Fig. 5D). These findings suggest that the vaccine effectively induced antigen-specific Th1-type (IFN-γ) and Th2-type (IL-4) cytokine responses, contributing to a balanced immune profile essential for effective viral infection control. Valkenburg et al. (2014) previously emphasized that the cross-protective efficacy of a poxvirus-based universal influenza vaccine is heavily reliant on CD4+ T cell activation (Lee, et al., 2008; Valkenburg, et al., 2014). Our results reinforce this concept, demonstrating a clear correlation among the degree of cellular immune activation, reduced viral shedding, and improved histopathological outcomes (Nakayama, et al., 2025).

Although the vaccine successfully induced an sIgA response in the intestinal mucosa, no significant increase in antigen-specific IgA was observed in respiratory samples (bronchoalveolar lavage fluid and tracheal wash fluid). This disparity is attributable to the antigen-presentation efficiency at mucosal sites and the characteristics of the local immune microenvironment. Future research should investigate alternative immunization routes or the incorporation of mucosal adjuvants to further enhance respiratory immune protection.

As regards protective efficacy, while the vaccine exhibited slightly lower effectiveness than the traditional inactivated vaccine in controlling viral shedding, it demonstrated considerable advantages in terms of clinical symptoms, weight gain (Fig. 7A), and histopathological outcomes (Fig. 7D-E). Notably, the S615 group exhibited optimal preservation of tracheal ciliary structure and epithelial integrity. These findings indicate that the self-replicating Salmonella vector vaccine possesses considerable potential for mitigating disease severity. Its ability to concurrently activate local, systemic, and cellular immune responses may render it particularly suitable for addressing evolving H9N2 viral challenges in practical farming settings.

Conclusions

This study demonstrates that the self-amplified RNA Salmonella vector vaccine co-expressing NA and HA1 antigens of the H9N2 virus represents an effective vaccine platform capable of eliciting comprehensive immune responses, including systemic humoral, mucosal, and cellular immunity. The SFV replicon vector significantly enhances antigen expression, while the dual-antigen strategy provides synergistic protection through balanced Th1/Th2 responses and reduced inflammatory injury. Although respiratory mucosal immunity warrants further optimization, the vaccine outperforms inactivated vaccines in alleviating clinical symptoms and pathological damage, underscoring its potential for practical application against evolving H9N2 challenges in poultry farming.
