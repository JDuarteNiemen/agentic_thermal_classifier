Strawberry is a temperate fruit specie grown all year round in both open and protected cultivation around the world and consumed with pleasure by consumers for its extraordinary appearance and rich taste and aroma. Like other berries, strawberries are highly suitable for environmentally friendly treatments and benefits greatly from these practices. The aim of this study is to reveal the effects of bacterial combination applications on soil properties, plant growth, and fruit content in Albion and Monterey strawberry varieties grown in open-field conditions in Erzurum province, Türkiye. The study included 5 groups: 4 different formulations (single, double, triple, and quadruple combinations) and a control group (without application). In this study, F1 (Rhizobium sp.), F2 (Priestia megaterium + Pseudomonas chlororaphis), F3 (Pseudomonas chlororaphis + Bacillus pumilus + Acinetobacter genospecies) and F4 (Bacillus safensis + Pseudomonas alcaligenes + Bacillus pumilus + Priestia megaterium) bacterial combinations were applied. In the experimental design, a total of 180 plants (2 × 5x18) were used, with 18 plants in each group, and Duncan's multiple range test was performed. The results of the two-year study showed that bacterial combination applications had a statistically significant effect on soil properties, plant morphology, fruit pomological traits, fruit chemical and bioactive content, and yield. When years and cultivars were evaluated together, the highest increase in yield compared to the control group was 72.5% with the F4 application, followed by F3 (62.3%), F1 (30.3%), and F2 (30%). When cultivars and applications were evaluated together, the average yield in the first year of the study was 176.04 g, while the average yield in the second year of the study was determined to be 227.03 g. When years and applications were evaluated together, the Albion strawberry cultivar had an average yield of 157.97 g, while the Monterey strawberry cultivar had an average yield of 245.09 g. Among bacterial combination, F3 and F4 found to be more effective for sustainable strawberry production in particular to obtain high yield and more health fruits.

Introduction

Strawberries, cultivated worldwide, exhibit rich genetic diversity particularly in terms of ploidy levels, and originate from North and South America [1, 2]. They can grow under diverse ecological conditions from cold regions to subtropical regions, from irrigated deserts to the equatorial areas, and at different altitudes above sea level [3, 4]. Strawberry production has increased considerably in the last decade throughout the world. According to 2021 global strawberry production figures, total production reached 9.175.384 tons, and this production continues to increase [5]. Strawberry production in Türkiye is expanding. In the 2019–2022 period, strawberry production increased from 486.705 tons to 728.112 tons in Türkiye [6]. However, due to its suitability for industrial use, it is widely used not only in frozen form but also as a processed food, particularly in the production of jams, marmalades, and fruit juices. Due to the highly effective aroma, flavor and very attractive appearance of strawberry fruits, they are one of the most preferred fruits globally in all seasons productions [7–9].

There is pressure to increase crop yields and maintain fruit quality, especially since much food is wasted and the global population is projected to reach around 9 billion by 2050. In recent years, human and environmental health problems have seriously increased in the world due to excessive and unbalanced chemical fertilization and pesticide use in order to increase the productivity of crops grown in different agricultural areas [10]. Chemical fertilizers caused soil and air pollution, contamination of underground water, nutrient depletion, and the loss of soil organic matter and structure. These factors create a complex environmental challenge, necessitating alternative approaches to overcome these problems and reduce their harmful effects [11]. In this context, soil organic matter plays a key role in sustainable agricultural systems. Organic matter, which encourages aggregation in the soil, increases water and cation exchange capacity, provides nutrients to the plant through mineralization, and supports microorganism activities, is of great importance in terms of sustainable agriculture. Lack of organic matter in soils causes the soil structure to deteriorate, and accordingly, results in weak plant root development. This condition adversely affects plant growth, yield, and quality [12]. One of the applications in agricultural production to mitigate the harmful effects of chemical fertilization and decrease its application rates is the use of microbial fertilizers. These environmentally friendly applications, which include the interactions between plants and microorganisms and between microorganisms, hold great promise in terms of sustainable agriculture [13]. Microbial fertilizers help plants absorb useful or unused nutrients from the air and soil, allowing less chemical fertilizer to be used [14]. It has led to the emergence of commercial microbial products such as biofertilizers and biostimulants that are compatible with environmental changes, reduce agricultural carbon emissions, reduce energy inputs and prove themselves in terms of product efficiency [15]. Microorganisms that promote plant growth are naturally found in the dense, narrow, and nutrient-rich soil rhizosphere near plant roots, and may consist of fungi or bacterial organisms. Bacteria that form symbiotic relationships with plant roots are called root bacteria, and can grow in the root rhizosphere, on plant surfaces, or within plant tissues. In general, beneficial root bacteria increase plant development and growth by manipulating plant hormonal signals, producing polysaccharides and amino acids that the plant can use, preventing pathogenic microorganisms, and directly or indirectly facilitating the uptake of nutrients from the soil [16].

In regions dominated by a continental climate, strawberry cultivation is known to be limited by increasing altitude, particularly due to low temperatures. In addition to the Albion strawberry variety, which has been accepted as the standard variety in Erzurum's central conditions for many years, the Monterey strawberry variety, which has been identified as a potential alternative in some preliminary studies, was selected for this study [17].

Numerous studies have demonstrated that different bacterial species, such as Rhizobium, Pseudomonas, and Bacillus, have significant effects on strawberry plant development and fruit biochemical properties [18–20]. The selection of a functionally effective PGPR strain is critical, and plant responses typically vary depending on the bacterial strain, plant genotype, and experimental environment. Studies on the effects of different combinations of bacterial strains with nitrogen-fixing and phosphorus-solubilizing properties on Albion and Monterey strawberry varieties, which have high cold climate adaptation ability, are insufficient in high-altitude areas such as Erzurum.

This study investigates the effects of PGPR combinations, including Rhizobium sp., Priestia megaterium, Pseudomonas chlororaphis, Acinetobacter genospecies, Bacillus safensis, Pseudomonas alcaligenes, and Bacillus pumilus, on growth parameters, fruit biochemistry, and yield of Albion and Monterey strawberries under Erzurum conditions. These novel combinations promise valuable insights for high-altitude strawberry production. Our hypothesis was that PGPR combinations (multi-strain PGPR inoculations) can produce significant improvements in growth, yield, and fruit quality compared to uninoculated controls or single-strain F1 application, with responses varying among cultivars due to genotype-specific interactions. This hypothesis was tested through comparative analysis of morphological, biochemical, and yield parameters.

Methods

Materials

In the study, day-neutral Albion and Monterey strawberry varieties, which have high adaptation abilities to the continental climate in Erzurum province at an altitude of 1.850 m above sea level, were chosen as plant material in the experiment. The strawberry varieties used in the study were obtained in the form of frigo seedlings from the INTFA agricultural company, which operates in the Konya province of Türkiye. The study was carried out in open-field conditions in Erzurum city center for 2022–2023 and 2023–2024 growing seasons. In both years of the study, strawberries were harvested and analyzed in August. After root pruning was performed on strawberries provided as frigo seedlings, the roots were dipped in different bacterial combinations and planted in the prepared mulches in open-field conditions in accordance with the procedure in April 2022. The study included 5 groups: 4 different formulations (single, double, triple, and quadruple combinations) and a control group (without application). In this study, F1 (Rhizobium sp.), F2 (Priestia megaterium + Pseudomonas chlororaphis), F3 (Pseudomonas chlororaphis + Bacillus pumilus + Acinetobacter genospecies) and F4 (Bacillus safensis + Pseudomonas alcaligenes + Bacillus pumilus + Priestia megaterium) bacterial combinations were applied.

Some differences in climate data have been observed during the study. In the first year, the lowest average temperature was found to be ˗13.4 °C in January, while the highest average temperature was 19.5 °C in August. When examining the monthly average total precipitation (mm), the lowest average precipitation occurred in January with 15.2 mm, and the highest average precipitation occurred in May with 69.4 mm. In the second year, the lowest average temperature was found to be ˗12.4 °C in January, while the highest average temperature was found to be 21.2 °C in August. When examining the monthly average total precipitation (mm), the lowest average precipitation occurred in January with 13.2 mm, and the highest average precipitation occurred in May with 65.6 mm [21]. The study was conducted at the Department of Horticulture, Atatürk University, Erzurum-Türkiye (39̊ 53̍ 59̎ N, 41̊ 14̍ 17̎ E). Soil samples were taken from the top 0–20 cm of soil before mulching in 2022 and prepared for basic analysis by passing through a 2 mm sieve. Soil texture, aggregate stability, pH, electrical conductivity (EC), macroelement, and organic matter values were determined in soil samples taken from the root zones of the plants.

Method

Preparation of bacteria

The bacterial strains used in the study were transferred to 250 mL of liquid medium and incubated in a shaker for 24 h. The prepared bacterial suspensions were adjusted to an application concentration of 10⁸ CFU mL⁻1 in all four different combinations using spectrophotometric measurements. Frigo seedlings of both cultivars were rinsed with sterile distilled water. The roots of the strawberry seedlings were dipped into the bacterial formulations prepared before planting and kept for 2 h. As a negative control, the roots of the seedlings were dipped into the liquid medium used in the development of bacteria and kept in this medium for the same period.

After the treatments, the seedlings were transplanted to the field and each treatment was made with 3 replications and 6 plants in each replication. In the experimental design, a total of 180 plants (2 × 5 × 18) were used, with 18 plants in each group, and Duncan's multiple range test was performed. The experimental design was organized as a completely randomized design (CRD), with plants spaced 50 cm apart in rows. Weed control and irrigation were carried out regularly throughout the vegetation period.

Plant properties and fruit biochemical characteristics

In the study, plant morphological and fruit pomological, biochemical and bioactive characteristics, and yield values ​​were examined depending on the changes in the experimental soils. In this context, the number of fruits per plant (number), number of clusters, number of fruits in cluster, number of shoots, fruit width (cm), fruit length (cm), leaf stalk length (cm), number of branches, leaf width (cm), leaf length (cm), leaf area (cm2), average fruit weight (g) and per plant yield (g plant⁻1) values were determined according to Küpe et al. [30]. In addition, SSC (Soluble Solid Content) (%), radical scavenging capacity, DPPH (1,1-diphenyl-2-picrylhydrazyl) IC50 (mg × mL−1), antioxidant capacity by ABTS (2,2’-Azinobis 3-ethylbenzothiazoline-6-sulfonic acid) IC50 (mg × mL−1) and FRAP (Fluorescence recovery after photobleaching) assays (µmol TE (Trolox equivalents) × (g FW) −1), total phenolic content (mg GAE (Gallic acid equivalent) × (100 g FW) −1), total flavonoid content (mg QE (Quercetin equivalent) × (100 g FW) −1), total monomeric anthocyanin (mg Pg-3-glk × (kg FW) −1), and fruit flesh color values ​​(L, a, b) were determined according to Küpe and Ercisli [31].

Extraction method

Five g of strawberry fruit samples were taken and 25 mL of 80% methanol was added. Ultraturrax (Heidolph Silent Crusher M, Berlin, Germany) was applied to make the mixture homogeneous. Then, the samples were extracted in an ultrasonic water bath (Bandelin Sonorex Super RK 103H, Berlin, Germany) for 30 min. The mixture was taken into falcon tubes and the samples were centrifuged (Hettich Mikro 22 R, Berlin, Germany) at 6000 rpm for 15 min at 10 °C. After centrifugation, the samples were filtered through Whatman no:1 (Sigma-Aldrich, Fukuşhima, Japan) filter paper and the extracts were kept at ˗20 °C until analysis of total monomeric anthocyanin, total phenolic content, total flavonoid and antioxidant activity with DPPH, ABTS, and FRAP assays.

Total phenolic content

Total phenolic content (TPC) was measured colorimetrically using Folin-Ciocalteu reagent. After adding 0.2 N 2.5 mL Folin–Ciocalteau reagent to 100 µl of extract placed in test tubes, it was shaken well and waited for 3 min. Then, 2 mL of 7.5% Na2CO3 solution was added and waited for 2 h in the dark and absorbances were read at 760 nm with UV–Vis spectrophotometer (PG Instruments, T60, London, United Kingdom). Using the standard gallic acid graph, the phenolic substance amount of the extracts was calculated as gallic acid equivalent (mg GAE × (100 g FW) −1) [33].

Total flavonoid content

Total flavonoid content was determined spectrophotometrically according to the method suggested by Kocak et al. [34]. First, 250 µl of strawberry fruit extracts were taken and 1250 µl of distilled water was added. Then, 0.075 mL of 0.05 g × mL−1 NaNO2 was added and vortexed and incubated for 6 min. At the end of the period, 0.15 mL of 0.1 g × mL−1 AlCl3·6H2O was added and vortexed and waited for 5 min. Finally, 0.5 mL of 1 mol × L−1 NaOH was added and mixed and incubated for 15 min. At the end of the period, the absorbance values ​​of the samples were read at 510 nm wavelength on a spectrophotometer (PG Instruments, T60, London, United Kingdom). The equation obtained from the calibration curve drawn as a result of the measurements made by preparing 10–250 mg × kg−1 FW quercetin was used in the calculations. Total flavonoid content was calculated as mg quercetin equivalent (QE) × 100 g−1 FW.

DPPH analysis

To determine the 1,1-diphenyl-2-picrylhydrazyl (DPPH.) free radical scavenging activity of the extracts, 10–30 μg × mL−1 of stock extracts were taken and then completed to 2 mL with methanol, then 500 μl of DPPH. (0.1 mM) was added and a homogeneous mixture was achieved with the help of vortex and kept in the dark at room temperature for 30 min and the absorbance value of the mixture was read at 517 nm. % Inhibition was calculated using the formula given below. In addition, IC50 values ​​were determined using the %inhibition values [32].

%Inhibition = Control ABS-Extract ABS × (Control ABS × 100)−1

Control ABS: The absorbance measured for the control;

Extract ABS: The absorbance measured for the sample that contains the extract

ABTS + ˑ analysis

ABTS + ˑ Radical scavenging activity was determined according to Zor et al. [35]. First, 7 mM ABTS (2,2’-Azinobis 3-ethylbenzothiazoline-6-sulfonic acid) solution was prepared. This solution was mixed with 2.45 mM potassium persulfate and kept in the dark for 12–16 h. ABTS + ˑ radical solution was added to the extracts at a concentration of 10–30 μg × mL−1 to make a final volume of 2500 µl and incubated for 6 min in the dark at room temperature. Absorbances were recorded at 734 nm. Using the absorbance values ​​obtained, % inhibition values ​​were calculated, and IC50 values ​​were calculated using these values.

FRAP analysis

The determination of antioxidant activity by the FRAP method (µmol Fe2⁺ × g−1) is based on the reduction of Fe3+ ions in the Fe (TPTZ)3+ mixture in the radical to the blue colored Fe (TPTZ)2+ complex in an acidic environment [30]. In the determination of antioxidant activity by the FRAP method, some modifications were made to the method reported by Kocak et al. [34]. The solvents used in the study; 1- Acetate buffer (pH 3.6) such that 3.1 g sodium acetate + 16 mL acetic acid in 1 L solution, 2- 0.156 g TPTZ (2,4,6-tripydryl-s-triazine) was dissolved in 50 mL ethanol and 3- 0.5404 g FeCl3∙6H2O + 2 mL HCl (37% m x m−1) in 100 mL solution were prepared daily. After the solutions were prepared, 80 mL of solution 1, 8 mL of solution 2, and 8 mL of solution 3 were taken and mixed and the FRAP reagent was prepared in this way. 2.4 mL of FRAP reagent was added to 0.1 mL of sample extract and the mixture was vortexed. After 4 min, the absorbance was measured at 593 nm. A calibration curve was obtained using 5–25 micromolar solutions of trolox prepared with methanol. The results from the calibration curve were calculated as µmol TE × g−1 FW.

Statistical analysis

Statistical analysis was performed using ANOVA in the SPSS 2020 (IBM Corp., Armonk, NY, USA) software package. A three-way ANOVA test was performed for the morphological and pomological characteristics of the fruit, and the chemical analysis of the fruit. Tests were conducted at a significance level of p < 0.05, and differences between means were tested using Duncan's multiple range test [37].

Results and discussion

Phosphate solubilization and nitrogen fixation are among the most important criteria for selecting bacterial strains as plant growth-promoting rhizobacteria (PGPR). Previous studies have reported that various PGPR strains not only enhance plant growth but also reduce phytopathogenic infections by activating plant defense mechanisms under adverse environmental conditions [38, 39]. Based on these criteria, the bacterial strains used in the present study were selected from a pool of approximately 1.500 isolates exhibiting phosphate-solubilizing, nitrogen-fixing, and bioagent potential. The selection of a functionally effective PGPR strain is of critical importance, and plant responses generally vary depending on the bacterial strain, plant genotype, and experimental environment. Moreover, it has been suggested that PGPR isolates obtained from a specific crop or ecological region are more likely to produce consistent and reliable results when reapplied to the same crop under identical ecological conditions [40, 41]. Accordingly, a similar strategy was adopted in the present study, in which the same crop was treated under the same open-field condition, resulting in more pronounced effects.

All bacterial strains included in the formulations possessed phosphate-solubilizing and nitrogen-fixing capacities. In addition to these shared traits, members of the genus Pseudomonas are known for their ability to suppress soil-borne fungal pathogens [42]. In contrast, Bacillus and Priestia species are characterized by strong rhizosphere colonization, endospore formation, tolerance to abiotic stress, and long-term persistence in soil environments, while Acinetobacter species contribute to nutrient cycling and exhibit high adaptability to diverse soil conditions [43, 44]. In general, the improvements observed in growth, yield, and fruit quality parameters under PGPR combinations in high-altitude environmental conditions support and validate our hypothesis that multi-strain PGPR inoculations can produce significant beneficial effects compared to uninoculated controls and single-strain applications. Prior to field application, in vitro compatibility assays using dual, triple, and quadruple strain combinations showed no inhibition zones or growth suppression, confirming compatibility among strains. While this supports their combined field application, synergism requires field validation against single-strain controls. Considering these complementary functional traits, the combined application of bacterial strains was associated with improvements in both plant performance and selected soil physical and chemical properties. As indicated in the table, statistically significant differences (p < 0.01) were observed among the bacterial combination treatments with respect to soil aggregate stability. In both Albion and Monterey strawberry cultivars, the lowest aggregate stability values were recorded in the control groups, whereas the highest values were obtained from the F2 bacterial combination treatment. Compared to the control, aggregate stability increased by 22.5%, 27.0%, 16.6%, and 13.1% in the Albion cultivar under the F1, F2, F3, and F4 treatments, respectively. Similarly, in the Monterey cultivar, the corresponding increases were 26.6%, 29.6%, 23.5%, and 20.6%.

These findings are consistent with previous studies reporting strong relationships among soil organic matter content, soil aggregate stability, microbial activity, and plant productivity [43, 45–47]. One of the most important mechanisms by which plant growth-promoting rhizobacteria (PGPR) enhance aggregate stability is through the secretion of exopolysaccharides (EPS), which act as natural binding agents that cement clay, silt, and sand particles together, facilitating the formation of micro- and macro-aggregates. In addition, PGPR indirectly contribute to soil aggregation by stimulating root elongation and increasing lateral root density, thereby mechanically reinforcing soil structure [48]. In support of these findings, Rekasi et al. [49] reported that applications of Glomus intraradices or Pseudomonas mendocina significantly increased soil aggregate stability within two months, accompanied by marked improvements in root and shoot development as well as leaf nutrient content in lettuce.

Statistically significant differences (p < 0.05) were also detected among treatments in terms of soil lime (%), P₂O₅ (kg da⁻1), K (me 100 g⁻1), Ca (me 100 g⁻1), Mg (me 100 g⁻1), and cation exchange capacity (CEC, me 100 g⁻1). The influence of PGPR applications on soil nutrient dynamics and plant nutrient uptake has been widely documented [48, 50]. For example, studies investigating the effects of Bacillus subtilis, Bacillus megaterium, Azospirillum spp., and Spirulina spp. on Kırkağaç melon varieties demonstrated significant increases in leaf mineral contents, including N, P, K, Fe, Zn, Mn, Cu, Ca, and Mg [50]. Similarly, applications of Bacillus megaterium var. phosphaticum were shown to enhance phosphorus availability and increase the uptake of several macro- and micronutrients, thereby promoting aboveground plant development [51].

Evaluation of soil organic matter content (Table 4) revealed that the lowest values (1.1%) were consistently observed in the control treatments of both strawberry cultivars. In contrast, the highest organic matter content was recorded in the F2 treatment (3.1%) for the Albion cultivar and in the F1 treatment (3.3%) for the Monterey cultivar. All bacterial combinations resulted in a statistically significant increase in soil organic matter content in both cultivars (p < 0.01). These results align with previous reports indicating that increases in soil organic matter enhance aggregate stability and resistance to soil degradation and dispersion [52, 53]. Such improvements in soil structure are critical for establishing optimal rhizosphere conditions that support root growth and overall plant development [54, 55]. When basic soil properties measured at the end of the second year (Table 4) were compared with the initial soil properties (Table 3), decreases were observed in organic matter content, CaCO₃ (%), and available P₂O₅, K, and Ca levels. This decrease is thought to be related to the plants' utilization of nutrients in the soil over the course of two years.

Numerous studies have demonstrated that PGPR applications positively affect vegetative growth and mineral nutrient uptake in plants [56–58]. In the present study, bacterial treatments generally increased soil aggregate stability and organic matter content, thereby improving soil physical structure. Enhanced aggregation and organic matter accumulation contributed to a more favorable balance of air, water, and nutrients in the root zone, which positively influenced plant growth and yield parameters, as shown in Table 5. When PGPR applications and cultivars were evaluated together, statistically significant differences (p < 0.05) were observed between years for most parameters. The average yield increased from 176.04 g in the first year to 227.03 g in the second year. This improvement may be attributed to increased microbial activity, enhanced decomposition processes, higher organic matter and nutrient availability, and the resulting improvements in soil physical properties that collectively promoted root development. Variations in temperature, soil moisture, nutrient dynamics, and annual changes in soil microbial composition likely contributed to year-to-year differences in plant growth and yield [59]. When PGPR applications and years were evaluated together, statistically significant differences (p < 0.05) were found between cultivars for several parameters, including number of fruits per plant, number of clusters, number of fruits per cluster, average fruit weight, leaf stalk length, number of stolons, leaf width, leaf length, leaf area, and yield. The average yield of the Albion cultivar was 157.97 g, whereas the Monterey cultivar exhibited a higher average yield of 245.09 g. These differences can be attributed to cultivar × environment interactions, which reflect cultivar-specific physiological responses and adaptive capacities under the prevailing environmental conditions. When cultivars and years were evaluated together, significant differences (p < 0.05) were observed among bacterial treatments for fruit width, average fruit weight, number of shoots, leaf stalk length, number of stolons, leaf width, leaf length, leaf area, and yield. Compared with the control, the highest yield increase was achieved with the F4 treatment (72.5%), followed by F3 (62.3%), F1 (30.3%), and F2 (30%). Furthermore, three-way interactions (year × cultivar × application) were statistically significant (p < 0.05) for number of clusters, leaf width, and leaf area (Table 5). Similar positive outcomes have been reported in numerous previous studies examining the effects of different bacterial treatments on vegetative growth and yield parameters of strawberry plants [19, 60, 61]. The interaction between year and cultivar was highly significant for yield (p < 0.01), whereas the interactions between year and bacterial application, as well as between cultivar and bacterial application, were not statistically significant. This finding suggests that the influence of PGPR applications on soil properties and yield performance may vary over time depending on environmental conditions. Comparable results were reported by Küpe et al. [30], who demonstrated that combined PGPR applications (Pseudomonas chlororaphis, Paenibacillus pabuli, Bacillus simplex, and Pseudomonas fluorescens) significantly improved plant growth parameters, leaf macronutrient content, and total yield in Karaerik and Narince grape varieties. Yield increases of 42.8% and 35.7% were recorded in Karaerik and Narince, respectively. In addition, PGPR-treated soils exhibited substantial improvements in soil quality, including increases in organic matter content (76.2%), aggregate stability (49.5%), and porosity (5.5%), along with reductions in water permeability (18.3%) and bulk density (3.9%) compared to the control.

It is well established that bacterial applications not only enhance soil properties and nutrient availability but also increase plant tolerance to biotic and abiotic stress conditions. In contrast, control treatments without bacterial application may render plants more susceptible to environmental stress factors, potentially resulting in reduced plant growth and yield performance [11].

Fruit color parameters are known as varietal characteristics, but ecological factors also influence coloration [62]. Although some studies have stated that bacterial applications have no effect on fruit color values [63], a number of similar studies have also stated that bacteria have a partial effect on coloration [64].

For SSC, the values changed from 13.49 to 13.92% between years, 13.37 to 14.04% between cultivars and 12.92 to 14.16% between PGPR combination treatments, respectively. DPPH, ABTS and FRAP values were between 4.71–5.42 mg × mL−1, 1.65–1.83 mg × mL−1 and 53.22–62.23 µmol TE × g−1 for years, 4.80–5.34 mg × mL−1, 1.57–1.91 mg × mL−1 and 51.74–63.70 µmol TE × g−1 for cultivars and 4.82–5.38 mg × mL−1, 1.68–1.81 mg × mL−1 and 53.25–62.03 for µmol TE × g−1 between PGPR combination treatments, respectively (Table 6). The bioactive content such as total phenolic content, total flavonoid content and total monomeric anthocyanins were between 103.64–117.26 mg GAE × 100 g FW−1, 93.08–121.87 mg QE × 100 gFW−1 and 95.95–141.20 mg Pg-3-glk × kg FW−1 among years, 97.33–123.57 mg GAE × 100 g FW−1, 78.69–136.27 mg QE × 100 g FW−1 and 115.29–121.85 Pg-3-glk × kg FW−1 among cultivars and 103.31–114.23 mg GAE × 100 g FW−1, 98.14–114.58 mg QE × 100 g FW−1 and 107.93–124.36 Pg-3-glk × kg FW−1 among PGPR combination treatments. In studies on the biochemical contents of strawberry fruits, bacterial treatments generally showed positive effects on phenolic and flavonoid contents and antioxidant activity [65]. A detailed evaluation of Table 6 indicates that the effects of PGPR applications were treatment-specific rather than uniform. In particular, the F2 combination was associated with higher total phenolic content, FRAP values, and chroma (C), whereas F4 was more effective in enhancing total monomeric anthocyanin content. Previous studies also indicated the effect of years, cultivars and PGPR treatments on color, SSC, antioxidant capacity and bioactive content on strawberries [4, 8, 66–72]. Differences observed among ABTS, FRAP, total flavonoid content (TFC), and total monomeric anthocyanins (TMA) are not unexpected, as these parameters reflect different antioxidant mechanisms and metabolite groups. While ABTS and FRAP assays measure antioxidant capacity through distinct reaction pathways, phenolic, flavonoid, and anthocyanin compounds differ in their biosynthesis and accumulation patterns. Previous studies have also reported that antioxidant assays and bioactive compounds may respond differently to cultivar, year, and microbial treatments in strawberries [67, 68, 73].

Soluble solid content (SSC) is primarily influenced by carbohydrate metabolism, and environmental conditions during fruit development. Although PGPR applications may enhance nutrient uptake and metabolic activity, increases in SSC are not always proportional, as sugar accumulation can be strongly affected by cultivar characteristics and seasonal conditions. Similar observations, where bacterial treatments did not consistently result in higher SSC compared to the control, have been reported in previous studies [69, 70, 74]. The observation of DPPH, ABTS, and SSC values in the control group fruit batches that did not receive bacterial treatment suggests that this may be related to the stress response they were exposed to.

The effects of PGPR on fruit biochemical composition are generally attributed to indirect mechanisms such as improved nutrient availability, modulation of phytohormone balance, and enhanced plant stress tolerance, which may influence secondary metabolite biosynthesis. However, the extent and direction of these effects depend on cultivar, environmental conditions, and microbial strain compatibility, as reported in earlier studies [65, 68]. The observation that the control treatment did not consistently show the lowest values for all biochemical parameters suggests that inherent cultivar traits play a substantial role in determining fruit quality attributes. PGPR effects are often modulatory rather than dominant and may enhance specific metabolic pathways without uniformly increasing all quality parameters. Such findings are consistent with previous reports indicating that microbial applications may have selective rather than universal effects on fruit biochemical composition [75, 76].

Despite the findings of this study, some limitations should be acknowledged. The experiment was conducted over a limited number of growing seasons and cultivars under specific environmental conditions. In addition, the physiological mechanisms underlying PGPR-induced changes in fruit quality were not directly examined. Future studies including longer-term trials, additional cultivars, and mechanistic analyses would contribute to a better understanding of PGPR effects on strawberry fruit quality.

Conclusion

This two-year field study demonstrated that bacterial combination applications were associated with improvements in soil properties, vegetative growth, yield, and fruit quality parameters of strawberry under open-field conditions. The results indicate that the use of multi-strain plant growth-promoting bacteria (PGPB) may represent a promising and environmentally friendly approach for enhancing strawberry productivity and fruit nutritional quality under terrestrial climatic conditions such as those prevailing in Erzurum, Türkiye.

The novelty of this study lies in the comparative evaluation of single, double, triple, and quadruple bacterial combinations under real field conditions and across two commercially important strawberry cultivars. Unlike many previous studies that have focused on single microbial strains or short-term experiments, the present study demonstrates that combined bacterial formulations can exert positive effects on plant yield and quality parameters. Among the tested formulations, the F3 and F4 combinations were associated with higher yield increases compared to the control group, while the F2, F3, and F4 combinations were associated with improvements in selected pomological traits and bioactive compound contents.

In addition, treatment-dependent increases in soil organic matter content were observed, which may have contributed to improved soil structure and nutrient availability, thereby supporting root development and overall plant growth. From a sustainability perspective, these findings suggest that bacterial combinations could be considered as supportive tools alongside conventional agricultural practices, with the potential to reduce chemical input requirements while maintaining yield and fruit quality.

Future research should aim to clarify the physiological and microbiological mechanisms underlying the observed responses, particularly those related to microbial interactions, nutrient mobilization, phytohormone regulation, and stress tolerance. Overall, the results of this study provide a useful basis for further development and optimization of microbial-based strategies in sustainable strawberry production.
