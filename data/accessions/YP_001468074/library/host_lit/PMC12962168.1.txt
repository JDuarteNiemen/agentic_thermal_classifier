Ferroptosis is a non-apoptotic cell death characterized by iron-dependent lipid peroxidation and is implicated in renal diseases, including acute kidney injury and diabetic nephropathy. In renal proximal tubular cells, the regulation of ferroptosis is particularly critical for maintaining cellular homeostasis. While inducing ferroptosis in normal rat kidney proximal tubular epithelial (NRK-52E) cells, we observed the emergence of resistant subpopulations and established two ferroptosis-resistant clones, designated clone A and clone B, to investigate the underlying mechanisms. Transcript and immunoblot analyses revealed that clone A lacked acyl-CoA synthetase long-chain family member 4 (ACSL4), and this deficiency conferred ferroptosis resistance. Furthermore, although clone B expressed ACSL4, its enzymatic activity was markedly reduced, leading us to hypothesize that clone B harbors a mutation that impairs ACSL4 function. Therefore, we performed sequence analysis and identified a novel T237A missense mutation in ACSL4. Sequence alignment and structural superposition of rat ACSL4 and long-chain fatty acyl-CoA synthetase from Thermus thermophilus suggested that Thr237 in rat ACSL4 cooperates with Glu429 in Mg2+ coordination. Functional assays using ACSL4-deficient cells expressing ACSL4 variants (T237A or E429A) confirmed that both residues are essential for catalytic activity. These findings provide new insights into the structural and functional roles of mammalian ACSL4 and may facilitate the development of ACSL4-targeted therapeutics.

Ferroptosis, a distinct form of regulated cell death, was first described in 2012 (1). Unlike other forms of cell death, ferroptosis is characterized by the accumulation of lipid peroxides in cell membranes. These peroxides form through reactions involving iron, oxygen, and polyunsaturated fatty acid (PUFA)-containing phospholipids (2). This lipid damage ultimately leads to membrane rupture and cell death. Notably, ferroptosis can be inhibited by lipid peroxidation inhibitors such as ferrostatin-1 (3). The regulation of ferroptosis primarily involves phospholipid remodeling and detoxification pathways that prevent excess lipid peroxidation. Key enzymes involved in lipid metabolism, including acyl-CoA synthetase long-chain family member 4 (ACSL4) and lysophosphatidylcholine acyltransferase 3 (LPCAT3), play crucial roles in phospholipid synthesis and remodeling, ultimately rendering cells susceptible to ferroptosis (4). Conversely, lipid peroxidation is counteracted by detoxification pathways. The system xc-/glutathione peroxidase 4 (GPX4) pathway, which contains target enzymes of canonical ferroptosis inducers, is a central defense, along with the FSP1-CoQ10-NAD(P)H pathway, which functions as an independent parallel detoxification system (5). Despite advances in understanding ferroptosis, the precise mechanisms remain incompletely understood (6).

Recent research suggests that ferroptosis plays critical roles in both physiological and pathological contexts. Under physiological conditions, ferroptosis contributes to processes, such as aging and embryonic erythropoiesis (7). In contrast, under pathological conditions, ferroptosis has been implicated in retinal degeneration, neurodegeneration, and tissue injury (8, 9). In particular, ferroptosis in renal proximal tubular cells appears to contribute to the development of renal diseases such as acute kidney injury (AKI) and diabetic nephropathy (10). Therefore, understanding the mechanisms underlying ferroptosis susceptibility could enhance our knowledge of these processes and guide the development of therapeutic strategies.

The ACSL enzyme family, which utilizes saturated and unsaturated fatty acids with chain lengths of 8 to 22 carbon atoms as substrates, includes ACSL1, ACSL3, ACSL4, ACSL5, and ACSL6 in mammals (11). Among them, ACSL4 preferentially catalyzes the conversion of arachidonic acid (C20:4) and adrenic acid (C22:4) to their acyl-CoA derivatives, thereby promoting ferroptosis (12). Due to its central role, ACSL4 is considered a potential drug target for ferroptosis-related conditions.

While investigating the link between ferroptosis and proximal tubular cells using ferroptosis-sensitive normal rat proximal tubular cells (NRK-52E cells), we observed the emergence of ferroptosis-resistant subpopulations. In this study, we established two ferroptosis-resistant NRK-52E clones and explored the causal factors contributing to their resistance. Our findings provide new insights into the structural and functional roles of mammalian ACSL4 and inform the development of ACSL4-targeted therapeutics.

Results

Clones derived from NRK-52E cells exhibit ferroptosis resistance

NRK-52E cells, a normal rat renal proximal tubular epithelial cell line, were repeatedly treated with a lethal concentration (200 nM) of 1-[2-(2-chloro-4-nitrophenyl)ethyl]-3-(2,4-dichlorophenyl)urea (RSL3), a canonical ferroptosis inducer, for 2 weeks. Although most cells were eliminated, a small subpopulation persisted unexpectedly. Two resistant clones were subsequently isolated and designated as clone A and clone B. To characterize these clones, we assessed their sensitivity to various cell death inducers. Both clone A and clone B were resistant to RSL3 compared with the parental cells, with clone A exhibiting a higher level of resistance (Fig. 1A). Both clones also showed resistance to other ferroptosis inducers, ML162 and erastin (Fig. 1, B and C). ACSL4 expression is substantially downregulated in clone A

Ferroptosis is regulated by several genes involved in phospholipid remodeling, lipid peroxidation, and detoxification of lipid peroxidation products. To identify the key factors conferring ferroptosis resistance in clone A and clone B, the expression levels of seven ferroptosis-related genes (Acsl4, Aifm2, Alox15, Gch1, Gpx4, Lpcat3, and Slc7a11) were assessed by quantitative real-time PCR. The expression of Alox15 was too low to quantify across all cell lines (data not shown). Among the remaining genes, Acsl4 expression was almost undetectable in clone A compared with the parental NRK-52E cells (Fig. 3A). Although Slc7a11 was also downregulated in clone A, it was excluded from further analysis as it is a known anti-ferroptosis gene (1). Immunoblotting confirmed that ACSL4 protein was undetectable in clone A (Fig. 3B). ACSL4 enzymatic activity is reduced in clone B

In contrast to clone A, clone B expressed ACSL4 at both the mRNA and protein levels. (Fig. 3). Unlike the marked reduction in ACSL4 expression observed in clone A, clone B showed no prominent changes in gene expression, although some genes exhibited slight alterations (Fig. 3A). To investigate the causal factors underlying ferroptosis resistance in clone B, we focused on the three major ferroptosis pathways: (i) the system xc-/GPX4 pathway, (ii) the FSP1-CoQ10-NAD(P)H pathway, and (iii) the phospholipid remodeling pathway. As shown in Figures 1 and 2, both ferroptosis-resistant clones were insensitive to the system xc- inhibitor (erastin) and GPX4 inhibitors (RSL3 and ML162) compared with the parental NRK-52E cells, indicating that the system xc-/GPX4 pathway was not involved in the ferroptosis resistance of these clones. To examine whether the FSP1-CoQ10-NAD(P)H pathway contributed to ferroptosis resistance in clone B, we tested the effect of viFSP1, a non-species-specific FSP1 inhibitor (13), on RSL3-induced ferroptosis. Although viFSP1 enhanced RSL3-induced ferroptosis in all cell lines, it did not eliminate the difference in RSL3 sensitivity between the clones and the parental cells, suggesting that the FSP1-CoQ10-NAD(P)H pathway did not contribute to the ferroptosis resistance in these clones either (Fig. 4A). Given the importance of PUFA incorporation into phospholipids in ferroptosis, we next focused on the phospholipid remodeling pathway. To evaluate whether this pathway contributes to ferroptosis resistance in clone B, the amount of cellular non-esterified arachidonic acid (AA), a PUFA closely associated with ferroptosis, was quantified using LC-MS/MS. As expected, clone A showed more than a twofold increase in non-esterified AA compared with the parental NRK-52E cells (Fig. 4B), which aligns with the low ACSL4 expression in clone A, as non-esterified AA is the primary substrate of ACSL4 (4, 12, 14). Unexpectedly, although clone B expressed ACSL4 at levels comparable to the parental cells, it also exhibited significantly elevated non-esterified AA levels, similar to those in clone A (Fig. 4B). As expected, ACSL4 activity was markedly reduced in clones A; notably, clone B also showed a significant decrease in enzymatic activity compared with the parental cells (Fig. 4C). The T237A mutation of ACSL4 confers ferroptosis resistance in clone B

To investigate the cause of reduced ACSL4 catalytic activity in clone B, we cloned the full-length ACSL4 cDNAs from both clone B and the parental NRK-52E cells for sequence analysis. A single-nucleotide substitution (G→A) was identified in clone B, resulting in a missense mutation that converts threonine to alanine at amino acid residue 237 (T237A) of the ACSL4 protein (Fig. 5A). No additional mutations were detected in ACSL4 sequence of clone B. To evaluate the functional consequences of this mutation, we stably introduced wild-type ACSL4 (WT), the T237A mutant (T237A), or an empty vector into clone A, an ACSL4-deficient cell line (Fig. 5B). Upon treatment with ferroptosis inducers, WT-expressing cells completely lost ferroptosis resistance and exhibited a concentration-dependent decrease in viability (Fig. 5C), confirming that the loss of ACSL4 is the primary determinant of ferroptosis resistance in clone A. In contrast, T237A mutant retained partial resistance: its sensitivity was greater than that of the empty vector control but lower than that of WT. Notably, neither WT- nor T237A-expressing cells showed any concentration-dependent difference in apoptosis inducer-mediated cell death compared with empty vector controls. In both WT- and T237A-expressing cells, ferroptosis inhibitor Fer-1 fully rescued cell viability following exposure to ferroptosis inducers (Fig. 6A). RSL3-induced lipid peroxidation was markedly increased in WT-expressing cells and reversed by Fer-1 (Fig. 6B). In addition, non-esterified AA level was significantly lower in WT-expressing cells compared with empty-vector control cells, whereas T237A-expressing cells maintained high AA levels (Fig. 6C). Thr237 and Glu429 of ACSL4 are crucial for its catalytic activity

To elucidate how the T237A mutation impairs ACSL4 function, we compared the sequence of rat ACSL4 with those of other ACSL isozymes and long-chain fatty acyl-CoA synthetase from Thermus thermophilus (ttLC-FACS), the only ACSL family protein with a resolved crystal structure (15). Although rat ACSL4 shares only 28.1% overall amino acid sequence identity with ttLC-FACS, Thr237 of rat ACSL4 is conserved across both proteins (Fig. 8A). The crystal structure of ttLC-FACS (PDB ID: 1v26), solved in complex with Mg2+ and the adenylate intermediate myristoyl-AMP, shows that the corresponding Thr184 participates in Mg2+ coordination and indirectly interacts with the α-phosphate of AMP. Based on the sequence conservation, Thr237 of rat ACSL4 is predicted to serve the same role. Furthermore, Glu429 of rat ACSL4 (corresponding to Glu328 in ttLC-FACS) is also expected to coordinate the same Mg2+ ion. To further support these expectations, rat ACSL4 structure complexed with AMP, oleic acid, and Mg2+ was predicted using AlphaFold 3. Arachidonoyl-AMP was considered the most suitable ligand; however, we used oleic acid together with AMP instead, as neither arachidonoyl-AMP nor arachidonic acid was available in AlphaFold 3. Although oleic acid is not a preferred substrate, it can be utilized by ACSL4 (14). AlphaFold 3 modeling and structural superposition with ttLC-FACS suggested that Thr237 and Glu429 of rat ACSL4 are located in close spatial proximity to the Mg2+ ion (Fig. 8B). AMP and the fatty acid were positioned within the same binding pocket as observed in ttLC-FACS, and the surrounding residues showed low root mean square deviation (RMSD) values, indicating that ttLC-FACS is a structurally suitable reference for ACSL4 (Fig. 8B).

To investigate the role of Glu429 in ferroptosis resistance, we also generated ACSL4-deficient clone A cells stably expressing the E429A mutant (Fig. 9A). Cells expressing E429A displayed marked resistance to ferroptosis, showing a phenotype similar to empty vector controls (Fig. 9B). Consistently, E429A-expressing cells exhibited reduced ACSL4 activity compared with WT-expressing cells, and lipid peroxidation was not enhanced in E429A-expressing cells by RSL3 (Fig. 9, C and D). To further validate the effects of these mutations, we produced recombinant GST-tagged proteins of rat ACSL4 WT, T237A, and E429A and compared their enzymatic activities. Both the T237A and E429A mutants exhibited significantly lower catalytic activity than the WT enzyme (Fig. 9E), consistent with observations in cell lysates (Figs. 6C and 9D). Discussion

Ferroptosis, a regulated form of cell death characterized by iron-dependent lipid peroxidation, has emerged as a critical driver of kidney injury and a promising therapeutic target. Hallmarks of ferroptosis, such as elevated lipid peroxidation and reduced glutathione (GSH) levels, have been observed in kidneys affected by folic acid-induced AKI (16, 17). Notably, radical-trapping antioxidants, which act as ferroptosis inhibitors, can modulate lipid composition and effectively attenuate both AKI and renal fibrosis, suggesting that targeting ferroptosis may represent a promising therapeutic strategy for kidney disease (18, 19, 20). In this study, we demonstrate that loss or mutation of ACSL4 confers ferroptosis resistance (Figs. 3 and 5). ACSL4, an enzyme that preferentially converts PUFA into their CoA derivatives (PUFA-CoAs), is a well-established key promoter of ferroptosis (4). In models of ischemia/reperfusion (I/R) injury, ACSL4 expression is upregulated, and genetic deletion of ACSL4 in renal tubular cells confers resistance to AKI (21). Pharmacological inhibition of ACSL4 similarly ameliorated I/R-induced AKI in mice (22). Furthermore, in diabetic models, including streptozotocin-induced and db/db mice, ACSL4 levels were increased, and GPX4 levels decreased. Treatment with rosiglitazone, an ACSL4 inhibitor, reversed these changes and significantly improved kidney function and survival rates in streptozotocin-induced diabetic mice, indicating that ACSL4 may also serve as a therapeutic target for diabetic nephropathy (23). Collectively, these findings underscore the critical role of ferroptosis and ACSL4 in various kidney diseases, including AKI, renal fibrosis, and diabetic nephropathy.

Despite its functional importance, the crystal structure of ACSL4 has not yet been resolved, and the key amino acid residues required for its enzymatic activity have remained unclear. In this study, we identified a T237A mutation in one of the ferroptosis-resistant clones, demonstrating that Thr237 is critical for ACSL4 catalytic activity (Figs. 5 and 6). Moreover, we found that Glu429 also significantly contributes to ferroptosis. Both Thr237 and Glu429 are conserved across various species and ACSL isozymes (Fig. 7A), suggesting that these residues may be involved in the fundamental functions of ACSL family proteins. Similarly, Kochan et al. reported that these residues are highly conserved among the structurally characterized adenylate-forming enzymes (24). While previous studies suggested that the residues corresponding to Thr237 and Glu429 in rat ACSL4 may coordinate Mg2+ in ttLC-FACS (15), our study is the first to experimentally demonstrate that mutations at these positions significantly reduce ACSL4 activity in rats. Mg2+, owing to its high charge density, acts as a strong Lewis acid (25). When complexed with a phosphate group, Mg2+ can attract electrons from the phosphorus atom, thereby enhancing the electrophilicity of the phosphorus and facilitating the subsequent catalytic reaction. This mechanism likely underlies the critical role of Thr237 and Glu429 in positioning Mg2+ for efficient thioesterification by ACSL4. In ACSL4, Thr237 and Glu429 may contribute to the proper positioning of Mg2+, enhancing the electrophilicity of the phosphorus atom of AMP and thereby promoting thioesterification. These observations are consistent with findings from Blinova et al., who demonstrated through simulations that residues corresponding to Thr237 and Glu429 (Ser197 and Glu343 in firefly luciferase) coordinate Mg2+ during catalysis (26). Taken together, our findings provide a new mechanistic understanding of ACSL4 and may facilitate the development of ACSL4-targeted therapeutics.

Large-scale genetic loss-of-function studies, including genome-wide CRISPR-based genetic screens, have been instrumental in identifying genes that promote ferroptosis in response to pro-ferroptotic stimuli. Magtanong et al. integrated data from 24 published screens, involving a variety of cell lines and pro-ferroptotic conditions, to identify common regulators of ferroptosis (27). Although no gene was universally essential for ferroptosis across all contexts, ACSL4 emerged as one of the most frequently identified regulators. Notably, ACSL4 appears to be more critical for ferroptosis induced by GPX4 inhibition than by cystine deprivation (27). This is consistent with our findings that the GPX4 inhibitors exhibited larger differences in ferroptosis sensitivity between the ferroptosis-resistant clones and the parental cells than the system xc- inhibitor (Fig. 1A).

Since mesenchymal and dedifferentiated cancer cells are often resistant to apoptosis yet remain sensitive to ferroptosis, ferroptosis inducers have emerged as promising anti-cancer therapeutic agents. Compounds such as erastin and RSL3 were originally identified in screens for small molecules that induce non-apoptotic cell death in cancer cells harboring oncogenic RAS mutations (28, 29). However, our results suggest that mutations in key ACSL4 residues, such as Thr237 or Glu429, may impair ACSL4 activity (Figs. 6 and 8) and thereby reduce cancer cell susceptibility to ferroptosis-inducing therapies. These findings might provide clues to select a useful strategy for cancer therapy.

In addition, we considered the molecular basis of the T237A substitution observed in clone B. Ferroptosis involves the production of reactive oxygen species (ROS), which can induce mutations via the incorporation of oxidized guanine and adenine bases, 7,8-dihydro-8-oxoguanine (8-oxoG) and 7,8-dihydro-8-oxoadenine (8-oxoA), respectively. These lesions can lead to G→A and A→G substitutions (30). Although 8-oxoA is less common than 8-oxoG, A→G substitutions are frequently observed in several cancers, including those of the liver, stomach, thyroid, and kidney (31). The A→G substitution identified in clone B (Fig. 5A) may have resulted from the misincorporation of 8-oxoA during DNA replication under oxidative stress.

In clone B, we observed not only the ACSL4 mutation but also slight reductions in the expression of several other genes (Fig. 3A). Among these genes, all except LPCAT3 are known to be anti-ferroptotic and are therefore unlikely to contribute to the ferroptosis resistance observed in clone B. LPCAT3 contributes to ferroptosis by catalyzing the incorporation of PUFA-CoA into lysophospholipids, including lysophosphatidylcholine, lysophosphatidylethanolamine, and lysophosphatidylserine (32, 33). Although LPCAT3 expression was reduced by approximately 27% in clone B (Fig. 3A), the T237A ACSL4-transduced clone A exhibited a level of ferroptosis resistance similar to that of clone B, suggesting that LPCAT3 is less critical than ACSL4. Indeed, LPCAT3 inhibition conferred only partial protection against ferroptosis (34), whereas ACSL4-deficient cells exhibit stronger resistance than LPCAT3-deficient cells (4). Therefore, while the modest reduction in LPCAT3 expression may partially contribute to the moderate ferroptosis resistance observed in clone B, its impact is likely minor compared with that of the ACSL4 T237A mutation.

In summary, we established two ferroptosis-resistant NRK-52E cell clones and identified distinct underlying mechanisms: ACSL4 deficiency in clone A and a T237A point mutation in clone B. The T237A mutation impaired ACSL4 catalytic activity, likely by disrupting Mg2+ coordination, thereby conferring resistance to ferroptosis. Together with our findings on the functional importance of Glu429, these observations provide new mechanistic insights into the structural and functional roles of mammalian ACSL4 and may facilitate the development of ACSL4-targeted therapeutic strategies. Although rat ACSL4 shares high sequence homology with human ACSL4, further studies are warranted to confirm the functional importance of these residues in human systems and to explore their implications for ferroptosis-targeted therapies.

Experimental procedures

Materials

Mouse monoclonal antibodies against β-actin, ACSL4, and FLAG were purchased from Proteintech, Santa Cruz Biotechnology, and Fujifilm Wako (Osaka, Japan), respectively. Arachidonic acid (AA) and arachidonic acid-d8 (AA-d8) were obtained from Cayman Chemical (Ann Arbor, MI, USA). DTT and GSH were purchased from Fujifilm Wako. ATP was obtained from Sigma-Aldrich. CoA was purchased from Oriental Yeast Co. All other reagents used in this study were of analytical grade and obtained from commercial sources.

Cell culture

Normal rat renal tubular epithelial cells (NRK-52E; JCRB IFO50480) were obtained from the JCRB Cell Bank. Cells were maintained in Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle’s Medium (DMEM; Fujifilm Wako) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS; Sigma-Aldrich) and 1% penicillin/streptomycin (Nacalai Tesque) at 37 °C in a humidified atmosphere containing 5% CO2. The ecotropic murine leukemia virus-packaging cell line, Platinum-E (PLAT-E) (35) (kindly provided by Dr Toshio Kitamura, The University of Tokyo) was maintained in DMEM supplemented with 10% FBS, 1 μg/ml of puromycin (InvivoGen), and 10 μg/ml of blasticidin (InvivoGen).

To establish ferroptosis-resistant cells, NRK-52E cells were exposed to 200 nM (1S,3 R)-ethyl-3-(2,3,6-trichlorophenyl)-4,5-dihydro-1H-pyrazole-1-carboxylate (RSL3; Cayman Chemical) for 2 weeks. Two resistant clones were subsequently isolated and designated clone A and clone B.

Cell viability assay

Cells were seeded into 96-well plates at a density of 3000 cells per well and cultured overnight. After preincubating with 2 μM ferrostatin-1 for 30 min, the cells were treated with the indicated compounds for 20 or 24 h. Cell viability was determined using the Cell Counting Kit-8 (CCK-8; Dojindo) according to the manufacturer’s instructions, and viability values were normalized to those of untreated controls. The following compounds were used: RSL3, ML162, erastin, viFSP1 (all from Cayman Chemical); etoposide (Fujifilm Wako); staurosporine (Tokyo Chemical Industry); and ferrostatin-1 (Sigma-Aldrich). Each experiment was performed in triplicate. Data are presented as mean ± SD

Lipid peroxidation measurement

Cells were seeded into 60-mm dishes at a density of 2 × 105 cells per dish and cultured for 2 days. After preincubation with 2 μM of ferrostatin-1 for 30 min, the cells were treated with 500 nM RSL3 for 3 h, collected, and stained with 5 μM BODIPY 581/591 C11 (Invitrogen; D3861) at 37 °C for 30 min. Subsequently, the cells were washed with PBS, resuspended in 500 μl of PBS containing 1% FBS, and analyzed using a Sony SH800 flow cytometer (Sony Corp.). At least 5000 cells were analyzed per sample. Each experiment was performed in triplicate. Data are presented as mean ± SD.

Plasmid construction

The pCX4pur-Flag-Acsl4WT plasmid was generated by inserting a PCR-amplified fragment of rat Acsl4 cDNA, derived from NRK-52E cells and tagged with a C-terminal FLAG sequence, into the EcoRI and NotI sites of the pCX4pur vector. The pGEX-Acsl4WT plasmid was constructed by inserting a PCR-amplified fragment of rat Acsl4 cDNA derived from NRK-52E cells into the EcoRI and NotI sites of the pGEX-6p-1 vector. The mutant plasmids pCX4pur-Flag-Acsl4T237A, pCX4pur-Flag-Acsl4E429A, pGEX-Acsl4T237A, and pGEX-Acsl4E429A were generated using the QuikChange Lightning Site-Directed Mutagenesis Kit (Agilent). The primers for mutagenesis were designed using the Agilent QuikChange Primer Design Tool and synthesized by Hokkaido System Science. The primer sequences are listed in Table S1.

Retroviral infection

To generate clone A or clone B stably expressing FLAG-ACSL4WT, FLAG-ACSL4T237A, or FLAG-ACSL4E429A, PLAT-E retrovirus packaging cells were transfected with pCX4pur, pCX4pur-Flag-Acsl4WT, pCX4pur-Flag-Acsl4T237A, or pCX4pur-Flag-Acsl4E429A using FuGENE6 (Roche Diagnostics, Basel, Switzerland), according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Stable cell lines were generated by retrovirus infection and selected with puromycin (1 μg/ml) for at least 1 week as previously described (36).

Immunoblot analysis

Cells were lysed in RIPA buffer (50 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.5; 150 mM NaCl; 1% NP-40; 0.5% sodium deoxycholate; 0.1% SDS) supplemented with a protease inhibitor cocktail (Nacalai Tesque). Lysates were sonicated for 5 min using a Micro Smash MS-100R (TOMY, Tokyo, Japan), and centrifuged at 21,500g for 10 min at 4 °C. Protein concentrations were determined using the Protein Assay BCA Kit (Nacalai Tesque). The supernatants were mixed with 5 × SDS sample buffer (200 mM Tris-HCl, pH 6.8; 9% SDS; 15% glycerol; 6% 2-mercaptoethanol; bromophenol blue) and boiled at 80 °C for 10 min. The samples were subjected to reducing SDS-PAGE using 10% gels. Immunoblotting was performed as previously described (37). Protein bands were visualized using Chemi-Lumi One Super (Nacalai Tesque) and the WSE-6100 LuminoGraph I chemiluminescence imaging system (ATTO, Tokyo, Japan).

Quantitative real-time PCR (qRT-PCR)

Total RNA was extracted from cells using ISOGEN Ⅱ (NIPPON GENE). RNA concentrations were measured using a V-730 BIO spectrophotometer (JASCO). Reverse transcription was performed using ReverTra Ace qPCR RT Master Mix with gDNA Remover (TOYOBO), according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Quantitative real-time PCR was carried out using TB Green Premix Ex Taq II (Tli RNaseH Plus) (Takara Bio Inc.). The primer sequences used in this study are listed in Table S1. Each experiment was performed independently in triplicate. Data are presented as mean ± SD

Measurement of arachidonic acid

Cells were seeded into 100-mm dishes at a density of 6 × 105 cells per dish and cultured for 2 days. After washing with PBS, the cells were lysed in RIPA buffer supplemented with a protease inhibitor cocktail. Lysates for protein quantification were sonicated for 5 min using a Micro Smash MS-100R (TOMY), centrifuged at 21,500g for 10 min at 4 °C, and protein concentrations were determined using the Protein Assay BCA Kit (Nacalai Tesque), according to the manufacturer’s instructions.

For AA quantification, the lysates were spiked with 8 pmol of AA-d8 per injection and subjected to lipid extraction using the Bligh-Dyer method (38). Briefly, the lysates were mixed with MeOH and CHCl3 in a volume ratio of 10:5:4 (MeOH:CHCl3:H2O, v/v/v) and incubated in the dark at room temperature for 10 min CHCl3 was then added (CHCl3:sample, 5:19, v/v) and vortexed, followed by the addition of 1 M acetic acid (acetic acid:sample, 5:24, v/v) and further vortexed. The mixture was centrifuged at 1500×g for 10 min at 4 °C, and the lower layer was collected and evaporated using a CVE-3110 centrifugal evaporator (EYELA). For standard curve generation, AA standards (0.03125–32 pmol per injection) containing 8 pmol of AA-d8 per injection were prepared and evaporated. All samples and standards were stored at −80 °C until analysis. Before analysis, the samples and standards were derivatized with amino methyl phenyl pyridinium (AMPP). A labeling mixture was freshly prepared by combining (i) ice-cold acetonitrile-N,N-dimethylformamide (4:1, v/v; Fujifilm Wako), (ii) ice-cold 640 mM (3-(dimethylamino)propyl)ethyl carbodiimide hydrochloride (Tokyo Chemical Industry) in Milli-Q water, and (iii) 5 mM N-hydroxybenzotriazole (Sigma-Aldrich) with 15 mM AMPP (Cayman Chemical) in acetonitrile, mixed as a ratio of 1:1:2 (v/v/v). An aliquot (40 μl) of this mixture was added to each vial and vortexed. The samples were then incubated at 95 °C for 30 min, diluted with Milli-Q water, centrifuged at 21,500g for 5 min at 4 °C, and analyzed by LC-MS/MS.

LC-MS/MS analysis was performed using a UPLC Acquity system (Waters) coupled with a triple-quadrupole XevoTQD mass spectrometer (Waters) and a reverse-phase Acquity UPLC BEH C18 column (2.1 × 100 mm, 1.7 μm, Waters). The mobile phase consisted of 0.1% formic acid in water (A) and 0.1% formic acid in acetonitrile (B), delivered at a flow rate of 0.3 ml/min at 40 °C. The gradient was as follows: 0 to 0.5 min, 10% B; 0.5 to 0.51 min, 10 to 45% B; 0.51 to 5 min, 45 to 70% B; 5 to 7 min, 70 to 100% B; 7 to 7.01 min, 100-10% B; 7.01 to 10 min, 10% B. The injection volume was 7.5 μl. Analysis was performed in positive ion mode with the following settings: source temperature, 500 °C; corn voltage, 25V; capillary voltage, 3 V; desolvation gas, 1000 L/h; cone gas, 50 L/h; desolvation temperature, 500 °C. MassLynx v 4.1 software (Waters) was used for instrument control and data processing. AA and AA-d8 were detected using their specific multiple reaction monitoring transitions, with detailed settings listed in Table S2.

Multiple sequence alignment and structure prediction

Multiple sequence alignments of rat ACSL4 with ACSL4 from different species, as well as rat ACSL4 with rat ACSL isozymes and LC-FACS from T. thermophilus, were performed using Clustal Omega (39). Sequence identities were also calculated using Clustal Omega. Uniprot IDs are as follows: rat ACSL4, O35547; human ACSL4, O60488; mouse ACSL4, Q9QUJ7; bovine ACSL4, F1MBW3; pig ACSL4, A0A287BK93; T. thermophilus LC-FACS, Q5SKN9; rat ACSL1, P18163; rat ACSL3, Q63151; rat ACSL5, O88813; rat ACSL6, P33124. The AlphaFold 3 algorithm was used to predict the structure of rat ACSL4 in complex with Mg2+, oleic acid, and AMP (40). The sequence of rat ACSL4 (NP_446075.1) was used for AlphaFold 3 prediction. The highest confidence structure of rat ACSL4 and the crystal structure of T. thermophilus LC-FACS (PDB ID: 1v26) were superimposed using the Integrated Computer-Aided Molecular Design Platform Molecular Operating Environment (MOE, version 2020.09). The resulting structures were colored by root mean square deviation (RMSD) values.

ACSL4 activity assay

For ACSL4 activity measurement using whole-cell lysates, 80 μg of NRK-52E, clone A, or clone B cell lysates, or 32 μg of empty vector-, ACSL4 WT-, T237A-, or E429A-expressing clone A cell lysates in 100 mM Tris-HCl (pH 7.4) were incubated with 200 μM rosiglitazone, an ACSL4 inhibitor, or DMSO at room temperature for 10 min (41). Subsequently, 90 μl of the sample was mixed with a 10 × reaction cocktail (50 mM ATP, 100 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.4; 2.5 mM CoA; 100 μM AA-d8; 0.3% Triton X-100; 10 μM EDTA; 80 mM MgCl2; 50 mM DTT) and incubated at 37 °C for 15 min. The reaction was stopped by adding 600 μl of acetonitrile and vortexing, followed by centrifugation at 18,400g for 30 min at 4 °C. The supernatant was collected and evaporated at 35 °C, and samples were stored at −80 °C until analysis. The ACSL4 activity was calculated by the peak area of AA-d8-CoA of the whole-cell lysate without rosiglitazone minus the peak area of AA-d8-CoA of the whole-cell lysate with rosiglitazone, in order to eliminate the effects of other ACSL isozymes on AA-d8-CoA production. The ACSL4 activity was expressed relative to the average activity of NRK-52E cells or WT ACSL4-expressing clone A.

For ACSL4 activity measurement using recombinant ACSL4 proteins, 90 μl of samples containing 800 ng of recombinant ACSL4 WT, T237A, or E429A proteins in 100 mM Tris-HCl (pH 7.4) were mixed with a 10 × reaction cocktail for recombinant protein (50 mM ATP, 100 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.4; 2.5 mM CoA; 100 μM AA-d8; 0.3% Triton X-100; 10 μM EDTA; 80 mM MgCl2; 50 mM DTT; 1% NP-40) and incubated at 37 °C for 15 min. Samples were prepared for LC-MS/MS analysis as described above.

Before LC-MS/MS analysis, dried samples were reconstituted in 400 μl of Milli-Q water and centrifuged at 21,500g for 10 min at 4 °C. The supernatant was collected and subjected to LC-MS/MS analysis using a UPLC Acquity system (Waters) coupled with a triple-quadrupole Xevo TQD mass spectrometer (Waters) and a reverse-phase Acquity UPLC BEH C18 column (2.1 × 100 mm, 1.7 μm, Waters). The mobile phases were solvent A (5 mM ammonium formate and 0.01% ammonia in water/acetonitrile, 85:15, v/v) and solvent B (water/acetonitrile, 10:90, v/v), delivered at 0.3 ml/min at 40 °C. The gradient was as follows: 0 to 0.5 min, 20% B; 0.5 to 7.5 min, 20 to 70% B; 7.5 to 7.51 min, 70 to 99% B; 7.51 to 9 min, 99% B; 9 to 9.01 min, 99-20% B; 9.01 to 14 min, 20% B. The injection volume was 7.5 μl. Analysis was performed in positive ion mode with settings described above. AA-d8-CoA was detected using its specific multiple reaction monitoring transitions, with detailed settings listed in Table S2.

Preparation of recombinant ACSL4 proteins

Escherichia coli Rosetta 2(DE3)pLysS (Sigma-Aldrich) was used as an expression host. E. coli Rosetta cells harboring pGEX-Acsl4WT, pGEX-Acsl4T237A, or pGEX-Acsl4E429A plasmids were cultivated in Luria-Bertani (LB) medium supplemented with ampicillin (100 μg/ml) at 37 °C with shaking. Overnight cultures were diluted 1:120 into fresh LB medium supplemented with ampicillin (100 μg/ml) and grown at 37 °C for 2.5 h with shaking. The cells were then collected, resuspended in LB medium supplemented with ampicillin (100 μg/ml), isopropyl β-D-1-thiogalactopyranoside (0.1 mM), betain (2.5 mM), and sorbitol (0.5 M), and incubated at 18 °C overnight with shaking.

The cultured cells were pelleted at 8100g for 15 min at 4 °C, resuspended in PBS supplemented with 1% protease inhibitor cocktail (Nacalai Tesque), 5 mM DTT, and 2 mg/ml lysozyme (Tokyo Chemical Industry), and incubated on a seesaw rocker at 4 °C for 30 min. After the addition of 1% Triton X-100, the lysates were incubated again on a seesaw rocker at 4 °C for 1 h. The lysates were treated with a Benzonase endonuclease (Merck Millipore) and MgCl2 (1 mM) for 10 min at 4 °C, followed by centrifugation at 12,000g for 20 min at 4 °C. The supernatant was filtered through a 0.45 μm Durapore PVDF membrane (Merck Millipore) and applied to Pierce Glutathione Spin Columns (0.2 ml resin bed; Merck Millipore). The GST fusion proteins were eluted with 10 mM GSH in 150 mM NaCl and 50 mM Tris-HCl buffer (pH 8.0). The buffer of the eluted protein was exchanged for PBS using a 50 kDa Amicon Ultra-0.5 ml centrifugal filter (Merck Millipore). The protein concentrations were measured using the Protein Assay BCA Kit (Nacalai Tesque).

Statistics

Statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism 9.0 (GraphPad Software). Data are presented as mean ± SD. Comparisons between two groups were performed using an unpaired two-tailed Student’s t test. For multiple group comparisons, one-way ANOVA or two-way ANOVA followed by multiple comparisons test was used. Statistical significance is indicated as follows: p < 0.05 (∗, #, or $), p < 0.01 (∗∗, ##, or $$), p < 0.005 (∗∗∗, ###, or $$$).
