Concise Summary:
An obligate thermophile, the organism exhibits optimal growth at 70°C, with a general growth range spanning 45 to 75°C. It was isolated from an acidic hot spring, confirming its adaptation to extreme, hot, and acidic conditions. The organism demonstrates remarkable metabolic flexibility, maintaining constitutive activity of trace gas oxidation (H† and CO) across a wide thermal gradient. While its optimal growth is at 70°C, H†oxidation activity was successfully measured at significantly lower temperatures, including 10°C, 25°C, and 37°C, and this activity persisted for over two weeks at 10°C. Furthermore, the ability to scavenge trace gases was shown to enhance cell viability during persistence at temperate conditions (25°C) and high temperatures (70°C) when carbon-starved. The study highlights that this metabolic capability allows the organism to sustain energy needs during dispersal through temperate environments, suggesting a broad metabolic plasticity beyond its typical hyperthermophilic niche.

Concise Summary:
The microbial communities inhabiting the Kuril hot springs exhibit distinct thermal zonation correlated with pH. The overall temperature range studied was $30^	ext{o}	ext{C}$ to $100^	ext{o}	ext{C}$, with sampled sites ranging from $40^	ext{o}	ext{C}$ to $94^	ext{o}	ext{C}$. Three primary community groups were identified based on physicochemical parameters: the Circumneutral Bacterial Group (CNB) ($40^	ext{o}	ext{C}$–$79^	ext{o}	ext{C}$), the Acidic Bacterial Group (AB) ($41^	ext{o}	ext{C}$–$64^	ext{o}	ext{C}$), and the Acidic Archaeal Group (AA) ($50^	ext{o}	ext{C}$–$94^	ext{o}	ext{C}$). The AA group is characterized by the highest temperatures and is dominated by taxa adapted to extreme thermophilic conditions. The CNB group shows the highest bacterial diversity and includes both thermotolerant and mesophilic components. The overall diversity is strongly correlated with pH, while temperature correlation was found to be low. The study highlights the presence of taxa adapted to varying thermal niches, from mesophilic to hyperthermophilic, within a single geothermal system.
