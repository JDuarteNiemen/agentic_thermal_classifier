Understanding bacteriophage propagation on bacteria in different physiological conditions is imperative for predicting phage therapy efficacy on various bacterial infections, especially chronic ones. We investigated phage T4 propagation on bacteria E.coli grown in a chemostat at very low dilution rates extending down to 0.027 h−1 and bacteria exposed to nutrient deprivation. An increase in adsorption constant and latent period with dilution rate D decrease and burst size being proportional to dilution rate (D) was confirmed, consistent with previously published results, extending validity of previous findings. Additional bacterial exposure to starvation, either through nutrient cessation or transferring bacteria into SM buffer, sustained phage propagation during first hours of starvation and diminished to formation of a single phage per infected cell after 24 h. Nutrient deprivation effects were investigated on fast growing bacteria and bacteria in a death phase. While no phage generation was observed within bacteria in death phase, fast growing bacteria transferred into SM buffer generated a single phage within 48 h without lysis, indicating that bacterial exposure to nutrient depleted conditions triggers a so called “scavenger response” whose intensity depends on starvation exposure time.

Background

One of the alarming and recurring problems in modern medicine are bacterial infections. These can be acute bacterial infections caused by fast-growing, planktonic bacteria, which are usually treated with antibiotics, or chronic bacterial infections, which are usually less affected by most antibiotics and can overcome the human immune system. Chronic bacterial infections persist for long periods of time and are rarely completely eradicated due to bacteria genetic resistance to antibiotics or phenotypical difference, e.g., slow growth or biofilm formation. In the search for an alternative solution to bacterial antibiotic resistance, bacteriophage therapy has proven to be particularly promising. Bacteriophages, or phages for short, are viruses that infect and kill bacteria. Phages used as therapeutics have many advantages over antibiotics, such as high host specificity, targeting only disease-relevant bacteria and excluding the natural microflora in the body, and the ability to auto-dose at the site of bacterial infection. Numerous studies have also confirmed that phages also affect bacteria in biofilms [1–9].

Despite the benefits of phages as antibacterial agents, phage therapy is not yet widely used clinically as there are still some challenges to overcome, such as standardisation of protocols for accurate dosing, duration of application, prediction of results, clinical monitoring and several others [10, 11]. Strictly lytic phages are suitable for phage therapy since they infect specific bacteria, use bacterial metabolism for profileration and kill the bacteria by lysis [12, 13]. However, clinical efficacy is closely related to how efficiently the phages replicate in situ, which is determined by nutrient environment and the physiological state of the host, often described by a specific growth rate of bacteria (µ) [14–16]. Therefore, understanding the interplay between bacterial growth dynamics and phage replication is crucial. To describe the efficacy of phage infecting bacteria, phage growth parameters are used, such as the adsorption constant (Ka), which represents the rate of adsorption of phages to the bacteria and is usually determined by observing the decrease in phage concentration over time after contact with the bacteria, a latent period (LP), which represents the time period between infection and bacterial lysis, and the burst size (BS), which indicates the number of newly generated phages per infected bacterial cell [17].

The interactions between phages and bacteria growing exponentially under optimal laboratory conditions and in a nutrient-rich environment, are already well known and described. Their metabolic activity and energy pool are high. Phages are highly effective for such bacteria, with short latent periods and high burst sizes. However, successful phage therapy of chronic, long-standing bacterial infections requires also knowledge of phage effect on bacteria growing under the suboptimal conditions, to which bacteria respond with many different strategies [18, 19]. Some bacterial species respond to starvation by differentiating into specialised forms of long-term survival or, more simply, by sporulation, e.g. Bacillus, Streptomycetes and Clostridia [20, 21]. However, most bacterial species, including Gram-negative Escherichia coli, do not form spores but respond to starvation either through an immediate response in which the bacteria execute their protein-based and transcription-based programmes to down-regulate the protein biosynthesis machinery [22], or by reducing their metabolism to a dormant cell form [19–21, 23–29].

To get additional insight into the effect of phages under such conditions, well described model microorganisms bacteria Escherichia coli K-12 (DSM 18039) and an obligately lytic bacteriophage T4 (DSM 4505) were used. We evaluated the ability of bacteriophages to infect and replicate in long-term slow growing bacteria, which are prevalent in nature and frequently associated with chronic and persistent bacterial infections [3, 6, 18, 19]. These bacteria exhibit reduced growth primarily due to a limited nutrient availability in their environment, leading often to increased bacterial resilience and antibiotic resistance, as the efficiency of antibiotics is closely tied to the bacterial metabolism. With lower metabolic activity of bacteria, phages may have limited energy resources to exploit for their own progeny development. Consequently, the efficacy of phage therapy could be compromised in such cases. Slow growing bacteria in nutrient-poor environments allocate their energy and metabolism predominantly to essential functions [30, 31]. Additionally, bacterial general stress responses are upregulated, enhancing bacterial survival mechanisms [32, 33]. Apart from its importance for phage therapy, the study of phage and bacterial dynamics under nutrient-depleted conditions also contributes to a fundamental understanding of microbial ecology [34]. Most natural environments are nutrient-poor, and slow growing bacteria dominate these ecosystems [35]. Investigating how phages behave under starvation conditions provides insights into long term co-evolutionary interactions and survival strategies of phages in real microbial communities.

To mimic such conditions under controlled environment, bacteria were cultured in a continuous operating system called a chemostat, allowing the precise regulation of the specific growth rate of the bacteria, thereby controlling their physiological state through dilution rate adjustments. This setup enabled us to evaluate phage efficacy across different steady state populations of slow growing bacteria.

We also investigated the efficacy of phages on such long-term slow growing bacteria after exposing the cultures to starvation by completely omitting the nutrient supply. In parallel, we investigated the impact of phages on initially fast growing bacteria that were subjected to nutrient deprivation similar to previous experiments, prior to phage infection. Understanding how the initial physiological conditions and starvation affect phage generation and propagation can help predict the potential efficacy of phage therapy.

Materials and methods

Bacteria, bacteriophage and media

The bacterial strain Escherichia coli K-12 MG 1655 (DSM 18039), sourced from the Leibniz Institute DSMZ Germany, and the lytic bacteriophage T4 (DSM 4505) were employed in all experiments. The E.coli bacteria for titration of the bacteriophage and inoculation of the chemostat bioreactor were prepared in laboratory flasks containing Lysogeny Broth (LB medium) (LLG Labware, Meckenheim, Germany) and incubated overnight at 37℃ [36]. LB medium was utilised for continuous and batch bacterial culture cultivation.

For phage titer determination, bacterial sample dilution, and starvation, SM buffer (0.1 g gelatin (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA), 5.8 g NaCl (Merck KGaA, Darmstadt, Germany), 2 g MgSO4 ∙7H2O (Merck KGaA, Darmstadt, Germany), 50 mL 1 M Tris-HCl (pH 7.5) (Fisher Scientific F, Loughborough, UK), diluted with deionized water to a total volume of 1 L) was employed. For determination of Limiting source a standard SM buffer was supplemented individually with 3 different salts, namely 10 mM sodium acetate (Carl ROTH, Karlsruhe, Germany), 10 mM sodium phosphate (Honeywell Fluka, Seelze, Germany), and 10 mM ammonium chloride (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA). Besides, SM buffer without gelatin, and SM buffer without gelatin with 10 mM sodium acetate (Carl ROTH, Karlsruhe, Germany) were prepared.

Enumeration of bacteria, bacteriophages and infective centers

The concentration of E. coli bacteria was assessed using the agar layer method and quantified as colony forming units per mililiter (CFU/mL). Meanwhile, free phages (P) and infective centers (IC), encompassing infected bacteria (IX) and free phages (P), were counted using the double agar overlay plaque assay following the standard method [37], and their concentration was expressed as plaque forming units per mililiter (PFU/mL). All assays were conducted in triplicate, and the average value was computed.

Chemostat setup

The cultivation of E. coli bacteria took place in a continuously operating chemostat system, which consisted of a 25 mL working volume glass bioreactor, positioned on a magnetic stirrer and operated at a stirring speed of 350 rpm. Detailed information regarding chemostat configuration can be found in previous publications [38–40]. Air saturated LB medium was consistently supplied to the chemostat bioreactor at different flow rates of 0.135, 0.060, 0.035, 0.03, 0.0256, 0.0238, and 0.0112 mL/min. These flow rates corresponded to dilution rates of 0.324, 0.144, 0.084, 0.072, 0.0614, 0.0517, and 0.02688 h−1, respectively. The bacterial concentration was monitored through on-line optical density measurements at a wavelength of 600 nm. Steady state conditions were assumed to be achieved after 10 generations of continuous bacterial growth.

After achieving a steady state in the chemostat at various dilution rates, we determined the steady state concentration of bacteria. Subsequently, a bacterial aliquot of 250 µl was withdrawn from the bioreactor and transferred into a sterile 1.5 mL Eppendorf tube to perform one step growth experiment (see section One step growth experiment).

Sample of long-term slow growing bacteria exposed to lack of nutrients

Several 250 µl samples of bacteria growing in chemostat under stead-state conditions at dilution rates of 0.144, 0.0517, and 0.027 h−1 were withdrawn from the bioreactor and transferred into a sterile 1.5 mL Eppendorf tube.

In the first set of samples, we allowed the bacteria to propagate within the Eppendorf tube under batch conditions in the same LB medium.

In the second set, bacteria were centrifuged at 8000 x g for 2 min at 37 ℃ to obtain a bacterial pellet. Subsequently, this pellet was resuspended in 250 µl of SM buffer, representing a nutrient-limited environment.

All samples were incubated at 37 ℃ with agitation at 200 rpm for 3 h. For the lowest dilution rate of 0.027 h−1, the incubation period was extended to 24 h. Following incubation, bacterial concentration was determined, and one step growth experiments were performed (see section One step growth experiment).

Sample of batch growing bacteria

A 3% (v/v) inoculum of overnight-cultured bacteria was introduced into a sterile 25 mL volume of fresh LB medium in a glass Erlenmeyer flask. Bacterial concentration was monitored over time by plating and quantified as colony forming units per milliliter (CFU/mL).

6 mL samples were collected after 2.5 h of bacterial growth, corresponding to exponential growth phase (transfer time 0), and after 26.5 h, corresponding to the death phase. One step growth experiments were conducted for both samples.

Another sample withdrawn after 2.5 h of bacterial growth was centrifuged at 8000 x g for 5 min at 37 ℃ to form a bacterial pellet. The pellet was resuspended in an equal volume of SM buffer, transferred to a sterile Erlenmeyer flask, and the bacterial concentration was monitored. One step growth experiment (see section One step growth experiment) was performed after 0, 3, 24, and 48 h of exposure to SM buffer.

One step growth experiment

During a one step growth experiment, we monitored the progression of free phages (P) and the infective centers (IC). The multiplicity of infection (MOI) ranging from 0.001 to 0.01 was used in all experiments to ensure, according to the Poisson distribution, the infection of the majority of bacteria by a single bacteriophage. Four minutes after infection, the sample containing bacteria and bacteriophages was subjected to a tenfold dilution with SM buffer to decelerate the adsorption of bacteriophages [17]. At each time point, 20 µl of the sample was withdrawn and transferred to a new sterile 1.5 mL Eppendorf tube containing 180 µl of SM buffer. To determine the concentration of free and infective intracellular phages together (Pcl), five drops of chloroform were added to the tube prior to sampling. The sample was incubated on ice for at least 15 min [36, 41]. All samples were plated using the plaque assay method. All one-step growth experiments under each tested condition were performed in biological triplicates to ensure reproducibility and statistical reliability.

Determination of limiting nutrient element

After reaching a steady state in the chemostat at a dilution rate corresponding to the specific growth rate of the bacteria of 0.312 h−1, the steady state concentration of bacteria was determined. Five bacterial aliquots of 250 µl were withdrawn from the bioreactor and transferred to a sterile 2 mL Eppendorf tubes to perform one step growth experiments (see section One step growth experiment). Different formulations of SM buffer were used to dilute the samples containing bacteria and bacteriophages four minutes after infection. The samples were diluted with SM buffer without gelatin, SM buffer without gelatin, supplemented with 10 mM sodium acetate, SM buffer with 10 mM sodium acetate, SM buffer with 10 mM sodium phosphate and SM buffer with 10 mM ammonium chloride.

Bacteriophage growth parameters determination

The adsorption constant (Ka) was determined following a standardized protocol [17]. The adsorption constant (Ka) was obtained from the initial time points of the one step growth experiment after introducing bacteriophages to the bacteria. P represents phage concentration at a specific time point, P0 concentration of phages added to the bacteria and X the initial concentration of bacteria.

From analysis of the experimental data, we determined different time periods: eclipse (EP) and latent period (LP) according to standardized protocol [17], and generation period (GP), defined as a time from the phage infection till formation of intracellular infective phages was completed. GP was employed only in cases where no bacterial lysis occurred, therefore latent period determination was not possible. Burst size (BS) was also calculated according to standardized protocol [17], by dividing the number of phages formed during rise period with the concentration of infected bacteria (IX). The number of generated phages (GF) was calculated as the difference between Pcl stabilized value after rise and the concentration of residual free phages (P) after adsorption was decelerated (after SM dilution). Infected bacteria concentration (IX), was determined by subtracting the concentration of residual free phages (P) after the adsorption was decelerated from the initial concentration of free phages (P0). Determination of all parameters was calculated after first phage rise was completed. The growth parameters of bacteriophages obtained for each physiological state of bacteria were subsequently used to calculate bacteriophage fitness (F). Statistical analysis

All parameters were determined using three independent biological replicates (n = 3) for each experimental condition, which Limits statistical significance. Therefore, results should be interpreted with caution, especially for marginal p-values. Statistical analyses were performed in OriginPro 8.5 (OriginLab Corp., USA), and significance was set at p < 0.05.

For chemostat experiments (nine dilution rates, D [h⁻¹]), the variation of phage growth parameters (GF, IX, EP, LP, Ka, F) across dilution rates was assessed by one-way ANOVA. The initial concentrations of bacteria (X₀) and bacteriophages (P₀) were measured at the beginning of each experiment to ensure consistency between replicates. The number of generated phages (GF) and the concentrations of infected bacteria (IX) were log₁₀-transformed prior to statistical testing to stabilize variance and improve normality. Burst size (BS) was analysed separately using unweighted linear regression, with standard deviations displayed as error bars.

For one step growth experiments with long-term slow growing bacteria exposed to lack of nutrients in LB and SM, two designs of two-way ANOVA were performed. First, the effects of dilution rate (D = 0.144, 0.057, 0.027 h⁻¹) and medium (LB vs. SM) were tested after 3 h of bacterial batch growth. Second, the effects of incubation time (3 h vs. 24 h) and medium (LB vs. SM) were tested at a fixed dilution rate (D = 0.027 h⁻¹). In both experimental designs, interactions between the factors (time × medium, D × medium) were included, and post-hoc pairwise comparisons with Bonferroni and Tukey corrections were performed.

In batch-to-batch transfer experiments, bacteria grown in LB for 2.5 h were either maintained in the same medium (batch growth: 2.5 h vs. 26.5 h) or transferred to SM buffer. In LB, Student’s t-test was used to compare X₀, IX, LP, Ka, BS, and F between the two time points. In SM buffer, one-way ANOVA was performed for each parameter (X₀, IX, LP, Ka, BS, F) across incubation times (0 h, 3 h, 24 h, 48 h), followed by Bonferroni and Tukey post-hoc tests when significant effects were detected. For BS and F, ANOVA was not applicable due to lack of variance. Finally, a two-way ANOVA was performed to test the effects of growth time (0 h vs. 24 h) and medium (LB vs. SM), including interaction effects, with post-hoc pairwise comparisons by Bonferroni and Tukey.

Normality and homogeneity of variance were verified visually for all tests.

Results

Bacteriophage propagation in long-term slow growing bacteria

E. coli bacteria were cultivated in a continuously operated chemostat system, growing in LB medium at varying flow rates, defining a specific growth rate of the bacteria in the range between 0.027 and 0.324 h−1. Upon achieving steady state conditions, the bacteria were sampled and subjected to bacteriophage infection, with subsequent monitoring of free phage (P) concentration and concentration of infective centers (IC) over at least two multiplication cycles. Results are presented in Fig. 1. Notably, given that implemented E. coli strain exhibits its maximal specific growth rate of 1.018 h−1 in the absence of nutrient limitation, it can be reasonably inferred that, under the employed dilution rates, the bacterial population was growing under nutrient limitation.

Fig. 1 Concentration of free bacteriophages (P) and infective centers (IC) during one step growth experiment performed on bacteria growing in chemostat under dilution rates of 0.324 h−1, 0.312 h−1, 0.24 h−1, 0.144 h−1, 0.084 h−1, 0.072 h−1, 0.061 h−1, 0.057 h−1 and 0.027 h−1. Data represent mean values from three independent biological replicates, with error bars indicating standard deviation (SD)

For all investigated dilution rates (D), one can observe an initial decrease in the concentration of free bacteriophages (P) as a consequence of phage adsorption to bacteria. An increase in the concentrations of free phages (P) and infective centers (IC) transpired within the time frame between 27 and 55 min. This is interesting, especially for the lowest dilution rate of 0.027 h−1, which corresponds to a doubling time of the bacteria of approximately 26 h, as previous reports speculated that there might be a critical threshold for the dilution rate of about 0.033 h−1 [16], below which, no phages are generated.

To quantify bacteriophage propagation, phage growth parameters were determined for each dilution rate and their trend was plotted in Fig. 2. Besides, steady state concentration of the bacteria in chemostat (X0), phage concentration immediately after injection (P0) (Fig. 2A), concentration of infected bacteria (IX) and generated phages (GF) within infected bacteria during the first lytic cycle were determined (Fig. 2B). One-way ANOVA performed on log₁₀-transformed values showed significant effects of dilution rate on both IX (p < 0.001) and GF (p < 0.0001). Two distinct time periods, namely latent (LP) and eclipse period (EP) were evaluated. Similar to the previous reports [15, 38, 39], the latent period decreases with an increase of dilution rate, but trend is less pronounced (Fig. 2C). Both LP and EP increased significantly with decreasing dilution rate (p < 0.0001 for each, one-way ANOVA). Also, the adsorption constant (Ka) followed the same trend as described previously [38] (Fig. 2D), with no significant variation between biological replicates (p = 0.588).

Fig. 2 Phage growth parameters obtained from the first lytic cycle of one step growth experiments with E.coli cultured in a chemostat at the dilution rates shown in Fig. 1: (A) Initial concentration of bacteria (X0) and bacteriophages (P0); (B) Concentration of infected bacteria (IX) and generated phages (GF); (C) Latent period (LP) and Eclipse period (EP); (D) Adsorption constant (Ka); (E) Burst size (BS), which increased linearly with dilution rate (slope = 86.69 ± 3.98, R² = 0.985). The dashed Line indicates the regression fit, and dotted Lines represent the 95% confidence interval; (F) Bacteriophage fitness (F, red squares) decreased with decreasing dilution rate, in agreement with model predictions λ = λmax*D/(Kλ+D) (λmax = 11.1 h−1; Kλ=0.39 h−1 [38], black dashed line). ANOVA confirmed significant differences between all dilution rates (p < 0.0001). Error bars indicate ± SD of three independent biological replicates

Burst size (BS) values increased proportionally with dilution rate (slope = 86.69 ± 3.98, R² = 0.985, p < 0.0001; Fig. 2E), which is consistent with findings in several other studies [16, 38, 39]. The 95% confidence interval for the slope ranged from 77.27 to 96.11, indicating the robustness of the observed trend. As phage growth parameters define phage fitness the decrease in dilution rate was associated with a corresponding decrease in phage fitness, albeit slightly slower than previously predicted (Fig. 2F) [16, 38].

Bacteriophage propagation in long-term slow growing bacteria exposed to lack of nutrients

Bacteria cultivated in a chemostat at different dilution rates, were latter subjected batch conditions, omitting any supply of fresh nutrients. The main idea was to further slow-down the growth of bacteria and thereby enhance their maintenance mechanisms, conditions under which bacteria became more resistant to antibacterial agents [3, 18, 19]. Three dilution rates, corresponding to specific bacterial growth rates of 0.144, 0.057, and 0.027 h−1, were tested. Lack of nutrients was investigated by exposing them to two different environments. After bacteria sample was withdrawn from the chemostat, half of the sample was transferred into the Eppendorf tube, while the other half was centrifuged, washed and resuspended in the same volume of SM buffer to remove residual medium, before being transferred to another Eppendorf tube. Bacteria were left without nutrient addition for a different period of time before infection with phages. Experiment performed with modification of LB medium and SM buffer composition (see Supplement Figure S1, Table S1), demonstrated that for both media, carbon is a limiting source, thus no metabolic shift due to transfer is expected.

Measurement of bacterial concentration during exposure to lack of nutrients revealed that in both cases bacteria remained viable without significant decrease in concentration and even expressed some additional growth (Table S2). Bacteria remaining in LB medium exhibited pronounced phage adsorption for all dilution rates. Phages effectively propagated and lysed bacteria to which no fresh nutrients were supplied for 3 h (Fig. 3A, C, E), period time being shorter than doubling time of fastest growing bacteria. However, no increase in free phage concentration (P) was observed for bacteria grown initially at a rate of 0.027 h−1 which were exposed to batch conditions for 24 h, being close to bacteria doubling time (Fig. 3G). These results seem to indicate absence of bacterial cell lysis, what can be concluded from the constant concentration of infective centers (IC) over time. Interestingly, infective phages were generated within the bacteria (demonstrated by an increase of phage concentration after approximately 90 min after infection, if sample was treated with a chloroform) and have reached the same level as the concentration of infective centers (IC). Due to the absence of infective centers (IC) concentration increase, no latent period can be determined, therefore generation period (GP) was used instead to allow comparison of results. Data are summarised in Table S2.

Fig. 3 Concentration of free bacteriophages (P) and infective centers (IC) during one step growth experiment performed on bacteria exposed to 3 h incubation in LB medium, initially growing in chemostat at dilution rate of (A) 0.144 h−1; (C) 0.057 h−1; (E) 0.027 h−1; (G) exposed to 24 h incubation in LB medium, initially growing at dilution rate of 0.027 h−1; exposed to 3 h incubation in SM buffer, initially growing at dilution rate of (B) 0.144 h−1; (D) 0.057 h−1; (F) 0.027 h−1; (H) exposed to 24 h incubation in SM buffer, initially growing at dilution rate of 0.027 h−1. Error bars represent ± SD of three independent biological replicates

Bacteria resuspended in SM buffer also exhibited phage adsorption but to smaller extend, resulting in a lower concentration of infected bacteria, even though the initial bacterial concentration was practically the same as in LB medium. This cannot be attributed to a pH shock as pH value of both media was similar (between 6.7 and 7 in LB medium and between 7 and 7.5 in SM buffer). The reason for the lower adsorption in SM buffer after exposure to lack of nutrients is to be further investigated, as infection of bacteria with phages immediately after transfer into SM buffer exhibited same adsorption kinetics as in the LB medium (Fig. 4A, C). Similar to LB medium, phages propagated and lysed bacteria when lacking nutrient supply for 3 h (Fig. 3B, D, F), again less intensively than in LB medium. Same was true for infection performed after 24 h of bacteria exposure to SM buffer (Fig. 3H). Again, some infective phages were formed inside the bacteria, reaching concentration of infective centers (IC), while the concentration of infective centers (IC) remained constant.

Fig. 4 Concentration of free bacteriophages (P) and infective centers (IC) during one step growth experiment performed on bacteria growing in LB medium under batch conditions for (A) 2.5 h and (B) 26.5 h, and on bacteria exposed to incubation in SM buffer for (C) 0 h; (D) 3 h; (E) 24 h, and (F) 48 h following an initial 2.5 h growth period in LB medium. Error bars represent ± SD of three independent biological replicates

Comparing the values in Table S2 it can be concluded that there are differences between phage parameters obtained from bacteria in LB medium and SM buffer. For all dilution rates, LB medium exhibited a higher adsorption constant, shorter latent period and higher burst size or number of generated phages, overall resulting in higher phage fitness. More importantly, fitness value above 2 was obtained in all cases except for 24-hour starvation, indicating efficient phage propagation. However, if exposure to lack of nutrients lasts too long, no phage propagation occurs despite their generation within bacterial cells.

Two-way ANOVA of the 3 h batch experiments showed significant effects of both dilution rate (D) and medium on all parameters, with significant D × medium interactions (p < 0.05). IX and LP were significantly higher in LB than in SM at D = 0.144 and 0.057 h⁻¹, but not at 0.027 h⁻¹. Ka, BS, and F decreased with decreasing D in both media, although the values were consistently higher in LB. At D = 0.027 h⁻¹, comparison between 3 h and 24 h incubation showed significant effects of both time and medium on all parameters except LP, for which only time was significant (p < 0.05). IX, Ka, BS, and F were higher in both media after 3 h than after 24 h, with a greater decrease in LB. LP increased after 24 h regardless of the medium.

Bacteriophage effect on fast growing bacteria exposed to lack of nutrients

Complementary to the previous experiments with long-term slow growing bacterial cultures, we focus here on the dynamics of phage infection in initially fast growing bacteria that prioritise growth over maintenance mechanisms. To investigate this, we performed a batch cultivation in LB medium. After 2.5 h of bacterial growth (transfer time equal to 0), when the bacterial population was already in the exponential growth phase with a maximum specific growth rate of 1.018 h−1, two samples were taken. The first sample was transferred directly into an Erlenmeyer flask, while the other was first centrifuged, washed and resuspended in SM buffer before being transferred to another Erlenmeyer flask.

Bacteria samples were taken from both Erlenmeyer flasks, as indicated in Figure S2. Samples of bacteria in LB medium, were taken after a transfer time of 0 and 24 h (when the bacterial population had already entered the death phase, see Figure S2), and infected with phages. The concentration of free phages (P) and infective centers (IC) was monitored over time (Fig. 4A, B). Bacteria in the exponential growth phase (transfer time 0) allowed rapid phage propagation and yielded high burst sizes (Fig. 4A). However, this was not the case for bacteria in the death phase (Fig. 4B). Although the phages adsorbed on the bacteria, no subsequent increase in their concentration was observed during the experiment, indicating that no infective phages were generated. In agreement with this, Student’s t-test confirmed significant differences between phage growth parameters (X₀, IX, LP, Ka, BS, F; p < 0.05).

In contrast to a decrease in the bacterial concentration that remained in the LB medium, the bacteria resuspended in SM buffer exhibited almost constant concentration over a period of several days (Figure S2). The effect of phages was investigated on samples taken at 0, 3, 24, and 48 h after transfer (Fig. 4C – F). Notably, a decrease in the concentration of infected bacteria (IX) is observed with prolonged exposure to nutrient deprivation, together with a prolongation of generated period (GP). The number of infective centers (IC) remained constant over time in all samples, indicating the absence of lysis. The parameter values are summarized in Table S3. One-way ANOVA across incubation time experiments in SM buffer revealed significant effects for X₀ (p = 2.65 × 10⁻⁵), IX (p = 2.16 × 10⁻⁶), LP (p = 2.47 × 10⁻⁵), and Ka (p = 1.56 × 10⁻³), with post-hoc analyses showing a decrease in X₀ and IX, prolonged LP, and decreased Ka after 24–48 h. In contrast, BS and F showed no variance and could not be statistically analysed, suggesting impaired propagation under prolonged starvation.

Interestingly, despite the strong adsorption and generation of infective phages within the bacterial cells, their value, after completion of generation corresponds to the number of infective centers (IC) (Fig. 4C – F). Similar to the adsorption process at low MOI, where one free phage is generated per infected bacterial cell, after adsorption and thus the number of infective centers (IC) is maintained, lysis of an infected bacterial cell containing a single phage is an opposite process, where the number of infective centers (IC) is also maintained. To investigate whether lysis occurred, sample filtration was performed through a 0.22 μm filter, which allowed the released phages to pass through but retained the bacterial cells, including the infected ones. In the case of bacterial lysis, the concentration of phages in the permeate would correspond to the number of infective centers (IC) after latent period, whereas in the absence of lysis, the concentration should be lower, and correspond to the concentration of remaining free phages. The results of a representative experiment are shown in Figure S3, and demonstrate that the concentration of free phages on the filtered sample remained constant after adsorption was completed, even when chloroform detected the generation of intracellular phages. The results thus confirm that there is no bacterial lysis, as previously suspected. Finally, two-way ANOVA showed that both incubation time (0 h vs. 24 h) and medium (LB vs. SM) significantly affected all parameters (X₀, IX, Ka, LP, BS, F), with strong interaction effects in each case (all p < 0.05). Post-hoc comparisons confirmed that the time effects were strongly dependent on medium, with IX, Ka, and F decreasing more steeply over time in LB, while LP increased regardless of medium.

Discussion

In this work, we reported different outcomes of phage infection on slow and fast growing bacteria being exposed to different substrate limitations. The experiments were performed with Escherichia coli K-12 bacteria and obligately lytic phage T4. Generally, development of phages depends on the physiological state of bacterial hosts [14–16, 38, 39]. In long-term slow growing bacteria, phages tend to reduce burst size and lengthen latent period, whereas in fast growing bacteria phages propagate fast and in large numbers. In nutrient rich environment bacteria tend to grow faster and as bacteria adjusts its physiology toward growth, it becomes less adapted for survival, whereas with the depletion of nutrients bacterial growth rate decreases and bacteria increases its investment into maintenance mechanisms enabling survival [30]. As the growth conditions of E. coli before starvation influence the later death rate during carbon starvation [42, 43], it is reasonable to hypothesize that this might also impact the ability of phages to propagate within such bacterial hosts. Therefore, our study was segmented into two main parts where phage efficacy was tested on slow growing and fast growing bacteria exposed to starvation.

Phage development in slow growing bacteria

Long-term, slow growth of bacteria was achieved in a continuously operating system chemostat enabling to establish steady state of the bacterial population and to predetermine the growth rate by adjusting the dilution rate. Bacteria were cultured in LB medium, in which the growth of E.coli is carbon limited [44], at several growth rates, well below the maximum growth rate of 1.018 h−1, obtained by dilution rates of 0.324, 0.312, 0.24, 0.144, 0.084, 0.072, 0.061, 0.057, and 0.027 h−1, representing 31.8, 30.6, 23.6, 14.1, 8.3, 7.1, 6.0, 5.6 and 2.6% o the maximum growth rate, respectively. When steady state conditions in the chemostat were achieved, bacteria were sampled and one step growth experiments where performed. To provide short phage adsorption time, the samples were diluted with SM buffer four minutes after the bacteriophage addition [17]. During the experiments, the concentration of infective centers (IC) and free phages (P) was monitored together with infective phages that were generated within bacteria but not released (Pcl). For all dilution rates, successful phage development and propagation occurred. Phage propagation was quantitatively described through determination of phage growth parameters such as adsorption constant (Ka), eclipse (EP) and latent period (LP), burst size (BS) and calculated phage fitness (F). With these experiments it was demonstrated that infective phages were generated and released in all E. coli bacterial cultures, even when the specific growth rate was as low as 0.027 h−1, corresponding to a doubling time of approximately 26 h (Fig. 1).

Expectedly, as the dilution rate decreased, the adsorption constant (Ka) and latent period (LP) slightly increased (Fig. 2C, D). However, trends were less pronounced than in published studies [15, 45–47], with latent period being between 36 and 55 min for highest dilution rates. Also, there was a consistent decrease in burst sizes (BS) in accordance with decreasing dilution rates (Fig. 2E) as already reported by Nabergoj et al.(2018) [38], where the relation between the burst size and the dilution rate is a Linear function with the characteristic slope of 89.5. Our results also indicated a Linear trend between burst size and dilution rate with a similar slope of 86.69 extending even below the dilution rate of 0.03 h−1. This linearity was statistically supported (unweighted regression, R² = 0.985, p < 0.0001), which emphasizes the robustness of the observed relationship despite the small number of replicates. Since the phage growth parameters determine phage fitness (Fig. 2F), representing the rate of phage propagation, this also means a more efficient propagation of phages under extreme limitation than previously predicted [16, 38, 39]. This is an indication that phages efficiently lyse bacteria even under long-term nutrient deprivation, which to some extend mimics chronic infections, suggesting the potential of phage therapy under such circumstances.

Phage effect on long-term slow growing bacteria exposed to lack of nutrients

Encouraging results of previous set of experiments stimulated us to investigate even more extreme nutrient Limitation conditions. Already long-term slow growing bacteria were exposed to additional starvation, to further slow down their growth and enhance their maintenance mechanisms, potentially rendering them even more resistant to antibacterials. Three dilution rates were investigated, corresponding to specific bacterial growth rates of 0.144, 0.057, and 0.027 h−1, defining the initial physiological state of the bacteria. E. coli was exposed to two different environments deprived of nutrients to assess the effectiveness of phages.

One environment was achieved by switching from a chemostat to a batch cultivation mode, simply by stopping inlet and outlet flow. Under such conditions, the bacteria experience a gradual depletion of carbon sources in LB medium over time [44]. Experiments demonstrated that most bacteria growing at initial growth rate of 0.027 h−1 remained viable in the medium after 24 h incubation, consistent with the bacterial behaviour described in the literature [48]. There the growth and death trends of bacteria in common culture media was investigated and found that some bacterial species could persist in the culture medium for extended period after inoculation, even without additional nutrients.

Second starvation environment was prepared by centrifuging the bacteria from the chemostat, washing them and resuspending in SM buffer. In this way, we exposed them to a carbon- limiting environment and removed toxic metabolites that may have accumulated in the chemostat.

Experiments with phages demonstrated, that they effectively adsorbed to and lysed bacteria that had been exposed for 3 h to batch cultivation across all initial bacterial growth rates in LB medium as well as in SM buffer. Notably, the increase in phage concentration was more substantial and faster in bacteria growing in LB medium compared to SM buffer which resulted in higher burst size and shorter latent period (Table S2).

However, in the case of bacteria initially growing at a rate of 0.027 h−1 that had been incubated for 24 h, both in LB medium and in SM buffer, phages successfully generated within the bacterial cells, but no increase in free phages was observed at a monitored time frame. Trend was more pronounced in LB medium than in SM buffer (Fig. 3G, H) where twice as many bacteria were infected at the same MOI ratio. Data indicate that inside each infected bacteria only a single phage was generated, disabling their further propagation.

It seems that under tested conditions, a phenomenon mentioned as “scavenger response” took place [49]. During experiments studying hibernation, author speculated, that in certain cases, when phage infection occurs during bacterial stationary growth phase, on average a single phage per infected cell is produced. However, no details how to systematically induce this phenomenon were provided. The quantitative evaluation of this phenomenon, indication its dependence on environmental conditions, is presented in Table S2.

Phage effect on batch growing bacteria exposed to lack of nutrients

While in previous experiments lack of nutrients was investigated on long-term slow growing bacteria, we also extended this research to fast growing and non-growing bacteria, achieved through bacteria batch cultivation. In particular, we were interested what effect has lack of nutrients on bacteria growing at maximal specific growth rate and death phase. Nutrient deprivation was implemented in a similar manner as previously described for long-term slow growing bacteria.

Fast growing bacteria in general prioritise growth over maintenance mechanisms, and when exposed to carbon starvation, they remain metabolically active due to the rapid utilisation of intracellular glycogen reserves [50]. Therefore, bacteria should have enough energy for phages to be utilised for their propagation. On the other hand, during death phase, achieved by allowing batch bioprocess to run for additional 24 h (Figure S2), energy reserve should be minimal.

Comparison of phage propagation for infection performed during maximal growth rate and death phase demonstrated, expectedly, significant differences (Fig. 4A, B, Table S3). While fast growing bacteria allowed fast propagation of phages (Fig. 4A, Table S3), no new phages were detected during the bacterial death phase (Fig. 4B, Table S3). As most nutrients in the environment were already depleted [30, 44, 51], the maintenance mechanisms were enhanced to facilitate the survival of the bacterial population [31, 52]. Phages successfully adsorbed, but in contrast to initially long-term slow growing bacteria at the dilution rate of 0.027 h−1 exposed to lack of nutrients supply for 24 h in LB medium (Fig. 3G), no phage generation inside bacteria was detected during the monitored period. One explanation for this could be hibernation, a phenomenon described as a reversible dormant state in which phage enzymes are produced by infected cells and the development of phage particles is halted until nutrients become available [49] or pseudolysogeny [53–56], a similar phenomenon described with a broad definition as arrested phage development in the host cell, without replication of the phage genome, without integration into the host cell, without degradation of the viral genome and with the potential to reactivate to lytic activity under favourable conditions [55]. Both phenomena describe a state in which phage development is presumably halted, therefore no infective intracellular phages are generated.

Bacteria that were initially fast growing and were then exposed to lack of nutrients may be less adapted for survival than initially slow growing bacteria. Slow growth of bacteria may result in longer survival, as the anticipation of starvation conditions leads to higher investment in maintenance [43]. Initially fast growing bacteria exposed to nutrient deprivation during starvation appears unable to switch between growth and maintenance in a short period of time, leading to a different phage response. On the other hand, when initially long-term slow growing bacteria, prioritising maintenance, are exposed to starvation, phages seem to be able to utilise remaining bacteria energy for their propagation.

Parallel to experiments in LB medium, we exposed the bacteria to lack of nutrients in SM buffer for several days by removing original medium and resuspending them in SM buffer (Figure S2 – blue triangles). Fast growing bacteria may possess the ability to adapt swiftly to diverse environmental conditions and its adaptability is presumed to be attributed to glycogen storage, which can act as a critical reservoir of essential nutrients when external nutrient sources are lacking [50], thus initiating maintenance mechanisms essential for sustaining the bacterial population. As we monitored the bacterial population in SM buffer, we observed a striking stability in bacterial concentration. It resembled to the population in the stationary phase, due to absence of any growth (Figure S2). To evaluate the efficiency of phage propagation within this specific bacterial state, we conducted one step growth experiments at intervals of 0, 3, 24, and 48 h after transfer into SM buffer (Fig. 4C – F).

Interestingly, even at the beginning of bacterial cultivation in SM buffer, where one might anticipate ample energy available for phage proliferation, no increase in phage titer was detected. The phages successfully adsorbed to the bacterial cells and new intracellular phages were generated, but only a single phage per bacterial cell (Fig. 4C). Experiments with filtration confirmed the absence of bacterial lysis within the monitored time frame (Example shown in Figure S3). A similar, but less pronounced trend, was observed when examining bacteria incubated for 3, 24 and 48 h in SM buffer (Fig. 4D, E, F). It looks as if the bacteria possess sufficient energy for the phage generation but the cycle is stopped before lysis. Similar trends are observed for long-term slow growing bacteria exposed to lack of nutrients for 24 h (Fig. 3G, H), while in bacteria infected during the death phase in LB medium (Fig. 4B), no phages were generated.

By exposing bacteria to nutrient deprivation either by prolonged cultivation in LB medium or transferring bacteria to SM buffer, three different regimes can be envisioned: phage propagation regime representing well investigated complete lytic cycle within which phages are generated and released during cell lysis, scavenger response regime, where single phage per infected cell is generated by utilising whatever few nutrients the host possesses [49] but without cell lysis, and hibernation or pseudolysogenic regime, where infection occurs, host DNA is degraded and utilised for phage DNA construction but no infective phages within the cell are generated [49, 53–56]. Propagation regime is characterised by a positive phage fitness dependent on a long-term stable bacterial physiological state (maximal growth rate in batch experiment, chemostat growth). Scavenger regime seems to be limiting regime of infective phage generation, as only single phage per infected cell is formed. Further investigations are required to determine whether cell lysis can also occur in this case. Finally, by further lack of cell energy, no infective phages can be formed, but only particular phage components are synthesised. Understanding the parameters that influence phage propagation of the particular bacteria– phage system under different conditions could therefore facilitate the prediction of the efficacy of phage therapy in certain types of infections. However, it should be noted, that the statistical analyses were performed with data from three biological replicates per condition. Whilst this limits the statistical power and increases the uncertainty around some estimates, the consistent trends in the independent experiments, and agreement with the published literature, give confidence in the robustness of the conclusions. The inclusion of exact p-values, ANOVA tests, and regression statistics in this study increases transparency without overstating the significance.

Conclusions

In this study, we investigated the effects of bacterial growth conditions on phage infection and multiplication under different substrate limitations. Our results emphasise the influence of bacterial physiological state on bacteriophage efficacy and suggest potential implications for phage therapy applications. In T4 bacteriophages exposed to slow–growing E. coli bacteria, the phages were able to successfully multiply even in long-term slow-growing bacteria, resulting in a smaller burst size and longer latent periods. A Linear relationship between burst size and bacterial growth rate was observed, which is consistent with previous studies. The phages efficiently lysed the bacteria even under extreme nutrient Limitation, indicating their potential to treat chronic infections. Under extreme nutrient deprivation, the phages also successfully infected bacteria, but showed a shift in multiplication dynamics. In bacterial batch cultures, the bacteriophages showed higher burst sizes and shorter latent periods compared to those in SM buffer. In bacteria exposed to starvation for 24 h, phages were generated but no increase in free phages was observed in the medium, indicating a scavenger-like response in which only a single phage was produced per cell. Fast-growing bacteria allowed for rapid phage propagation, whereas phage infection of bacteria in the death phase did not result in detectable phage production. Phages were able to successfully adsorb to nutrient-deprived bacteria but showed arrested development, similar to a very interesting phage regime, hibernation or pseudolysogeny. Bacteriophages infecting fast-growing bacteria exposed to long-term starvation lead to intracellular phage generation without lysis. The results suggest that bacterial adaptation to nutrient limitation has a crucial influence on phage propagation. Demonstrated trends are so far limited to the bacteria – bacteriophage system E.coli – T4 and LB medium and SM buffer. Before general conclusions can be drawn, the described approach should be extended to other media containing other limiting sources and to other bacteria – bacteriophage systems. Understanding these dynamics may improve phage therapy strategies, particularly in the control of infections where bacterial growth rates and thus physiological state vary.
