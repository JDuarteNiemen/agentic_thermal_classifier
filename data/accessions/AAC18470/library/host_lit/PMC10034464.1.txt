The most unique characteristic of Mycobacterium tuberculosis is persistence in the human host, and the biofilm formation is related to the persistance. Polyphosphate (polyP) kinase 1 (PPK1) is conserved in Mycobacteria and is responsible for polyP synthesis. polyP is a chain molecule linked by high-energy phosphate bonds, which is considered to play a very important role in bacterial persistence. However, the relationship of PPK1 and mycobacterial biofilm formation is still adequately unclear. In current study, ppk1-deficient mutant (MT), ppk1-complemented (CT) and wild-type strains of M. smegmatis mc2 155 were used to investigate the formation, morphology and ultramicrostructure of the biofilm and to analyze the lipid levels and susceptibility to vancomycin antibiotic. And then WT, MT and CT strains were used to infect macrophages and to analyze the expression levels of various inflammatory factors, respectively. We found that PPK1 was required for M. smegmatis polyP production in vivo and polyP deficiency not only attenuated the biofilm formation, but also altered the phenotype and ultramicrostructure of the biofilm and reduced the cell lipid composition (except for C16.1 and C17.1, most of the fatty acid components from C8–C24). Moreover, the ppk1-deficient mutant was also significantly more sensitive to vancomycin which targets the cell wall, and its ability to survive in macrophages was decreased, which was related to the change of the expression level of inflammatory factors in macrophage. This study demonstrates that the PPK1 can affect the biofilm structure through affecting the content of short chain fatty acid and promote intracellular survival of M. smegmatis by altering the expression of inflammatory factors. These findings establish a basis for investigating the role of PPK1 in the persistence of M. tuberculosis, and provide clues for treating latent infection of M. tuberculosis with PPK1 as a potential drug target.

Highlights

• PPK1 is essential for polyP accumulation in bacterial cells of M. smegmatis.

• ppk1 is vital for formation, the morphology and ultramicrostructure of biofilm formed by M. smegmatis.

• ppk1 is related to the reduction of short chain fatty acids and the sensitivity of M. smegmatis to vancomycins.

• ppk1 is necessary for intracellular survival of M. smegmatis and alternation of expression levels of inflammatory factors.

1 Introduction

Tuberculosis (TB) remains a major global health problem [1,2]. There are many difficulties in successfully controlling TB, including the prolonged treatment course required to cure active TB. The standard chemotherapy for drug sensitive M. tuberculosis is to treat with isoniazid, rifampicin, ethambutol and pyrazinamide for two months, and then isoniazid and rifampicin for another four months [3]. Although two months of treatment can make the sputum of most patients free of M. tuberculosis, another four months of treatment can minimize the probability of recurrence [4]. The purpose of long-term chemotherapy is to kill rare drug-resistant M. tuberculosis, although it is still unclear how and where these persisters exist [5]. Another challenge for TB control is latent infection of M. tuberculosis. According to the statistics of the World Health Organization, about one third of people in the world are latent infected with M. tuberculosis, indicating that a large number of people are at risk of relapse [6]. Therefore, there are two measures to control TB: first, prevent latent infections from becoming active tuberculosis patients. Second, for active tuberculosis, develop shorter and more effective treatment regimens. It is not easy to achieve this goal, because we know very little about the mechanism underlying M. tuberculosis can persist even when the host's immunity is normal and antibiotics are treated.

In infectious diseases, the biofilm provides an important source of recurrent infection and is associated with bacterial persistence [[7], [8], [9]]. In fact, in vitro, Mycobacteria naturally grow in the form of biofilm which is not only controlled by special genetic pathways, but also related to antibiotic tolerance. Richards, J. P. reviewed in detail the research progress of Mycobacteria biofilm [10], while Xiang, X. elaborated on the regulatory and inhibitory factors involved in the development and maturation of M. tuberculosis biofilm [11]. The correlation between biofilm and antibiotic resistance has also been reported in detail [12]. However, in vivo, M. tuberculosis is a facultative intracellular parasitic microorganism. Since the discovery of the phenomenon that M. tuberculosis infects macrophages and can survive in them, there have been many studies on how M. tuberculosis evades the killing mechanism in cells. Little is known about the pathogenesis of extracellular M. tuberculosis and the mechanism of drug resistance in active tuberculosis. In vivo, extracellular M. tuberculosis mainly exists in the form of biofilm [13,14]. Basaraba, R. J. and others discussed in detail that M. tuberculosis may grow in the form of biofilm in the host [14]. Actually, M. tuberculosis can form biofilms in vivo and in vitro, and its biofilm is not only related to the occurrence of infection, but also involves tissue necrosis, cavity formation and recurrence of tuberculosis [13,14].

Polyphosphate kinase 1 (PPK1) catalyzes the synthesis of polyphosphate (polyP) using ATP [15]. Polyphosphate kinase 2 (PPK2) uses polyP as substrate to synthesize nucleoside triphosphates [16]. Exopolyphosphatases (PPXs) can progressively hydrolyze polyP into inorganic phosphate [17]. polyP is a chain molecule linked by high-energy phosphate bonds and widely exists in nature, including organisms [18]. In Mycobacteria, polyP has extensive biological functions such as biofilm formation and pathogenesis [[19], [20], [21], [22]]. Genes encoding PPK1 have been identified in both M. tuberculsosis and M. smegmatis. Because M. smegmatis is fast-growing and non-pathogenic, it is widely used as a model system to study mycobacterial physiology. Due to the wide existence of PPK1 is in pathogenic and non-pathogenic mycobacterial species, its function appears to be conserved. In view of this, we used M. smegmatis as the model bacteria to study the physiological function of PPK1 in Mycobacteria. Our previously published articles showed that the deletion of polyP affected the biofilm forming ability of M. smegmatis, which was related to the change of lipid composition on the surface of bacteria [19].

The objective of the present study is to determine the function of PPK1 in M. smegmatis mc2 155 in terms of persistence such as biofilm formation and evasion of macrophages. In this study, a ppk1-deficient strain and its complemented strain were constructed. Furthermore, the biofilm formation, morphology and ultramicrostructure of biofilm, and the survivability in macrophages of the wild type (WT), mutant (MT) and complemented (CT) strains were evaluated. Cell lipid composition and sensitivity to antibiotics of the WT, MT and CT strains were measured to explore the role of PPK1 in M. smemgatis biofilm formation, and the expression levels of inflammatory factors were tested to investigate the role of PPK1 in M. smegmatis survivability in macrophages. Overall, our results establish a basis for investigating the role of PPK1 in the persistence of M. tuberculosis, and provide clues for treating persistent infection of M. tuberculosis with PPK1 as a potential drug target.

2 Materials and methods

2.1 Bacterial strains, plasmid, and growth conditions

E. coli DH5α, E. coli HB101, M. smegamtis, pMV361, p0004s and phAE159 were preserved by our laboratory. M. smegmatis mc2 155 was grown in either Middlebrook (MB) 7H9 broth or on MB 7H10 agar containing 10 g/L glucose, 2% (v/v) glycerol, and 0.05% (v/v) Tween 80. When required, the antibiotics were used at the following concentrations: kanamycin (25 μg/mL) and hygromycin B (50 μg/mL for E. coli and 150 μg/mL for M. smegmatis).

2.2 Construction of ppk1-deficient mutant and complemented strains of M. smegmatis

The ppk1-deficient mutant (MT) strain of M. smegmatis mc2 155 was constructed according to previous publications [23,24]. Firstly, recombinant cosmids were constructed containing allelic exchange substrates (AESs). We successfully cloned the ppk1 gene upstream and downstream arm by using the M. smegmatis mc2 155 genomic DNA and specific primers (Table 1). The PCR products were directly ligated to p0004s plasmid and transferred into E. coli DH5α. Second, the phAE159-AES plasmids were constructed. The phAE159 and p0004s-AES plasmids were extracted respectively using plasmid extraction kit (Omega Co., Ltd.). The two plasmids were subjected to PacI restriction enzyme digestion, and then purified and recovered using the DNA purification kit, respectively. The two resulting linearized plasmid fragments were linked by T4 DNA ligase and then transferred into E. coli HB101. Third, the specialized transducing mycobacteriophages were constructed. The recombinant plasmid phAE159-AES was then transferred into M. smegmatis mc2 155 using electroporation method [25]. Next, the M. smegmatis mc2 155 was transferred into 1 mL 7H9 medium and cultured overnight, after which the cells were then pelleted by centrifugation, then resuspended in 1 mL 7H9 medium and plated on 7H10 plates. The plates were cultured at 37 °C for 2–3 days and phage plaque was screened. The plaque on the plate was picked out and incubated in MP buffer at 4 °C overnight. The liquid containing phage was mixed with 1 mL of freshly cultured M. smegmatis mc2 155, and then 100 μL of mixture was transferred to the plate which was incubated at 30 °C for 2–3 days. 1 mL of MP buffer was added to the plate with plaque and the plate was placed at 4 °C overnight. Then, the MP buffer containing phages was collected and filtered with a 0.22 μm sterile filter to obtain high titer phages, which were stored in 4 °C refrigerator for standby. Fourth, construction of the mutant strains by transfecting with phage lysate. M. smegmatis mc2 155 infected with high-titer phage was plated on 7H10 solid plates containing hygromycin (75 μg/mL) and cultured at 37 °C for 2–3 days, which were cultured at 37 °C for 2–3 days. Then, the single colony was picked out, inoculated in 7H9 liquid medium (containing 75 μg/mL hygromycin B) and cultured at 37 °C for 2–3 days. ppk1-complemented (CT) strain was constructed as follows. The ppk1 gene (Msmeg_2391) was amplified by high fidelity DNA polymerase using the specific primers (Table 1). After recovering the target DNA fragment, it was recombined with linearized pMV361 plasmid (EcoRI and HindIII double enzyme digestion) and transformed into E. coli DH5 α competent cells. 2.3 polyP quantification using PPK1 in the reverse direction

To study the effect of ppk1 gene knockout on intracellular polyP level, the polyP level in Mycobacteria were quantified according to previous publications [26]. In brief, the strains were grown in 7H9 medium and 1 mL bacterial culture added to 1.5 mL EP tube at indicated growth stages, centrifuged at 12,000 rpm to collect bacteria and then washed twice with PBS, resuspended in 500 μL of GITC lysis buffer (4 M guanidine isothiocyanate, 50 mM tris-HCl [pH 7.0]) and then sonicated (to prevent temperature rise, place EP tube in ice bath) to break cells. 10 μL of sample was taken for protein quantification and the remaining samples were used for polyP extraction using Glassmilk (Geneclean kit; MP Biomedicals) according to the instructions. polyP levels were measured through the PPK1 reverse reaction [26]. The ATP levels were assayed using Bioluminescence Assay Kit CLSII (Roche), and the concentration of polyP was given in terms of Pi residues.

2.4 MIC determination values

Then the strains were inoculated in flat-bottomed conical flask containing 20 mL of Middlebrook 7H9 broth with Tween 80 and cultured at 37 °C to logarithmic growth period. And then the strains were inoculated to test tubes containing 5 mL of Middlebrook 7H9 without Tween 80 and with two-fold serial dillutions of vancomycin or isoniazid. Bacterial growth was decided by the presence of visible pellets after 7 days of standing culture at 37 °C. The MIC was recorded as the lowest concentration of antibiotic at which there was no visible pellet.

2.5 Growth curve, biofilm formation and its detection of M. smegmatis

The WT, MT and CT strains were inoculated separately into 200 mL of 7H9 liquid medium containing 0.05% Tween 80 and cultured at 37 °C to logarithmic growth period. The OD600 was measured at appointed time for a total of 43 h. The growth curve of M. smegmatis was plotted according to OD600 data.

The pretreatment of M. smegmatis for biofilm formation was carried out as follows. The WT, MT and CT strains were inoculated into 5 ml 7H9 liquid medium containing 0.05% Tween 80 and cultured at 37 °C, 200 rpm to logarithmic growth period. And then 2 mL of culture was taken, inoculated into 200 mL of 7H9 liquid medium containing 0.05% Tween 80 and cultured at 37 °C, 200 rpm to stationary phase. Then according to 5% inoculation amount, cultures were inoculated into Sutong medium and mixed throughly.

For observation of biofilm phenotype, 4 mL of pretreated bacterial solution was taken to each well of the 12-well plate, cultured still at 37 °C, and observed the formation of biofilm and photographed at indicated time.

The detection method used for biofilm formation was percentage transmission by measuring optical density in microtitre plate with ELISA reader [27]. Briefly, 100 μL of pretreated bacterial solution was taken to pre-sterilized, polystyrene, flat-bottomed 96-well microtitre plate, cultured still at 37 °C for biofilm formation. On the 96-well plate, the WT, MT and CT strains were inoculated into 28 wells respectively, and the other 12 wells were only added with Sutong medium as control. At the specified time point, reading was taken in microtitre plate reader at 405 nm. The percent transmittance which gave optical density was considered as biofilm formation.

2.6 Scanning electron microscope analysis of the biofilm of M. smegmatis

For ultrastructure of biofilm analysis, the biofilm was cultured as for observation of phenotype and samples were extracted at different times for examination by scanning electiron microscopy (JSM-IT800). Briefly, the samples were deposited at 0.22 μm Nucleopore track-etch membranes (What-man, United Kingdom), and then dried in air for next treatment. Then the samples were fixed in the laminar flow hood by osmium and glutaraldehyde vapor impregnation for 48 h, which were then mounted on adhesive carbon films and sputter-coated with gold in a Balzers MED 010 unit (Vaduz, Liechtenstein) before being further examined under a scanning electron microscope at an accelerating voltage of 10–15 kV.

2.7 Detection of fatty acid levels in various strains of M. smegmatis

The fatty acid levels were detected using gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (Trace1310 ISQ) as described previously [28]. Briefly, the culture of WT and MT strains were collected by centrifugation. All bacteria pellets were weighed and put into the flask. Then, the extraction and detection of fatty acids were completed. The level of fatty acid was calculated according to the following formula:(1) W=C*V*Nm*k

(where, W: the level of fatty acid (mg/kg); C: the concentration of fatty acid methyl ester (mg/L); V: volume (mL); k: conversion coefficient of fatty acid methyl ester into fatty acid; N: dilution ratio; m: the weight of the sample (g)).

2.8 Cell culture of macrophages and intracellular survival of various strains of M. smegmatis

THP-1 cells were cultured in RPMI1640 medium containing 10% (v/v) FBS, 100 U/mL penicillin, streptomycin, 2 mM glutamine at 37 °C and 5% CO2. When the number of cells reached 1 × 106, phorbol 12-myristate 13-acetate (PMA, Sigma) was added to the cell culture to the final concentration 100 ng/mL, which was then seeded at 1 × 106 cells per well in 12-well plate and induced to differentiate into adherent macrophages. The logarithmic phase mycobacterial cultures (in 7H9 liquid medium containing 0.05% Tween 80) were centrifugated at 8000 rpm for 10 min, rinsed with 1 × PBS for three times, re-suspended with RPMI1640 medium, then centrifugated with 1000 rpm for 5–10 min to remove the bacteria with low activity. The bacterial concentration was adjusted to OD600 = 0.4 and then was used to infect macrophage cells with MOI = 10 (bacteria-to-THP-1 cell ratio). After 4 h of infection, the supernatant was removed, and the cells were washed with 1 × PBS for 3 times and transferred into fresh RPMI1640 containing glutamine (2 mM) and gentamicin (200 μg/mL) for further culture. After infection 6, 24, 48 and 72 h, the supernatant was removed and the cells were washed with 1 × PBS once respectively, then the cells were lysed with 0.025% SDS for 10 min (1 mL per well of 12-well plate), and the lysate was collected and spotted on 7H9 agar plates with tenfold serial dilution. The bacteria were counted after 3–5 days culture.

2.9 Cytokines analysis

2, 4, 6, 8 or 24 h after THP-1 cells were infected by WT, MT or CT strain, the supernatant was removed and cells were washed with 1 × PBS 3 times and lysed with RNA lysate (TIANGEN) by shaking to facilitate lysis for 5 min. Then mRNA was extracted with RNA Extraction Kit (Promega) and reverse transcribed into cDNA (Takara) through the parameters: 37 °C/20 min→85 °C/5 s. Then, the concentration of cDNA was adjusted to the same by nucleic acid quantizer, generally at 100 ng/μL, the error of different cDNA concentration should not exceed 5 ng/μL. The transcription levels of cytokines were determined by RT-PCR with primers (Table 1).

2.10 Statistical analysis

Data from at least three biological replicates were analyzed with unpaired Student's t-test. The differences between the experimental group and the control group were analyzed by using the GraphPad Prism 7. Statistically significant P Values were marked as: *p＜0.05, **p＜0.01 and ***p＜0.001.

For construction of complemented strain, we selected an integrated expression vector, pMV361, to achieve this goal. The pMV361 vector harbours M. tuberculosis heat shock protein promoter, polyclonal restriction sites and integrase-encoding gene (int), which was transformed into M. smegmatis and could integrate into the chromosomal attB site [29]. Therefore, we constructed the pMV361-ppk1 plasmid in which ppk1 gene could be expressed under control of the HSP60 and HSP70 promoters [30], and then pMV361-ppk1 plasmid was transformed into M. smegmatis through electrotransformation to MT competent strains. The integration of ppk1 gene in the CT strain was confirmed by PCR method (Fig. 1E). PPK1 is responsible for the polyP synthesis in M. tuberculosis and M. smegmatis [31]. In order to further confirm the success of the construction of MT and CT strains, therefore, the polyP levels in WT, MT and CT strains were measured. As shown in Fig. 1F, the polyP level in WT and MT strains was 31.02 ± 2.12 and 5.21 ± 1.02 nmol/mg (P = 0.0034), respectively, in the mid-log period. In the late-log period, the polyP level in WT and MT strains was 59.25 ± 3.41 and 6.12 ± 1.37 nmol/mg (P = 0.005), respectively. Unexpectedly, the CT strain had higher polyP level compared to MT strain (25.32 ± 1.26 vs. 5.21 ± 1.02 nmol/mg in mid-log period, P = 0.0025; and 30.33 ± 3.27 and 6.12 ± 1.37 nmol/mg in the late-log period P = 0.0012) (Fig. 1F). However, the polyP levels in WT was higher compared to CT strain (P = 0.043). Taken together, the quantitative analysis of polyP showed that the polyP in the MT strain was significantly lower than that in the WT strain, and the polyP level was partially restored in the CT strain. Thus, we successfully constructed the ppk1-deficient mutant of M. smegmatis mc2 155 and a ppk1-complemented M. smegmatis mc2 155 mutant.

3.2 ppk1 is vital for biofilm formation and the morphology and ultramicrostructure of biofilm formed by M. smegmatis

Our previous studies have shown that polyP deficiency (by overexpression PPX of M. tuberculosis) was compromised in terms of the ability to form biofilm of M. smegmatis [19]. In this study, a percentage transmission by measuring optical density in microtiter plate was conducted to quantitate the biomass of the biofilm [27]. Consistent with our previous studies finding, we found that at different time points, the biofilm formation of the ppk1-deficient mutant was significantly attenuated compared with WT (Fig. 2A). On day 2, the optical density of WT and MT was 0.69 ± 0.04 and 0.08 ± 0.04 (P = 0.00008), respectively. On day 3, the optical density of WT and MT was 0.84 ± 0.02 and 0.18 ± 0.13 (P = 0.0029), respectively. On day 5, the optical density of WT and MT strains was 1.05 ± 0.03 and 0.30 ± 0.16 (P = 0.002), respectively. Consistent with expectations, CT strain exhibited significantly higher optical density relative to MT strain (0.46 ± 0.06 vs. 0.08 ± 0.04 on day 2, P = 0.001; 0.47 ± 0.17 vs. 0.18 ± 0.13 on day 3, P = 0.002; and 0.59 ± 0.20 vs. 0.30 ± 0.16 on day 5, P = 0.002). 3.3 ppk1 is related to the reduction of short chain fatty acids

Biofilm formation involves a series of developmental processes which are divided artificially into initial attachment, growth, maturation and detachment [32]. Previous studies suggested that the initial attachment was based on the surface interaction between mycobacterial cells [33]. It is particularly notorious that the main component of mycobacterial cell wall is lipid. So in order to explore whether the reduction of extracellular substances caused by ppk1 mutation was related to the lipid, we detected the lipid composition of WT and MT strains. In order to facilitate detection, it was transformed into fatty acids in this study. Then, we detected the fatty acids in WT and MT strains on day 2, 3 and 5, respectively and the levels of various fatty acids were calculated according to formula (1). As shown in Fig. 4, we detected 25 short chain fatty acids through gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GCMS) in WT and MT strains. The results revealed that there were significant differences in 20 kinds of fatty acids between WT and MT strains on day 2 (Fig. 4A). 3.4 ppk1 is required for the sensitivity of M. smegmatis to vancomycin

Given that ppk1 is related to the alteration of lipid composition of cell wall, the possibility of whether this alteration affected the sensitivity of MT strain to antibiotics that target the cell wall was investigated. Previous studies have shown that polyP-deficient or -accumulating M. tuberculosis strains showed increased or decreased susceptibility to INH, respectively [20,21,34,35]. However, we did not find any difference in the sensitivity of WT strain (MIC: 16 μg/mL) and MT strain (MIC: 16 μg/mL) to isoniazid. Then, we tested the MIC of WT and MT strains against vancomycin. Compared with WT (MIC: 20 μg/mL), the sensitivity of MT strain (MIC: 2.5 μg/mL) to vancomycin increased by 8-folds. These results suggest that ppk1 is required for the sensitivity to vancomycin.

3.5 ppk1 is necessary for intracellular survival of M. smegmatis and alternation of the expression levels of inflammatory factors in macrophages infected by M. smegmatis

Mycobacteria do not have endotoxin, nor do they produce exotoxin and invasive enzymes. The pathogenicity of Mycobacteria is related to the bacterial colonization and indomitable proliferation in tissue cells, and its bacterial components especially the lipid in the cell wall, which can escape the innate immune clearance, delay the establishment of immune response in infected cells, and cause inflammatory reaction. Given that ppk1 is related to the alteration of the lipid composition of cell wall, we further studied the intracellular survival of the WT, MT and CT strains in macrophages. To achieve this objective, we infected THP-1-derived macrophages with WT, MT and CT strains, respectively, as described in Materials and Methods. As shown in Fig. 5A, after 24 h of infection to macrophages, MT strain could't form colonies on the solid plate after 1000 times dilution, while WT strain could form colonies. Similarly, after 48 h of infection to macrophages, MT strain could't form colonies after being diluted 10 times, WT strain could form colonies. 4 Discussion

A large number of studies showed that polyP plays a very important role in the physiology and virulence of bacteria, and polyP is described as a “multifaced biopolymer” [36]. PolyP was found in M. tuberculosis 60 years ago [37]. The major synthetase of polyP, PPK1, is highly conserved in bacteria and plays many important roles. M. tuberculosis has the gene encoding PPK1 [38]. Similarly, the Gene encoding PPK1 has been identified in fast-growing non-pathogenic M. smegmatis which is widely used as a model system to study mycobacterial physiology [31]. Whether M. smegmatis is a non-realistic model for tuberculosis has always been controversial. Tyagi, J. S. considered that the conserved pathway could be studied using M. smegmatis as model strain [39]. Given that M. smegmatis and M. tuberculosis possess a highly homologous ppk1 genes [31,40] and Mycobacteria possess a strong propensity to form biofilms [41,42], we used M. smegmatis as the model bacteria to study the physiological function of PPK1 in Mycobacteria. In the present study, we found that ppk1 deficiency mutation led to the morphological and ultramicrostructure changes of biofilm, and the reduction of short chain fatty acids of M. smegmatis. We also found that ppk1 deficiency mutation led to increased susceptibility of bacteria to vancomycin. In addition, we demonstrated that PPK1 was an important virulence factor responsible for survival in macrophages of Mycobacteria by altering the expression levels of various inflammatory factors.

In the present study, we knocked out the ppk1 gene through using mycobacteriophages, which was confirmed by two-fragment PCR method and full-length PCR method using locus-specific primers, respectively (Fig. 1 A, B, C, D) and by polyP quantification assay (Fig. 1F). The polyP levels in MT strain were significantly lower than the corresponding values in WT strain. Integration of ppk1 gene in the CT strain was confirmed by PCR (Fig. 1E) and the level of polyP was partially restored in the CT strain (Fig. 1F). The intracellular polyP levels of M. tuberculosis increase in the late stage of growth and once exposed to various stress conditions [21,35]. In accordance, the polyP level in the late-log period increased compared to that in the mid-log period in WT (Fig. 1F). However, the polyP level was nearly constant in the mid-log and late-log period in CT strain (Fig. 1F), which might be related to the integrated expression vector. The integration vector, pMV361, possesses an expression cassette harboring regulatory region from BCG hsp60 gene and has the ability to induce its downstream gene expression through temperature [29]. Therefore, the expression level of ppk1 gene in CT strain was regulated by the regulatory region of BCG hsp60 gene not by the self-promoter of M. smegmatis ppk1 gene, which meant that the expression of ppk1 gene in CT strain could not be fine-tuned as in WT strain (as discussion later). Therefore, the polyP levels and biofilm formation were not completely complemented in CT strain in our study.

polyP homeostasis is required for the formation of biofilm. For example, polyP accumulation (ppx2 deficiency strains [35]) and deficiency (deficient in ppx1 or ppk2 [43]) in M. tuberculosis are associated with defective biofilm formation. Consistent with previous results, the MT strain was compromised in ability to form biofilm (Fig. 2A). However, to our knowledge, the morphological and ultramicrostructure of biofilm formed by polyP deficient strains has not been reported before. In the present study, we found that the biofilm formed by WT strain was flocculent, while the biofilm formed by MT strain was sand-like or globular (Fig. 2B). Microstructure observation showed that the biofilm formed by WT strain possessed more extracellular substances around bacteria to tightly wrap the bacteria, while there were lesser extracellular substances between polyP-deficient strains which were just piled up with each other (Fig. 3). However, confoundedly little was known about the mechanism underlying how polyP affected the structure of biofilm. The morphological differences might be related to the effect of polyP on the development process of biofilm formation. The biofilm formation process includes four stages: attachment, sessile growth, extracellular matrix synthesis and dispersal [44]. Briefly, bacteria are firstly adsorbed to the surface by cell surface substances. Once adsorbed, bacteria that will form biofilm begin to synthesize extracellular substances. These extracellular substances include polysaccharides, proteins and nucleic acids. Mycobacteria can form a robust biofilm, either attached to the solid surface or floating on the surface of liquid medium. Previous studies have shown that the glycopeptidolipids play a very important role in the surface adsorption [33], and a class of fatty acids (C56–C68) influences the development of biofilm structure of M. smegmatis [45]. We found previously [19] and in the current study that maintenance of the intracellular polyP deficiency affected the fatty acid species and levels in M. smegmatis (Fig. 4). Notably, the current study found that in the early stage of biofilm formation, there were significant differences in the levels of 20 kinds of fatty acids between WT and MT strains, while in the later stage, the fatty acid species with significant difference between WT and MT strains decreased to 15. One intriguing possibility is that these fatty acid species (C8–C24 except C16.1 and C17.1) are important for initial surface adsorption during biofilm formation. However, how does the polyP deficiency lead to the alteration of fatty acid levels? Mycobacteria are different from other bacteria in fatty acid synthase (FAS) systems, because they possess both FAS Ⅰ and FAS Ⅱ systems. In the process of fatty acid synthesis, FAS Ⅰ is responsible for de novo synthesis fatty acids from acetate [46], producing acyl chains with length 16 and 24 carbon [47,48]. On the other hand, the FAS Ⅱsystem cannot de novo synthesize fatty acid from acetate, but extends preferentially palmitoyl-CoA (C16) to 18 to 30 carbon fatty acid [47]. Interestingly, the ratio of 16 and 24 carbon fatty acids produced by the FAS Ⅰ system is regulated by specific polysaccharides: 3-3-O-methylmannose-containing polysaccharides (MMP) and 6-O-methylglucose-containing polysaccharides (MGLP) [47,49], which increase the synthesis of C16 fatty acids instead of C24 by accelerating the dissociation of 16 carbon from FAS Ⅰ system [50]. One hypothesis is that polyP as chaperone is involved in regulating the ratio of 16 and 24 carbon fatty acids. Ojha, A. found that chaperone GroEL1 is involved in the synthesis of C56–C68 fatty acids, which is related to the formation of mature biofilm in M. smegmatis [45]. Although the exact role of this chaperone protein is unclear, groEL mutant produces a significant reduction in the KasA and KasB of the FAS Ⅱ system. Similarly, polyP is a primordial chaperone with protein-like chaperone properties and binds to unfolding proteins with high affinity [51,52]. Therefore, it is hypothesized here that polyP, as a molecular chaperone, is potentially involved in regulating the synthesis of these fatty acid species (C8–C24) in M. smegmatis by affecting the FAS Ⅰ system, and/or MMP and MGLP synthetase. Another possible hypothesis is that the effect of polyP on fatty acid synthesis is mediated through the stringent response. Stringent response is an adaptive response that down-regulates translation potential during starvation, which appears to be a positive-feedback loop in Mycobacteria. First, polyP activates two-component system MprAB, the activated MprAB induces expression of sigE and relMtb [31,53], and then leads to increased RelMtb levels. Therefore, polyP deficiency should result in this regulatory circuit disruption and finally, defective levels of RelMtb. On the other hand, the FAS Ⅱ system is multi-protein synthase, that includes β-ketoacyl synthase (KasAB), β-ketoacyl reductase (MabA), β-hydroxyacyl dehydratases (HadABC) and enoyl reductase (InhA). The hadABC genes are negatively regulated by RelMtb [54]. Therefore, when RelMtb decreases, the expression of hadABC gene will increase. However, inactivation of the FAS Ⅱ system induces accumulation of the end products of FAS Ⅰ system. For example, inactivation of InhA in M. smegmatis resulted in decrease in hexadecanoic acid (C16) and increase of tetracosanoic acid (C24) [55]. Conversely, activation of FAS Ⅱ system can inhibit the accumulation of end products of FAS Ⅰ system. It is reasonable to hypothesise that polyP deficiency could result in polyP-MprAB-sigE-relMtb regulatory circuit disruption, which increases FAS Ⅱ system gene expression and leads to the enhanced activation of the FAS Ⅱ system and ultimately activation of the FAS Ⅱ system promotes an increase of hexadecanoic acid (C16) and a decrease in tetracosanoic acid (C24). Further studies are needed to investigate the detailed mechanisms underlying the effect of polyP on fatty acid synthesis. Consistent with our finding, previous studies showed that polyP accumulation changes the metabolic profile of M. tuberculosis. Strains that accumulate polyP and are deficient in ppx1, ppk2 or ppx2 have significantly higher levels of lipid oxidation and citrate cycle activity, and significant decrease in malonyl-coenzyme A levels relative to wild strain [35,43].

Vancomycin can target Mycobacteria deleted outside the cell, for example, some researchers use vancomycin sensitivity analysis to detect drugs that hinder mycobacterial lipid synthesis [56]. In the present study, we found that polyP deficiency could result in an 8-fold increase in susceptibility to vancomycin. Our previous study showed that toxin Rv2872 changes the cell wall structure and fatty acids levels, and then affects vancomycin stress response and biofilm formation [57]. Several similar reports have shown that the lipid of bacterial cell correlates with vancomycin resistance. Treatment with vancomycin at a concentration of 10 times MIC results in a strong inhibitory effect on the lipid metabolism gene of M. tuberculosis [58]. Consistently, MSMEG_6171 plays a role in the lipid synthesis and biofilm formation in M. smegmatis, of which over-expression is 16-fold more resistant to vancomycin [59]. Therefore, increase in vancomycin susceptibility of MT strain further confirmed that polyP deficiency might be associated with the lipid composition of bacterial cells. Since, the SigE is related to presistance to vancomycin [58], the polyP-MprAB-SigE signalization pathway may contribute to regulate the resistance to vancomycin. Furthermore, the rpoB gene encodes the β subunit of RNA polymerase, of which mutant shows altered cell wall metabolism and an 8-fold increase in susceptibility to the vancomycin in M. tuberculosis [60]. polyP is found to be tightly associated with RNA polymerase, and then controls the promoter selectivity of RNA polymerase [61]. For example, in Helicobacter pylori, polyP directly targets RNA polymerase by binding to the principal sigma factor to adapt stress [62]. So another possibility is that polyP could bind to RNA polymerase to regulate the resistance to vancomycin. Further studies are required to determine the mechanism underlying how polyP regulates the susceptibility to vancomycin. Interestingly, previous studies have shown that polyP-deficient or -accumulating M. tuberculosis strains display increased or decreased susceptibility to INH, respectively [20,21,34,35]. However, our MIC results showed that polyP deficiency did not alter the susceptibility to INH in M. smegmatis. Future studies will address whether the discrepancies in antibiotic sensitivity result from differences in the M. smegmatis and M. tuberculosis.

Recent studies have shown that long-chain polyP (generally bacteria produce long-chain polyP [63]) regulates the expression of extensive LPS/TLR4-related genes in macrophages and resultes in polarization of macrophages from M1 (pro-inflammatory) phenotype to M2 (anti-inflammatory) phenotype, which suggested that prokaryotic polyP controls a large number of macrophage functions for evading host immunity [64]. Our results showed that ppk1 mutant-infected macrophages decreased expression of anti-inflammatory factors such as PPAR-γ and IL-10, and increased expression of pro-inflammatory factors such as IL-6 and TNF-α compared to WT-infected macrophages, suggesting that polyP altered macrophage polarization from M1 phenotype to M2 phenotype, and then facilitated bacterial survival in macrophages (Fig. 5A and B). Consistent with these results, Rijal has recently reported that Mycobacteria secrete polyP, which increases survival of these bacteria after phagocytosis by human macrophages [65]. In agreement, we found that MT strain survived poorly in macrophages compared with WT strain, which indicated that the functions of ppk1 mutant-infected macrophages were not disturbed and supported the idea that bacterial polyP plays an important role in regulating innate immune response and promotes persistence of Mycobacteria. Consistent with our findings in M. smegmatis, previous studies showed that polyP-acculating M. tuberculsosis increased expression of IL-2 and IL-10 in macrophages [20]. These results show that polyP homeostasis appears to be responsible for the intracellular survival of Mycobacteria, through altering the expression levels of various inflammatory factors of macrophages.

In summary, we have discovered that PPK1 is of great significance for the biofilm formation and survival in macrophages of Mycobacteria. In the future, we will continue to explore mechanisms underlying PPK1 altered lipid composition of Mycobacteria and expression levels of inflammatory factors of macrophages. These findings lay a foundation for establishing enzymes involved in polyP synthesis as a potential drug target for the treatment of persistent infection of M. tuberculosis.

5 Couclusions

This study demonstrates that ppk1 is vital in M. smegmatis not only for the biofilm formation but also for the morphology and ultramicrostructure of biofilm, susceptibility to vancomycin and the intracellular survival. The alteration of morphology and ultramicrostructure of biofilm formed by ppk1-deficient mutant is related to the extracellular matrix, especially short chain fatty acids. Since biofilm formation and its intrinsic resistance to antimicrobial agents are the root cause of much persistent and chronic infection, PPK1 is suitable as a potential therapeutic target for controlling biofilm infection by interfering with the synthesis of extracellular matrix. The intracellular survival ability of M. smegmatis is related to the alteration of inflammatory factor, which potentially shift macrophage polarization from M1 phenotype to M2 phenotype regulated by polyP. Therefore, the PPK1 is suitable as a potential therapeutic target for controlling bacterial intracellular survival by regulating the synthesis of polyP. As the development of antibiotics has stagnated in the past 40 years [66], many pathogens have developed resistance to escape traditional treatment [67,68]. In order to solve this growing threat, the anti-virulence strategy targeting bacterial virulence factors (such as persistence and biofilm) to replace traditional antibiotics is proposed [69]. This method aims to reduce toxicity and avoids direct killing effect on bacteria, so as to reduce the inevitable selection pressure of traditional antibiotics [70,71]. In addition, many antibiotics can not only kill pathogens, but also destroy symbiotic microbiota, leading to re-infection or increasing the risk of opportunistic infection [72]. The anti-virulence strategy can avoid these consequences [73]. In view of these reasons, polyP and PPK have become the hot spots in the study of anti-virulence to explore new strategies to deal with drug-resistant pathogens. In many pathogenic bacteria, including M. tuberculosis, the regulatory network of polyP and PPK is closely related to virulence factors and antibiotic sensitivity [20,[74], [75], [76]]. It has been reported that anti-virulence agents can reduce the load of M. tuberculosis in vivo [77,78] and in vitro [79,80]. Since no homologue of PPK family enzymes has been found in higher eukaryotes, PPK is considered as a potential target for the design of anti-virulence therapy [81]. Overall, the findings in this study establish that PPK1 is an attractive target to develop novel interventions to combat persistent infection of M. tuberculosis.
