Background

Soils suppressive to fungal pathogens harbor microbiomes that can inhibit disease development despite the presence of virulent pathogens and susceptible hosts. Fluorescent Pseudomonas are often implicated in such suppressiveness, but their genomic determinants and distribution in suppressive vs. non-suppressive (i.e., conducive) soils remain unclear.

Results

We investigated the taxonomic and functional diversity of Pseudomonas populations from wheat rhizospheres in four agricultural soils with contrasting suppressiveness to Fusarium graminearum-induced seedling disease. rpoD-based metabarcoding and culture-dependent isolation revealed distinct Pseudomonas community structures linked to soil suppressiveness. However, major phylogenetic groups were shared across soils. From 406 isolates, 29 representative strains spanning seven subgroups of the P. fluorescens group were selected for whole-genome sequencing. Comparative genomics revealed 14 putative novel Pseudomonas genomospecies (dDDH < 70% with closest described type strains). Genomic screening revealed wide distribution of genes linked to biocontrol and plant-growth promotion, including siderophore biosynthesis, hormone modulation, phosphate solubilization, and production of antimicrobial compounds. Biosynthetic genes for phenazine and pyrrolnitrin were detected exclusively in P. chlororaphis strains isolated from suppressive soils, and rpoD alleles corresponding to these strains were not found in conducive soils within our metabarcoding dataset. Other traits such as hydrogen cyanide, ACC deaminase, and auxin biosynthesis were broadly distributed across isolates from all soils. Functional assays demonstrated variable expression of predicted traits, indicating regulatory or environmental influence. Several strains inhibited F. graminearum mycelial growth via volatile organic compounds, while two strains also reduced conidia germination, including isolates from both suppressive and conducive soils.

Conclusions

This study demonstrates that Pseudomonas genomic traits important for biocontrol are not restricted to suppressive soils, and that functional redundancy and context-dependent expression may shape the contribution of Pseudomonas to disease suppression. Our results highlight the need for integrative analyses combining community profiling, genome-based prediction, and phenotyping to better understand microbiome-mediated plant protection. The identification of novel genomospecies and lineage-specific biosynthetic traits advances our knowledge of Pseudomonas diversity in agricultural soils and supports future development of targeted microbial consortia.

Introduction

Disease-suppressive soils maintain plant health despite the presence of a susceptible host, a virulent pathogen, and environmental conditions favorable for disease development [1–4]. This phenomenon entails various mechanisms of soil microbiota such as direct antagonism and competitive exclusion of phytopathogens, or the activation of plant defenses i.e., induced systemic resistance (ISR) [5–7]. Among the microbial taxa frequently associated with soil suppressiveness, fluorescent Pseudomonas spp. play a prominent role owing to their diverse set of biocontrol and plant-growth promoting properties, including the ability to produce antimicrobial compounds and metabolites modulating plant hormonal balance [8, 9]. In certain types of soil suppressiveness, such as take-all decline, fluorescent Pseudomonas are more abundant in suppressive soils compared to conducive (non-suppressive) ones [10], whereas in other cases, such as black root rot suppressive soils, these bacteria are found in comparable numbers in both suppressive and conducive soils [11, 12]. Early studies using DGGE targeting the phlD gene involved in 2,4-diacetylphloroglucinol (DAPG) production [12] or rrs-based analyses [13] highlighted compositional differences in fluorescent Pseudomonas populations between suppressive and conducive soils. These findings suggest that soil suppressiveness may be linked to the presence of particular Pseudomonas lineages possessing distinct functional traits.

To better understand the ecological basis of these observations, it is necessary to explore the phylogenetic and genomic diversity of fluorescent Pseudomonas, which are known to exhibit remarkable taxonomic and functional heterogeneity. Comparative genomics has revealed that the genus Pseudomonas comprises at least 16 phylogroups [14, 15], among which the P. fluorescens group is the most diverse, currently encompassing over 130 described species—well beyond the 51 species found in the second largest group, P. putida [15]. The P. fluorescens group is further subdivided into 11 phylogenetic subgroups (comprising species P. asplenii, P. chlororaphis, P. corrugata, P. fluorescens, P. fragi, P. gessardii, P. jessenii, P. kielensis, P. koreensis, P. mandelii, and P. protegens) and displays a pangenome exceeding 30,000 genes [16]. This extensive genomic repertoire includes numerous traits relevant to plant health, such as the production of antibiotics (phenazines, pyrrolnitrin, DAPG), volatile organic compounds (VOCs), siderophores, enzymes involved in cell wall degradation [9, 17–19], as well as the ability to trigger ISR in plants and phytohormone interference [20]. However, these traits are not uniformly distributed across species and strains of the P. fluorescens group. Notably, traits related to biocontrol (e.g., DAPG, phenazines, pyoluteorin, and FitD insect toxin biosynthesis) are primarily enriched (but not necessarily present in all species or strains) in the P. protegens, P. chlororaphis, and P. corrugata subgroups [16, 21, 22]. Not surprisingly, promising biocontrol strains belonging to these subgroups—such as Pseudomonas sp. Q2-87 (P. corrugata subgroup; [10]) and P. protegens CHA0 [23]—have been isolated from disease-suppressive soils. However, other traits such as hydrogen cyanide (HCN) production, siderophore biosynthesis, and phytohormone modulation appear to be more broadly distributed across fluorescent Pseudomonas subgroups [16]. Comparative genomics has further revealed that the distribution of biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs) involved in antimicrobial production is highly variable among groups, suggesting functional specialization within certain clades [14]. Large-scale phylogenomic reconstructions also support this pattern, showing that the P. fluorescens and P. putida groups, both part of the broader Pseudomonas lineage E, follow distinct evolutionary trajectories and display divergent ecological repertoires [24]. Together, these studies underscore the influence of phylogenetic structure on the functional potential of fluorescent Pseudomonas, while also highlighting that the evolutionary and ecological drivers shaping these patterns remain incompletely understood. Notably, systematic comparisons of the genomic and functional characteristics of fluorescent Pseudomonas strains from disease-suppressive versus conducive soils are lacking. Yet, such comparisons are critical, as Pseudomonas spp. have been repeatedly implicated in the natural suppression of Fusarium wilts in various agroecosystems [7, 25, 26]. In particular, well-characterized biocontrol strains such as P. defensor WCS374 and P. simiae WCS417 have shown the ability to reduce disease severity through multiple mechanisms, including siderophore-mediated iron competition and ISR [26, 27]. Based on current evidence, we hypothesize that Pseudomonas strains thriving in suppressive soils may harbor distinct genetic features and enhanced functional capacities contributing to pathogen inhibition, thereby conferring a stronger biocontrol potential against soil-borne fungal phytopathogens.

This study aimed to assess the genomic and functional diversity of fluorescent Pseudomonas populations in four agricultural soils previously characterized for their contrasting levels of suppressiveness to damping-off disease caused by Fusarium graminearum, a major fungal pathogen affecting wheat [28]. The goal was to determine whether the biocontrol traits of Pseudomonas are unique to suppressive soils or differentially distributed according to suppressiveness. Using rpoD metabarcoding, we first assessed the diversity and taxonomic composition of fluorescent Pseudomonas communities from the rhizosphere of wheat grown in suppressive and conducive soils. We then obtained Pseudomonas isolates from all combinations of soil and pathogen-inoculation conditions and selected representative strains for whole-genome sequencing. The taxonomy, presence of plant-beneficial genes and ability to produce antifungal compounds, VOCs, siderophores and phytohormones, to solubilize phosphate, and to inhibit F. graminearum growth and sporulation were compared between isolates from suppressive and conducive soils. In addition to their suppressiveness profiles, the four soils also differed in their history of manure application and fungistatic properties [28], providing a relevant framework to explore how soil legacy and microbiome composition shape Pseudomonas diversity and biocontrol potential.

Materials and methods

Fusarium graminearum strain and preparation of spore suspension

The highly virulent and toxin-producing F. graminearum strain MDC_Fg1 (hereafter termed F. graminearum Fg1) was isolated from naturally infected cereal grains in northern France [29, 30]. Spore suspension of F. graminearum Fg1 was prepared by growing the fungus in Mung Bean Broth (MBB) [31] for 6 days at 22 °C, with shaking at 180 rpm (Incubator Shaker Series I26, New Brunswick Scientific Co., Inc., Edison, NJ, USA). After incubation, a volume of the preculture was taken and diluted to one tenth in fresh MBB and incubated for 10 days under the same conditions. The resulting culture was vortexed, filtered to discard mycelium and centrifuged at 4700 g for 10 min (Avanti J-E Series, Beckman Coulter, Fullerton, CA, USA). Supernatant was discarded and the resulting pellet was washed twice with sterile water. Titration of spores in the suspension was estimated using a Thoma counting chamber.

Soil description

Four soils (MI2, MI3, MI4, and MI5) collected near Mionica, Serbia (Table 1), were selected for their contrasting suppressive properties towards F. graminearum [28]. All are vertisols, and the topsoil displayed clay texture, with organic matter ranging from 5.9% (MI4, MI5) to 7.7% (MI2), pH from 6.0 (MI4) to 7.8 (MI3), and cation exchange capacity (CEC) from 33 (MI4, MI5) to 49 (MI2) cmol/kg. Full physicochemical properties (pH, texture, organic matter, total N, available P and K, Fe, and CEC) are detailed in [28]. Soils were collected in spring (June 2021), sieved to 0.5 cm, and stored field-moist at 4 °C for less than two weeks before their use. During the greenhouse assays, soil water content was maintained at approximately 21% (w/w) by regular watering, following the procedure described in [28], ensuring microbial viability and reproducibility of suppressiveness assays.

Based on previous characterizations [28], soils MI2 and MI3 are both fungistatic (reducing pathogen survival in soil) and suppressive (reducing disease development), MI4 is non-fungistatic and non-suppressive, and MI5 is non-fungistatic but suppressive. Specifically, in the damping-off assays described in Todorović et al. [28], wheat seedlings grown in MI2 and MI3 displayed high survival rates (≥ 85%) and limited disease symptoms after F. graminearum inoculation, whereas seedlings in MI4 suffered severe damping-off (≈ 30% survival). Soil MI5 showed intermediate suppression (≈ 70% survival) despite the absence of fungistasis. Fungistasis assays performed in [28] further showed that DNA levels of F. graminearum Fg1, quantified by qPCR, declined over 15 days in soils MI2 and MI3, indicating poor pathogen survival, whereas they had increased in MI4 and MI5, suggesting active persistence or growth.

Isolation of Pseudomonas

The protocol for the isolation of Pseudomonas strains from rhizosphere samples was adapted from a previously published procedure [32], with modifications as described below. The same rhizosphere samples obtained from wheat plants grown in these soils [28] were used here for both Pseudomonas isolation and rpoD metabarcoding. Briefly, for each soil, 100 seeds of winter wheat (Triticum aestivum cv Récital) were sown into 20 pots (12 × 10 × 10 cm), each filled with 250 g of soil mixed in a 1:1 ratio with sterile siliceous sand (granulometry 0.6–1.6 mm). In 10 pots, the seeds were inoculated with 100 µl of F. graminearum Fg1 spore suspension (106 spores per seed), and the remaining 10 pots received 100 µl of water per seed (control). Plants were grown under greenhouse conditions for 28 days. Plants were harvested and shaken to dislodge loosely adhering soil. Rhizosphere samples were prepared by collecting roots with tightly adhering soil and suspending them in 50 mL of phosphate buffered saline (NaCl, 8 g; KCl, 0.2 g; KH2PO4, 0.24 g; Na2HPO4, 1.42 g; H2O, 1 L). The suspensions were shaken for 1 h at 160 rpm, after which roots were discarded and suspensions centrifuged at 4000 g for 20 min. The resulting pellet was resuspended in 20 mL of 0.8% NaCl, constituting the rhizosphere extract.

Pseudomonas strains were isolated from rhizosphere extracts of each soil and treatment condition (4 soils × 2 conditions (inoculated or not with F. graminearum Fg1)) using a most probable number (MPN) approach adapted from Vacheron et al. (2016) [33]. Briefly, 20 µL of rhizosphere extract was mixed with 180 µL of King’s B+++ [King’s B (Condalab, Madrid, Spain) supplemented with ampicillin (40 µg.mL− 1), chloramphenicol (13 µg.mL− 1 ) and cycloheximide (100 µg.mL− 1) [34] in 96-well microplates. Serial dilutions were prepared, with five replicates per dilution. The microplates were incubated at 28 °C for 24 h; 1 µL from the last positive well of each dilution series was plated onto King’s B agar. After growth, at least 48 isolates per condition were randomly picked and purified through three consecutive subcultures.

MPN estimates were obtained from a single biological replicate per soil and treatment condition, with five tubes per dilution following standard MPN procedures. Values are reported with 95% confidence intervals, as calculated from the number of positive tubes using the conventional MPN method [35].

Analysis of rhizosphere Pseudomonas diversity through rpoD sequencing

To assess root-associated Pseudomonas populations, non-inoculated wheat plants from the same experimental setup described above were used at 28 days after sowing. For each soil, six rhizosphere replicates were collected. Root systems were shaken to remove loosely adhering soil, then flash-frozen in liquid nitrogen and lyophilized at − 50 °C for 24 h. The rhizosphere soil (root-adhering soil) was separated from the roots using sterile brushes and stored at − 80 °C prior to DNA extraction. DNA was extracted using the FastDNA Spin Kit for Soil (MP Biomedicals, Illkirch-Graffenstaden, France) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. DNA was eluted in 50 µL of sterile ultra-pure water, and concentrations were measured using a NanoPhotometer NP80 UV spectrophotometer (Implen, Munich, Germany).

The rpoD gene, encoding the RNA polymerase sigma 70 (sigma D) factor, was selected as a phylogenetic marker to assess Pseudomonas diversity [15, 36]. A 356 bp fragment of rpoD was amplified using primers rpoD_F (TCGCCAAGAAGTACACCAAC) and rpoD_R (CCATGGAGATCGGCTCTT) [37]. Although not specific to the P. fluorescens group, these primers efficiently target a broad diversity of Pseudomonas species across the taxon E sensu [24], which includes the P. fluorescens and P. putida lineages, thereby enabling a comprehensive characterization of rhizosphere-associated Pseudomonas populations. PCR amplification was performed with an initial denaturation at 94 °C for 1 min, followed by 35 cycles of denaturation at 94 °C for 30 s, annealing at 59 °C for 40 s, and extension at 72 °C for 45 s, with a final elongation step at 72 °C for 10 min. Amplicons were purified, and sequencing libraries were prepared before being sequenced on an Illumina MiSeq platform (2 × 300 bp paired-end reads) at Microsynth (Vaulx-en-Velin, France).

Demultiplexed reads were quality-checked using fastp v.0.23.2 [38], and primers were removed with cutadapt v.4.1 [39] using default parameters. Paired-end reads were processed using R v.4.2.1 and the DADA2 pipeline v.1.26 [40], including filtering, trimming, denoising, dereplication, merging of paired-end reads, and chimera removal. This pipeline retained 883,315 high-quality reads, which were clustered into 823 amplicon sequence variants (ASVs). Taxonomic assignment was performed using the DADA2 naïve Bayesian classifier method [41] in conjunction with a custom rpoD sequence database (available here: https://zenodo.org/records/16269906). This bespoke database was constructed by compiling all available Pseudomonas rpoD sequences from public repositories, supplemented with rpoD sequences characterized from our own isolates. This comprehensive approach ensured robust and precise classification of Pseudomonas populations within the analyzed samples.

A total of 24 samples (six per non-inoculated soil: MI2, MI3, MI4, MI5) were initially processed for rpoD metabarcoding. Following quality control, three samples were excluded: one replicate from MI2 and two from MI4 were discarded due to a low number of usable reads, clearly deviating from other replicates. The final dataset thus included at least four biological replicates per condition, ensuring sufficient robustness and comparability for downstream diversity and community analyses.

Identification of putative Pseudomonas isolates

Genomic DNA of all putative Pseudomonas isolates was extracted from overnight cultures grown in Tryptic Soy Broth (TSB; Carl Roth, Karlsruhe, Germany) using the NucleoSpin 96 Tissue kit (Macherey-Nagel, Düren, Germany), following the manufacturer’s instructions. Isolate characterization was performed by sequencing the rpoD gene using primers rpoDf (5’-ACTTCCCTGGCACGGTTGACCA-3’) and rpoDr (5’-TCGACATGCGACGGTTGATGTC-3’) [42]. The primers rpoDf and rpoDr amplify an internal rpoD fragment of 695 bp, which provides sufficient phylogenetic resolution for discriminating Pseudomonas species.

PCR reactions were performed in a 50 µL reaction volume containing 5 µL of 10 × DreamTaq Green Buffer, 5 µL of dNTPs (2 mM), 0.25 µL of DreamTaq DNA polymerase (5 U/µL) (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA), 2.5 µL of each primer (10 µM), 50 ng of DNA, and RNase-free water to a final volume of 50 µL. The PCR cycling conditions for rpoD amplification were 94 °C for 150 s, followed by 30 cycles of 94 °C for 30 s, 60 °C for 30 s, and 72 °C for 1 min, with a final elongation step at 72 °C for 10 min. PCR products were sequenced using Sanger sequencing at Microsynth (Vaulx-en-Velin, France) in both forward and reverse directions. Isolates were identified at the genus level using BLAST analysis against the GenBank database, with species assignment based on sequences showing 100% query coverage and > 99% percentage identity.

Sequences obtained from rpoD amplification were aligned using Muscle5 v.3.8.31 [43] and manually curated to remove gaps and low-quality regions. Poorly aligned positions and highly divergent regions were removed using Gblocks [44, 45]. To ensure a representative selection of isolates, seqkit [46] was used to identify a single representative isolate for each distinct rpoD sequence. Sequence analysis was conducted using the SeaView multiplatform [47]. A phylogenetic tree was constructed using the Distance method with 1000 bootstrap replicates and visualized using iTol [48]. Taxonomic assignments were based on clustering rpoD sequences at 98% nucleotide identity, in line with established thresholds for species-level discrimination in the genus Pseudomonas [15].

Sequencing and assembly of genomes

Out of the 185 isolates affiliated to fluorescent Pseudomonas, 29 were selected for whole-genome sequencing. The selection aimed to (i) represent as many unique rpoD sequence types as possible, and (ii) ensure an even distribution of isolates across soils and plant inoculation conditions (with or without F. graminearum Fg1). The resulting panel included 16 isolates from inoculated and 13 from non-inoculated wheat plants, distributed across the four soils as follows: 8 isolates from MI2, 5 from MI3, 9 from MI4, and 7 from MI5 (Fig. 1). It should be noted that this selection was not based on the relative abundance of each rpoD sequence in the natural communities. Therefore, subsequent genomic and phenotypic analyses are based on a targeted yet non-quantitative representation of the Pseudomonas diversity isolated from the rhizosphere. Genomic DNA for whole-genome sequencing was extracted from overnight cultures grown in TSB using the NucleoSpin Tissue kit (Macherey-Nagel), following the manufacturer’s instructions. DNA sequencing was performed by Novogene (Cambridge, England) using the Illumina NovaSeq 6000 platform, generating 2 × 150-bp paired-end reads. Raw sequencing data were recorded in FASTQ format, which contains both sequencing reads and associated quality scores. Sequence quality control and trimming were carried out using fastp v.0.23.1 [38] with default parameters. De novo genome assembly was performed using Unicycler v.0.5.0 [49] under default settings.

For taxonomic identification and phylogenetic analysis, genome comparisons were conducted using the Type Strain Genome Server (TYGS) (https://tygs.dsmz.de/) [50, 51]. Phylogenetic tree construction was based on genome-wide comparisons within TYGS.

Genome annotation and analysis

Genome annotation was performed using the MicroScope platform (v.3.15.4) [52]. Identification of genes associated with biocontrol and plant-growth promotion was conducted using DIAMOND blastP (v.2.0.8.146) [53], with genome protein sequences queried against a curated database of phytobeneficial functions. The search was performed using the parameters --query-cover 80 --id 70, ensuring a minimum 80% query coverage and 70% amino acid identity. A complete list of the targeted genes and their accession numbers is provided in Table S1.

The searched functions included: (i) antimicrobial compound biosynthesis, targeting genes encoding phenazine (phzABCDEFG) [54], 2-hexyl-5-propyl-alkylresorcinol (HPR) (darABC) [55], DAPG (phlABCD) [56], pyrrolnitrin (prnABCD) [57], hydrogen cyanide (hcnABC) [58], and pyoluteorin (pltABCDEFGLM) [59]; (ii) FitD insect toxin production (fitD); (iii) alkaline metalloproteinase AprA (aprA) production [9]; (iv) siderophore biosynthesis, with genes encoding pyoverdine (pvdL) [60], pyochelin (pchABCDEF) [61], and pseudomonine (pmsABCE) [62]; (v) signaling and modulation of plant hormonal balance, including genes involved in ethylene precursor deamination (acdS) [63], ethylene biosynthesis (efe) [64], auxin biosynthesis (iaaMH, ipdC/ppdC) [9, 65], auxin catabolism (iacABCDEFGHI) [9], acetoin synthesis (budB/ilvNB/alsS, budA/alsD) [9, 66], 2,3-butanediol biosynthesis (budC/ydjL) [67], 2,3-butanediol conversion to acetoin (adh/bdhA/ydjL) [67, 68], and acetoin catabolism (acoABCX) [68]; (vi) nutrient transformation, including phosphate solubilization (gcd, gad) [69], nitrogen fixation (nifHDK) [70], and denitrification (nirK, nirS) [70, 71].

When a functional pathway required multiple genes (e.g., both iaaM and iaaH for auxin biosynthesis via the indole-3-acetamide pathway) but only a subset of the required genes was found in a genome, the presence of missing genes was verified using less stringent BLAST criteria (--query-cover 80 --id 30). In addition, putative biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs) were identified using antiSMASH [72] within the MicroScope platform, and all annotations were manually curated.

Carbohydrate-active enzymes (CAZymes) were predicted using dbCAN2 v.3 [73] and further compared against the CAZy database using HMMER v.3.3 [74]. The predicted CAZyme families and subfamilies were classified based on known enzymatic activities characterized in the CAZy database (https://www.cazy.org/) [75] and manually curated following previously established methods [76]. A heatmap based on CAZyme abundance was generated using the pheatmap v.1.0.12 package [77].

Phenotypic characterization of Pseudomonas

The isolates were screened in vitro for plant growth-promoting (PGP) and biocontrol traits, including the production of HCN and lytic enzymes. HCN production was assessed following the protocol of Bakker & Schippers (1987) [78]. The activity of extracellular proteases, chitinases, and cellulases was tested on specific media, i.e., milk agar for protease activity, minimal medium supplemented with 10% colloidal chitin solution for chitinase activity [79], and carboxymethyl cellulose (CMC) medium for cellulase activity [80, 81]. The ability of isolates to solubilize inorganic phosphate was tested on NBRIP medium (National Botanical Research Institute’s Phosphate medium) following the method of Nautiyal (1999) [82]. Siderophore production was evaluated using the protocol of Pérez-Miranda et al. (2007) [83].

To assess metabolites involved in plant hormonal balance modulation, ACC deaminase activity was tested according to the method of Penrose & Glick (2003) [84], which detects α-ketobutyrate production. The production of various phytohormones was analyzed by Ultra-High Performance Liquid Chromatography (UHPLC). The targeted phytohormones included (i) seven auxins: indole-3-acetic acid (IAA), indole-3-lactic acid, indole-3-carboxylic acid, indole-3-pyruvic acid, indole-3-butyric acid (IBA), tryptophol, and indole-3-propionic acid; (ii) five cytokinins: trans-zeatin, trans-zeatin riboside (ZR), kinetin, 6-benzylaminopurine (BAP), and isopentenyl adenosine (IPA); (iii) two gibberellins: gibberellin A1 (GA1) and gibberellic acid (GA3); (iv) abscisic acid (ABA); and (v) kynurenic acid. For UHPLC analysis, all isolates were cultured at 28 °C for 3 days (300 rpm) in M9 minimal medium [85] supplemented with 0.4 mM tryptophan and 0.1 mM adenine. After incubation, cultures were centrifuged at 4500 g for 8 min and filtered through 0.2 μm filters. Supernatants were lyophilized (Alpha 1–4 LSC, Martin Christ, Osterode, Germany) for 24 h, and the resulting powder was extracted twice with methanol, then dried using a speed-vac concentrator (Centrivap Cold Trap Concentrator, LABCONCO, Kansas City, MO, USA). UHPLC separation was performed on an Agilent 1290 Series instrument (Agilent Technologies France, Les Ulis, France) using a 100 × 3 mm reverse-phase column (Agilent Poroshell 120 EC-C18, 2.7 μm particle size). A sample volume of 3 µL was injected into the column equilibrated with solvent A (water + 0.4% formic acid) and solvent B (acetonitrile) in a 98:2 ratio. The acetonitrile concentration was increased to 40% over 6 min, then to 100% over 4 min, followed by an isocratic step of 2 min, at a flow rate of 0.5 mL min⁻¹. Hormones were detected using a diode array detector (DAD) and an Agilent 6530 Q-TOF mass spectrometer in both positive and negative electrospray ionization modes. Identification was based on comparison with commercial standards, considering mass spectra, UV absorption (190–600 nm), and accurate mass measurements. A medium blank containing M9 minimal medium supplemented with tryptophan and adenine but without bacterial inoculation was processed in parallel under identical conditions and analyzed by UHPLC to correct for background signals originating from the medium components.

Inhibitory effect of Pseudomonas isolates towards F. graminearum

The inhibitory effect of VOCs produced by Pseudomonas isolates on F. graminearum Fg1 was assessed using a sealed dual-Petri dish system. For each assay, bacterial suspensions were adjusted to an optical density of OD600 = 1 (measured with an Ultrospec 10 Cell Density Meter, Amersham Biosciences, UK), and 30 µL was spread onto Tryptone Soya Agar (TSA; Carl Roth) plates. In parallel, Potato Dextrose Agar (PDA; Condalab) plates were inoculated at the center with 7 mm fungal plugs excised from the margin of 8-day-old F. graminearum Fg1 colonies. After 24 h of bacterial and fungal growth at 28 °C and 22 °C, respectively, the lid of the TSA plate was replaced with the PDA plate containing the fungus, and both plates were sealed together with parafilm to allow VOC diffusion without direct contact. Control plates were prepared under the same conditions but without bacteria. After 72 h of incubation at 22 °C, the radial growth of F. graminearum was measured, and mycelial growth inhibition was calculated using the formula (1 - r₂/r₁) × 100, where r₁ represents fungal growth in control plates and r₂ in the presence of bacterial VOCs [86].

The effect of bacterial isolates on F. graminearum Fg1 conidia germination was evaluated in microplates following the protocol of Besset-Manzoni et al. (2019) [87]. Bacterial supernatants were obtained from overnight cultures in TSB, filtered through 0.2 μm filters, and tested against fungal spores. A 10⁴ spores/mL suspension of F. graminearum was prepared by growing the fungus in MBB. Each well contained 100 µL of bacterial supernatant, 100 µL of Potato Dextrose Broth (Condalab), and 50 µL of fungal spore suspension. Positive controls included 100 µL of TSB instead of bacterial supernatant, while negative controls included 50 µL of Potato Dextrose Broth instead of fungal spores. Microplates were incubated at 28 °C for 5 days, after which fungal growth was assessed by measuring turbidity at 492 nm using an Infinite M200 Pro microplate reader (TECAN, Mannedorf, Switzerland). Absorbance values from the negative control were subtracted from each bacterial treatment and compared to the positive control.

Statistical analyses

All statistical analyses were conducted at P < 0.05, using R software v.4.2.1 (https://www.r-project.org/). For most probable number (MPN) calculations, data were log-transformed to achieve normal distribution and variance homogeneity. The effect of F. graminearum Fg1 inoculation on putative Pseudomonas was assessed using Student’s t-tests, and results are presented as means ± standard deviation.

For rpoD microbiota analysis, diversity metrics were computed using the phyloseq [88], vegan (Dixon, 2003), and ade4 [89] packages. Alpha diversity was assessed by calculating observed richness and Chao1 index for species richness [90], while Shannon [91] and inverse Simpson indices [92] were used to evaluate diversity and evenness. Beta diversity was assessed using non-metric multidimensional scaling (NMDS) based on Bray–Curtis dissimilarity matrices, and differences in community composition were tested using PERMANOVA (function adonis from vegan). Relationships between soil conditions and the presence/absence of Pseudomonas were analyzed using the envfit function from the vegan package. Graphical representations and visualizations were generated using the ggplot2 package [93].

Results

Population levels of fluorescent Pseudomonas in the rhizosphere

Population levels of culturable fluorescent Pseudomonas were estimated using the MPN method (see Materials and Methods), based on a single biological replicate per soil and treatment. In the absence of Fusarium inoculation, the highest levels of culturable fluorescent Pseudomonas were observed in the soils MI3 (fungistatic/suppressive) and MI5 (non-fungistatic/suppressive), both reaching 8.0 × 10⁹ cells/root system (Table S2). MI4 (non-fungistatic/conducive) and MI2 (fungistatic/suppressive) displayed lower abundances, with 4.5 × 10⁸ and 1.7 × 10⁸ cells/root system, respectively.

Following F. graminearum Fg1 inoculation, a marked decline in Pseudomonas abundance was observed in MI3 and MI5, down to 4.5 × 10⁷ and 2.7 × 10⁷ cells per root system, respectively. In contrast, abundance in MI2 and MI4 remained relatively stable between inoculated and non-inoculated conditions.

rpoD diversity of fluorescent Pseudomonas in the rhizosphere

To characterize the Pseudomonas communities in wheat rhizospheres across soils of varying suppressiveness (MI2, MI3, MI4, MI5), we performed a high-resolution metabarcoding analysis targeting the rpoD gene. Rarefaction curves based on rpoD amplicon data reached a plateau across samples, indicating sufficient sequencing depth to capture the majority of Pseudomonas diversity in each soil (Figure S1). Alpha diversity metrics based on species richness (i.e., observed richness and Chao1 index) showed no significant differences between soils (P > 0.05; Figs. 2A). However, diversity indices accounting for species abundance revealed significant variation. In particular, the inverse Simpson index indicated significantly higher evenness in MI2 compared to other soils (P < 0.05), while the Shannon index differed significantly between MI2 and MI3 or MI4, but not between MI2 and MI5 (P < 0.05; Fig. 2A), suggesting that fungistatic/suppressive soil MI2 supports a more balanced Pseudomonas community.

Beta diversity analysis based on Bray–Curtis dissimilarities revealed a significant effect of soil on Pseudomonas community composition (PERMANOVA, P < 0.005). NMDS ordination showed that communities from fungistatic/suppressive soils (MI2 and MI3) clustered separately from those in non-fungistatic/conducive soil MI4 and non-fungistatic/suppressive soil MI5 along the first axis, with further separation between MI4 and MI5 along the second axis (Fig. 2B). This indicates a clear structuring of Pseudomonas subcommunities according to both soil suppressiveness and soil history (e.g., manure input).

The NMDS ordination combined with envfit analysis (Fig. 2B) further revealed that certain Pseudomonas taxa were significantly associated with specific soil conditions. For instance, the taxa corresponding to P. chlororaphis IT-324P was associated with suppressive soils, while ASVs affiliated with P. koreensis and P. serboccidentalis were enriched in MI4. These results point to a partial phylogenetic structuring of the community in relation to suppressiveness status and possibly linked to functional traits involved in biocontrol.

Taxonomic composition inferred from ASV abundances showed strong contrasts between soils (Fig. 2C). Dominant taxa in suppressive soils included P. chlororaphis IT-324P and Pseudomonas GN-6 IT-P100/IT-P171 (P. mandelii subgroup), whereas conducive soil MI4 was characterized by higher relative abundances of P. koreensis and P. serboccidentalis. Several clades such as P. brassicacearum/P. fluorescens were common across multiple soils, indicating shared core taxa. Nonetheless, soil-specific enrichment of certain ASVs was consistent with NMDS patterns and may reflect differential ecological niches or functional contributions to suppressiveness.

Pseudomonas isolates, taxonomic diversity, and genome sequencing

A total of 406 putative Pseudomonas isolates were obtained from wheat rhizosphere samples. Amplification and sequencing of the rpoD gene were successful for 185 isolates (Figure S2), revealing 69 distinct rpoD sequences. These isolates were taxonomically distributed across seven of the eleven subgroups within the P. fluorescens group, as defined by Girard et al. [15]: P. fluorescens, P. kielensis, P. mandelii, P. jessenii, P. koreensis, P. corrugata, and P. chlororaphis. No isolates were recovered in the P. protegens, P. asplenii, P. gessardii, or P. fragi subgroups.

From these, 29 representative isolates were selected for whole-genome sequencing (selection criteria detailed in Materials and Methods), covering all seven detected subgroups (Fig. 3). Digital DNA-DNA hybridization (dDDH) between the 29 strains and type strains available in the TYGS database revealed 14 novel genomospecies (GN-1 to GN-14) among 16 isolates, with dDDH values below the 70% threshold (Table S3).

The 29 sequenced Pseudomonas strains exhibited genome sizes ranging from 5.841 to 7.602 Mb, with 5.415 to 7.598 coding DNA sequences (CDS) and a GC content between 58.53% and 63.09% (Table 2). These values were generally consistent within taxonomic subgroups, without significant deviations in genome size or composition. The presence of plasmids was detected in two strains, i.e., a 1.059-kb megaplasmid in P. serbica IT-P366 and a 9.3-kb plasmid in Pseudomonas GN-2 IT-4P. When considering soil origins, no clear pattern in genome size or GC content was observed (Table 2). These results suggest that genome size and GC content are more influenced by phylogeny than by soil condition. In summary, whole-genome analyses revealed substantial taxonomic diversity among the sequenced Pseudomonas strains, including 14 novel genomospecies, with no apparent association between genome size, GC content, or plasmid presence and soil suppressiveness status.

Distribution of genes involved in biocontrol or plant growth promotion

Across the 29 Pseudomonas genomes, phytobeneficial traits were broadly but unevenly distributed, with individual strains harboring between 0 and 13 relevant genes (Fig. 4). The most common genes were those associated with phosphate solubilization (21 strains), pyoverdine biosynthesis (19), ethylene production (18), alkaline metalloproteinase (18), hydrogen cyanide (HCN, 13), auxin biosynthesis (11), and denitrification (11). No clear correlation was observed between gene presence and soil origin or suppressiveness status. However, biosynthetic genes for antimicrobials such as phenazine, HPR, and pyrrolnitrin were restricted to P. chlororaphis strains, suggesting a subgroup-specific enrichment in biocontrol potential.

The P. chlororaphis subgroup exhibited the most extensive set of plant-beneficial traits among those studied (n = 14, Fig. 4). The four P. chlororaphis strains (IT-196P, IT-201P from MI3; IT-324P, IT-373P from MI5) contained genes for phenazine, pyrrolnitrin, HCN, pyoverdine, ethylene, auxin, phosphate solubilization, denitrification, and alkaline metalloproteinase (aprA) production. All strains carried HPR biosynthesis genes, except IT-201P, which lacked darC. Notably, P. chlororaphis IT-324P harbored the fitD insect toxin gene. This subgroup exhibited the highest number of biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs), with up to 16 detected (Table S4). AntiSMASH analysis identified a complete biosynthetic region for massetolide A in strains IT-196P, IT-201P, and IT-373P (Figure S3). Additionally, all P. chlororaphis strains contained operons for a type VI secretion system (T6SS), and strain IT-324P also carried genes for a type III secretion system (T3SS). Annotation of CAZymes showed that these strains possessed genes for chitinase and beta-glucanase production, except for IT-201P (Figure S4). They also contained genes of the Auxiliary Activity family 10 (AA10), encoding lytic polysaccharide monooxygenases that may target chitin (Figure S5). However, no genes for cellulases or mannanases were detected.

In the P. koreensis subgroup, P. serboccidentalis strains IT-215P (MI4) and IT-P374 (MI5) carried genes for HCN, pyoverdine, alkaline metalloproteinase, and phosphate solubilization (gcd/gad). Both strains contained an operon for T6SS (Figure S3), as well as genes encoding chitinases and AA10 family enzymes (Figure S4). Other strains within this subgroup, including Pseudomonas GN-1 IT-2P (MI2), GN-10 IT-260P (MI4), GN-13 IT-347P (MI5), GN-14 IT-395P (MI5), P. zeae IT-265P (MI4), and P. siliginis IT-1P (MI2), harbored similar genes, with variations in the presence of T3SS (P. zeae IT-265P) and beta-glucanase genes (IT-1P, IT-2P, IT-347P).

In the P. kielensis subgroup, strain Pseudomonas GN-7 IT-176P (MI3) harbored genes for 2,3-butanediol conversion, acetoin catabolism, phosphate solubilization (gcd), and CAZymes for chitinase and β-glucanase. A complete T6SS operon was also identified.

In the P. jessenii subgroup, strains P. serbica IT-194P (MI3) and IT-P366 (MI5) carried genes for auxin and ethylene biosynthesis. IT-194P additionally possessed pvdL for pyoverdine biosynthesis. Both strains encoded a T6SS operon, and IT-P366 carried a megaplasmid-borne Dot/Icm type IV secretion system (Figure S3). Both harbored genes for chitinase, β-glucanase, and AA10 enzymes, while IT-194P also had genes for cellulases.

In the P. mandelii subgroup, Pseudomonas GN-6 IT-100P and IT-171P (MI3) had genes for pyoverdine, ACC deaminase, alkaline metalloproteinase, and denitrification. Both strains exhibited operons for T3SS and T6SS (Figure S3) and contained genes for chitinase, beta-glucanase, and AA10 enzymes (Figures S4, S5).

In the P. corrugata subgroup, P. brassicacearum IT-228P (MI4) was the only strain harboring DAPG biosynthesis genes. It also contained genes for HCN, ACC deaminase, auxin, alkaline metalloproteinase, denitrification, acetoin metabolism, and operons for both T3SS and T6SS.

Finally, in the P. fluorescens subgroup, P. marginalis IT-357P (MI5) had genes for ethylene and alkaline metalloproteinase production, phosphate solubilization, denitrification, and acetoin metabolism. It also contained operons for T3SS and T6SS (Figure S3), along with genes for chitinase, cellulase, and AA10 enzyme production (Figures S4, S5).

In vitro biocontrol and plant growth promoting activity

The 29 Pseudomonas strains expressed diverse biocontrol and PGP traits in vitro, with varying abilities to produce lytic enzymes, siderophores, and phytohormones, as well as to solubilize phosphate (Fig. 5). While most strains produced at least one metabolite involved in plant-beneficial interactions, the expression of these traits differed between Pseudomonas subgroups.

In the P. chlororaphis subgroup, the MI3 isolates IT-196P and IT-201P and MI5 isolates IT-324P and IT-373P were all able to produce HCN, siderophores, and chitinase (except IT-324P), as well as protease (except IT-196P), but none produced cellulases. These strains also synthesized indole-3-acetic acid and indole-3-carboxylic acid (except IT-324P), trans-zeatin, isopentenyl adenosine, and kynurenic acid, while trans-zeatin riboside and 6-benzylaminopurine were only detected in IT-201P. None of these isolates solubilized phosphate.

In the P. koreensis subgroup, all eight strains produced HCN, siderophores, chitinase, indole-3-acetic acid, and indole-3-lactic acid. All but P. serboccidentalis IT-215P (from MI4) and Pseudomonas GN-13 IT-347P (MI5) were able to solubilize inorganic phosphate and produced indole-3-propionic acid. Protease activity was detected in five strains (P. siliginis IT-1P from MI2, P. serboccidentalis IT-215P and P. zeae IT-265P from MI4, and GN-13 IT-347P and GN-14 IT-395P from MI5), whereas cellulase was only produced by P. siliginis IT-1P. Indole-3-carboxylic acid was synthesized by four strains (IT-215P and IT-260P from MI4, IT-P374 and IT-395P from MI5), trans-zeatin by five strains, trans-zeatin riboside by three strains, 6-benzylaminopurine by four strains, and isopentenyl adenosine by three strains, while kynurenic acid was detected only in IT-2P and IT-215P.

In the P. kielensis subgroup, Pseudomonas GN-7 IT-176P (MI3) exhibited siderophore and protease production, along with phosphate solubilization. This strain also produced trans-zeatin, trans-zeatin riboside, 6-benzylaminopurine, isopentenyl adenosine, abscisic acid, and kynurenic acid.

In the P. jessenii subgroup, P. serbica IT-P366 (MI5) and IT-194P (MI3) solubilized phosphate and produced siderophores, chitinase, and phytohormones including trans-zeatin, 6-benzylaminopurine, isopentenyl adenosine, abscisic acid, and kynurenic acid, although only IT-P366 produced trans-zeatin riboside. Pseudomonas GN-2 IT-4P (MI2) and IT-P258 (MI4) exhibited phosphate solubilization, siderophore production, and synthesis of chitinase, indole-3-acetic acid, indole-3-lactic acid, and indole-3-propionic acid. However, only IT-P258 produced indole-3-carboxylic acid, whereas IT-4P uniquely produced trans-zeatin. Other P. jessenii subgroup strains (Pseudomonas GN-5 IT-74P from MI2, GN-11 IT-291P and GN-8 IT-218P from MI4, and P. jessenii IT-43P) shared the ability to produce siderophores, chitinase, trans-zeatin, and solubilize phosphate. Indole-3-acetic acid, indole-3-lactic acid, and indole-3-propionic acid were detected in all but IT-218P, while indole-3-carboxylic acid, trans-zeatin riboside, and kynurenic acid were exclusively produced by IT-218P. Protease production was only observed in IT-74P.

In the P. mandelii subgroup, Pseudomonas GN-6 IT-100P and IT-171P (both from MI3) displayed siderophore production, ACC deaminase activity, protease production, and phosphate solubilization, along with synthesis of indole-3-acetic acid, indole-3-lactic acid, and indole-3-propionic acid. IT-171P also produced chitinase and indole-3-carboxylic acid. Pseudomonas GN-3 IT-12P (MI2), GN-9 IT-253P (MI4), GN-12 IT-294P (MI4), and GN-4 IT-44P (MI2) all shared the ability to produce chitinase, indole-3-propionic acid, trans-zeatin, trans-zeatin riboside, and solubilize phosphate. While siderophores were synthesized by all but IT-294P, and indole-3-acetic acid and indole-3-lactic acid were produced by all but IT-253P, additional functional variations were noted: IT-12P and IT-44P exhibited ACC deaminase activity, 6-benzylaminopurine and isopentenyl adenosine production, IT-44P produced protease, and IT-12P was the only strain to produce indole-3-carboxylic acid.

In the P. corrugata subgroup, P. brassicacearum IT-228P (MI4) displayed a broad spectrum of biocontrol and PGP traits, including HCN, siderophore, ACC deaminase, and protease production, along with the synthesis of multiple phytohormones, such as indole-3-acetic acid, indole-3-lactic acid, indole-3-propionic acid, trans-zeatin, trans-zeatin riboside, 6-benzylaminopurine, and isopentenyl adenosine.

In the P. fluorescens subgroup, P. marginalis IT-357P (MI5) exhibited phosphate solubilization, as well as siderophore, ACC deaminase, protease, cellulase, and chitinase production. This strain also synthesized phytohormones, including indole-3-lactic acid, trans-zeatin, trans-zeatin riboside, 6-benzylaminopurine, isopentenyl adenosine, and kynurenic acid.

Correspondence between gene presence and in vitro activity

The comparison between genomic data and phenotypic assays revealed a strong relation for certain functions, while others exhibited discrepancies, suggesting the involvement of additional genetic pathways or regulatory factors. The production of HCN and ACC deaminase showed a clear correspondence between gene presence and in vitro activity, with these traits being detected in Pseudomonas strains from all soils (Fig. 5).

Siderophore production was recorded in nearly all strains, except Pseudomonas GN-12 IT-294P from MI4 and P. chlororaphis IT-324P from MI5, despite the presence of the corresponding genes in most cases. Similarly, phosphate solubilization activity matched the presence of gcd and/or gad genes in 15 out of 22 strains, but was absent in seven strains carrying these genes, suggesting the involvement of alternative phosphate solubilization mechanisms. Additionally, six strains possessed both gcd and gad but failed to solubilize phosphate under the tested conditions.

The presence of aprA, encoding an alkaline metalloproteinase, generally correlated with proteolytic activity, as 10 strains displayed both the gene and its associated phenotype. However, five strains exhibited protease activity without aprA, suggesting the involvement of other protease-encoding genes, while three strains carried aprA but did not express protease activity, likely due to regulatory differences.

Chitinase production was consistent with the presence of chitinase-related genes (Figure S4), whereas cellulase activity was only detected in P. siliginis IT-1P and P. marginalis IT-357P. Genomic analysis indicated that P. siliginis IT-1P harbored GH3 family genes targeting cellobiose, while P. marginalis IT-357P contained genes encoding enzymes involved in cellulose degradation (Figures S4 and S5).

The production of IAA was widespread, yet only 11 strains harbored iaaMH genes involved in auxin synthesis. None of the isolates carried ipdC or ppdC genes, and no strain produced indole-3-pyruvic acid, indole-3-butyric acid, tryptophol, kinetin, gibberellin A1, or gibberellic acid. However, all other phytohormones tested were produced by strains from all soils categories, further supporting the functional versatility of Pseudomonas isolates across different conditions.

In conclusion, the tested phenotypic traits were detected in strains from all three soils categories, with no apparent restriction to specific environmental conditions. Strains exhibiting a high number of phytobeneficial functions (12 or 13 traits) were found across all soils.

Effect of Pseudomonas volatile organic compounds on fungal growth and of Pseudomonas exudates on fungal sporulation

Out of the 29 Pseudomonas strains tested, seven—originating from all four soils (MI2, MI3, MI4, and MI5)—were able to inhibit the growth of F. graminearum Fg1 through VOCs production, and two strains (both from MI5) also inhibited fungal spore germination. Notably, VOCs-mediated growth inhibition exceeded 20% for P. marginalis IT-357P (31.6%) and Pseudomonas GN-14 IT-P395 (30.6%) from soil MI5, and P. serboccidentalis IT-215P (21.5%) from MI4. Lower inhibition levels were recorded for P. brassicacearum IT-P228 (15.4%) and Pseudomonas GN-9 IT-P253 (14.0%) from MI4, Pseudomonas GN-1 IT-2P (14%) from MI2, and P. chlororaphis IT-196P (18.9%) from MI3. In terms of spore germination, inhibition levels reached 13.5% for P. marginalis IT-357P and 18.1% for Pseudomonas GN-14 IT-P395.

Discussion

Comparisons of soils suppressive and conducive to Fusarium diseases have consistently revealed differences in microbial communities, with certain taxa being more prevalent in suppressive conditions [7, 94–97]. Among these, Pseudomonas is frequently associated with disease suppressiveness due to its ability to colonize diverse soil environments and exert multiple biocontrol effects [8, 10, 98]. However, it was unclear whether these Pseudomonas traits are exclusive to suppressive soils or also present in conducive soils but differentially expressed.

In this study, we analyzed the taxonomic composition and genomic and functional traits of Pseudomonas from wheat rhizosphere soils with different levels of suppressiveness to F. graminearum damping-off [28]. While suppressiveness was associated with both fungistatic (MI2 and MI3) and non-fungistatic (MI5) mechanisms [28], Pseudomonas populations were detected in all soils but showed contrasting patterns of abundance and composition depending on the suppressiveness status. Notably, MPN-based enumeration revealed a marked decrease in culturable fluorescent Pseudomonas populations in suppressive soils (MI3 and MI5) following Fusarium inoculation, contrasting with their stability in other soils. Although preliminary, these results suggest that biotic stress may differentially affect specific subcommunity members depending on the soil context, echoing the idea that microbial responses are shaped by both abiotic and biotic environmental cues [99–101]. Beyond abundance, fungistatic soils (MI2 and MI3) were characterized by more even and phylogenetically structured Pseudomonas communities, potentially supporting antagonistic activity, while non-fungistatic suppressive soil (MI5) showed enrichment of different lineages. This suggests that differences in Pseudomonas community composition or functional potential—rather than their mere presence—might underlie the contrasting mechanisms of suppression. This aligns with recent microbiome studies showing that suppressiveness is an emergent property of microbial network interactions rather than the sole presence of specific taxa [94, 102]. These findings are consistent with the differences in manure application history across the soils (Table 1), a factor known to shape soil microbiota and influence suppressiveness [28, 103]. However, since no Pseudomonas lineage was solely present in suppressive soils, it suggests that suppressiveness arises from shifts in relative abundance and possibly functional gene expression, rather than from the dominance of a single taxa [94, 97, 102].

In the present study, a total of 406 putative fluorescent Pseudomonas isolates were obtained from wheat rhizosphere samples collected in soils MI2, MI3, MI4, and MI5. From these, 65 isolates with unique rpoD sequences were identified, and 29 representative strains were selected for whole-genome sequencing. Whole-genome analyses revealed a remarkable taxonomic diversity, as the 29 sequenced isolates were affiliated to seven subgroups of the P. fluorescens group, with species distribution showing partial soil specificity—6 out of 7 species in soil MI2, 2 out of 4 in MI3, 7 out of 9 in MI4, and 3 out of 6 in MI5 (Table 2). This pattern broadly mirrored the soil-specific Pseudomonas community structure observed by rpoD metabarcoding but contrasted with distribution of phlD+ pseudomonads observed in suppressive soils by Frapolli et al. (2010) [12], who used DGGE-based fingerprinting targeting a single biocontrol gene.

Notably, our study on Mionica suppressive and conducive soils led to the identification of two novel Pseudomonas species we described recently [32] and 14 novel genomospecies presented here, pending formal taxonomic description. These findings underscore the unexplored taxonomic richness of Pseudomonas in agricultural soils. The occurrence of potentially endemic or novel Pseudomonas lineages in specific agroecosystems has been previously highlighted [104], supporting the idea that local soil conditions can select for distinct biocontrol populations. Despite this high taxonomic diversity, comparative genomic analysis did not reveal any genomic markers exclusive to suppressive soils, suggesting that biocontrol-related traits are not restricted to specific Pseudomonas lineages or soil conditions. This observation is consistent with large-scale comparative studies [105, 106] which have shown that many adaptive traits in Pseudomonas, particularly those associated with plant interactions, are distributed according to ecological context rather than strict phylogenetic boundaries. Such traits—encompassing secondary metabolism, hormone signaling, and secretion systems—often follow a mosaic pattern, shaped by environmental selection pressures and horizontal gene transfer.

Although genes encoding antimicrobial compounds such as HCN, siderophores, and pyoverdine were broadly distributed across isolates, regardless of the soil of origin, genome analysis of the 29 strains evidenced taxonomic particularities (Fig. 4). Notably, the four P. chlororaphis strains, which originated from MI3 and MI5 (both suppressive to F. graminearum disease), harbored as many as 11 to 13 genes involved in the biosynthesis of antimicrobials (phenazines, pyrrolnitrin), insecticidal toxins (fitD; [21]), and plant-beneficial compounds. This may reflect the taxonomy rather than the soil origin of the strains, as the ability of P. chlororaphis to produce compounds with antimicrobial activity [107] and protect plant is well documented [108]. P. brassicacearum IT-228P (from the conducive soil MI4) was the only isolate able to produce DAPG, a prominent biocontrol metabolite in several types of suppressive soils [10, 12]. These findings are consistent with the observations of Biessy et al. (2018) [22], who showed that the capacity to produce antimicrobials such as phenazines is widely distributed among Pseudomonas lineages across different agricultural soils, and is governed by complex regulatory mechanisms rather than being restricted to given soils. However, as reported in other systems [16, 97], biosynthetic pathways for phenazines and pyrrolnitrin were detected only in P. chlororaphis strains from MI3 and MI5, suggesting that specific lineages may contribute disproportionately to suppressiveness. However, the overall diversity of biocontrol-related traits across all soils suggests that suppressiveness is not driven by a narrow set of elite strains but rather reflects a collective functional potential within the community.

Beyond antimicrobial production, genes involved in plant-beneficial traits such as ethylene modulation (acdS, efe), auxin biosynthesis, and phosphate solubilization were also broadly distributed across isolates from all soils. This wide distribution indicates that Pseudomonas strains, irrespective of their origin, carry genetic determinants associated with PGP, supporting previous reports on the ubiquity of such traits in rhizosphere communities [109, 110]. However, phenotypic assays revealed that the presence of these genes did not always correlate with their expression in vitro, in agreement with earlier observations that gene presence is not a sufficient predictor of trait expression [87, 97]. These findings highlight the context-dependency of PGP and biocontrol traits, which may be conditionally expressed depending on biotic or abiotic stimuli. This is consistent with recent work demonstrating that the production of secondary metabolites by Pseudomonas can be strongly enhanced in plant-associated environments compared to artificial media [99], reflecting the influence of root exudates, nutrient availability, or stress signals on regulatory networks [106]. In our study, while HCN and ACC deaminase production showed good correspondence between gene presence and phenotype, other functions such as phosphate solubilization, protease activity, siderophore and IAA production exhibited notable discrepancies. For example, several strains harbored the genes gcd and gad but did not solubilize phosphate, and others displayed protease activity in the absence of aprA, suggesting the involvement of alternative pathways. Such discrepancies have also been reported in other systems [111], where gene expression varied depending on soil context. These findings emphasize that environmental factors—not just gene content—may play a critical role in modulating the expression of biocontrol and plant-growth-promoting traits in Pseudomonas.

Together, our results reinforce the idea that biocontrol is a multifactorial, polygenic, and context-dependent trait rather than a property restricted to a specific subset of strains. The diversity of gene repertoires, combined with partial redundancy and functional plasticity, suggests that microbial communities may collectively buffer environmental fluctuations and modulate plant health outcomes in complex and dynamic ways.

Functional assays in vitro revealed variability in biocontrol potential among isolates, regardless of their soil of origin. While seven strains inhibited F. graminearum growth via VOCs, two strains significantly reduced fungal spore germination. This raises the possibility that volatile-mediated antagonism may be a critical and underappreciated component of biocontrol in field soils. Biosynthesis of such volatiles in Pseudomonas has been characterized in model strains [18], and their antifungal activity is increasingly recognized [19], but their ecological roles remain difficult to quantify and deserve further investigation through in situ metabolomics and synthetic community experiments. Interestingly, some strains carrying biosynthetic genes for antifungal compounds exhibited weak in vitro inhibition, reinforcing the notion that gene presence alone does not predict biocontrol efficiency [87]. This highlights the importance of transcriptional regulation and microbial interactions in determining suppressiveness, as observed in studies where gene expression varied in response to soil conditions [102, 111]. In addition to microbial and regulatory factors, edaphic properties may also underlie some of the differences observed between soils. As shown previously [28], manured and non-manured Mionica soils differ in pH, organic matter, and nutrient content, with higher carbon and nitrogen availability in manured soils (MI2 and MI3) and lower values in non-manured ones (MI4 and MI5). While the present study focused on Pseudomonas communities, such abiotic contrasts are likely to influence community assembly and functional expression, potentially modulating the expression of biocontrol and plant-growth-promoting traits in situ.

Concluding, fluorescent Pseudomonas from this study displayed high taxonomic diversity and a broad range of biocontrol and plant-growth promoting features, regardless of the suppressiveness status of their soil of origin. We have revealed 14 putative novel Pseudomonas species with plant-beneficial traits, originating from both suppressive and conducive soils from the Mionica region. Our data show that Pseudomonas biocontrol functions are not exclusive to suppressive soils and suggest that the soil suppressiveness potential is likely dependent on soil-specific combinations of Pseudomonas strains with multiple and redundant functional traits, differing in their relative abundance and gene expression. Therefore, it is not possible to identify unique genomic markers distinguishing Pseudomonas from suppressive versus conducive soils. While the present study was limited to four soils from a single geographic region (Mionica, Serbia), this focused design provided a robust comparative framework to investigate microbial mechanisms underlying disease suppressiveness under particular climatic and edaphic conditions. The restricted sampling scope inevitably limits the generalization of our conclusions; however, it allowed us to minimize confounding factors and to focus on microbial and functional contrasts directly linked to suppressiveness. Future work extending this approach to soils from different agroecological regions will be necessary to determine the broader relevance of these mechanisms. Future research should also integrate transcriptomic and metabolomic approaches to assess in situ gene expression and Pseudomonas community dynamics in suppressive soils, along with their interactions with other types of microorganisms. Understanding how biocontrol traits are modulated by environmental factors will be critical for optimizing microbial-based plant protection strategies.
