Abstract

Prokaryotic Argonaute proteins (pAgos) are emerging as versatile tools in nucleic acid processing; however, their roles in bacterial physiology remain poorly defined. Here, we demonstrate that overexpression of Thermus thermophilus Argonaute (TtAgo) promotes transient bacterial filamentation in both T. thermophilus and Escherichia coli through disruption of cell division checkpoints. Scanning electron microscopy and nucleoid DNA staining revealed defective septum formation and aberrant nucleoid segregation in filamentous cells. By observing the effect of truncated TtAgo variants on septum formation and nucleoid segregation in E. coli, we found that impairment of septum formation in the filamentous cells was independent of the DNA cleavage activity of the TtAgo protein. Further, we demonstrate that TtSSB interacts with TtAgo and recruits it to the replication fork, where it facilitates the DNA-binding activity of TtAgo. TtAgo acquires short DNA guides from broken double-stranded DNA and cleaves complementary chromosomal sequences, leading to DNA damage and filamentation. This filamentation triggers homologous recombination-mediated repair in T. thermophilus, allowing cells to return to a rod-shaped state. These findings reveal a mechanism by which the SSB-TtAgo interaction can modulate the bacterial cell cycle and DNA repair pathways, highlighting its potential for synthetic biology and biotechnology applications.

Introduction

Ago proteins belong to a family of guide-dependent nucleases, which were first identified in eukaryotes as key components of RNA interference pathways [1, 2]. Homologous prokaryotic Argonaute (pAgo) proteins have since been discovered in bacteria and archaea. However, pAgos differ markedly from eukaryotic argonautes (eAgos) in that they can use DNA and/or RNA guides to target single-stranded RNA and/or double-stranded DNA for cleavage [3, 4], and play important roles in host defense [5, 6], DNA replication [7, 8], and DNA repair [9, 10]. The pAgo proteins have attracted widespread attention due to their potential applications in genome editing [11–13]. In contrast to CRISPR-associated (Cas) nucleases that depend on protospacer-adjacent motifs (PAMs) or protospacer flanking sequences (PFSs) for target recognition [14], pAgo can employ DNA guide strands, demonstrating superior stability compared to the RNA guides utilized in CRISPR-Cas systems [13].

Numerous pAgo proteins from diverse sources have been characterized, and they are classified into three structural categories: long-A pAgos, long-B pAgos, and short pAgos [15, 16]. Long-A type pAgos exhibit a fully conserved bilobed structure comprising N-terminal, L1, PAZ, L2, MID, and PIWI domains, with the PIWI domain demonstrating intrinsic catalytic activity [5, 17]. In contrast, long-B and short pAgos have amino acid substitutions at key catalytic residues in the PIWI domain and thus lack independent catalytic function. This functional deficiency necessitates their frequent genomic association and co-expression with dedicated nucleases and predicted effector proteins [18, 19]. Nevertheless, despite extensive structural and biochemical characterization of Long-A pAgo proteins and their catalytic mechanisms, their physiological roles and operational mechanisms in vivo remain poorly understood.

T. thermophilus is a model organism for studying thermophilic bacteria; unlike traditional model bacteria, it is polyploid, carrying approximately 4–5 copies of both its chromosome and megaplasmid per cell [20]. T. thermophilus encodes a pAgo homolog (termed TtAgo) from the megaplasmid. Swarts et al. [3] demonstrated that TtAgo autonomously cleaves double-stranded DNA (dsDNA) into short fragments via its PIWI domain catalytic residues—a guide-independent nuclease activity termed “DNA chopping”. This process degrades unstable dsDNA substrates into fragments that are subsequently loaded onto TtAgo and utilized as guides for sequence-specific target cleavage. However, how TtAgo locates or unwinds such DNA substrates in vivo remains unclear, and more broadly, the mechanisms by which pAgos are directed to specific genomic loci or foreign replicons (e.g. extrachromosomal DNA, transposons, plasmids, or phages) are poorly understood [21]. More recently, Kuzmenko et al. [6] reported that the Clostridium butyricum Argonaute (CbAgo) preferentially acquires small DNA guides from chromosomal replication termination (terC) sites and multicopy genomic elements. This bias depends on the nuclease activity of CbAgo and the DNA break–repair machinery of the host. Similarly, Jolly et al. [7] showed that in T. thermophilus, TtAgo binds 15–18-nt DNA guides derived from the chromosomal terminus. These TtAgo-guide complexes interact with DNA replication proteins to facilitate the resolution of intertwined daughter chromosomes. Nevertheless, the specific host factors responsible for this chromosome-processing function of TtAgo remain unknown.

Here, we investigate how TtAgo overexpression induces pronounced filamentation in both T. thermophilus and E. coli. We demonstrate that this filamentation phenotype is conserved across multiple pAgos. Furthermore, we reveal that single-stranded DNA-binding protein SSB directly interacts with TtAgo, significantly enhancing its guide DNA capture and target DNA cleavage activity. Our findings identify the SSB-TtAgo interaction as a critical regulatory node. Perturbation of this node via TtAgo overexpression disrupts replication fork stability and repair, providing a mechanistic framework to understand pAgo multifunctionality and its potential applications.

Materials and methods

Strains and growth conditions

Bacterial strains and plasmids used in this study are listed in Supplementary Table S5. The overexpression mutant strain (HB27/pMK-slp-AGO-sGFP_OE) and its control strain (HB27/pMK-slp-sGFP_OE) were constructed by introducing the respective vectors into wild-type T. thermophilus HB27. E. coli DH5α was used for DNA manipulations, E. coli BL21(DE3) and Rosetta (DE3) strains were used for protein expression. T. thermophilus HB27 and its derivative strains were grown at 60°C or 65°C with vigorous shaking in modified TB medium [22]. Kanamycin (30 μg/mL for E. coli and 20 μg/ mL for T. thermophilus), ampicillin (100 μg/ mL for E. coli), and chloramphenicol (30 μg/ mL for E. coli) were supplemented into the growth medium when required.

Plasmid construction and genomic mutants

The construction of T. thermophilus HB27 gene knockout strains followed previously described methods [22]. Based on the pRKP31-AC2 shuttle vector (containing a set of self-targeting artificial mini-CRISPR array repeats), the spacer fragment matching the editing site, and the upstream and downstream sequences (approximately 500 bp each) of the target region, were inserted into the corresponding positions of the plasmid using BbsI digestion and a seamless cloning kit (Abclonal, Wuhan, China). The replicative vectors in T. thermophilus were based on the E. coli/T. thermophilus shuttle vector pMK18 [23]. The sgfp gene was chemically synthesized based on the published data [23], and cloned into the pMK18 vector, resulting in pMK-slp-sGFP. The plasmids were introduced into T. thermophilus HB27 via natural transformation, and transformants were screened by PCR validation and Sanger sequencing (Sangon, Shanghai, China). Finally, for the gene knockout strains, the editing plasmids were eliminated by repeated subculturing under non-selective conditions.

All prokaryotic expression-related vectors (Supplementary Table S5) were derived from pET28a or pGEX-6p-1 and constructed via seamless cloning. The TtAgo coding sequence was amplified using primers TtAgo F NdeI/TtAgo R HindIII and cloned into the pET28a vector (containing an N-terminal 6 × His tag) to generate pET28a-TtAgo. The Flag-tagged vector pET28a-Flag-TtAgo was constructed by PCR amplification of pET28a-TtAgo with primers Flag-TtAgo-F/Flag-TtAgo-R2, followed by circular ligation of the amplified fragment. The CbAgo coding sequence was amplified using primers Cb-NdeI F/Cb-XhoI R and cloned into the pET28a vector (containing an N-terminal 6 × His tag) to generate pET28a-CbAgo. Using the primers listed in Supplementary Table S6, sequential domain deletions of TtAgo were generated by PCR amplification from this vector, followed by T4 DNA ligase-mediated ligation and recircularization to produce a series of truncated expression plasmids. The TtSSB or EcSSB coding sequence was amplified using primers BamHI-TtSSB-F/NotI-TtSSB-R or EcoRI-ssb-F/ssb-XhoI-R and cloned into the pGEX-6p-1 vector (containing an N-terminal GST tag) to generate pGEX-TtSSB or pGEX-EcSSB. The Flag-tagged vector pGEX-Flag or pGEX-Flag-TtSSB was constructed by PCR amplification of pGEX-6p-1 or pGEX-TtSSB with primers pGEX-Flag-F/R, followed by circular ligation of the amplified fragment.

Filamentation assay

For filamentation assays, wild-type and mutant T. thermophilus strains were revived from glycerol stocks and cultured overnight in 5 mL TB medium (plus kanamycin when applicable) at 65°C, 200 rpm. Cultures were diluted 1:100 into fresh TB medium with or without antibiotics and incubated at 60°C to OD600 values of 0.4, 0.6, or 1.2. Samples were collected for morphological analysis and RNA extraction. For E. coli, glycerol stocks were inoculated into LB medium with 30 μg/mL kanamycin and grown overnight at 37°C. Cultures were diluted 1:33 into fresh LB with antibiotics and grown to OD600 = 0.6, followed by 0.1 mM isopropyl-β-d-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG) induction at 16°C for 6 h before sampling.

Microscopy

(i) Light Microscopy

Cell samples (20 μL) were diluted into 180 μL fresh medium and pelleted at 4°C, 5000 × g for 5 min. Cells were washed twice with PBS and resuspended in 200 μL PBS. A 5 μL aliquot was spread on glass slides and Gram-stained. Images were acquired using Nikon Eclipse 80i and analyzed with ImageJ (NIH, USA).

(ii) Fluorescence microscopy

Localization of sGFP-tagged and mCherry-tagged proteins in E. coli was assessed by fluorescence microscopy. Cells were harvested by centrifugation and washed twice with PBS. DAPI (4′,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole, 2 μg/mL) was added to the pellet for DNA staining and incubated in the dark at room temperature for 20 min. Stained cells were imaged using an Olympus BX63 microscope equipped with a 100 W mercury lamp and appropriate filters. Images were captured using Olympus BX63 and processed with OlyVIA software (Olympus, Japan).

(iii) Scanning Electron Microscopy

Cultures were harvested at mid-log phase by centrifugation (3000 × g, 5 min), washed five times in PBS, and fixed in 2.5% glutaraldehyde at 4°C for ≥4 h. Samples were dehydrated using graded ethanol (20–100%), freeze-dried, and imaged using a Zeiss Sigma560 scanning electron microscope (Carl Zeiss AG, Germany).

RT-qPCR

Total RNA was extracted from fresh cultures of bacteria grown to the appropriate time point using the U-Fast Bacterial RNA Extraction Kit (Zoman Biotech, Beijing). Reverse transcription was performed on 1 μg total RNA using ABScript III RT Master Mix (Abclonal, Wuhan, China) with random primer in a 20 µL reaction (37°C for 2 min, 55°C for 15 min, 85°C for 5 min). Residual genomic DNA in the total RNA sample is also eliminated at this step. qPCR was performed in 10 µL reactions on a QuantStudio 3 Real-Time PCR System (Applied Biosystem) using Universal SYBR Green Fast qPCR Mix (Abclonal, Wuhan, China). Primers used were listed in Supplementary Table S6. Cycling conditions: 95°C for 3 min; 40 cycles of 95°C for 5 sec, 60°C for 31 sec; melt curve 60°C to 95°C. TT_C1610 was used as an internal control. Relative quantification was performed using the 2^-ΔΔCT method. Each sample was run in four biological replicates.

Protein expression and purification

E. coli Rosetta (DE3) harboring expression constructs for His-tagged proteins (TtAgo, truncation mutants (ΔPIWI, ΔMP, ΔLMP), TtSSB and TtSSBΔIDR), or GST-tagged proteins (GST, GST-EcSSB and GST-TtSSB) were grown in LB with appropriate antibiotics. Cultures were induced with 0.1 mM IPTG at OD600 ≈ 0.6 and incubated at 16°C for 20 h. Cells were harvested by centrifugation at 7000 × g for 10 min at 4°C and resuspended in His-lysis buffer [50 mM Tris–HCl pH 7.5, 300 mM NaCl, 10% glycerol, 5 mM MgCl₂, 20 mM imidazole, 1 mM Tris(2-chloroethyl) Phosphate (TCEP), and 1 mM PMSF] or GST lysis buffer (50 mM Tris–HCl pH 7.5, 300 mM NaCl, 10% glycerol, 1 mM TCEP, and 1mM PMSF) at a ratio of 25:1 (culture volume:buffer, v/v). Cells were disrupted by high-pressure homogenization, and the lysates were cleared by centrifugation at 15 000 × g for 30 min at 4°C to remove cellular debris. The resulting crude lysate was subjected to affinity purification using Ni-charged Resin FF columns (GenScript, Nanjing, China) or GST-Sepharose Resin 4FF columns (Sangon, Shanghai, China). His-tagged proteins were eluted with lysis buffer containing a series of gradient concentrations of imidazole (50, 100, 200, 500, 1000 mM). GST-tagged proteins were eluted using an elution buffer (GST lysis buffer supplemented with 10 mM reduced glutathione, pH 7.5). The fractions containing target proteins were concentrated employing an Amiconultra centrifugal filter with appropriate molecular weight cutoffs (Millipore, Billerica, MA, USA) and then loaded onto a Superdex 200 Increase 10/300 GL column (Cytiva, Marlborough, MA, USA). The proteins were eluted with buffer B (20 mM Tris–HCl, pH 7.5, 300 mM NaCl) and analyzed by sodium dodecyl sulfate-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS–PAGE). Finally, the target proteins were concentrated again and stored at -80°C in storage buffer [20 mM Tris–HCl (pH 7.5), 500 mM NaCl, 1 mM TCEP, and 10% glycerol].

IP-MS

(i) Sample preparation

TtAgo and related constructs were expressed in E. coli and cultured as described in the filamentation assay section. Cells were harvested and resuspended in IP Buffer I (20 mM Tris–HCl, pH 7.5, 300 mM NaCl, 5 mM MgCl₂, 10% glycerol, 1 mM TCEP, and 1 mM PMSF), followed by lysis via sonication. Lysates were cleared by centrifugation at 15 000 × g for 30 min at 4°C, and the supernatant was subjected to affinity purification using High Affinity Ni-NTA Resin (GenScript, Nanjing, China). The resin was washed with IP Buffer II (50 mM Tris–HCl, pH 7.5, 300 mM NaCl, 1 mM TCEP, and 1 mM PMSF) containing 20 mM imidazole and resuspended in PBS buffer. For T. thermophilus HB27, WT and Δago strains were grown to mid-log phase (OD600 = 0.5), and lysates were prepared using the procedure described above. Cell lysates were incubated with rabbit polyclonal anti-TtAgo antibody (8 μg antibody per mg total protein) at 4°C for 12 h. The immune complexes were then incubated with Protein A + G agarose for 4 h with gentle inversion. The resin was washed with IP Buffer II and recovered by resuspension in PBS buffer.

(ii) LC-MS/MS analysis

LC-MS analysis was outsourced to SpecAlly Life Technology Co., Ltd (Wuhan, China), and performed as follows. Samples were treated with a reaction solution containing 1% SDC, 100 mM Tris-HCl (pH 8.5), 10 mM TCEP, and 40 mM CAA, and incubated at 95°C for 10 min to achieve protein denaturation, reduction, and alkylation. After centrifugation, the supernatant was collected and diluted with an equal volume of ddH₂O. Trypsin was added at a 1:50 (enzyme: protein, w/w) ratio, and the mixture was incubated overnight at 37°C with gentle agitation for enzymatic digestion. The reaction was terminated by the addition of trifluoroacetic acid (TFA), and the digested samples were centrifuged at 12 000 × g. The resulting supernatants were desalted using in-house packed SDB columns, vacuum-dried, and stored at −20°C.

Peptides were analyzed using an UltiMate 3000 RSLCnano system coupled to a Q Exactive HF mass spectrometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA). Samples were loaded via autosampler onto a C18 trap column (75 μm × 2 cm, 3 μm particle size, 100 Å pore size, Thermo) and separated using a custom-packed analytical column (75 μm × 25 cm, 1.9 μm particle size, 100 Å pore size). Gradient elution was performed using mobile phase A (0.1% formic acid, 3% DMSO in H₂O) and mobile phase B (0.1% formic acid, 3% DMSO in ACN) at a flow rate of 300 nL/min. The mass spectrometer was operated in data-dependent acquisition (DDA) mode with a top-20 method and a full scan range of 350–1500 m/z. MS data were processed using MaxQuant software (v1.6.10.43) with the Andromeda search engine. Protein identification and quantification were performed by searching against the UniProt databases of E. coli K12 (Taxon ID: 83 333, 6461 entries) and T. thermophilus HB27 (Taxon ID: 262 724, 2 201 entries).

Protein-protein interaction validation

To investigate the interaction between TtAgo and SSB, two expression plasmids bearing different antibiotic resistance markers were co-transformed into E. coli Rosetta (DE3) cells. Co-transformants expressing both proteins were selected on LB agar plates containing 30 μg/mL kanamycin and 100 μg/mL ampicillin. Single colonies were inoculated into 5 mL LB medium supplemented with the same antibiotics and grown overnight at 37°C with shaking at 200 rpm. Overnight cultures were diluted 1:100 into fresh antibiotic-containing LB medium and incubated at 37°C until OD600 reached 0.6. Protein expression was induced with 0.1 mM IPTG for 16 h at 16°C, after which cells were harvested. Cells were rinsed with PBS and resuspended in 1 mL of co-immunoprecipitation (Co-IP) lysis buffer (20 mM Tris–HCl, pH 8.0, 300 mM NaCl, 5 mM MgCl₂, 10% glycerol, 1 mM PMSF, 1 mM TCEP). Cells were lysed by sonication, and crude lysates were clarified by centrifugation at 15 000 × g for 30 min at 4°C. The supernatant was incubated with ANTI-FLAG Affinity Gel (Beyotime Biotechnology, Shanghai, China) at 4°C for 4 h. Eluted proteins were collected in 100 μL of 3 × Flag peptide buffer (50 mM Tris–HCl, pH 8.0, 150 mM NaCl, 200 μg/mL 3 × Flag peptide). Immunoblot analysis was subsequently performed using either Rabbit anti GST-Tag mAb (Abclonal, Wuhan, China) or Mouse anti DDDDK-Tag mAb (Abclonal, Wuhan, China), followed by incubation with HRP-conjugated Goat anti-rabbit IgG (H + L) or Goat anti-mouse IgG (H + L) (Abclonal, Wuhan, China). Protein bands were visualized using Omni-ECLTM Ultrasensitive Chemiluminescent Detection Kit (Epizyme Biomedical Technology, Shanghai, China). For the DNase I-treated group, the resin was washed three times with Co-IP lysis buffer after incubation with the supernatant, then resuspended in 100 μL DNase I buffer. Subsequently, 4 U (4 μL) of DNase I (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA) was added, followed by incubation at 37°C for 1 h. Following three washes with Co-IP lysis buffer, the ANTI-FLAG Affinity Gel was subjected to identical elution procedures as described above. Control groups were processed identically but with an equivalent volume of deionized water substituted for DNase I. Using the SYBR II dye (Biosharp, Beijing, China) to quantify and compare nucleic acid levels between the DNase I-treated and untreated groups.

Predicted protein-protein interaction interfaces were modeled using AlphaFold 3. Structural visualization and interface mapping were performed using PyMOL.

Activity assays

(i) Electrophoretic mobility shift assay(EMSA)

To assess the nucleic acid-binding affinity of TtAgo and its truncation variants, a 59-nt DNA probe containing the predicted ter site of E. coli BL21(DE3) was used. The putative replication terminus of E. coli was predicted using Ori-Finder 2022 (https://tubic.org/Ori-Finder2022/public/index.php/index) [24] (Supplementary Fig. S7D). Purified proteins were incubated with either 5′-phosphorylated ssDNA (5′P-ssDNA) or hydroxylated ssDNA (5′OH-ssDNA) (sequences listed in Supplementary Table S6) at a 4:1 molar ratio (TtAgo: ssDNA) in reaction buffer containing 20 mM Tris-HCl (pH 7.5), 250 mM NaCl, and 0.5 mM MnCl₂. Reactions were incubated at 37°C for 30 min and subsequently mixed with 10 × EMSA/Gel-shift loading dye (Beyotime, Shanghai, China). Samples were resolved on a 12% native polyacrylamide gel and stained with SYBR Green Nucleic Acid Gel Stain (Invitrogen). Nucleic acids were visualized using a gel imaging system (Clinx, Shanghai, China).

For the TtSSB-added experimental group, purified TtAgo protein was mixed with 5′-hydroxylated ssDNA (DE3 terminus-R, 21, 39, 59nt; sequence listed in Supplementary Table S6) at a molar ratio of 2:1 (TtAgo:ssDNA), and the concentration of TtSSB in the system was gradually increased, and the reaction was carried out at 60 °C under the same conditions and treatments as described above.

For TtSSB-related proteins, purified TtSSB or TtSSBΔIDR was mixed with 5′-hydroxylated single-stranded DNA (DE3 terminus-R, 59 nt; sequence listed in Supplementary Table S6) at a molar ratio of 8:1 (protein:ssDNA), and the reactions were then carried out at 60°C under the same conditions and processing procedure described above.

(ii) TtAgo cleavage assay

Cleavage reactions were performed using purified TtAgo or its variants, ssDNA guides, and ssDNA targets at a molar ratio of 5:2:1 in reaction buffer. Reactions were incubated at either 16°C or 37°C for 6 h, or at 65°C for 1 h. Reactions were terminated by the addition of gel loading buffer and heat denatured at 95°C for 10 min. Products were resolved on a 12% denaturing polyacrylamide gel, stained with SYBR Green, and visualized using a gel imaging system (Clinx, Shanghai, China).

For cleavage assays at varying protein concentrations, the substrate concentration was fixed at 200 nM while the concentrations of TtAgo and the TtAgoΔMP variant were incrementally increased. Reactions were incubated at 37°C for 6 h and analyzed as described above. In assays involving EcSSB protein, TtAgo and TtAgoΔMP concentrations were fixed at 1 μM while the SSB concentration was titrated. Reactions were performed at 37°C for 6 h and analyzed identically. In the TtSSB or TtSSBΔIDR-assisted cleavage experiments, 0.8 μM TtAgo was incubated with gradually increasing concentrations of TtSSB at 50°C or 55 °C for 0.5 h or 1 h, followed by the same termination and treatment procedures. In the TtAgo pre-loading experiment, 2 μM TtAgo was first pre-incubated with 0.4 μM guide DNA (hereafter, termed as gDNA) at 55°C for 30 min. Subsequently, increasing concentrations of TtSSB (0–3.6 μM) and 0.2 μM target DNA (hereafter, termed as tDNA) were added to the reaction mixture. The cleavage reaction proceeded at 55°C for 1 h before termination. In the control group, TtSSB was added concurrently with TtAgo and gDNA in the pre-incubation step.

Results

TtAgo overexpression induces transient filamentation in T. thermophilus

To elucidate the role of T. thermophilus Argonaute (TtAgo) in DNA replication and its potential interactions with auxiliary proteins, we engineered T. thermophilus HB27 strains, including an ago gene deletion mutant (Δago), an AGO-sGFP overexpression strain (HB27/pMK-slp-AGO-sGFP_OE), and a control strain overexpressing sGFP alone (HB27/pMK-slp-sGFP_OE). Genotypes were verified by PCR and sequencing (Supplementary Fig. S1A).

These strains, alongside wild-type (WT) HB27, were cultured under optimal conditions with 20 μg/mL kanamycin for plasmid-containing strains (Transformed). Morphological analyses were conducted at early (OD600 = 0.4), mid (OD600 = 0.6), and late (OD600 = 1.2) log growth phases. In the early log phase, all strains exhibited comparable rod-shaped morphology. However, at mid-log phase, the HB27/pMK-slp-AGO-sGFP_OE strain displayed pronounced cell elongation, while WT, Δago, and HB27/pMK-slp-sGFP_OE strains retained short rod-like morphologies (Fig. 1A). By late log phase, the cell lengths of the AGO-sGFP strain reverted to near-normal, indicating a transient filamentation phenotype. Quantitative analysis of cell lengths from microscopic images confirmed a significant increase in the HB27/pMK-slp-AGO-sGFP_OE strain at mid-log phase, with mean cell lengths 2.07-fold and 1.78-fold longer than WT and sGFP-only strains, respectively (Fig. 1B, Supplementary Table S1). At late log phase, the AGO-sGFP strain’s cell length was comparable to the sGFP-only strain, though both were slightly longer than WT, suggesting that excess TtAgo disrupts the DNA replication checkpoint, delaying septation and division.

Figure 1. Filamentation Induced by Ago Overexpression. (A, B) Morphological analysis and cell length quantification of T. thermophilus HB27 wild-type (WT), ago deletion strain (Δago), Ago-overexpressing strain (HB27/pMK-slp-AGO-sGFP_OE), and vector control strain (HB27/pMK-slp-sGFP_OE) at different growth stages (OD600= 0.4, 0.6, and 1.2). (C, D) Morphology and cell length measurements of E. coli expressing heterologous CbAgo, TtAgo, or PfAgo. (E) Schematic representation of TtAgo truncation constructs and the corresponding filamentation phenotypes. (F) Cell length analysis of E. coli expressing full-length TtAgo or its truncated variants. The scale bars in (A, C) are 20 μm. The dashed line in (B, D, and F) means the mean value of cell length. Error bars indicate the standard deviations (SD). The numerical values in panel D represent the cell lengths of the corresponding groups, with data presented as mean ± SD. Different letters above bars indicate significant differences (P < 0.05) according to Duncan’s test. The number of cells counted, denoted as “n”, is indicated below each panel.

To investigate the correlation between filamentation and TtAgo expression levels, we quantified ago transcript abundance across growth phases using RT-qPCR (Supplementary Fig. S1B and C). The HB27/pMK-slp-AGO-sGFP_OE strain exhibited significantly elevated ago expression at all time points, peaking at mid-log phase, which coincided with the pronounced filamentation phenotype. Despite persistent high ago transcript levels at late log phase, cell morphology normalized, suggesting activation of compensatory repair mechanisms that mitigate TtAgo-induced elongation. These findings establish that elevated TtAgo expression drives transient filamentation during exponential growth in T. thermophilus, likely through interference with DNA replication and cell division processes.

pAgo overexpression triggers filamentation in E. coli

To determine whether the filamentation phenotype is specific to TtAgo or a broader characteristic of prokaryotic Argonaute proteins (pAgos), we heterologously expressed TtAgo, Clostridium butyricum Argonaute (CbAgo), and Pyrococcus furiosus Argonaute (PfAgo) in E. coli BL21 (Rosetta). Strikingly, all three pAgos induced significant cell elongation in E. coli, with mean cell lengths increased by approximately 2.4-fold (TtAgo), 3.2-fold (CbAgo), and 1.6-fold (PfAgo) compared to control cells harboring an empty vector (Fig. 1C and D). The filamentation phenotype was heterogeneous within the cell population, likely reflecting variable pAgo expression levels among individual cells. These results demonstrate that pAgo-mediated filamentation is a conserved phenomenon across diverse bacterial species and pAgo orthologs, suggesting a shared mechanism linked to their nucleic acid-processing activities. This observation sets the stage for subsequent investigations into the structural and molecular basis of pAgo-induced filamentation, particularly the contributions of specific protein domains and their interactions with cellular machineries.

Bacterial filamentation often represents a stress response that enhances survival under challenges such as antibiotic treatment or phage infection [25]. Thus, the observed elongation in E. coli upon pAgo overexpression implies a connection to DNA damage induction, chromosomal maintenance, or division delays. Given that TtAgo comprises multiple domains, we systematically truncated its sequence (Fig. 1E) and cloned each variant into an overexpression vector for heterologous expression in E. coli Rosetta (DE3), evaluating their impact on cell morphology. Strains expressing N-terminally truncated TtAgo variants (TtAgoΔN, TtAgoΔNL, TtAgoΔNLP, TtAgoΔNLPL, TtAgoΔNLPLM) exhibited no filamentation. Similarly, full or partial C-terminally truncated variants (TtAgoΔLPLMP, TtAgoΔPLMP, TtAgoΔLMP) failed to induce elongation. In contrast, variants lacking the PIWI domain (TtAgoΔPIWI) or both MID and PIWI domains (TtAgoΔMP) retained the ability to trigger filamentation. The amino acids D478 and D546, located in the PIWI domain, were shown to be critical for the catalytic activity of TtAgo [3]. Thus, to avoid the impact of domain deletion on the overall folding of the protein, we constructed a catalytically inactive version of TtAgo (TtAgoDM, D478A/D546A) and also expressed it in E. coli for phenotypic analysis (Fig. 1E). The result showed that this catalytically inactive TtAgo mutant retained the ability to trigger filament formation (Supplementary Fig. S2A).

Statistical analysis corroborated these observations (Fig. 1F, Supplementary Table S2). Control cells with an empty vector displayed a mean length of 2.5 ± 0.9 μm, whereas those expressing full-length TtAgo, TtAgoDM, lacking the PIWI domain or both MID and PIWI domains were substantially longer with cell lengths of 5.9 ± 4.9, 6.3 ± 6.0, 6.4 ± 5.4, and 6.0 ± 4.7 μm, respectively. No significant differences among these four groups were observed, but each was significantly longer than the control. Other truncated variants showed lengths akin to control, confirming loss of the filamentation phenotype. Prior research has underscored the necessity of an intact N-terminal domain for Ago catalytic function [26]. Aligning with this, most N-terminally truncated TtAgo variants partitioned to the insoluble fraction post-lysis (Supplementary Fig. S2B), indicating compromised stability and folding that abolishes activity. Consequently, these strains maintained normal morphology (Fig. 1F, Supplementary Fig. S2A). Collectively, these data affirm that the integrity of the N-terminal domains is indispensable for TtAgo’s solubility and, consequently, for its ability to elicit bacterial filamentation, providing a structural foundation for its disruptive effects on cellular processes observed in prior sections.

TtAgo and C-terminal truncated variants disrupt septum formation

Bacterial filamentation typically results from defective septum formation, halting cell division and yielding elongated structures, or from impaired chromosomal segregation [27–29]. To explore the mechanistic basis of TtAgo-induced filamentation, we examined septum formation in filamentous (TtAgo, TtAgoΔPIWI) and non-filamentous (empty vector, TtAgoΔPLMP) E. coli strains using scanning electron microscopy (SEM). Non-filamentous strains displayed clear mid-cell septal invaginations, indicative of normal division (white arrows, Fig. 2A). In contrast, filamentous strains lacked proper septa, with some cells showing surface damage or breakage, suggesting compromised mechanical stability during division attempts (red arrows, Fig. 2A). These findings indicate that TtAgo and TtAgoΔPIWI overexpression disrupts septum formation, contributing to the elongated morphology.

To assess whether TtAgo affects DNA structure and segregation, we expressed sGFP-tagged full-length TtAgo and its truncated variants in E. coli, followed by DAPI (4′,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole) staining for nucleoid visualization under fluorescence microscopy. Cells expressing full-length TtAgo exhibited distinct nucleoid segregation but with multiple (>2) daughter nucleoids within a single cell, indicating a decoupling of DNA partitioning from cell septation (yellow arrows, Fig. 2B, top). Conversely, TtAgoΔPIWI and TtAgoΔMP variants displayed continuous nucleoid staining, suggestive of defective chromosome segregation alongside septum formation issues (yellow arrows, Fig. 2B, middle and bottom). pAgos have been reported to participate in various DNA-associated processes [7], including DNA replication [8, 30], repair [6, 9, 31], and transcriptional regulation [18]. Full-length TtAgo localized primarily within nucleoids, forming discrete foci at daughter nucleoid poles, consistent with its role in nucleic acid processing (Fig. 2B).

Notably, the C-terminal truncated variants TtAgoΔPIWI and TtAgoΔMP, although they also cause filamentalization, accumulated aberrantly at nucleoid junctions (white arrows, Fig. 2B), potentially obstructing chromosome segregation. The aberrant accumulation of TtAgoΔPIWI and TtAgoΔMP at nucleoid junctions likely stems from their retained DNA-binding capacity, disrupting segregation and driving filamentation independently of cleavage activity. In contrast, non-filamentous strains expressing TtAgoΔN, TtAgoΔNL, TtAgoΔNLP, TtAgoΔNLPL, or TtAgoΔNLPLM variants showed protein accumulation at cell poles, a compartment associated with sequestration of misfolded proteins (Supplementary Fig. S3A and B). Additionally, in vivo FRAP experiments further confirmed that sGFP-TtAgoΔN lacks recovering capacity, manifesting as immobile, incompatible, and irreversible aggregates. In contrast, fluorescence within sGFP-TtAgoΔPIWI foci exhibited partial recovery after photobleaching, demonstrating mobility and dynamics incompatible with static, irreversible aggregates (Supplementary Fig. S4). This aligns with our earlier finding that N-terminal truncations yield insoluble, likely inactive proteins (Supplementary Fig. S2B), explaining their failure to induce filamentation. Similarly, the TtAgoΔLMP variant’s loss of DNA-binding activity accounts for its normal morphology. Non-filamentous variants, either misfolded or lacking DNA-binding capacity, do not interfere with cellular processes, reflecting the critical roles of the N-terminal in TtAgo’s physiological effects. These findings set the stage for exploring how TtAgo interfaces with cellular stress responses, such as the SOS pathway, to further modulate filamentation.

TtAgo overexpression activates the SOS response and disrupts the division machinery

The bacterial SOS response is a tightly regulated transcriptional network activated by DNA damage, orchestrating repair and cell-cycle arrest [25]. To determine whether TtAgo-induced filamentation is associated with DNA damage and SOS pathway activation, we quantified the expression of canonical SOS response genes (recA, sulA, lexA) and rpoB, which encodes the RNA polymerase β subunit critical for transcription elongation and responsive to chromosomal stress, in E. coli strains expressing full-length TtAgo and truncated variants. Quantitative RT-PCR analysis revealed significant upregulation of recA, sulA, lexA, and rpoB in these strains, with full-length TtAgo eliciting the strongest induction, mirroring its pronounced filamentation phenotype (Fig. 3). This correlation extended to other pAgo orthologs (CbAgo and PfAgo), where the degree of gene upregulation scaled with filamentation severity (Fig. 1C, Supplementary Fig. S5), suggesting that pAgo-induced filamentation is tightly coupled to DNA damage and SOS pathway activation.

To assess impacts on cell division, we examined ftsZ expression, which encodes the master regulator of bacterial septation, forming the Z-ring scaffold essential for cytokinesis [32]. Overexpression of full-length TtAgo, TtAgoΔPIWI, and TtAgoΔMP significantly elevated ftsZ transcript levels (Supplementary Fig. S5C). Given that excessive ftsZ can form aberrant, spiral-like Z-ring macrostructures that impede normal constriction [33], these data suggest that filamentation results from synergistic effects of DNA damage and dysregulated division machinery.

EcSSB enhances TtAgo cleavage activity

In order to investigate the role of Ago’s nucleic acid processing activity in the formation of the filamentous phenotype, we investigated the context-dependency of TtAgo’s catalytic function. In vitro assays revealed that full-length TtAgo exhibited detectable DNA cleavage at 65°C, near its native T. thermophilus growth temperature, but lacked activity at 37°C (E. coli growth temperature) or 16°C (filamentation induction temperature in E. coli) (Fig. 4A, Supplementary Fig. S7A and B). Furthermore, we examined the guide DNA binding activity of TtAgo and three representative mutants to the guide DNA at 37°C. The TtAgoΔPIWI and TtAgoΔMP variants lacked DNA cleavage but retained nucleic acid binding activity, whereas the TtAgoΔLMP variant lost both (Fig. 4B). This further demonstrates that the filamentous phenotype is not dependent on the target DNA cleavage activity of Ago, but is highly correlated with its ability to capture guide DNA.

Previous studies have reported that TtAgo co-purifies with DNA-processing proteins, including single-stranded DNA-binding proteins (SSBs), AddA, AddB, Rad52, ArgR, and the histone-like protein HU, indicating potential modulation by replication and repair machinery [7]. To identify TtAgo-interacting proteins in E. coli, we performed Ni²⁺-affinity chromatography on strains harboring pET28a, pET28a-His-TtAgo, or pET28a-His-CbAgo, followed by mass spectrometry analysis of co-purified proteins (Supplementary Fig. S6A). This identified 630, 590, and 586 proteins from the control, CbAgo, and TtAgo-overexpressing strains, respectively, with 13 proteins uniquely enriched in both TtAgo and CbAgo groups (Fig. 4C, Supplementary Table S3, and Supplementary File 3). These included DNA replication and transcriptional regulators, such as SSB, the HTH-type transcriptional regulator YfhH, and a tRNA-specific adenosine deaminase, supporting TtAgo’s role in nucleic acid processing. Unexpectedly, metabolic proteins, including 2-amino-3-ketobutyrate CoA ligase (Kbl), nitrogen regulatory protein P-II (GlnB), iron export ABC transporter FetA, and aspartate-ammonia ligase (AsnA), were also enriched. The functional significance of these associations remains to be determined; however, they point to a possible link between TtAgo-mediated nucleic acid processing and cellular metabolic states. This represents a new frontier for future research.

Given the high abundance of E. coli SSB (EcSSB) in both of the TtAgo and CbAgo co-immunoprecipitates, we tested its impact on TtAgo’s cleavage activity. Neither full-length TtAgo nor TtAgoΔMP (0.4–1.6 μM) exhibited detectable cleavage at 37°C after 6 h. However, addition of EcSSB enabled cleavage by full-length TtAgo (1 μM) in a dose-dependent manner (Fig. 4D, Supplementary Fig. S7C). Time-course assays confirmed that cleavage activity increased progressively with EcSSB (1.4 μM) at 37°C (Fig. 4E), likely by stabilizing single-stranded DNA substrates or facilitating their loading into TtAgo’s guide channel. These results establish EcSSB as a critical cofactor that activates TtAgo’s cleavage function under mesophilic conditions, enhancing its utility in non-native systems like E. coli and potential synthetic biology applications. This interplay between TtAgo and replication-associated proteins suggests a broader regulatory network modulating filamentation, which we explore further in the context of endogenous T. thermophilus interactions.

TtSSB recruits TtAgo to replication sites and enhances its nucleic acid processing activity

To investigate TtAgo’s interactions within its native cellular network, we performed immunoprecipitation-coupled tandem mass spectrometry (IP-MS/MS) on T. thermophilus HB27 compared with the ago deletion strain (HB27Δago) as a negative control (Supplementary Fig. S6B). Gene Ontology (GO) and KEGG enrichment analyses revealed significant overrepresentation of TtAgo-interacting proteins in pathways related to DNA replication, repair, and structural modulation (Fig. 5A, Supplementary Fig. S6C). Notably, TtAgo exhibited a robust interaction with the endogenous T. thermophilus SSB (TtSSB), as confirmed by proteomic analysis (Fig. 5B, Supplementary Table S4). To validate this interaction, we conducted pull-down assays in E. coli expressing Flag-tagged TtAgo and GST-tagged TtSSB. These assays confirmed a robust association between the two proteins, which was maintained after DNase I treatment of the cell lysate (Fig. 5C). Quantification revealed a marked reduction (a 2.1-fold decrease) in the nucleic acid content of the purified TtSSB complex after DNase I treatment. Nevertheless, a residual amount of nucleic acids was still present in the system. AlphaFold predictions further showed that nucleic acid inclusion elevated the complex’s interface confidence score from 0.29 to 0.57 (Supplementary Fig. S8B). Collectively, these results suggest that nucleic acids likely co-reside within the TtAgo-SSB complex, contributing to complex stabilization through ternary interactions. Moreover, based on the AlphaFold predictions, the IDR domain of TtSSB was important for the two protein interactions. To verify this, a TtSSB variant (TtSSBΔIDR, 1–224 aa) was co-expressed with TtAgo. As expected, no protein interaction was observed between TtSSBΔIDR and TtAgo (Fig. 5C). Notably, because the C-terminal IDR of TtSSB primarily mediates protein–protein interactions rather than DNA binding, TtSSBΔIDR retains ssDNA-binding activity (Supplementary Fig. S9). Therefore, the loss of its interaction with TtAgo is more likely attributable to disruption of a direct protein–protein interaction interface. Furthermore, co-expression of fluorescently tagged TtSSB and TtAgo in E. coli confirmed their in vivo co-localization (Supplementary Fig. S8A). Taken together, the series of in vivo and in vitro experiments deeply confirmed protein interaction between TtSSB and TtAgo.

Electrophoretic mobility shift assays (EMSA) further demonstrated that TtSSB forms a complex with TtAgo and nucleic acids (Fig. 5D, Supplementary Fig. S10A and B). Specifically, when using 39-nt or 59-nt ssDNA, distinct ternary complex bands (containing TtAgo, TtSSB, and DNA) were observed. However, with shorter 21-nt ssDNA, no distinct ternary complex band was detected due to TtSSB’s weak binding affinity for nucleic acids shorter than 30 nt [34]. Nevertheless, as the concentration of TtSSB increased, more nucleic acids were retained in the wells. In summary, these results demonstrate that TtSSB facilitates TtAgo’s binding to nucleic acids. Similarly, TtSSB also significantly enhances TtAgo’s nucleolytic activity in a dose-dependent manner, whereas TtSSBΔIDR lacks this promoting effect (Fig. 5E, Supplementary Fig. S10C). Additionally, to distinguish whether the TtSSB enhances the binding or cleavage activity of TtAgo to improve cleavage efficiency, we pre-incubated an excess amount of TtAgo with gDNA, followed by the addition of tDNA and TtSSB to initiate the cleavage reaction. The results show that when TtSSB was co-incubated with TtAgo and gDNA simultaneously, as the amount of TtSSB added increased, the tDNA cleavage activity of TtAgo also enhanced. But when TtAgo was pre-loaded with gDNA, and then TtSSB was added, as the concentration of TtSSB increases, the target DNA cleavage activity of TtAgo is actually inhibited (Supplementary Fig. S10D and E). This might be because TtSSB is bound to the tDNA, preventing TtAgo from cleaving it. This indicates that TtSSB plays a more significant role in facilitating the binding of TtAgo to gDNA, thereby enhancing the cleavage efficiency of TtAgo on tDNA.

Beyond TtSSB, IP-MS/MS identified additional TtAgo-interacting proteins, including replication fork-associated enzymes such as RecG (a helicase that remodels stalled forks), PriA (primosome assembly protein), RecJ (5′–3′ exonuclease for replication intermediate processing), Topoisomerase I (relieves superhelical stress), and DNA polymerase I (fills post-priming gaps) (Fig. 5B, Supplementary Table S4). These interactions support a model wherein TtAgo is recruited to sites of DNA replication stress in T. thermophilus, potentially utilizing nascent or damaged DNA as guides for its activity, consistent with the previously proposed replication-fork model for TtAgo and its catalytically inactive variant (TtAgoDM) [7]. This recruitment likely amplifies TtAgo’s role in modulating replication and repair processes, contributing to the transient filamentation observed during exponential growth and suggesting a mechanism by which TtSSB and other replication factors mitigate TtAgo-induced DNA damage in its native host.

DNA repair pathways activation downregulates ago-induced filamentation in T. thermophilus

The transient filamentation observed in T. thermophilus upon TtAgo overexpression, which peaks at mid-log phase and resolves by late-log phase (Fig. 1A and B), suggests activation of endogenous DNA repair mechanisms to neutralize TtAgo-induced chromosomal damage. Previous studies suggest that pAgos recognize and cleave non-canonical DNA structures, including broken replication forks, 5′-flaps, Holliday junctions, and R-loops, thereby facilitating processing by cellular nucleases and repair factors [17]. Consistent with this, our proteomic analysis in T. thermophilus identified TtAgo associations with homologous recombination proteins, including Rad52 and AddB (a RecBC-like complex component) (Supplementary Table S4), supporting a role for TtAgo in activating DNA repair pathways to resolve replication stress.

To test whether Rad52 mediates TtAgo-induced filamentation, we co-expressed TtAgo and TtRad52 in E. coli BL21 (DE3). Compared to cells expressing TtAgo alone, the co-expression strain exhibited a ∼2-fold reduction in mean cell length, restoring near-normal morphology (Fig. 6A and B). This suggests that TtRad52 facilitates repair of TtAgo-induced DNA damage, thereby restoring normal cell division. Subcellular localization studies further clarified this interaction: TtAgo alone formed distinct intracellular foci, frequently located at daughter nucleoid poles (Fig. 2B and Supplementary Fig. S8A), whereas co-expression with TtRad52 resulted in a diffuse TtAgo distribution throughout the nucleoid without aggregation (Fig. 6C). Since TtSSB also formed punctate foci when co-expressed with TtAgo and it recruits TtAgo to the replication sites (Supplementary Fig. S8A), we hypothesize that TtRad52 may disrupt TtSSB-mediated recruitment of TtAgo to replication termini, reducing guide acquisition and subsequent DNA damage. The absence of TtAgo-TtRad52 co-localization during late-log phase imaging (Fig. 6C), despite their co-immunoprecipitation, likely reflects dynamic interaction changes across growth phases. These findings indicate that homologous recombination, mediated by TtRad52, neutralizes TtAgo-induced filamentation by repairing DNA damage and restoring cell cycle progression, providing a mechanistic link between TtAgo activity, replication stress, and endogenous repair pathways in T. thermophilus.

Discussion

The multifaceted roles of pAgo in bacterial physiology extend beyond nucleic acid interference and defense against mobile genetic elements. Here, we show that TtAgo overexpression induces transient filamentation during exponential growth, a phenotype associated with replication checkpoint activation, SOS response, and interactions with SSB and repair factors. By characterizing domain-specific mechanisms, SSB-mediated enhancement of TtAgo activity, and proteomic associations with replication and metabolic proteins, we demonstrate that dysregulated TtAgo activity impacts genome maintenance and stress adaptation. Collectively, our work identifies the SSB-TtAgo partnership as a molecular module that requires precise regulation: its uncontrolled activity triggers a cell cycle checkpoint, suggesting that SSB-mediated enhancement of TtAgo could serve as a regulatory target in bacterial cell cycle dynamics. This mechanism provides new insights into pAgo evolution and a programmable tool for synthetic biology.

Central to our results is the observation that TtAgo overexpression triggers reversible filamentation, peaking at mid-log phase and resolving by late-log phase, concomitant with SOS gene upregulation (e.g. recA, sulA, lexA, ftsZ). This phenotype reflects a replication stress-induced checkpoint, where TtAgo disrupts septum formation, leading to elongated cells with multiple nucleoids or continuous staining. Full-length TtAgo decouples DNA partitioning from septation, while PIWI/MID-truncated variants (TtAgoΔPIWI/TtAgoΔMP) impair segregation via aberrant binding at nucleoid junctions, independent of cleavage activity. The transient nature of this response highlights an adaptive mechanism, allowing cells to delay division until damage is resolved, thereby enhancing survival under replication stress.

This checkpoint role aligns with emerging evidence that pAgos participate in DNA replication and repair. Notably, Jolly et al. demonstrated that TtAgo acquires guides from the replication terminus, facilitating chromosome decatenation under gyrase inhibition and preventing filamentation in T. thermophilus [7]. Our findings complement this by showing filamentation in both native and heterologous systems upon TtAgo overexpression, suggesting that elevated TtAgo levels mimic replication delaying, inducing SOS-mediated arrest similar to gyrase dysfunction. Therefore, complementing the work of Jolly et al. [7], who observed filamentation upon TtAgo knockout under gyrase stress, our overexpression model reveals a dosage-dependent toxicity of TtAgo. Together, these findings from loss- and gain-of-function approaches demonstrate that both the absence and excess of TtAgo can disrupt cell division, highlighting the critical importance of its precise cellular abundance. Furthermore, our findings extend prior reports on mesophilic pAgos like CbAgo and KmAgo, which target replication termini and induce SOS without reported filamentation phenotypes [35, 36]. The resolution of filamentation via homologous recombination proteins (e.g. Rad52, AddB) in our study further bridges TtAgo to HR pathways, akin to NgAgo’s RecA-mediated editing [37].

A key innovation of our study lies in the functional and mechanistic dissection of the SSB-TtAgo interaction. Previous work established that pAgos, including TtAgo and SeAgo (Synechococcus elongatus Argonaute), associate with SSB and other DNA replication/repair proteins in proteomic surveys [7, 38]. Hunt et al. further demonstrated that thermostable SSBs substantially enhance the guide-directed DNA cleavage activity of TtAgo in vitro, even enabling processing of linear dsDNA substrates [39]. Moreover, SSB-mediated stimulation of Argonaute activity is not restricted to prokaryotic systems, as evidenced by its role in promoting dsDNA cleavage by eukaryotic CsAgo (Chaetomium sp. Argonaute) in vitro [40]. While these studies highlighted SSB as an auxiliary factor for pAgo activity primarily in biochemical contexts, the in vivo functional significance of this interaction remained unclear.

Our work extends these earlier findings by establishing that the SSB–TtAgo interaction is functionally consequential and physiologically relevant in vivo. Structural and pulldown assays confirm that TtAgo binds to the C-terminal tail of TtSSB, and their interaction may be stabilized by the very DNA substrates they coordinately bind. This model is supported by AlphaFold predictions, wherein the presence of nucleic acid in the complex correlates with higher interface confidence scores (Supplementary Fig. S8B). Our in vivo localization studies demonstrate that TtSSB recruits TtAgo to replication forks (Fig. 2B and Supplementary Fig. S8A), positioning SSB as a scaffold that directs TtAgo to ssDNA-rich sites for guide loading from nicks or breaks, followed by exonuclease processing (e.g. AddAB) [41]. Thus, our results establish that SSB, in addition to its known functions in stabilizing ssDNA and recruiting repair factors like RecG and PriA [42–45], serves as a critical recruitment cofactor for TtAgo localization and function in vivo. This SSB-dependent targeting not only facilitates robust TtAgo activity at mesophilic temperatures but also underlies a clear physiological outcome: TtAgo-induced SOS activation, filamentation, and transient cell cycle arrest.

The interaction between TtAgo and SSB represents a distinct mode of recruitment—one that extends beyond nucleic acid binding to include specific protein–protein scaffolding, a feature not previously emphasized for pAgos [46–48]. Building on the guide acquisition model proposed by Jolly et al. [7], we propose a refined model (Fig. 7A) in which SSB actively recruits TtAgo to replication forks (a–b), facilitating its binding to 5ʹ-phosphate nicks with cytidine-starred guides (c) and subsequent exonuclease trimming (d, e). This model underscores the pivotal role of SSB in directing TtAgo to replication-derived DNA breaks [49], thereby linking SSB-mediated recruitment to genome integrity pathways and cell cycle control.

Figure 7. Proposed Model of TtAgo-Induced Bacterial Filamentation. (A) Refined model of how TtAgo acquires guide DNA, based on the mechanism proposed by Jolly et al. [7]. (B) Working model illustrating TtAgo-induced filamentation. Cells expressing TtAgo at normal levels undergo normal division [1], whereas TtAgo overexpression leads to filamentous morphology accompanied by DNA damage. Homologous recombination–mediated repair of the damage enabled recovery of rod-shaped morphology [2].

The broader proteomic landscape of TtAgo interactors—encompassing transcriptional regulators, metabolic enzymes (e.g. MobA, TrpC), and membrane/cell wall factors (e.g. PSD, MurD)—implies multifunctionality beyond replication. This supports Jolly et al.’s [7] observation of TtAgo’s growth advantage under non-stress conditions, potentially through subtle contributions to metabolic homeostasis or stress adaptation. Unlike the lethal defense mechanisms orchestrated by short pAgos [50], our transient filamentation model (Fig. 7B) reveals a reversible, adaptive cell cycle delay that permits DNA repair. Integrating these findings, we hypothesize that native TtAgo may function as a dosage-sensitive modulator of cell cycle progression under genomic stress.

Our overexpression approach in heterologous systems represents a key methodological innovation, enabling the decoupling of TtAgo function from native regulatory networks and revealing its potent, dosage-dependent impact on the bacterial cell cycle. This strategy uniquely allowed us to dissect the SSB–TtAgo interaction as a central regulatory node controlling DNA replication fidelity or even SOS-mediated cell cycle arrest. Looking forward, this study establishes a robust framework for directly monitoring endogenous TtAgo dynamics and complex formation in T. thermophilus under replication stress. Furthermore, it opens exciting avenues for exploring TtAgo’s potential roles in cellular metabolism and for engineering synthetic SSB-Ago fusion systems to precisely manipulate DNA transactions in biotechnology applications.

Collectively, our work elevates TtAgo’s significance in bacterial physiology by positioning pAgos as versatile regulators that integrate genome stability with cell cycle checkpoints. The demonstration of SSB-enhanced Argonaute activity provides a novel, programmable tool for targeted DNA manipulation and replication control, significantly extending the functional scope and utility of mesophilic pAgos in synthetic biology and genome engineering [8, 35].
