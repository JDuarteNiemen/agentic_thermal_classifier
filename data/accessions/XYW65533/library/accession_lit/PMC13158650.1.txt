ABSTRACT

Acute pancreatitis (AP) is a common clinical inflammatory condition whose severity is closely associated with both local pancreatic injury and systemic complications. Research indicates that gut microbiota dysbiosis and subsequent bacterial translocation can exacerbate disease progression via the gut–pancreas axis, representing a critical factor influencing patient outcomes. Although antibiotics remain a primary approach for infection control, their extensive use has led to increasingly prominent issues of bacterial resistance, and their broad‐spectrum nature may further disrupt microbial homeostasis. Within this context, phage therapy, by virtue of its high specificity for targeted bacterial elimination, offers a novel approach for precisely modulating the gut microbiota, demonstrating unique therapeutic potential. This review explores the emerging role of phage therapy in regulating the pancreas‐gut axis, focusing on mechanisms such as precise pathogen eradication, immunomodulatory effects, and restoration of microbial equilibrium, thus effectively inhibiting the progression of pancreatitis. Finally, we discuss the challenges and future prospects of translating this promising strategy into clinical practice for AP.

1 Introduction

Acute pancreatitis (AP) is a common inflammatory condition characterized by a complex pathogenesis involving the aberrant activation of pancreatic enzymes, local inflammation, and potentially systemic inflammatory response syndrome [1]. In conventional therapeutic strategies, antibiotics are extensively employed to manage infectious complications; however, their broad‐spectrum activity frequently leads to intestinal microbiota dysbiosis and promotes the proliferation of drug‐resistant bacteria [2, 3]. This dysbiosis is recognized for exacerbating the severity of AP in patients [3]. Recent research has increasingly elucidated the pivotal role of the “gut–pancreas axis” in the progression of AP. Dysbiosis of the gut microbiota can trigger bacterial translocation, which in turn activates immune‐inflammatory responses, significantly aggravating the severity of AP [4]. Within this context, phage therapy has regained prominence as a precise antibacterial approach. Distinct from traditional antibiotics, phages possess the ability to specifically target and eliminate pathogenic bacteria, while exerting minimal impact on beneficial commensal microbiota [5, 6]. This specific characteristic endows phage therapy with the unique potential not only to directly eradicate pathogens but also to modulate the gut–pancreas axis through precise manipulation of the gut microbiota, thereby potentially improving AP outcomes. This review aims to systematically elaborate on the potential mechanisms and therapeutic applications of phage therapy in the management of AP.

2 Gut Dysbiosis and AP

2.1 Gut Dysbiosis

The intestinal microbiota plays an important role in human physiological functions through a variety of mechanisms, including metabolic regulation, immune system modulation, vitamin synthesis, promotion of digestion, and maintenance of intestinal structure. Normal intestinal microbiota is essential for maintaining the intestinal mucosal barrier function. The intestinal mucus barrier effectively protects the body from infection by pathogenic bacteria. Once the dynamic balance between the gut microbiota and the host immune system and gut barrier function is disrupted, it leads to dysbiosis of the intestinal microbiota [7, 8] Numerous studies have shown that dysbiosis has been shown to be closely related to the pathogenesis of a variety of gastrointestinal diseases (e.g., Crohn's disease, ulcerative colitis) [9], and may also predispose to metabolic disorders such as diabetes mellitus, obesity, metabolic syndrome, and other metabolic disorders [10, 11], and has been implicated in the development of pancreatic diseases (including pancreatic cancer) [12].

2.2 The Gut–Pancreas Axis and Microbial Translocation

2.2.1 The Concept of the Gut–Pancreas Axis

Traditionally, the pancreas has been considered a purely digestive and exocrine organ. However, growing research in recent years has revealed a close bidirectional communication between the pancreas and the intestine, known as the pancreas–gut axis [13]. The pancreas not only delivers digestive juices but also actively regulates intestinal homeostasis, while the gut microbiota and its integrity profoundly influence pancreatic health.

2.2.2 The Pancreas Secretes Antibacterial Mediators

The pancreas not only delivers digestive juices to the intestine but also actively secretes a series of defensive mediators, playing a critical regulatory role in maintaining gut microbial homeostasis and barrier integrity. These mediators primarily include: Reg3 proteins, which exert direct bactericidal effects by perforating bacterial membranes [14]; sPLA2, which targets bacterial membrane phospholipids [15]; pancreatic lipase, which indirectly promotes the growth of probiotics such as Akkermansia [16]; trypsin, which has a dual role in activating antimicrobial peptides and disrupting the barrier [17, 18]; and the glycoprotein GP2, which specifically prevents pathogenic Escherichia coli from adhering to the epithelium [13, 19].

2.2.3 Translocation of Gut Microbiota to the Pancreas: Pathways and Evidence

The intestinal state is a critical determinant of pancreatic health. Intestinal dysbiosis and impaired barrier function are pivotal events that can lead to bacterial translocation to the pancreas, activating potent immune‐inflammatory responses and thereby exacerbating pancreatic injury [20, 21, 22]. This dysregulation constitutes a core pathophysiological mechanism in AP. Pathobionts invading the epithelium can translocate to underlying layers and be disseminated to other organs via the lymphatic system, the portal vein, or directly through the pancreatic duct en route to the pancreas [21, 22, 23]. This translocation is not merely a passive leakage but an active process facilitated by barrier disruption. Supporting this, analysis of AP patients' feces reveals an increase in E. coli and Enterococcus, with functional analyses indicating an enhanced ability for bacterial invasion into epithelial cells [24]. Experimental models robustly confirm this causal link. For instance, studies using an E. coli MG1655 mono‐colonized acute necrotizing pancreatitis (ANP) rat model demonstrated significantly more severe pancreatic injury compared to controls, accompanied by upregulation of the TLR‐4‐MAPK pathway and activation of endoplasmic reticulum stress in intestinal epithelial cells [25]. Crucially, in situ hybridization confirmed the presence of E. coli specifically in the pancreata of mice with severe AP, while germ‐free and pseudo‐GF mice exhibited markedly reduced bacterial presence in the pancreas and less severe AP [20], directly linking gut‐derived bacteria to pancreatic injury severity.

2.2.4 Facilitating Mechanisms and the Vicious Cycle of Bacterial Translocation

Ahuja et al. revealed the central role of pancreatic alveolar cells in the maintenance of intestinal homeostasis through the secretion of antimicrobial peptides [26]. These antimicrobial peptides regulate intestinal microbiota composition upon entry into the intestine, and when pancreatic alveolar cells are deficient in the Ca2+ channel Orai1, this leads to a decrease in antimicrobial peptide secretion, which promotes intestinal microbial proliferation and dysbiosis, whereas inhibition of bacterial proliferation or exogenous supplementation with cathelicidin‐associated antimicrobial peptides is effective in reversing the bacterial imbalance and improving survival rates [26]. Microcirculatory damage leads to increased survival [26]. Microcirculatory injury leads to ischemia of the intestinal mucosa, which disrupts the protective function of the barrier and contributes to intestinal microbiota translocation [27]. In a meta‐analysis of 18 studies, Wu et al. found intestinal barrier dysfunction in 60% of AP patients [28]. In an experimental rat model of AP, it was learned that bacterial translocation was mainly from the small intestine rather than the colon [29] and that it could be translocated to the pancreas via the upper gastrointestinal tract [30]. In other related experiments, it was testified that necrotizing pancreatitis can induce small intestinal bacterial overgrowth and promote bacterial translocation [31].

2.3 Evidence for Specific Pathogenic Bacteria in AP

The gut microbiome has a significant impact on the severity of AP [24]. Gram‐negative bacteria ( E. coli, Enterococcus, Acinetobacter baumannii , and Klebsiella pneumoniae ) were predominantly detected in the bile of patients with AP [32]. Candida, Enterococcus spp., and coagulase‐negative Staphylococcus spp. were commonly detected in samples of isolated pancreatic necrosis [33]. It is important to note that these may be the same bacteria that survived antibiotic treatment, rather than the true primary causative agent. Still further exploration is needed [34]. In a prospective clinical study, patients with AP were found to be more prone to dysbiosis (increased enterococci and decreased bifidobacteria) than healthy volunteers [35] (Figure 1). A recent study by Liu et al. provides more insights into altered gut microbiota in patients with AP. The study found that the gut microbiota of AP patients was significantly reduced in terms of species and functional diversity compared to healthy individuals. Specifically, the relative abundance of E. coli , Enterococcus, Parabacteroides, Clostridium, and Veillonella was significantly increased in AP patients [36]. In a meta‐analysis, bacterial infections increased mortality in patients with AP, with mortality rates in patients with infectious necrosis and organ failure being nearly twice as high as those with sterile necrosis and organ failure [37].

This activation triggers signaling pathways such as NF‐κB, leading to the massive release of pro‐inflammatory cytokines (e.g., TNF‐α, IL‐6, IL‐1β), which amplifies systemic inflammation and ultimately exacerbates pancreatic injury and AP severity, forming a vicious pathological cycle.

3 Mechanisms of Phage Therapy

3.1 Specific Recognition and Elimination of Bacteria by Phages

Phage is classified as a virus and shares all of the characteristics of viruses: it cannot replicate itself outside of the host organism, its own genome is relatively small, it will extensively use the host cell's relevant mechanisms during the replication process, and it is highly specific to the host cell. Phages are classified into two types based on their life cycle: lytic phage, which enter the lysis cycle immediately upon infection, replicate rapidly, and kill the host; and lysogenic phage, in which phage DNA is integrated into the host chromosome, causing the bacterium to be in the state of lysogeny and allowing it to enter the lysis cycle only when induced to do so [5, 38]. Phage therapy is mainly with lysogenic phages, which are most commonly double‐stranded DNA plus‐tailed viruses, where the DNA is encapsulated in a capsid that is also connected to the tail [5]. The host specificity of phage is mainly determined by the tail filament protein. Tail filament proteins identify and bind to receptors on the bacterial surface via specific structural domains (e.g., D10 and D11 in T4 phage) [38], which include lipopolysaccharide, pore proteins, transmembrane proteins, phospholambanic acid, and even organelles [39]. This interaction is the first step in phage adsorption onto the host bacterial surface. After binding to the receptor, the tail filament protein penetrates the bacterial cell wall or cell membrane and injects the phage genome into the host cytoplasm, so commencing the infection process. The phage begins a lysis cycle, which finally lyses the host cell and releases newly generated phage particles [39, 40, 41, 42]. Because of their very specific and exact infection process, phages are uniquely advantageous in phage treatment for the targeted death of target bacterial pathogens while causing no harm to other beneficial microbes [6, 43, 44].

3.2 Immunomodulatory Mechanisms: Synergistic Interactions With the Host Immune System

3.2.1 Penetration of Biological Barriers

As a key mediator in the synergistic evolutionary process between prokaryotes and eukaryotic hosts, phages play a key role in the maintenance of microbial community homeostasis through the dynamic equilibrium regulatory mechanism by interacting with the triple realm constituted by the bacterial community and the host immune system [45]. However, the mechanism of direct interaction between phages and human immune responses remains largely unknown. Much of the current knowledge of the phage‐immune system stems from phage therapy studies: Although phages in the circulatory system are mainly cleared by phagocytes, they can penetrate the gut by transcytosis, endocytosis, or passive diffusion from damaged state epithelia [46, 47, 48]. It has been claimed that the coat protein gp24 of E. coli T4 phage contains a Lys‐Gly‐Asp motif that binds to the β3‐integrin receptor [49]. Partial phage uptake is associated with the involvement of chondroitin sulfate proteoglycans [50]. A cell type‐dependent endocytosis pathway has also been reported for the filamentous E. coli phage M13 [51]. It is noteworthy that phages may enter the host circulatory system indirectly through a “Trojan horse” strategy (i.e., internalization by the host cell after infection with a bacterium) after penetrating the barrier [52]. However, the mechanism of action of most phages with cell‐associated receptors remains unclear. Phages are able to escape degradation by intracellular lysosomal compartments to induce a cellular immune response [53, 54], and have been reported to enter the body by bypassing damaged epithelial cells or leaky blood vessels as a key initial step in initiating antimicrobial immunity [55, 56]. Notably, phages may possess symbiotic qualities within the host. Phage concentrations have been found to be higher in the intestinal mucus layer and other mucosal barriers, including the gingiva [57]. Furthermore, phage coat proteins can directly bind to mucin glycoproteins, indicating a potential symbiotic role in the host's mucosal environment [57]. The discovery that the distribution of phages near the intestinal epithelium could be part of a host defensive strategy to prevent bacteria from crossing the mucosal barrier opens up new paths for future research. However, phages may contribute to the integrity of the intestinal barrier by modifying the composition of the intestinal microbiota, which may result in an intestinal barrier breach [58]. Overall, these observations suggest that phages can exhibit the same symbiotic and pathogenic behavior as bacteria. Indeed, in sponges, phage‐dependent Ankyrin protein expression in bacteria can promote host‐bacteria coexistence by inhibiting inflammatory mechanisms in eukaryotic cells, including phagocytosis by macrophages and secretion of inflammatory cytokines [59].

3.2.2 Activation of Innate Immunity

Phage nucleic acids can trigger innate immunity through a variety of PRRs, and synergistic interaction with mammalian immune cells is critical for the clearance of pathogenic bacteria [60]. TLR9 recognizes unmethylated CpG motifs in phage DNA and induces the expression of proinflammatory cytokines such as IFN‐γ through MyD88 [61, 62, 63, 64]. In intestinal studies, TLR9 mediates the perception of phage DNA by dendritic cells to drive CD4+ T cells, thereby inducing a cellular immune response and cytokine release of IFN‐β [61]. According to the literature, among the Toll‐like receptor family, TLR3 has a unique signaling mechanism, which, as the only member of the signaling pathway mediated solely by the toll/interleukin‐1 receptor structural domain, significantly activates the efficient expression of antiviral cytokines [63]. A filamentous bacteriophage infecting Pseudomonas aeruginosa transcribes RNA within Dendritic Cells, which activates the TLR3‐TRIF signaling axis, resulting in the production of interferon‐beta [65].

3.2.3 Activation of Humoral Immunity

The interaction of phage with the eukaryotic immune system triggers a specific humoral immune response and stimulates the body to produce antiphage antibodies with neutralizing activity [66, 67, 68]. The spleen can act as a core immune organ [69]. Phagocytosis ingests and processes phage antigens and activates B/T cells via the MHC‐I/II pathway, thereby producing antibodies [70]. Its antibody type is dominated by IgM, accompanied by the synergistic involvement of IgG and IgA to generate phage‐specific humoral responses [71, 72].

3.2.4 Direct and Indirect Pathways of Immune Activation

Upon breaching the barrier, phages trigger the immune response in two ways: the indirect pathway, which modifies the bacterial host to indirectly alter immune cell function (e.g., by decreasing toxin release or reducing pathogenic bacterial colonization). For instance, phage‐mediated clearance of Enterococcus faecalis decreases its cytolysin production, which prevents commensal E. coli from causing damage [73]; in direct pathway: phages directly interact with immune cells (like B cells and T cells), activate the TLR signaling pathway (like TLR9), and cause the release of cytokines like IL‐12 and interferon, which set off both innate and adaptive immune responses [74, 75]. Recent studies have shown that phage therapy is effective in P. aeruginosa –infected wild‐type mice but not in neutrophil‐deficient models; in vitro experiments have confirmed that phage co‐culture with neutrophils significantly enhances the bactericidal effect [76]. This phenomenon may be related to the conditioning effect of phage opsonization: When phage attach to the bacterial surface, they can promote the recognition and clearance of pathogenic bacteria by phagocytes (e.g., neutrophils, macrophages) [47, 77].

3.2.5 Conclusion and Future Perspectives

The interaction mechanisms between bacteriophages and the host immune system have increasingly become a research hotspot. Bacteriophages can penetrate biological barriers through multiple pathways, activate the innate immune system via pattern recognition receptors, and cleverly regulate humoral immune responses. Elucidating these immune recognition mechanisms provides key directions for optimizing bacteriophage therapy. However, it must be acknowledged that there are still many unresolved mysteries regarding the behavior of phages within cells, such as the specific molecular mechanisms by which they evade lysosomal degradation, and the dynamic changes in immune regulation across spatiotemporal dimensions [53, 54] (Figure 2). Future research should focus on a careful balancing of risks and benefits: While vigilant about potential risks such as virulence gene transfer that may arise from phage intervention, it is also essential to fully explore their therapeutic potential. Integrating basic research with clinical practice is an inevitable trend, and clinical models such as inflammatory bowel disease and gut microbiota dysbiosis hold promise as breakthroughs for the translational application of phage therapy [54, 74].

4 Potential Applications of Phage Therapy in Pancreatic Diseases

4.1 Theoretical Basis for Targeting Pathogens Associated With Pancreatic Diseases

There is a large body of research that strongly supports the existence of an important link between the pancreas and the gut [7, 9, 10]. In recent years, there has been increasing evidence to support that pancreatic exocrine function influences intestinal immunity, which further supports the pancreas‐gut axis [4]. As a result, it is known that the intestinal microbiota can influence the severity of different pancreatic disorders [37, 78], and several specific bacterial pathogens have been associated with the development of pancreatic disease [32, 33, 79]. In order to modulate the gut microbiota, antibiotics and fecal microbiota transplantation are considered nontargeted microbial therapies [80]. These bacteria have been targeted for phage therapy, in which phages can act exactly on a specific bacterial host, displaying a high level of specificity. This property gives phages a distinct advantage when dealing with infections caused by drug‐resistant bacteria such as methicillin‐resistant Staphylococcus aureus , vancomycin‐resistant enterococci, and carbapenem‐resistant Enterobacteriaceae, as they can effectively remove drug‐resistant pathogens while minimizing disruption of the beneficial microbiota [81].

Currently, large‐scale randomized controlled trials directly investigating phage therapy for AP are lacking. However, the successful treatment of abdominal infections caused by common AP‐associated secondary multidrug‐resistant bacteria, such as A. baumannii and K. pneumoniae [82], provides strong theoretical validation and promising prospects for this innovative therapeutic approach. Schooley et al. reported the case of a 68‐year‐old patient with diabetes mellitus complicated by necrotizing pancreatitis, whose condition was further exacerbated by a pancreatic pseudocyst infected with multidrug‐resistant (MDR) A. baumannii . After failing months of various antibiotic regimens, including polymyxin and tigecycline, the patient deteriorated into a critical state involving coma and multiple organ failure. In this context, researchers rapidly screened environmental phages to develop a personalized cocktail therapy comprising nine lytic phages. Notably, within 48 h of initiating intravenous phage therapy, the patient's condition was controlled, leading to emergence from coma and restoration of communication ability [82]. Beyond this direct pancreatic infection case, phage therapy studies targeting other abdominal infections, such as complicated urinary tract infections, provide indirect support [89, 90]. These infections similarly involve gut microbiota translocation or are caused by gram‐negative bacteria frequently associated with AP, like K. pneumoniae and E. coli [89, 90]. The successful management of these intra‐abdominal infections demonstrates the capability of phages to specifically eliminate target pathogens within the complex abdominal environment. This holds significant implications for preventing secondary bacterial translocation and infection in AP patients. The underlying mechanism may involve reducing the load of potential pathogens within the gut, thereby mitigating their risk of migration to the pancreas via the gut–pancreas axis.

5 Phage Therapy for Pancreatic Diseases: Preparation and Delivery Strategies

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) has become a major challenge in modern medicine, leading to increased morbidity and mortality in diseases such as pancreatitis. The speed at which bacteria evolve and acquire resistance mechanisms has far outpaced the development of new antibiotics [91]. In this context, the development of preparation and delivery technologies for phage therapy, which serves as an alternative capable of precisely targeting drug‐resistant pathogens associated with pancreatitis, is particularly critical.

5.1 Construction of Phage Libraries Targeting Pancreatic Pathogens

To effectively intervene against common drug‐resistant pathogens in pancreatic diseases (e.g., E. coli, Enterococcus, K. pneumoniae, A. baumannii ) [32, 33], the construction of large‐scale, well‐characterized phage libraries is fundamental for supporting personalized therapy [92]. This resource library can serve as a source of therapeutic phages, provide starting materials for constructing genetically engineered phages, or offer a knowledge framework for fully synthetic phages [5]. Its construction strategy should focus on the screening and enrichment from clinical or environmental samples rich in the target pathogens (e.g., wastewater from medical institutions) [93], rather than general environmental sampling. In recent years, gene‐editing technologies based on the CRISPR‐Cas system have provided new avenues for phage therapy [81, 94]. This technology enables precise editing of phages to enhance their targeting of pathogens associated with pancreatitis or to specifically eliminate their drug‐resistant genes (e.g., resistance plasmids), thereby reversing bacterial resistance and offering a novel strategy to overcome the challenge of multidrug resistance in pancreatic infections [95]. For instance, CRISPR‐Cas9 can be used to specifically eliminate resistance plasmids or “immunize” bacterial populations, thus limiting the spread of resistance genes [95]; whereas Cas13a can recognize and cleave RNA molecules (e.g., drug‐resistant gene mRNA) within target bacteria, disrupting their protein synthesis [92].

5.2 Engineering and Delivery Strategies for Enhanced Therapeutic Efficacy

Oral administration is the most direct route to modulate the gut–pancreas axis; however, phages face inactivation by gastric acid and intestinal proteases during their passage through the gastrointestinal tract [96]. Therefore, developing delivery systems that can protect phages and ensure their precise delivery to the intestinal site of action is crucial for treating pancreatic diseases. Liposomes are nanocarriers composed of one or more phospholipid bilayers surrounding an aqueous core. By virtue of their excellent safety profile, ability to encapsulate a wide variety of drugs, and capacity to protect the payload from degradation or clearance, they are the most extensively researched and mature drug delivery nanocarriers [97]. Studies have shown that cationic liposome‐encapsulated phages exhibit significantly reduced susceptibility to acidic degradation and can kill bacteria more effectively, providing a solution for the oral delivery of phages to target intestinal pathogens and modulate the microbiota‐pancreas axis [96, 98]. Alginate, a polyanionic biopolymer, has become an ideal carrier for oral phage delivery due to its pH‐dependent swelling properties (stable in the acidic gastric environment, solubilizing and releasing in the neutral intestinal environment), effectively protecting phages from gastric acid and enzymatic degradation [99, 100]. Poly (lactic‐co‐glycolic acid), an FDA‐approved biodegradable polymer, boasts excellent biocompatibility, a long history of clinical use, and tunable degradation characteristics. It can serve as a long‐acting sustained‐release delivery system, prolonging the duration of phage action within the intestine, potentially contributing to the maintenance of gut microecological balance in patients with pancreatic diseases [99]. Furthermore, various inorganic nanoparticles can achieve precise drug delivery through the functionalization of specific tissue‐targeting molecules. Their most important feature is the low likelihood of pathogens developing resistance to these materials [101, 102].

6 Challenges and Future Directions of Phage Therapy

Although phage therapy demonstrates significant potential in treating infections associated with AP, its transition from laboratory research to routine clinical practice faces considerable challenges. A primary obstacle is bacterial resistance, a core issue analogous to that encountered with antibiotics. During treatment, bacteria may evade phage recognition and adsorption by altering or losing surface receptors (e.g., capsules or lipopolysaccharides), thereby developing resistance [103, 104]. Furthermore, the presence of bacterial biofilms substantially increases treatment difficulty. The physical barrier and metabolically dormant state of bacteria within biofilms confer high tolerance to both phages and antibiotics [105]. The dual role of the host immune system also presents a challenge. On one hand, phages can synergize with the immune system to clear pathogens, such as by enhancing phagocytosis through opsonization; on the other hand, the host may develop neutralizing antibodies that accelerate phage clearance from the bloodstream, limiting their delivery to the site of infection and reducing therapeutic efficacy [106]. Substantial hurdles also exist in clinical translation and regulatory approval. The absence of internationally unified standards for phage screening, amplification, purification, and final product quality control introduces uncertainty in evaluating consistency and safety [107, 108]. Moreover, the level of clinical evidence remains insufficient. Most current evidence derives from case reports or small‐scale clinical studies, highlighting a critical need for large‐scale, randomized, double‐blind, placebo‐controlled Phase III trials to establish robust efficacy and safety data [82]. Designing such trials for AP is particularly challenging due to patient heterogeneity, complexity of infection sites, and the selection of appropriate clinical endpoints [108]. The breadth and matching efficiency of phage librariesare equally crucial. The diversity of pathogens in AP patients (e.g., E. coli , K. pneumoniae , A. baumannii ) necessitates rapid screening of highly matched and effective phage cocktails, yet standardized high‐throughput platforms are currently lacking [109]. Future progress depends on technology‐driven therapeutic innovation. Engineering phages using synthetic biology and gene editing technologies (e.g., CRISPR‐Cas systems) represents a promising direction. This approach could broaden host range, equip phages with biofilm‐degrading capabilities (e.g., by integrating lytic enzyme genes), or precisely disrupt bacterial resistance genes, thereby reversing antibiotic resistance [94, 95, 109]. Developing novel delivery systems targeting the gut–pancreas axis is essential. Strategies such as pH‐sensitive enteric coatings (e.g., alginate) or nanocarriers (e.g., liposomes, PLGA nanoparticles) can protect phages from gastric acid, enabling targeted release in the small intestine to precisely modulate gut microbiota and reduce bacterial translocation [96, 99]. Additionally, in‐depth investigation into the synergistic mechanisms between phages and antibiotics (e.g., carbapenems commonly used in AP treatment) and the development of combination regimens could significantly enhance efficacy and delay resistance emergence [110]. Well‐designed clinical trials focusing on patients with AP‐related drug‐resistant infections will be instrumental in accelerating regulatory approval [111].

7 Summary

AP, as a common digestive system disease with relatively high mortality, relies on antimicrobial agents for clinical management. However, long‐term use of broad‐spectrum antibiotics may lead to gut microbiota dysbiosis and the emergence of drug‐resistant bacteria, which can potentially exacerbate the disease. In recent years, growing evidence has confirmed the critical role of the gut–pancreas axis in the pathogenesis of AP, making precise modulation of the intestinal microecology a promising novel strategy for improving AP prognosis. Against this backdrop, phage therapy offers a fresh perspective for AP treatment by virtue of its capacity for highly specific targeting and eradication of pathogenic bacteria. This approach holds potential for intervening in AP through regulation of the gut–pancreas axis, with distinct advantages over conventional antibiotics. Compared to traditional antibiotics, bacteriophages can precisely lyse specific pathogenic bacteria (such as E. coli, K. pneumoniae, A. baumannii , and other pathogens), maximally protecting beneficial commensal microbiota and thereby better restoring intestinal microecological balance. Furthermore, the complex interactions between phages and the host immune system, along with their synergistic potential with antibiotics, broaden the therapeutic prospects of phage therapy in AP. A case report has successfully demonstrated the rescue of a patient with necrotizing pancreatitis infected by an extensively drug‐resistant A. baumannii using a phage cocktail, which preliminarily validates its clinical potential. However, the widespread application of phage therapy in AP still faces multiple challenges. These include bacterial resistance to phages, biofilm barriers, uncertainties in host immune responses, the absence of standardized phage banks and quality control systems for production, as well as a lack of high‐level clinical evidence. In summary, as a highly promising precision antimicrobial strategy, phage therapy offers a new direction for addressing the challenge of drug‐resistant bacterial infections in AP. With continued technological advances, rigorous clinical validation, and the establishment of comprehensive regulatory and production frameworks, it holds promise to become an important component of the integrated therapeutic arsenal for AP in the near future.
