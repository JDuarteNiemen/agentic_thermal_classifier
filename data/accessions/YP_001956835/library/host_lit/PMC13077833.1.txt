Background

The growing need for sustainable farming methods has led to increased efforts to understand plant-beneficial microbes that can serve as alternatives to chemical fertilizers and pesticides. Among these, bacteria from the Pseudomonas chlororaphis group have emerged as particularly promising candidates due to their metabolic versatility and ability to produce a diverse arsenal of bioactive compounds.

Results

In this study, we conducted an extensive comparative genomic analysis of two Pseudomonas chlororaphis strains, FS-2 (endophytic) and RP-4 (rhizoplane-associated), to explore their plant growth-promoting and biocidal capacities. Whole-genome analysis using antiSMASH and functional annotation tools identified several biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs) involved in the production of secondary metabolites, such as phenazines, pyoverdine, pyrrolnitrin, hydrogen cyanide, and the cyclic lipopeptide white line-inducing principle (WLIP). Some additional clusters with low similarity to known references, including arylpolyenes, butyrolactones, and resorcinol derivatives, were also observed within these genomes, indicating the ability to produce uncharacterized bioactive compounds. Genes for the biosynthesis of plant growth-promoting features including indole-3-acetic acid, siderophore, biofilm formation, solubilization of organic compounds, and stress adaption were also present in both genomes. Additionally, bacteriocin-like operons pyocins and colicins were also present in the genomes of FS-2 and RP-4. Average nucleotide identity analysis indicated a high level of conservation (99.58% ANI) between FS-2 and RP-4, however, differences in the genome and the presence of strain-specific biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs) indicate adaptive evolution.

Conclusions

The overall comparative genomic insights into P. chlororaphis strains FS-2 and RP-4 indicate their metabolic and plant-beneficial characteristics for rhizosphere competence and their use as biological control agents.

Introduction

Modern agriculture relies more on synthetic fertilizers, fungicides, and pesticides to maintain high crop yields. However, excessive use of these chemicals has led to soil damage, an imbalance in nutrients, less microbial diversity, and harmful residues that threaten the environment and human health. Such practices exacerbate greenhouse gas emissions, and adversely impact beneficial soil microbes leading to the gradual depletion of soil fertility [1]. Therefore, agroecosystems are increasingly transitioning towards sustainable and ecofriendly methodologies that strategically employ beneficial microbes to improve soil ecosystem functions and agricultural productivity.

As eco-friendly biological alternatives, plant growth-promoting bacteria (PGPB) have demonstrated significant potential to replace or complement conventional agrochemicals. Their role in restoring soil health is facilitated through enhanced nutrient cycling, organic matter decomposition, and stabilization of the rhizosphere microbiome. These bacteria promote plant growth directly by improving nutrient availability and stimulating root development, while also conferring abiotic stress tolerance via the production of phytohormones, siderophores, and enzymes [2]. Additionally, PGPB suppress phytopathogens through multiple mechanisms, including the synthesis of antimicrobial secondary metabolites, the induction of systemic resistance in the host plant, and competitive exclusion in the rhizosphere [3, 4].

The Pseudomonas chlororaphis group is widely recognized for its exceptional capacity to produce bioactive metabolites that confer plant-protective benefits. Subspecies such as P. chlororaphis subsp. aurantiaca and subsp. chlororaphis possess a diverse arsenal of antifungal compounds, including phenazines, pyrrolnitrin, and hydrogen cyanide, which contribute to plant growth promotion and the suppression of soil-borne diseases [5, 6]. Their substantial metabolic versatility has consequently spurred research into novel strain discovery and the genomic decoding of their potential for sustainable agriculture.

This study characterizes the genomic potential of two plant growth-promoting Pseudomonas chlororaphis strains, FS-2 (Opuntia humifusa endophyte), and RP-4 (rhizoplane isolate from para grass) through genome mining and comparative bioinformatics. Building on prior phenotypic studies [7, 8], we identified biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs), metabolic pathways, and plant growth-promoting genes to infer their functional mechanisms. A comparative analysis with the reference strain P. chlororaphis subsp. aurantiaca PB-St2 further revealed genomic diversity, virulence factors, and biotechnological applicability. Collectively, these findings position FS-2 and RP-4 as ecologically relevant microbial resources for advancing sustainable crop production and soil health.

Materials and methods

Bacterial strains

The study utilized two Pseudomonas strains, FS-2 and RP-4, previously isolated by our group from cactus and para grass, respectively. Both strains were characterized in our prior work for their plant growth-promoting (PGP) traits, biocontrol activities against phytopathogens, and identified via 16S rDNA sequencing [8]. The GenBank accession numbers of Pseudomonas strains are LN898137 (FS-2) and KT888010 (RP-4). The strains represent geographically and host-distinct isolates and were explored for genomic variation underlying niche adaptation.

In vitro antifungal assay

The antagonistic activities of Pseudomonas strains FS-2 and RP-4 against Botrytis cineria and Alternaria alternata (generously provided by DF Grupo, Pamplona, Spain) were assessed using a plate confrontation assay [9]. Briefly, a small fungal plug of 5 mm in diameter was seeded at the centre of the PDA (g/L: dextrose 20; potato starch 4; bacteriological agar 15, Condalab, Spain) plate. Subsequently, 5 µL of 24 h old FS-2 and RP-4 bacterial suspensions (10⁸ CFU/mL) were spot-inoculated approximately 2.5 cm from the plate centre. Control plates were treated with an equivalent volume of sterile distilled water instead of the bacterial suspension. Each experiment was performed in triplicate. All plates were incubated at 25 °C for 7 days and observed for a reduction in fungal colony growth. Antifungal activity was quantified by measuring the radial growth of the fungal colony and the distance from the center of the original plug to the colony margin was recorded for both treated and control plates [10]. Bacterial genomic DNA extraction and sequencing

Pseudomonas strains FS-2 and RP-4 were individually grown in 10 mL of King’s B broth and incubated for 24 h at 28 °C [11]. The cultures were centrifuged at 3400 × g for 10 min at 4 °C, and genomic DNA was extracted using the GeneJET Genomic DNA Purification Kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA). DNA quality and quantity were validated on a 1% agarose gel and spectrophotometrically using a Nanodrop spectrophotometer (Spectrophotometer 2000, Thermo Scientific). High-quality DNA (OD 260/280 = 1.8-2.0, > 20 mg) was used for bacterial genome sequencing. In total, 50 µg of DNA was sent on dry ice to MicrobesNG (Commercial Microbial Sequencing Facility, UK). Pseudomonas chlororaphis subsp. aurantiaca PB-St2 (Accession No. EU761590, from our lab collection) was used as the reference strain.

Genome assembly and annotation

Whole-genome sequencing of Pseudomonas chlororaphis strain FS-2 was performed using the Illumina HiSeq 2000® platform (Illumina Inc.) by MicrobesNG (UK), following the manufacturer’s standard protocols for bacterial whole-genome sequencing. Paired-end reads were generated and delivered in FASTQ format. Raw Illumina reads were subjected to quality control prior to assembly. Adapter sequences and low-quality bases were removed, and reads containing ambiguous nucleotides (N), low average quality scores, or insufficient read length after trimming were discarded. Quality assessment and filtering were performed using the standardized MicrobesNG bioinformatics pipeline and further verified on the Majorbio Cloud Platform (Shanghai Majorbio Bio-Pharm Technology Co., Ltd.). High-quality filtered reads were assembled de novo into contigs using the SPAdes genome assembler (version 3.13.0), which is optimized for Illumina short-read data [12]. The resulting draft genome assembly was deposited in GenBank under accession number JBGONG000000000 and represents the only FS-2 genome dataset used in the present study. To assess genome synteny and structural conservation, contigs of strain FS-2 were ordered and aligned against the reference genome of Pseudomonas chlororaphis strain PB-St2 using Mauve alignment software [13]. This step was performed exclusively for comparative genomic visualization and did not involve reference-based genome assembly. For comparative purposes, the complete genome sequence of Pseudomonas chlororaphis strain RP-4 (GenBank accession number CP053057) was included. This genome was generated independently using the PacBio Sequel long-read sequencing platform and assembled using the Hierarchical Genome Assembly Process (HGAP v4). No PacBio sequencing data were used for the FS-2 genome assembly described in this study. Genomic completeness was assessed using CheckM (v1.2.4), and the potential for bacterial pathogenicity was predicted using the PathogenFinder 1.1 web server [14, 15].

Phylogenomics of Pseudomonas strains RP-4 and FS-2

Genome integrity was initially evaluated using the ContEST16S online server (https://www.ezbiocloud.net/tools/contest16s) to assess potential contamination based on 16S rRNA gene copy analysis [16]. Genome completeness and contamination were further estimated using CheckM v1.2.4, implemented via the KBase platform (https://www.kbase.us/). Only genomes meeting stringent quality criteria (≥ 98% completeness and ≤ 1% contamination) were retained for downstream analyses. High-quality genomes of Pseudomonas chlororaphis strains were retrieved from the PATRIC Bacterial Bioinformatics Resource Center (https://patricbrc.org/) and filtered according to the above quality thresholds. All genomes used in this study had a minimum sequencing coverage of ≥ 90×. The complete list of genomes included in the analyses is provided in Table 1. Gene prediction and functional annotation were performed using the NCBI Prokaryotic Genome Annotation Pipeline (PGAP) and Prokka v2.14.6 [17] to ensure consistency and annotation completeness. Whole-genome-based taxonomic analysis was conducted using the Genome BLAST Distance Phylogeny (GBDP) approach implemented in the Type Strain Genome Server (TYGS; https://tygs.dsmz.de). TYGS calculates intergenomic distances, infers phylogenetic relationships, estimates genomic G + C content, and determines species and subspecies boundaries based on digital DNA-DNA hybridization (dDDH) values using validated clustering algorithms [18, 19].

Comparative genomics of Pseudomonas strains FS-2 and RP-4

EDGAR version 3.0 (https://edgar3.computational.bio.uni-giessen.danalysesn/edgar_login.cgi?cookie_test) was used for comparative genomic analysis [20]. This approach calculates nucleotide identity through pairwise sequence alignment, resulting in an overall genome similarity average that is unaffected by the sequence length. The generated average nucleotide identity (ANI) matrix was used to create a heatmap for further interpretation. The OrthoVenn3 web server (http://orthovenn3.bioinfotoolkits.net) was used to perform comparative ortholog clustering and generate Venn diagrams to visualize shared and unique coding sequences (CDSs) between the Pseudomonas strains. The analysis was conducted using default parameters with an E-value cutoff of 1 × 10⁻⁵, an inflation value of 1.5 for Markov clustering, and a minimum sequence identity threshold of 30% to define orthologous groups. This approach revealed conserved and strain-specific CDS profiles, highlighting core genomic elements and potential accessory genomic regions contributing to phenotypic divergence. For protein similarity comparisons and orthologous cluster analysis generation, default parameters were applied with an e-value cutoff of 1e-5 and an inflation value of 1.5. Functional annotation was conducted using the Clusters of Orthologous Groups (COG) and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) databases with the EDGAR software. In addition, Gene Ontology (GO) annotation was performed to classify predicted proteins into biological process, molecular function, and cellular component categories. BAGEL4 (http://bagel4.molgenrug.nl) was used to identify bacteriocins in the two Pseudomonas genomes [21].

A comprehensive analysis of the biosynthetic gene clusters present in the genomes of the two strains was performed using antiSMASH bacterial version 8.0 https://antismash.secondarymetabolites.org using strict detection rules [22] and comparatively analyzed using PRediction Informatics for Secondary Metabolomes (PRISM3) [23]. Virulence-associated genes were identified using DIAMOND v2.0.15 against the Virulence Factor Database (VFDB, 2023 release). The search was performed with the following parameters: e-value cutoff ≤ 1 × 10⁻⁵, query coverage ≥ 80%, and percent identity ≥ 80%. Identified hits were subsequently assigned to functional categories based on the VFDB classification scheme [24]. Carbohydrate-active enzyme (CAZy) families were assigned with dbCAN2 databased with stringent e-value cutoff of < 1 × 10− 10 and a domain coverage threshold of > 0.30. The “best-hit” algorithm was used to resolve subfamily overlaps, ensuring each gene is represented by its most statistically significant functional assignment [25].

Results and discussion

Pseudomonas strains showed strong antifungal potential

Both Pseudomonas strains FS-2 and RP-4 showed considerable inhibition of Botrytis cinerea and Alternaria alternata in plate confrontation assays. Antifungal inhibition was highest for FS-2 against B. cinerea (19%), followed by its effect on (A) alternata (12%), whereas, the strain RP-4 demonstrated moderate inhibition against (B) cinerea (17%) and A. alternata (9.65%) (Fig. 1).

Phylogenomic analysis

Genome-based taxonomic analysis using the Type Strain Genome Server (TYGS) placed Pseudomonas chlororaphis strains FS-2 and RP-4 within the species P. chlororaphis. Phylogenomic reconstruction revealed that FS-2 and RP-4 clustered together in a well-supported monophyletic clade with high bootstrap values, together with several closely related P. chlororaphis strains, including PB-St2 (Fig. 2). However, this clade was phylogenetically separated from the clades containing the type strains of the currently described P. chlororaphis subspecies, indicating that subspecies-level assignment of FS-2 and RP-4 cannot be resolved based on the available genomic data. Genomic completeness, assessed using CheckM (v1.2.4), was 93.94% for both genomes. Assessment of pathogenic potential using PathogenFinder classified both isolates as non-human pathogens. The probability scores for human pathogenic origin were 0.075 for strain FS-2 and 0.154 for strain RP-4, well below the pathogenicity threshold. Supporting this classification, FS-2 showed homology to 81 non-pathogenic protein families, and RP-4 to 49 such families.

Genomic features and functional analysis of Pseudomonas FS-2 and RP-4

The P. chlororaphis FS-2 genome was 6,575,532 bp (6.6 Mb) long, with a GC content of 63.50%. It was assembled from 111 contigs, featuring a contig N50 of 195,642 kb and contig L50 of 11. Analysis using the NCBI Prokaryotic Genome Annotation Pipeline (PGAP) revealed 6019 genes. This gene count comprised 5815 protein-coding genes, one non-coding gene, 61 tRNA genes, six rRNA genes, 107 pseudogenes, and three uncharacterized genes, respectively. The size of the P. chlororaphis RP-4 was 6,613,826 bp (6.6 Mb) as shown by one contig N50 with the GC content of 63%. The NCBI prokaryotic genome annotation pipeline showed a total of 5982 genes. Among those, 5796 were reported as protein-coding genes, 71 tRNA genes, 15 rRNA genes, 95 pseudogenes, four genes with uncharacterized attributes, and a non-coding gene (Fig. 3A).

Comparative analysis of the two genomes under study with the reference genome of P. chlororaphis subsp. aurantiaca PB-St2 using the COG and KEGG databases revealed shared essential functions, predominantly for amino acids and carbohydrates metabolism, motility and biofilm formation, complementary cellular activities essential for ecological competence and viability, and processing of genetic information (Fig. 3B). Additionally, the COG and KEGG analysis also showed the secondary metabolite synthesis capacities, genes for environmental information processing of these strains underscoring their potential for producing biocides and adaptation to diverse environmental niches. Slightly distinct ecological adaptation was observed in the metabolic pathways of P. chlororaphis FS-2 with an overall suitability for biocontrol applications and resilience across diverse ecological habitats (Table 2).

A previous comparative genomic analysis of two Pseudomonas spp. strains harboring the qnrVC6 gene reported similar patterns in COG and KEGG annotations [26]. That study also noted key differences in virulence factors, antimicrobial resistance genes, and specialized gene clusters between the strains. Notably, while both strains exhibited elevated quinolone resistance due to qnrVC6, P. putida strain Guangzhou-Ppu420 was found to carry a duplicated qnrVC6 copy. Additionally, resistance to β-lactams and aminoglycosides was strain-specific, linked to distinct gene sets unique to each isolate [26].

Comparative analysis of core and accessory genomes in P. chlororaphis strains

Core genomes of P. chlororaphis strains FS-2 and RP-4, along with the reference strain P. chlororaphis subsp. aurantiaca PB-St2, were analyzed using OrthoVenn3 (Fig. 4A). The central overlap revealed a conserved core genome of 5,644 genes shared among all three strains, representing essential functions related to primary metabolism, cellular maintenance, and fundamental regulatory pathways. This extensive common gene set underscores the conserved genetic backbone of the P. chlororaphis species complex.

Outside the core genome, strain-specific overlaps were observed: 116 genes were shared exclusively between FS-2 and RP-4, reflecting their close phylogenetic relationship. In contrast, PB-St2 shared 16 genes solely with RP-4 and 11 genes only with FS-2, indicative of subspecies-level genetic distinctions. Unique gene complements were also identified: RP-4 harbored 8 exclusive genes, while FS-2 contained 67, suggesting functional differentiation within the clade. The reference strain PB-St2 possessed a markedly larger accessory genome of 181 unique genes, highlighting its genomic divergence.

These findings align with previous genomic studies in Pseudomonas, which consistently report a conserved core genome alongside variable accessory gene content. For instance, a pangenomic analysis of 413 P. putida group strains identified a core of 2,226 protein families essential for basic biological processes, while the accessory genome contributed to ecological adaptability and niche specialization [27]. Similarly, large-scale genomic comparisons across Pseudomonas species have revealed that pathogenic functions are largely absent in environmental isolates, underscoring the link between accessory genome content and ecological lifestyle [28]. In rhizosphere-associated pseudomonads, studies such as that of 394 maize rhizosphere isolates have demonstrated that conserved genetic modules including biocidal and metal resistance genes are maintained under selective pressures, whereas secondary metabolite clusters and virulence determinants vary significantly between strains [29]. This pattern of conserved resistomes alongside diverse biosynthetic capabilities mirrors our observation of a stable core genome coupled with strain-specific accessory genes in P. chlororaphis. Hence, the core and accessory genome analysis of Pseudomonas strains FS-2 and RP-4 not only validates the established genomic architecture of Pseudomonas but also provides a framework for interpreting the functional diversity observed in FS-2 and RP-4. The unique genes identified here particularly the expanded accessory genome in FS-2 likely contributes to phenotypic traits such as secondary metabolite production, stress adaptation, or ecological interactions, positioning these strains as distinct yet related members of the P. chlororaphis complex.

Average nucleotide identity

In this study, average nucleotide identity (ANI) heatmaps of strains RP-4 and FS-2 showed closed similarities with the estimated ANI value for the two strains was 99.96%. The high percentage similarity is indicative of closed genomic taxonomy and same species and is significantly above than the commonly accepted species boundary threshold of 95–96%. Furthermore, the ANI value of 99.96% indicates that only a small fraction (approximately 0.04%) of the nucleotide sequences is different between the two genomes and does not show any significant genomic divergence. P. chlororaphis strains RP-4 and FS-2 shared ANI values above 94% with other strains of the same group suggesting that despite the significant genetic similarity, they are somewhat less similar to other strains within their subspecies. Similarly, a significant genomic divergence can be observed from the lower ANI values between RP-4 and FS-2 compared to those of strains from the subspecies P. chlororaphis subsp. aurantiaca (~ 92–94%). This can further be witnessed from the clustering distance between P. chlororaphis subsp. chlororaphis and P. chlororaphis subsp. aurantiaca highlighting the ANI divergence between these subspecies, further supporting their differentiation at the genomic level (Fig. 4B).

Average Nucleotide Identity (ANI) analysis is extensively employed to evaluate taxonomic classifications and genomic relationships. For example, a large-scale genomic study by Tran et al. (2017) analyzed 373 Pseudomonas genomes and identified 207 taxonomic discrepancies, correcting 43 misclassifications and proposing reclassifications of strains previously labeled as P. syringae and P. fluorescens based on ANI and MLST data [30]. Similarly, Scortichini et al. (2013) employed genome-wide MLSA combined with ANI and TETRA analyses on phytopathogenic pseudomonads, revealing that strains previously classified as distinct species actually formed a single well-demarcated genomic cluster [31]. That study reported ANI values > 95% among P. synxantha, P. libanensis, P. psychrotolerans, P. oryzihabitans, and the P. kilonensis–P. brassicacearum complex, indicating closer relatedness than conventional taxonomy suggested. In addition, Yonezuka et al. (2016) reclassified 59 P. putida group strains into 26 distinct species using ANI, underscoring the necessity of genomic tools for accurate species delineation [32].

CAZyme repertoire and virulence associated genes in Pseudomonas strains

A total of 63 and 58 virulence-associated genes were identified in P. chlororaphis FS-2 and RP-4, respectively, using the VFDB (Fig. 5A). The genes were distributed across six functional categories including biofilm formation, adherence, motility, effector delivery systems (Type VI Secretion System-T6SS), nutritional/metabolic factors, and immune modulation. Comparative analysis revealed that 58 virulence genes were common to both strains, representing a highly conserved core virulence repertoire (Supplementary Tables S1 & S2). These included key genes involved in alginate biosynthesis (algU, algR, algB), flagellar assembly (flhA, fliG, motA), chemotaxis (cheY, cheR), T6SS structural components (tssA, tssB, tssM, hcp), and siderophore production (pvdF, pvdS). Two T6SS-related genes clpV1 and tssH were identified exclusively in FS-2, indicating a potential duplication or expansion of the T6SS ATPase machinery in this strain. No unique virulence genes were found in RP-4. The conserved virulence profile suggests both strains possess similar pathogenic potential, particularly in biofilm formation, motility, and interbacterial competition via T6SS. The additional T6SS ATPase genes in FS-2 may enhance its secretory capacity or regulatory flexibility, which could influence ecological competitiveness or host interactions.

Comparative annotation using the Carbohydrate-Active enZymes (CAZyme) database revealed highly similar CAZyme repertoires in P. chlororaphis FS-2 and RP-4 (Fig. 5B). A total of 30 and 31 CAZyme genes were identified in FS-2 and RP-4, respectively, distributed across five functional classes comprising glycosyl transferases (GT), glycoside hydrolases (GH), carbohydrate esterases (CE), auxiliary activities (AA), and carbohydrate-binding modules (CBM). No polysaccharide lyase (PL) genes were detected in either strain. The sole distinction between the strains was the presence of a gene encoding the AA3_3 family (alcohol oxidase) exclusively in RP-4, indicating a subtle divergence in oxidative carbohydrate metabolism.

Plant growth promoting and metabolic genes in Pseudomonas FS-2 and RP-4

Pseudomonas strains FS-2 and RP-4 exhibited diverse sets of genes associated with enhanced plant growth and various functional activities. The genomic analysis indicated the presence of genes for the synthesis of indole-3-acetic acid (IAA), including trpA, trpB, mtr, kynA, iacA, trpC, solubilization of organic zinc (zur, ftsH, znuB, znuC), and potassium (trkA, kdpA, kdpB, kdpC), synthesis of siderophores (lucA, lucC), and glycine betaine (opuaA, opuaD), suggesting their involvement in nutrient acquisition and hormone production enhancing plant’s resilience and growth [33]. Moreover, both strains harbored genes that confer resistance to heavy metals including arsenic, cobalt, and chromium, facilitate lipid and sulfur metabolism (ssuA, ssuB, ssuC, ssuD, tauE), enable the catabolism of aromatic compounds (fdHA, catA, catC), and promote adaptation to stress through cold- and heat-shock proteins (Table 3). Moreover, the genes for biofilm formation (pgaA, pgaB, pgaC, pgaD), and polysaccharide synthesis were also observed indicating the rhizosphere competence and root-colonization potential of these strains. The presence of a diverse set of genes configuring plant-beneficial and increased survival under extreme conditions in these strains is consistent with previously reported pseudomonads. For instance, a comprehensive analysis of rhizopseudomonads isolated from date palm roots showed high rates of plant growth-promoting activities, including IAA production (88.8%), phosphate solubilization (63.8%), and siderophore release (83.3%) [34]. Among those strains, P. vancouverensis and P. brassicacearum, harbored copA, arsR, and arsB, respectively, for heavy metal tolerance, while also producing biosurfactants [34]. Similarly, in Pseudomonas sp. UW4, four native IAA biosynthesis genes (ami, nit, nthAB, and phe) were characterized, and their overexpression led to increased indole-3-acetic acid production [35]. This strain also showed 1-aminocyclopropane-1-carboxylate deaminase (ACC deaminase) activity, although at lower levels in the transformant strains than in the wild-type strain. Biofilm formation is critical for the rhizosphere colonization and persistence of Pseudomonas species. Key extracellular matrix (ECM) components, such as alginate, PNAG, PsmE, and the Flp/Tad pilus, facilitate biofilm formation and competitive root colonization in pseudomonads, thereby supporting their plant-beneficial functions [36]. In our study, strains RP-4 and FS-2 possessed variable complements of biofilm-related and quorum-sensing genes, which may underpin their successful rhizosphere colonization and biocontrol efficacy against phytopathogens.

Bacteriocins in the genomes of Pseudomonas FS-2 and RP-4

Bacteriocins play important roles in the genomes of P. chlororaphis strains and contribute significantly to their rhizosphere interactions [37]. Strains FS-2 and RP-4 contained several bacteriocins, particularly pyocins and colicins, which may confer antimicrobial activity and immunity (Fig. 6). Strain FS-2 features multiple pyocins, including Colicin-E7 immunity proteins and Colicin-E9, along with an ABC-type bacteriocin exporter, LanT, which assists in the export and transport of bacteriocins. In contrast, strain RP-4 also contained several pyocins and Colicin_E9 proteins, albeit with fewer distinct bacteriocin-related open reading frames (ORFs) than FS-2. Both strains contained the LanT exporter; however, FS-2 had a broader array of bacteriocin types and immunity-related genes, potentially providing enhanced bacteriocin-mediated defense capabilities compared to RP-4 (Table 4). The diverse bacteriocins in P. chlororaphis strains, particularly FS-2, likely enhance their competitive advantage in ecological niches.

This expanded suite of metabolites can target more microbial competitors, leading to enhanced survival and colonization. Bacteriocin synthesis has been reported in several Pseudomonas species. A study on P. chlororaphis 30–84 strain demonstrated the genetic elements encoding the two distinctive R-tailocins that have different ancestral origins and killing spectra [38]. These tailocins share the identical lysis cassette for release, which reflects the efficiency of bacteriocin production systems in P. chlororaphis.

Biosynthetic gene clusters in the Pseudomonas strains FS-2 and RP-4

A rich biosynthetic repertoire comprising 17 biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs) was identified in P. chlororaphis strain FS-2 using PRISM and antiSMASH-based genome mining. These BGCs putatively responsible for the biosynthesis of diverse metabolites, antibiotics, and signaling compounds, underscore the ecological versatility of this strain in competitive soil environments (Table 5). The antiSMASH and PRSIM data showed multiple nonribosomal peptide synthetase (NRPS), RiPP (ribosomally synthesized and post-translationally modified), and hybrid gene clusters predicted to encode pyoverdines, phenazines, pyocyanin, and pyrrolnitrin-like metabolites with renowned antimicrobial, siderophore, and quorum-regulatory functions. Cluster 1 was identified as a putative N-acetylglutaminylglutamine amide (NAGGN) biosynthetic cluster, spanning nucleotides 114,134–128,970 (14.8 kb). The cluster indicated moderate similarity to the Pseudomonas fluorescens NAGGN operon signifying a conserved but potentially divergent function. NAGGN cluster is predicted to encode characteristic small NRPS-independent pathway for NAGGN biosynthesis in Pseudomonas sp., including genes for acyltransferase and amidase enzymes. However, transcription factor binding site (TFBS) analysis revealed only weak promoter hits, indicating that this cluster may be transcriptionally silent or expressed at low levels under standard conditions. A pyoverdine-like biosynthetic gene cluster (Region 2) was identified in Pseudomonas chlororaphis FS-2, spanning nucleotides 271,415–307,223 (35.8 kb). This cluster exhibited 83% similarity to the characterized Pseudomonas pyoverdine reference clusters in antiSMASH, suggesting a high degree of conservation of siderophore biosynthesis genes. The NRPS module architecture was composed of M1-NH₂, M2-Glu, M3-D-Tyr, and M4-Dab, reflecting a typical pyoverdine biosynthetic signature in the genome. Transcription factor binding site (TFBS) prediction revealed strong promoter confidence, indicating the potential active expression of this cluster under relevant environmental conditions (Fig. 7A). Pyoverdines are high-affinity iron-chelating siderophores that enable Pseudomonas species to acquire iron in nutrient-limited environments, indirectly promoting plant growth and competitive colonization of the rhizosphere [39]. The presence of this cluster in FS-2 correlated with the in vitro detection of pyoverdine-type metabolites previously reported by our group [8], confirming the functional expression of this biosynthetic pathway.

Two additional biosynthetic gene clusters were identified in P. chlororaphis FS-2, corresponding to phenazine and homoserine lactone synthesis pathways (Table 5). The phenazine-associated NRPS cluster (Region 3; 49,229–72,015 nt; 22.8 kb) showed 100% similarity to known Pseudomonas phenazine gene clusters, including those responsible for the production of phenazine-1-carboxylic acid, pyocyanin, and 2-hydroxyphenazine (Fig. 7B).

These results are in line with our earlier in vitro and metabolomic analyses conducted by Shahid et al. [7], which verified the presence of these compounds in strain FS-2. Phenazines are redox-active secondary metabolites with extensively documented ecological roles in microbial competition and antimicrobial activity [40, 41]. The distinctive orange pigmentation in P. chlororaphis FS-2 is the hallmark of its phenazines biosynthesis. Likewise, a homoserine lactone (HSL) cluster (Region 4; 122,119–142,799 nt; 20.7 kb) was found to be 100% similar to Pseudomonas quorum-sensing systems previously identified in these strains (Table 5). The characteristic genes including luxI/luxR-type regulatory elements, typical of N-acyl homoserine lactone (AHL) biosynthesis, were identified within the cluster. These genes serve as regulatory elements modulating secondary metabolite production and biofilm formation and indicate a functional quorum-sensing network coordinating phenazine biosynthesis and antimicrobial processes [42].

Cluster 5, encoding ribosomally synthesized and post-translationally modified peptides (RiPP), and cluster 9, associated with hydrogen cyanide production, showed 100% similarity to known biosynthetic clusters, suggesting conserved pathways for compounds that inhibit pathogens through direct toxicity or environmental competition (Fig. 7C). Another secondary metabolite biosynthetic gene cluster identified in P. chlororaphis FS-2 corresponded to pyrrolnitrin production (Region 12; 94,894–130,837 nt; 35.9 kb). This cluster showed 100% similarity to the canonical prnABCD operon responsible for pyrrolnitrin biosynthesis in Pseudomonas spp. The prnA and prnB genes encode halogenase and oxidase enzymes that catalyze the conversion of tryptophan to chlorinated pyrrole derivatives, conferring potent antifungal activity against a wide range of phytopathogens. The presence of this cluster supports the strong inhibitory activity of strain FS-2 against Botrytis cinerea and Alternaria alternata observed in vitro, as previously described by Shahid et al. [7]. Pyrrolnitrin production in Pseudomonas is a well-known determinant of biocontrol efficacy, and its conservation in FS-2 highlights the strain’s potential as a biofungicide agent for sustainable crop protection (Fig. 7D).

The genome of strain P. chlororaphis FS-2 also showed the presence of several other putative biosynthetic regions in addition to well-characterized BGCs for phenazines, hydrogen cyanide, pyrrolnitrin, and pyoverdines. Such as, a siderophore-associated region (Region 7; 160,543–197,512 nt; 36.9 kb) exhibited 56% similarity to the vibrioferrin cluster, indicating the putative iron-chelating mechanism. Likewise, region 13 encoded an arylpolyene (APE-Vf-like) cluster with 73% similarity, indicating potential oxidative stress protection functions through pigmentation. Arylpolyene (APEs) is a class of lipids characterized by an aryl head group connected to a polyene tail. These compounds are synthesized by all Proteobacteria subphyla and various fluorescent Pseudomonas species. The conjugated double-bond systems in APEs protect cells from oxidative damage by neutralizing reactive oxygen species (ROS), including peroxy radicals and singlet oxygen [43]. APE Vf-encoding cluster 13 in FS-2 showed 73% similarity to the genes of Pseudomonas sp. SK3 has been reported to be involved in the biosynthesis of APEs [44]. Region 14, classified as NRPS-resorcinol (63%), contained darA-C genes associated with dihydroaeruginoic acid/resorcinol-type metabolites, which may play roles in antifungal activity and biofilm regulation. The low-similarity betalactone (Region 15; 13%) and butyrolactone (Region 8) clusters may encode signaling or antimicrobial molecules that are yet to be characterized. Region 16 (NRPS; 89%) resembled the pseudodesmin A (WLIP-like) lipopeptide cluster, known for its surfactant and antifungal properties, whereas Region 17 (72%) showed partial homology to the chitinimide A biosynthetic system, potentially contributing to competitive fitness in soil ecosystems.

Diverse set of biosynthetic gene clusters in P. chlororaphis RP-4 demonstrated its significant biocontrol potential and ecological adaptability. Genome mining of strain RP-4 revealed 15 putative BGCs of diverse structural classes, including NRPS, RiPP, arylpolyene, siderophore, butyrolactone, and β-lactone. Among these, phenazine (Region 14; 5,520,571–5,543,357 nt; 93% similarity) and homoserine lactone (Regions 3 and 13; 81–100%), indicate conserved quorum sensing and antimicrobial capacities and were previously detected in vitro within this strain [8]. A siderophore-associated cluster (Region 5; 3,422,490–3,459,459 nt) showed moderate similarity (53%) to vibrioferrin, suggesting an alternative iron-chelating mechanism complementing pyoverdine biosynthesis (Region 11; 73% similarity). Notably, an NRPS cluster (Region 7; 3,792,349–3,870,058 nt; 77.7 kb) showed 87% similarity to the pseudodesmin A or white line-inducing principle (WLIP) biosynthetic cluster (MA026). This locus encodes a modular NRPS system composed of six core biosynthetic genes (wlpA-F), including adenylation (A), condensation (C), epimerization (E), and thioesterase (TE) domains arranged to assemble an eight-residue lipopeptide (Fig. 8A-C). The predicted domain architecture (A₁-C₁-E₁-A₂-C₂-KR-A₃-C₃-A₄-C₄-E₄-TE) corresponds to the biosynthetic logic of WLIP previously reported in P. chlororaphis PB-St2, supporting its potential to synthesize cyclic lipopeptide pseudodesmin [45]. WLIP is known for its strong surface-active and antifungal properties, particularly against Rhizoctonia solani and Botrytis cinerea, and it contributes to motility, biofilm formation, and root colonization efficiency [46]. Other minor clusters included an arylpolyene locus (Region 1; 63% similarity), potentially involved in oxidative stress protection; a putative betalactone cluster (Region 9; 13%), resembling fengycin-like systems; and a novel N-acetylglutaminylglutamine amide (NAGGN) cluster (Region 12; 14.6 kb) of uncertain function (Table 6). The detection of diverse BGCs associated to siderophore, phenazines, lipopeptides, and quorum-sensing signals underscore the metabolic flexibility of P. chlororaphis RP-4 and its possible rhizosphere-mediated interactions.

Conclusion

Comprehensive genomic analysis of P. chlororaphis strains FS-2 and RP-4 provides valuable insights into their genetic diversity, functional capabilities, and potential agricultural applications. A diverse array of genes associated with plant growth promotion, stress tolerance, and anti-phytopathogenic activities were observed in both genomes. The presence of multiple biosynthetic gene clusters including nonribosomal peptide synthetases and polyketide synthases, highlights their capacity to produce an array of biocides. Additionally, the presence of novel BGCs suggests that these strains may produce uncharacterized antimicrobials. The integrative genomic analysis supports the application of these strains as biocontrol agents and biofertilizers for plant protection and growth promotion. Subsequent analysis must focus on characterization of novel BGCs to fully understand the strain-specific traits of these pseudomonads.
