Reconstructing transcription–translation-coupled DNA replication (TTcDR) in artificial systems is crucial for creating synthetic life; however, existing approaches face limitations mainly due to their reliance on purified biological components. Here, we introduce LoopReX, a cell-free system that reconstitutes TTcDR using crude Escherichia coli extracts, offering a more complex native biological environment. LoopReX leverages a minimal machinery composed of phi29 DNA polymerase and T7 RNA polymerase, with the latter facilitating DNA replication initiation through the generation of primer RNAs. Using machine learning, we optimize LoopReX to enhance the efficiency of both DNA replication and protein expression, achieving scalable, sustainable genetic flow and high-yield protein production with robust iterative performance. Furthermore, artificial nucleoids, autonomously formed through CipB-based compartmentalization, improve DNA spatial organization and support multiple biological functions. This work advances the construction of artificial life by reconstituting TTcDR within a single, scalable, and functionalized system, opening exciting possibilities for synthetic biology, biotechnology, and bio-hybrid applications.

Introduction

The construction of artificial life aims to redesign and synthesize “living” systems capable of exhibiting fundamental characteristics such as replication, metabolism, compartmentalization, and communication1–4. At the core of these processes, the central dogma of molecular biology—the flow of genetic information from DNA to RNA to proteins—serves as a foundational framework. Reconstructing transcription–translation-coupled DNA replication (TTcDR) in vitro is an essential step toward the creation of artificial life systems5. The PURE (Protein synthesis Using Recombinant Elements)-based cell-free expression system, which functions in a well-defined (bio)chemical environment6, has been widely applied to reconstitute specific life-like processes such as DNA replication7–12, gene sequence evolution13–15, cell division-like events16–18, artificial–natural cell communication19–23, and light-driven ATP regeneration24. However, these efforts typically address isolated functions, and integrating multiple life characteristics into a single artificial system remains a major challenge given the relatively simplified nature of the PURE platform.

Recently, researchers have explored the use of cell lysates to construct artificial cells that more closely resemble the cytoplasm of living cells25,26. Cell lysates, which contain a broad array of native biomolecules and biocatalysts, provide a more complex and dynamic environment for reconstituting life-like processes. For example, autonomous ribosome biogenesis has been achieved in vitro using Escherichia coli cell extracts, marking a crucial step toward artificial cell development25. Additionally, the use of two bacterial lysates has enabled the creation of synthetic cells capable of transcription, translation, and some life-like features, such as proto-nuclei and cytoskeletal structures26. Despite these advances, to our knowledge, TTcDR has not yet been reconstituted within complex, lysate-based in vitro biological systems.

Here, we present the LoopReX (Loop of DNA replication and protein expression) system, an E. coli crude extract-based cell-free gene expression (CFE) platform designed to reconstitute TTcDR in vitro. Specifically, LoopReX integrates four key modules: (1) DNA replication, driven by phi29 bacteriophage DNA polymerase (phi29DNAP), (2) transcription, mediated by T7 bacteriophage RNA polymerase (T7RNAP), (3) translation, supported by ribosomes and translation-related factors within the cell extract, and (4) an artificial nucleoid, assembled using a bacterial CipB scaffold to spatially compartmentalize DNA for efficient TTcDR processes. In contrast to previous reconstituted systems such as PURE7–9, where replication machinery is encoded and expressed in situ, our approach uses a cell extract-based system supplemented with purified DNA polymerase. This enables robust, immediate replication and provides a versatile platform for tuning replication independently of protein synthesis, while leveraging the complex biochemical environment of the lysate.

We initially demonstrate that LoopReX effectively supports DNA replication and elucidate the synergetic mechanisms between phi29DNAP and T7RNAP. Additionally, through optimization via machine learning strategies, we achieve a robust and scalable system that enables sustainable genetic iteration with the production of both DNA and protein. Furthermore, we show that the artificial nucleoid not only enhances the performance of in vitro TTcDR but can also be used to integrate functional monomers, such as enzymes, to reconstitute artificial metabolic networks. By successfully reconstituting TTcDR within a complex crude extract-based cell-free expression system, the LoopReX platform represents a significant advancement in the construction of artificial life. We anticipate that this system will provide valuable insights and opportunities for the design, construction, and study of artificial cells in the future.

Results

Establishment of LoopReX by reconstituting TTcDR in vitro

To establish the TTcDR process in vitro, we selected the E. coli crude lysate-based CFE system, which has been extensively developed and widely used for various applications27–36, and phi29DNAP as the DNA replication machinery due to its exceptionally high processivity (up to 70 kb per single synthesis event) and its superior DNA synthesis fidelity, conferred by its 3’→5’ exonuclease proofreading activity37,38. We first assessed the activity of phi29DNAP in a pure chemical reaction buffer using circular single-stranded DNA (cssDNA, ssM13) as a template. The results demonstrated that phi29DNAP exhibited robust DNA polymerase activity in the reaction buffer (Supplementary Figs. 1 and 2). However, DNA replication efficiency declined over time (Supplementary Fig. 3), likely due to phi29DNAP’s exonuclease activity, depletion of replication initiation complexes (circular single-stranded DNA + initiation primer), and accumulation of inhibitory byproducts such as pyrophosphate (Supplementary Fig. 4)7,12,37,39.

Following the initial experiments, we introduced the DNA replication machinery into the crude extract-based CFE system, which we term LoopReX. It is important to note that the DNA replication in our system proceeds via phi29DNAP-based rolling circle amplification and is therefore non-recursive—the replicated DNA does not recapitulate the original circular template. This differs from canonical chromosomal replication in vivo, but still provides a powerful and programmable means of DNA amplification and template regeneration in vitro7–12. To begin, we used cssDNA (ssM13) as the template and confirmed that phi29DNAP is active in the CFE system (Fig. 1a). We then replaced the cssDNA template with circular double-stranded DNA (cdsDNA, pJL1-T7-sfGFP) and also observed successful replication of pJL1-T7-sfGFP in LoopReX (Fig. 1b). However, the replication efficiency of phi29DNAP was noticeably lower for cdsDNA compared to cssDNA (Fig. 1a, b). This suggests that cdsDNA requires strand separation and synthesis of replication initiation primers (which were not added to the reaction) before phi29DNAP can initiate the DNA replication process (Fig. 1b). In contrast, these prerequisites already exist in the cssDNA reactions from the outset (Fig. 1a). Next, we selected pJL1-T7-sfGFP as the template in our LoopReX system because cdsDNA mimics the natural double-stranded genome and remains stable in the extract-based CFE system (Fig. 1b, see the “CFE system” group). Using this, we simultaneously analyzed DNA replication and protein expression in LoopReX over a 6 h period (Fig. 1c, d). We observed that DNA replication and protein expression occurred on different temporal scales. Specifically, DNA content remained high during the first hour and then gradually decreased over the next 5 h (Fig. 1c), likely due to nuclease-mediated degradation in the cell lysate. Nonetheless, LoopReX consistently maintained higher DNA levels than the control CFE system at all time points. Despite the reduction in DNA, protein expression steadily increased over 5 h before plateauing (Fig. 1d). Additionally, protein expression in LoopReX reached its maximum earlier than in the control CFE system, suggesting a faster expression rate that offers advantages in efficiency and throughput. Taken together, these findings demonstrate that LoopReX outperforms the CFE system in both DNA replication and protein expression, providing a robust platform for subsequent optimization.

Having reconstructed TTcDR in vitro, we next sought to elucidate the mechanism of DNA replication initiation, which remains unclear. Previous studies have suggested that RNAs transcribed by T7RNAP could serve as primers for DNA replication, as observed in the PURE system and with phi29DNAP7. To determine if this phenomenon occurs in our LoopReX system, we first tested the efficiency of DNA replication with varying amounts of T7RNAP in the reactions. We found that replication efficiency was positively correlated with the amount of T7RNAP in LoopReX (Fig. 1e, Bars 1 to 4). However, higher amounts of T7RNAP slightly inhibited DNA replication efficiency (Fig. 1e, Bars 4 to 6). These results suggest that DNA replication efficiency is directly influenced by the amount of T7RNAP in vitro.

To further investigate the mechanism of DNA replication initiation by T7RNAP, we constructed different plasmids by replacing the original T7 promoter (φ10, class III) used in pJL1-T7-sfGFP with the class II (φ1.1B and φ1.3) and class III (φ13) promoters. Previous studies have shown that these T7-based promoters performed differently during transcription, producing two types of RNA products: abortive transcription products (short, non-functional RNA chains) and full-length mRNA (used for protein synthesis)40 (Fig. 1f). While these four promoters prefer to generate different RNA products, we found that DNA replication efficiency followed the order of the total RNA product yields (defined as the sum of abortive transcription products and full-length mRNA) (Fig. 1f). Based on this, we propose that T7RNAP and its promoters facilitate the DNA replication initiation process by generating RNA products that serve as primers (note that full-length mRNA may also be degraded into short RNAs by nucleases) for DNA replication (Fig. 1g). The use of abortive transcription products as replication primers has been observed in natural DNA replication initiation mechanisms41,42 (Fig. 1g, blue route). For instance, T7DNAP uses abortive transcription products to initiate genome replication in T7 bacteriophages41,42. However, unlike abortive transcription products, the involvement of mRNA as primer sources for DNA replication by phi29DNAP is unexpected. The essence of mRNA-based primers is that mRNA provides a nucleotide sequence that can generate diverse short RNA chains. To further validate whether long nucleotide chains can serve as primer sources, we attempted to replicate cssDNA (ssM13) in LoopReX by directly using the diverse nucleotide chains contained in cell lysates (Supplementary Fig. 5). Our findings indicate that phi29DNAP can indeed utilize long nucleotide chains as primer sources for DNA replication (Fig. 1g, green route)43,44.

Building on our investigation, we demonstrate that T7 promoter, T7RNAP, and phi29DNAP can form a minimal replication unit within the LoopReX system. To enhance the efficiency of in vitro DNA replication, we next constructed plasmids with tandemly repeated promoters, designed to serve as DNA replication initiation sites (Fig. 1h). These included 2*φ10, 3*φ10, and 4*φ10 promoters, all based on the strongest φ10 promoter (Fig. 1f). As a comparison, we also constructed a plasmid with the native replication origin of T7 bacteriophages, which consists of the φ1.1 A and φ1.1B promoters. Our data reveal that the tandemly repeated promoters are more effective at initiating DNA replication, with the 3*φ10 promoter improving replication efficiency by threefold compared to the 1*φ10 promoter. However, we also observed a slight reduction in protein expression yields with the tandem promoters (Fig. 1h). This suggests that using promoters as replication origins introduces a competitive dynamic between DNA replication and transcription at the promoter sites. To explore further, we investigated whether the position of the promoters affects both replication and transcription. Specifically, we defined the 3*φ10 promoter as an artificial replication origin and relocated it to different positions within the plasmid (Fig. 1i). Our results showed that when the artificial origin was positioned downstream of the terminator—far from the normal 1*φ10 promoter—the DNA replication efficiency increased compared to 1*φ10-based plasmid without the insertion of the artificial origin (Fig. 1i). In contrast, the replication efficiency of the 4*φ10-based plasmid (that is similar to the artificial origin placed upstream of the 1*φ10 promoter) was significantly reduced, reaching the lowest observed values. These findings support our hypothesis that the position of the promoters (i.e., the artificial origin) plays a crucial role in regulating DNA replication and transcription. Importantly, we also observed a remarkable increase in protein expression levels with the plasmid containing the artificial origin (Fig. 1i).

Taken together, we have established our LoopReX system with three key achievements: (i) successful reconstitution of TTcDR in vitro using phi29DNAP and T7RNAP in a cell extract-based CFE system—a more complex in vitro biological system; (ii) elucidation of the synergetic mechanisms of phi29DNAP and T7RNAP in DNA replication, enabling the construction of an efficient, two enzymes-based replication unit for LoopReX; and (iii) rational design of an artificial replication origin that enhances the overall performance of the TTcDR process, benefiting not only DNA replication but also transcription and translation.

Machine learning-driven optimization of the TTcDR process in LoopReX

In the LoopReX system, T7RNAP plays a crucial role in initiating DNA replication and driving transcription (Fig. 1e–i). However, in vitro processes for DNA replication, transcription, and translation often require distinct and optimized conditions within the cell-free reaction environment12. Achieving effective coordination among these processes requires careful optimization of experimental parameters to maximize overall system performance. Previous studies have employed single-factor optimization methods to improve in vitro TTcDR based on the simple PURE system7,9,12. However, due to the complexity of the components in LoopReX, single-factor optimization may not be sufficient to efficiently fine-tune the system. To overcome this, we applied machine learning to optimize both DNA replication and protein expression in LoopReX, systematically exploring millions of possible conditions to identify parameters that maximize the efficiency of both processes simultaneously.

To streamline the optimization process and allow the machine learning model to focus on key variables, we used the pJL1-T7-sfGFP plasmid (i.e., pJL1-1*φ10-sfGFP) as the template. For in silico optimization, we employed XGBoost (eXtreme Gradient Boosting)45, a decision tree-based ensemble method known for strong predictive performance, scalability, and native handling of missing values and complex feature interactions. Moreover, XGBoost mitigates overfitting and improves model generalization through regularization, learning-rate shrinkage, subsampling of rows and columns, and early stopping. We constructed XGBoost models to predict DNA replication and protein expression levels by analyzing the influence of factors from the CFE standard protocol46,47, including additional variables such as dNTPs and DTT (Fig. 2a). We evaluated 16 factors (see “Factor selection and data processing” in “Methods” for details), and all experimental data were generated in our laboratory. For model development, the datasets comprised 115 experimental conditions for DNA replication and 126 experimental conditions for protein expression, each representing a distinct combination of reactants and their concentrations in the LoopReX system. Across samples, the varying features were the identity and concentration of input reactants, while all other experimental parameters were kept constant. The collected data were randomly split, with 80% used for model training and 20% reserved for independent testing. Then, five-fold cross-validation was performed only on the training set to assess and enhance the robustness of our models. Both models achieved a strong R² value of 0.99 for the training set. For the test set, the DNA replication prediction model had an R² of 0.81, while the protein expression model reached 0.88, demonstrating the robustness and accuracy of our machine learning models (Fig. 2b). To validate these predictions, we selected five distinct reaction formulations (F1–F5) and conducted LoopReX reactions. For protein expression, we measured sfGFP fluorescence at 6 h, while DNA content was quantified after 0.5 h. The experimental results showed consistent trends in both DNA replication efficiency and protein expression efficiency that aligned with the model predictions (Fig. 2c, d and Supplementary Table 1). To further analyze the impact of the five formulations on DNA replication and protein expression (transcription-translation), we examined DNA replication efficiency using ssM13 (lacking the T7 promoter) and protein expression using pJL1-T7-sfGFP (without the addition of phi29DNAP) (Fig. 2e, f). This allowed us to separately evaluate the performance of the formulations for DNA replication and transcription-translation processes. In the absence of protein expression, DNA replication efficiency from formulations F1 to F5 progressively increased (Fig. 2e), suggesting that transcription-translation machinery in LoopReX increasingly inhibits DNA replication (Fig. 2c). Similarly, in the absence of DNA replication, protein expression efficiency also increased across formulations F1 to F5 (Fig. 2f), reinforcing this observation. However, it is worth noting that the protein expression efficiency without DNA replication (Fig. 2f) was overall lower than that in LoopReX reactions with DNA replication (Fig. 2d). This difference is likely because the newly synthesized DNA in LoopReX can also serve as templates for protein expression, thereby enhancing protein yields.

After optimization, formulation F3 (see Supplementary Table 1 for concentration details) emerged as the optimal combination for both efficient DNA replication and protein expression (Fig. 2c, d and Supplementary Fig. 8). This optimized formulation, referred to as LoopReX-Opt, was further investigated for its ability to enable coupled DNA replication and protein expression using the pJL1-T7-sfGFP plasmid. Control experiments with the pre-optimized LoopReX system (LoopReX-Ori) were also performed for comparison. We observed that DNA content in the LoopReX-Opt system approximately doubled within the first 0.5 h (Fig. 2g). This increase in DNA content within 0.5 h is in agreement with the findings from the screening experiments in Fig. 2c, where formulation F3 showed similar results. Protein expression was also significantly enhanced in LoopReX-Opt, reaching maximum yield after just 3 h (Fig. 2h). In contrast, the LoopReX-Ori system and traditional CFE systems required 8 h (Fig. 2h) and 14 h (Supplementary Fig. 9), respectively, to reach comparable protein yields, despite all three systems using the same initial DNA template concentration. Although the improvements in DNA replication and protein expression were moderate (Fig. 2g, h), these results reflect the complex interplay of reaction components in LoopReX and demonstrate the utility of machine learning for optimizing such multifactorial systems. Importantly, LoopReX-Opt accelerates genetic information transfer and provides a more efficient TTcDR framework, which could be valuable for advancing artificial life research.

In vitro TTcDR enables sustainable and iterative flow of genetic information

With the TTcDR framework established, we next evaluated the ability of LoopReX-Opt to support the flow of genetic information. Initially, a seed solution was incubated for 1 h to replicate DNA, which was then transferred into a fresh LoopReX-Opt reaction (without DNA template) at a 4-fold dilution for subsequent rounds of DNA replication (Fig. 3a). After four rounds of reactions, we observed that LoopReX-Opt could maintain stable DNA replication, whereas the traditional CFE system failed to replicate DNA without the TTcDR framework. Notably, when a plasmid with the artificial origin (Fig. 1i) was used, LoopReX-Opt reactions resulted in greater DNA replication compared to the use of the pJL1-T7-sfGFP plasmid (Fig. 3a). Next, we performed successive, saturated iterative reactions, in which the entire reaction mixture from the previous round was used as a seed to inoculate multiple fresh reactions with a consistent ratio of 1:3 (seed: fresh mix) for the next round (Fig. 3b). The theoretical protein accumulation was assessed by analyzing the minimal unit of each iterative reaction (see “Methods” for details). The results revealed that the LoopReX-Opt system substantially enhanced protein accumulation with each iteration, compared to traditional CFE reactions (Fig. 3b). After the fifth round (Round 5), 1 nM of initial DNA (added in the Round 0 reaction) yielded a cumulative, theoretical protein yield of approximately 760 µg in the LoopReX-Opt system, representing a 3.6-fold increase compared to traditional CFE systems (Fig. 3b). The theoretical protein accumulation plateaued in the fourth round, suggesting that DNA replication remained effective for at least four rounds, after which it gradually ceased (Fig. 3b).

We also conducted volume scale-up iterative reactions, aligning the experimental design with standard cell cultivation protocols. In this approach, small-scale seed reactions were incubated for 1 h to maintain high DNA levels, followed by inoculation into fresh reaction mixes at a 1:10 dilution (v/v). For comparison, traditional CFE systems were also used to prepare seed solutions. In the final large-scale reaction, the seed solution was added to the traditional CFE system for protein expression, allowing us to evaluate the impact of replicated DNA levels on protein synthesis. Note that the final CFE reaction was incubated for 6 h with shaking to ensure homogeneity. Our results showed that DNA was effectively replicated during the scale-up process, serving as a template for high-level protein synthesis (Fig. 3c). Specifically, protein yields per DNA input in (i) single- and (ii) double-amplification reactions (up to 1 mL) increased by 9.5- and 15-fold, respectively, compared to the standard 15 µL LoopReX-Opt reaction. This indicates that in vitro amplification not only reduces DNA input requirements but also substantially enhances overall product yield.

Lastly, we sought to enhance protein production by introducing the plasmid containing the artificial origin (Fig. 1i) and employing the LoopReX-Opt system for final protein expression (experiments were performed using the single amplification scale-up method, Fig. 3c). We observed that combining these strategies further boosted protein production, resulting in a 2.5-fold increase in protein yield compared to the control (Fig. 3d). Our findings demonstrate that the plasmid with artificial origin significantly enhances DNA replication efficiency, as noted in Fig. 1i, and that the LoopReX-Opt system supports higher protein production than the traditional CFE system. Collectively, these results illustrate that the LoopReX-Opt system, incorporating the in vitro TTcDR framework, enables an efficient, scalable, and sustainable flow of genetic information.

Artificial nucleoid enhances the performance of in vitro TTcDR

In nature, eukaryotic and prokaryotic cells have their own ways of organizing and protecting DNA. The key difference lies in the presence of a membrane-bound nucleus in eukaryotes, whereas prokaryotes contain a membraneless nucleoid. Here, we aimed to construct an artificial nucleus-like structure to enhance the performance of in vitro TTcDR within complex biological systems. To achieve this, we selected the bacterial crystalline inclusion protein (CipB)48–50, which assembles spontaneously through hydrophobic interactions to form protein scaffolds, for the attachment of DNA sequences. Specifically, CipB was fused with streptavidin (i.e., CipB-streptavidin), enabling the attachment of biotin-modified linear double-stranded DNA (ldsDNA) (Fig. 4a). We first characterized the formation of the artificial nucleoid via compartmentalization, using purified CipB-streptavidin and biotin-modified ldsDNA. Electrophoretic mobility shift assay confirmed that the biotin-modified DNA bound efficiently to the fused protein scaffold through the streptavidin-biotin interaction (Fig. 4b and Supplementary Fig. 10). Transmission electron microscopy revealed that the CipB-streptavidin monomers could assemble into a regular structure at the nanoscale (Fig. 4c, left and Supplementary Fig. 11). Upon adding DNA, the protein-DNA complex formed similar nano-compartments (Fig. 4c, right and Supplementary Fig. 11), resulting in artificial nucleoids. These structures further aggregated into protein-DNA clusters, visible as solid, green particles with FAM-labeled DNA (Fig. 4d). We next assessed the impact of CipB-streptavidin monomer concentration on DNA protection and observed that higher protein concentrations provided enhanced DNA protection (Supplementary Fig. 12). This effect is likely due to increased CipB concentrations promoting more efficient coating and compaction of DNA into artificial nucleoids, which in turn shields DNA from nuclease degradation in the cell extract, as reported in previous studies36,51–53.

To further investigate, we constructed artificial nucleoids using a ldsDNA template containing an artificial origin and the sfGFP gene (Fig. 4e, top). The template was modified with biotin at the forward (F) end, reverse (R) end, or both ends. We first evaluated the binding efficiency of DNA templates to artificial nucleoids and found that all biotin-modified templates bound with nearly 100% efficiency, whereas unmodified ldsDNA showed markedly lower binding efficiency, likely due to nonspecific interactions (Supplementary Fig. 13). With these various ldsDNA-loaded artificial nucleoids, we observed a significant improvement in TTcDR performance (Fig. 4e, f). For instance, the artificial nucleoid formed with dual-biotin-modified DNA showed a 7-fold increase in DNA replication efficiency, as evidenced by the greater amount of newly replicated DNA released into the supernatant (Fig. 4e; red bars). Additionally, this dual-biotin-modified DNA template exhibited superior DNA protection compared to the R-biotin-modified ldsDNA (Fig. 4e; orange bars). Notably, the highest protein expression was also achieved with the dual-biotin-DNA-formed artificial nucleoids (Fig. 4f). While the overall performance of TTcDR was improved with DNA-loaded artificial nucleoids, the efficiencies varied depending on the DNA–CipB binding format. Dual-biotin-modified DNA achieved the highest efficiency, as both ends were protected by CipB binding, preventing degradation and enabling more templates for protein synthesis. In contrast, single-biotin-modified DNA, with one free end exposed to nucleases, was more readily degraded, resulting in lower DNA replication and protein synthesis efficiency.

Due to the nature of our designed artificial nucleoids (which aggregate into larger structures), they can be easily collected via centrifugation and reused for subsequent rounds of reactions (Fig. 4g, top). Data demonstrated that artificial nucleoids could be reused for at least five cycles, maintaining substantial protein expression yields, with approximately 80% of the initial DNA remaining on the nucleoids (Fig. 4g). Taken together, these results highlight the advantages of artificial nucleoids, which not only mimic the compartmentalized structure of natural nuclei but also enhance the TTcDR performance.

Finally, we sought to realize the de novo, autonomous formation of artificial nucleoids in the LoopReX-Opt system by simply adding DNA as a single input. After cell-free expression, the fused CipB-mCherry protein self-assembled (self-growth) onto the pre-formed artificial nucleoids, resulting in a hybrid complex that exhibited red fluorescence (Fig. 5a). Next, we added two dual-biotin-modified ldsDNA templates to the LoopReX-Opt system for the co-expression of CipB-streptavidin and CipB-LacZ. This approach enabled the de novo, autonomous formation of artificial nucleoids through the self-assembly of newly expressed CipB-streptavidin, CipB-LacZ, and the two ldsDNA templates (Fig. 5b). The resulting artificial nucleoid complex exhibited two key functions: CipB-streptavidin facilitated the attachment of the dual-biotin-modified ldsDNA template, while CipB-LacZ acted as an enzyme for bioconversion. The data showed that both dual-biotin-modified ldsDNA templates were successfully attached to CipB-streptavidin, which was encoded by one of the DNA templates (Fig. 5c). Moreover, after three rounds of self-growth, more CipB-LacZ was expressed and assembled, resulting in increasing catalytic activity for the conversion of o-nitrophenyl-β-D-galactopyranoside (ONPG) to o-nitrophenol (ONP) (Fig. 5d). Additionally, we successfully constructed a two-enzyme metabolic pathway by co-expressing StyA (the oxygenase subunit of styrene monooxygenase) and SpEH (epoxide hydrolase)27 with CipB-streptavidin, which self-assembled into artificial nucleoids and catalyzed the conversion of styrene into (S)-1-phenyl-1,2-ethanediol (Supplementary Fig. 14). These results collectively demonstrate that our LoopReX-Opt system is robust for the de novo, autonomous formation of artificial nucleoids, where multiple biological processes—such as DNA replication, protein expression, protein assembly, DNA attachment, and enzymatic catalysis (metabolism)—occur at different tempo-spatial scales. Remarkably, these essential cellular processes can be triggered in a single, one-pot LoopReX-Opt reaction with DNA as the sole user-supplied input.

Discussion

In this study, we introduced LoopReX by reconstituting TTcDR within complex in vitro biological systems, derived from a crude extract-based cell-free platform. Using phi29DNAP and T7RNAP, we established a minimal yet effective set of machinery for in vitro DNA replication. A key aspect of our work was elucidating the synergetic interaction between phi29DNAP and T7RNAP in the initiation of DNA replication. We further designed an artificial replication origin based on the T7 promoter, which enhanced the efficiency of TTcDR processes. Subsequently, we optimized the system through machine learning, resulting in LoopReX-Opt, which significantly improves the flow of genetic information, offering scalability and sustainability for future applications. Additionally, we demonstrated the autonomous formation of artificial nucleoids via CipB-based compartmentalization, supporting the self-sustained execution of multiple biological processes within LoopReX-Opt.

Our work verifies that T7RNAP synthesizes primers essential for the initiation of DNA replication. These primers are either abortive transcription products, a mechanism similar to some natural RNA polymerases that also participate in DNA replication54–56, or the short RNA fragments derived from full-length mRNA that have not been reported to our knowledge. It is hypothesized that the use of abortive transcription products as primers for DNA replication may have played a critical evolutionary role57, bridging the RNA world to the DNA world. In this context, our findings suggest that T7RNAP retains this primitive function, as it can generate both primers and mRNA from a primary promoter41,42,57. Inspired by the structure of the origin (consisting of tandem φ1.1 A and φ1.1B) in the T7 bacteriophage genome, we successfully constructed an artificial replication origin using tandem repeats of the 3*φ10 promoters (Fig. 1i), which could enhance the efficiency of both DNA replication and transcription-translation processes.

To optimize LoopReX-Ori, we employed machine learning, creating LoopReX-Opt, a system that maximizes the efficiency of DNA replication and protein expression simultaneously (Fig. 2 and Supplementary Table 1). A critical optimization involved the addition of deoxynucleotide triphosphates (dNTPs), which are absent in traditional cell-free expression systems but essential for DNA replication. Our findings show that the addition of optimized dNTP concentrations to the LoopReX-Ori system significantly enhanced DNA replication (Fig. 2g and Supplementary Fig. 15a), while the protein expression plateaued earlier (3 h), yet with a low yield (Fig. 2h and Supplementary Fig. 15b), likely due to the differential effects of dNTPs on DNA replication and transcription-translation. Notably, LoopReX-Opt showed remarkable tolerance to higher dNTP concentrations in the replication process (Supplementary Fig. 16), a result consistent with previous studies on in vitro systems12. This tolerance may be attributed to the optimized formulation, where multiple components work synergistically to maintain system stability.

One of the major strengths of LoopReX is its ability to sustain iterative genetic processes in a scalable, robust manner, enabling high-yield protein production, which is important for the development of artificial life systems58,59. This is a notable advancement over existing systems, such as the PURE system, which face limitations in maintaining efficient, long-term genetic iteration4,11. In our system, LoopReX maintains high protein expression performance across multiple cycles and ensures stability throughout the process, addressing potential key limitations that have hampered the development of artificial life systems. As the field progresses, the iterative scale-up of LoopReX presents great promise for further enhancing protein production capacities60. Additional advantages of LoopReX include significant time savings and cost-effectiveness compared to in vivo protein production. Unlike in vivo systems, LoopReX bypasses cloning and cell cultivation, enabling protein synthesis directly from DNA within hours. Furthermore, when paired with recent machine learning-optimized cell-free formulations that further reduce cost47, LoopReX will provide a rapid, flexible, and scalable platform well-suited for prototyping and high-throughput applications.

In addition, LoopReX incorporates artificial nucleoids, distinguishing it from conventional cell-free systems. In most existing systems, DNA is introduced in an unorganized, non-compartmentalized manner, which leads to less efficient processes and a lack of spatial organization. In contrast, the incorporation of a nucleoid-like structure in LoopReX mimics the organization of DNA observed in real cells. As such, DNA templates could be well protected from degradation (Supplementary Fig. 12), and importantly, the DNA replication efficiency was increased up to 7-fold (Fig. 4e) while LoopReX reactions without artificial nucleoids exhibited less than a 3-fold increase (Figs. 1 and 2). This spatial compartmentalization is beneficial for efficient DNA replication, transcription, and translation, and opens up possibilities for incorporating additional functionalities. For example, the artificial nucleoid can serve as a scaffold for metabolic enzymes, creating the potential for more sophisticated artificial cells that perform integrated biological processes.

Despite these advances, there are still challenges to address. While LoopReX achieves sustainable and iterative genetic flow, enhancing system stability and efficiency, particularly with increased complexity, remains a key area for improvement. The introduction of more complex cellular processes, such as energy metabolism or signaling pathways, could introduce new variables that impact system performance over time. Additionally, while LoopReX is based on E. coli crude extracts, future work could explore adapting the system to other cell types or organisms, which would require further optimization to fully harness the potential of diverse biological components.

Looking forward, LoopReX holds great potential for constructing fully integrated artificial life systems capable of performing interconnected cellular functions. This system lays the foundation for developing synthetic cells with increasingly complex behaviors, such as self-replication, evolution, and environmental adaptation. LoopReX expands the possibilities for biotechnological applications, ranging from the development of advanced therapeutics to the creation of bio-hybrid systems and synthetic organisms.

Methods

Bacterial strains and media

The E. coli DH5α strain was utilized for molecular cloning and plasmid propagation, while E. coli BL21 Star (DE3) and its derivatives were employed for in vivo protein expression and preparation of cell extracts. For cultivation, E. coli was grown in LB (Luria-Bertani) medium, composed of 10 g/L tryptone, 5 g/L yeast extract, and 10 g/L sodium chloride. For cell extract preparation, cells were cultured in 2xYTPG medium, which includes 10 g/L yeast extract, 16 g/L tryptone, 5 g/L sodium chloride, 7 g/L potassium hydrogen phosphate, 3 g/L potassium dihydrogen phosphate, and 18 g/L glucose, with a final pH of 7.2.

Construction of expression templates

Coding sequences (CDSs) of phi29DNAP and streptavidin were codon-optimized and chemically synthesized by GENEWIZ (Suzhou, China). The CDSs are listed in Supplementary Data 1. For purification of phi29DNAP, a histidine-tag (6* his tag) was inserted at C-terminus of the phi29DNAP sequences and cloned into pET-28a. The plasmid pJL1-T7-sfGFP (Addgene #69496) used as a template for DNA replication and protein expression in CFE and LoopReX systems. All gene fragments were amplified by polymerase chain reaction (PCR). Then, the fragments were purified using the FastPure Gel DNA Extraction Mini Kit and ligated with the corresponding backbones using the ClonExpress Ultra One Step Cloning Kit (all purchased from Vazyme, Nanjing, China). The ligated products were then transformed into E. coli DH5α competent cells and grown on LB-agar plates containing the appropriate antibiotic for 12 h. After colony PCR, the correct samples were selected for further cultivation and plasmid extraction. DNA sequences were verified by Sanger sequencing (GENEWIZ, Suzhou, China). Additionally, all ldsDNA was cloned based on the pJL1 backbone, with biotin and FAM-labeled primers synthesized by GENEWIZ (Suzhou, China). To investigate the impact of transcript products on DNA replication, the φ10 promotor of pJL1-T7-sfGFP was replaced by φ13, φ1.1B and φ1.3. For the design of the artificial origin, the 1-3* φ10 promotor was inserted in situ based on the pJL1-T7-sfGFP. The promotor sequences are listed in Supplementary Data 1. All primer sequences used in this study are provided in Supplementary Data 2. Plasmids constructed in this work are listed in Supplementary Table 2.

In vivo protein expression and purification of phi29 DNA polymerase

E. coli BL21 Star (DE3) harboring the related plasmid was cultivated for protein expression. Seed culture was grown overnight in LB medium. Then, cells were used to inoculate 1 L of fresh LB medium with an initial OD600 of 0.05. When OD600 reached 0.6–0.8, cells were induced with 0.5 mM IPTG, followed by overnight cultivation at 220 rpm and 16 °C. Then, cells were harvested, and the pellets were washed twice with lysis buffer (40 mM Tris, 500 mM NaCl, and 10% glycerol, pH 8.0). After centrifugation at 5000 × g and 4 °C for 10 min, cell pellets were resuspended with lysis buffer and lysed by sonication (50% amplitude, 10 s on/off for a total of 40 min). The supernatant was collected by centrifugation at 20,000 × g and 4 °C for 30 min. The supernatant was collected and repeated once centrifugation. After collection, the supernatant was filtered using a 0.45 μm filter membrane and loaded onto a Ni Sepharose 6 Fast Flow column (Cytiva) for purification. Then, the column was washed five times with the lysis buffer containing gradient imidazole concentrations (0, 10, and 30 mM, respectively). After the final wash, the target proteins were eluted with elution buffer (40 mM Tris, 500 mM NaCl, 100 mM imidazole, and 10% glycerol, pH 8.0). Afterward, the eluted samples were desalted with the desalting buffer (40 mM Tris, 500 mM NaCl, and 10% glycerol, pH 8.0) using Amicon Ultra-15 centrifugal filters (10 kDa cutoff, Merck) by centrifugation at 5000 × g and 4 °C. The concentrations of purified proteins were determined using a Quick Start Bradford Protein Assay kit (Bio-Rad). The purified products were stored at –80  °C until use.

Replication assay with single-stranded M13mp18 DNA in reaction buffer

For DNA replication assay based on phi29DNAP, single-stranded M13mp18 DNA (ssM13 DNA, NEB) was hybridized with a 17-mer oligonucleotide primer. The incubation mixture (20 μL) contained 50 mM Tris-HCl, 10 mM magnesium chloride, 10 mM ammonium sulfate, 4 mM dithiothreitol (DTT), 0.2 mg/mL recombinant bovine serum albumin, 0.3 mM dNTPs, 5 nM ssM13 DNA, 25 nM oligonucleotide primer (Supplementary Data 2), and phi29DNAP. The effect of different phi29DNAP concentrations on DNA replication was examined in this experiment. ssM13 DNA and primers were preincubated at 65 °C for 2 min and subsequently cooled to 25 °C. After incubation for the indicated times at 30 °C, samples were analyzed by electrophoresis in 1.0% agarose gels alongside DNA markers (Trans2K® Plus II DNA Marker) and by real-time quantitative PCR. The results showed that adding 10 μM phi29DNAP to the reaction generated large-molecular-weight rolled-circle concatemer products (Supplementary Figs. 1 and 2). Therefore, subsequent experiments were performed with the concentration of 10 μM phi29DNAP.

Gel analysis of DNA replication products

DNA replication samples were separated by agarose gel electrophoresis in 1× TAE buffer (Tris-Acetate-EDTA) and stained with YeaRed Nucleic Acid Gel Stain (Yeasen, Shanghai, China) to visualize the replication products. A fraction of the total product remained in the gel pockets, likely due to the large size of some rolling-circle concatemers and/or the potential formation of MgPPi-DNA nanoparticles7.

Cell extract preparation

Cell growth, collection, and extracts were prepared as described previously34,35. In brief, all E. coli strains were cultured in 2x YTPG medium. Each 1 L culture was inoculated with an overnight preculture at an initial OD600 of 0.05. When the OD600 reached 0.6–0.8, 1 mM IPTG was added to induce T7 RNA polymerase expression, and cells were harvested at an OD600 of 3.0. The cells were washed three times with cold S30 Buffer (10 mM Tris-acetate, 14 mM magnesium acetate, and 60 mM potassium acetate). After the final wash and centrifugation, the cell pellet was resuspended in S30 Buffer (1 mL/g wet cell mass) and lysed by sonication (10 s on/off, 50% amplitude, input energy ~600 J). The lysate was then centrifuged twice at 12,000 × g for 10 min at 4 °C. The supernatant was flash-frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at −80 °C until use.

Cell extract-based in vitro DNA replication and gene expression reaction

CFE reactions for DNA replication and gene expression were performed in 1.5 mL microcentrifuge tubes. A standard reaction (15 μL) contained the following components: 12 mM magnesium glutamate, 10 mM ammonium glutamate, 130 mM potassium glutamate, 2.64 mM ATP, 1.89 mM each of GTP, UTP, and CTP, 74.8 μg/mL folinic acid, 375.3 μg/mL of E. coli tRNA mixture, 2 mM each of 20 standard amino acids, 0.4 mM nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD), 0.27 mM coenzyme A (CoA), 1.5 mM spermidine, 1 mM putrescine, 4 mM sodium oxalate, 33 mM phosphoenolpyruvate, 0.3 mM dNTPs, 1 nM pJL1-T7-sfGFP or 5 nM ssM13 and 25 nM oligonucleotide primer, and 27% (v/v) of cell extract. For the LoopReX reaction, an additional 10 μM phi29DNAP is required. All reactions were incubated at 30 °C before further analysis of the synthesized DNA and protein. To verify the relationship between DNA replication efficiency and T7RNAP, 27% (v/v) of cell extract without IPTG induction and 20-40 μM T7RNAP are required.

Relative DNA quantification by qPCR

Fold changes of DNA copy-number relative to input levels (t = 0) were measured by qPCR (ChamQ SYBR Color qPCR Master Mix, Vazyme) in a two-step Real-Time PCR method (CFX96TMReal-Time System, Bio-Rad). For each time point, three individual samples were taken and diluted to the appropriate fold for DNA template in ddH2O, which were further diluted 1:20 in the final qPCR reaction. In order to reduce the adverse effect of the random error of the experimenter’s operation on the machine learning of a large amount of data, the dilution process and qPCR sample loading process were completed by an automated liquid handling workstation (Agilent BRAVO liquid handler). The specific primers for each target amplicon are listed in Supplementary Data 2. The fold change is defined as DNA content at that time/DNA input, where “DNA content at that time” and “DNA input” were both detected by qPCR in each independent experiment. The qPCR standard curves involved in this experiment are listed in Supplementary Figs. 17–23.

Protein expression assay

In this experiment, the expression of sfGFP was uniformly used to represent the protein expression level. After cell-free reactions, the fluorescence of sfGFP was measured using a microplate reader (Synergy H1, BioTek). Briefly, 2 μL of the reaction sample was mixed with 48 μL of nuclease-free water and placed in a 96-well plate with a flat bottom. Then, measurements of the sfGFP fluorescence were performed with excitation and emission wavelengths at 485 and 528 nm, respectively. Finally, the fluorescence of sfGFP was converted to concentration (μg/mL) or total yield (μg or μg/nM) according to a linear standard curve listed in Supplementary Fig. 24.

Factor selection and data processing

The essential functions of CFE systems are derived from cell extracts. To sustain high transcription and translation activity outside the cellular environment, traditional cell-free reactions typically incorporate over 50 supplementary components. These components have been shown to influence DNA replication and protein expression through various mechanisms7,9,12. Additionally, within the complex milieu of cell extracts, interactions among these factors are expected. Therefore, we collected data on protein expression and DNA replication in our laboratory involving 16 factors in the standard protocol of CFE, including magnesium glutamate, ammonium glutamate, potassium glutamate, ATP, GTP, UTP, CTP, folinic acid, E. coli tRNA mixture, NAD, CoA, spermidine, putrescine, sodium oxalate, dNTPs, and DTT. To reduce noise and errors in the experimental data and improve the accuracy and reliability of the analysis, the Robustness Analysis method (Coefficient of Variation, CV) is used. 126 sets of effective data related to protein expression and 115 sets of effective data related to DNA replication were obtained, respectively.

Furthermore, for parallel experiments in the collected data, Z-score filtering was applied to detect and remove outliers from the experimental data. A threshold of Z = 1.5 was set, and data points with Z-scores exceeding 1.5 or less than –1.5 were filtered out. The step of computing the Z-score for each data point is as follows (Eq. 1):1 Z=X−μσ

where Z is the Z-score of data X, μ is the mean of the data point, and σ is the standard deviation. Then, to make the data more suitable for subsequent analysis, the filtered data was standardized using the Z-score normalization method, transforming the data to a distribution with a mean of 0 and a standard deviation of 3. To meet specific experimental requirements, the standardized data were further corrected to a range of 0–6 to ensure all data points are positive. Since DNA replication and protein expression were theoretically positive results, they were further corrected to a range of 0–6 to ensure that all data points were positive values for subsequent model construction.

Model development

After data processing, we utilized eXtreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost), an open-source library that efficiently implements the gradient boosting framework, to build the protein expression predictive model and the DNA replication predictive model, respectively. Gradient boosting reduces prediction error using a gradient descent algorithm and creates a model composed of multiple weak prediction models, specifically decision trees in this context. During training, new regression trees were added sequentially to minimize residuals (the difference between predicted and actual values), while existing trees remained unchanged to help prevent overfitting. The outputs of new trees were combined with those of existing trees until the loss was minimized to a certain threshold or the specified limit on the number of trees was reached. In this study, an XGBoost regressor, implemented through Python code, was trained for each predictor and for the full set of data to learn and understand how many concentrations of factors can influence the efficiency of protein expression and DNA replication. All the data was divided at random into training and test sets with a ratio of 8:2. Only the training set was applied in model training and hyperparameter tuning. To improve the robustness of the model, five-fold cross-validation was used to optimize the model hyperparameter using grid search over the range of possible hyperparameter values. The test set was reserved for testing the performance of the models. Prediction performance was measured using R2; the higher the value of R2, the more accurate the model.

In this study, we employed machine learning to model DNA replication and protein expression within the LoopReX system, allowing us to identify conditions that optimize both processes simultaneously. Specifically, to find the best conditions for improving protein expression and DNA replication efficiency, we traversed millions of combinations of conditions across the data range. This exhaustive search was designed to rapidly determine the conditions that maximize protein expression and DNA replication efficiency.

Iterative in vitro DNA replication and gene expression

To validate the DNA iterative replication capacity of the LoopReX-Opt system, we prepared a reaction using the F3 formulation according to the standard CFE protocol (15 μL, named standard LoopReX-Opt protocol), incubating it at 30 °C for 1 h as the seed solution. Subsequently, 5 μL of the seed solution was transferred into a fresh standard LoopReX-Opt reaction (15 μL), bringing the total volume to 20 μL, and incubated again at 30 °C for 1 h for the first iteration. This process was repeated, and DNA replication capacity was sustained through the fourth iteration.

For saturated iterative reactions, the entire reaction mixture from the previous round was used as the seed and combined with the fresh reaction mix for the next round, following the ratio of 1:3 (seed/fresh reaction). Since LoopReX reaches peak protein expression and maintains high DNA levels within 3 h, the iteration time was set at 3 h. Briefly, after a 15 μL standard LoopReX reaction incubated at 30 °C for 3 h, three aliquots of 5 μL were transferred into three fresh 15 μL reactions to begin the first iteration. Starting from this first round, each reaction has a total volume of 20 μL, allowing one 20 μL seed reaction to generate four subsequent reactions. In the second iteration, this process can expand into 12 reactions, and so on. The number of iterations (n) and reaction number (N) follows the formula (Eq. 2):2 N=3*4(n−1)

For volume-scaled-up iterative reactions, we adjusted the overall experimental design to align with cell culture protocols, leading to significant differences compared to saturated iterations. Preparation and recovery of artificial nucleoids

Except for the de novo synthesis strategy of artificial nucleoids within the LoopReX system, other related experiments used pre-prepared artificial nucleoids. To prepare artificial nucleoids, the structural monomers (CipB-streptavidin) were expressed in 200 μL of CFE system containing the expression plasmids (pJL1-φ10-cipB-streptavidin), which were incubated at 30 °C and 600 rpm in shaker for 10 h. At the end of the reaction, artificial nucleoids were harvested by centrifugation at 20,000 × g for 10 min at 4 °C. The nucleoid pellets were first washed and resuspended in PBS buffer (containing 1 mM EDTA, 1% Triton X-100, pH 7.4) at a 10-fold sample volume, followed by two additional washes and resuspensions with PBS buffer (pH 7.4) at the same 10-fold sample volume. The purified artificial nucleoids were then collected by centrifugation at 20,000 × g for 10 min at 4 °C and resuspended in nuclease-free water.

For concentration determination, the washed samples were dissolved in 2 M urea, and the protein concentration was measured using a UV-Vis micro-spectrophotometer (Nano-300, Hangzhou Allsheng Instruments, China) with absorbance at 280 nm (A280). Protein concentrations (mg/mL) were calculated by dividing the A280 value by the extinction coefficient. Next, the artificial nucleoid was mixed with biotin-labeled ldsDNA at an appropriate ratio, followed by incubation in DNA loading buffer (10 mM Tris-HCl, 1 mM EDTA, 0.1 M NaCl, 0.1 mM DTT, 10 μg/mL BSA, and 0.5% glycerol) at 25 °C for 30 min. After incubation, the mixture was washed twice with PBS (pH 7.4) buffer at a 10-fold sample volume. Finally, PBS was removed by centrifugation, and the resulting nucleoid pellet was resuspended with nuclease-free water to obtain the loading DNA artificial nucleoid.

In iterative reactions, artificial nucleoids (serving as the replication core) were collected after each cycle by centrifugation at 20,000 × g and 4 °C for 10 min. The nucleoid pellets were washed twice with PBS (pH 7.4) buffer at a 10-fold sample volume and then resuspended in nuclease-free water at an appropriate volume for the next round of reactions.

Transmission electron microscopy (TEM)

TEM images were acquired using a JEOL JEM-1400 Plus microscope at an operating voltage of 120 kV. Samples (10 μL of artificial nucleoids resuspended in nuclease-free water) were deposited on a carbon-coated copper grid (300 mesh), excess liquid was removed by filter paper after 1 min, and the grids were air-dried. Negative staining was performed with 2% uranyl acetate (10 μL, 30 s), followed by air-drying before imaging.

Catalysis assay of functionalized artificial nucleoids

We functionalized the artificial nucleoid by adding functional monomers (CipB-LacZ), with LacZ catalysis used to simulate the metabolic function of the artificial nucleoid. The enzymatic reactions were carried out in 1.5 mL tubes by adding ONPG as the substrate. After the de novo synthesis of the functionalized artificial nucleoid in the one-step reaction, the functionalized artificial nucleoid was harvested and washed. The functionalized artificial nucleoid was resuspended in 10 μL of nuclease-free water and transferred to 190 μL of reaction mixture containing 10 mM ONPG and 1 mM MgSO4 at 25 °C for 1 min. Then, 200 μL of termination buffer (1 M Na2CO3 and 0.1 M EDTA) was added to stop the reaction. The absorbance of the product ONP in the reaction mixture was measured using a microplate reader (Synergy H1, BioTek) at 420 nm. The absorbance of ONP was converted to concentration (mM) according to a linear standard curve listed in Supplementary Fig. 25. The conversion was calculated using the following formula (Eq. 5):5 Conversion=CafterCbefore×100%

where Cafter represents the concentration of ONP and Cbefore represents the initial concentration of ONPG added to the reaction. Comparison of enzymatic activities and structural predictions of CipB-LacZ versus LacZ is provided in Supplementary Figs. 26 and 27, respectively.

Statistical analysis

All experiments were performed in biological triplicates, unless otherwise specified. Data are presented as means, with error bars representing the standard deviation from three independent experiments (n = 3). Statistical analyses were conducted using two-tailed Student’s t tests. Statistical significance is denoted as follows: p < 0.01 (**), p < 0.001 (***), and p < 0.0001 (****); exact p-values are provided in the corresponding figure legends.
