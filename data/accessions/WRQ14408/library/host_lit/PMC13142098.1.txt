Abstract

Zinc finger proteins are essential for the proper functioning of eukaryotic organisms, while their artificial variants can be used in cancer therapy or gene editing via their specific DNA recognition. Zn(II) binding is indispensable for the structural integrity of zinc fingers. While metal ion binding of zinc fingers has been extensively investigated in vitro, intracellular studies are rather scarce. Herein, we utilized the novel I‐Block assay, based on the regulation of β‐galactosidase expression via the transcription inhibition ability of zinc finger proteins inside the modified ER1821ΔLacI Escherichia coli cells. The results indicated that the performance of the assay largely depends on the position of the DNA sequence targeted by the zinc finger protein, but once optimized, specific DNA binding of even the rather small three‐membered 1MEY# artificial zinc finger protein can be detected without large fusion tags. By fusion of 1MEY# with the nuclease domain of FokI restriction endonuclease leading to an artificial zinc finger nuclease, we demonstrated that the I‐Block system can distinguish between DNA binding and DNA cleavage inside the cells allowing for zinc finger nuclease screening. The investigation of metal ion/metalloid–zinc finger protein interactions within cellular conditions provided direct proof for the inhibitory effect of Cd(II), Hg(II), and As(III) on the DNA binding of a zinc finger protein at a molecular level.

1 INTRODUCTION

Zinc finger (ZF) motifs form an ordered secondary structure upon Zn(II) coordination (Kellis et al., 1988). By combining multiple Zn(II)‐bound ZF motifs into one sequence, the obtained full zinc finger proteins (ZFPs) can recognize macromolecules, for example, DNA, RNA and proteins (Bulaj et al., 1998; Frankel et al., 1987; Krishna et al., 2003; Lee & Lim, 2011; Maynard & Covell, 2001; Quintal et al., 2011; Smith et al., 2005). Thus, they play important roles in transcription, translation, zinc sensing, mRNA trafficking, and other processes in all eukaryotes and some prokaryotes (Malgieri et al., 2015). The largest and first discovered group is the Cys2His2 ZFPs, containing two cysteines and two histidines in each ZF motif that coordinate to the central Zn(II) in a tetrahedral geometry and thereby form the characteristic ββα secondary structure (Frankel et al., 1987). Metal ion interaction with zinc fingers was therefore, widely studied. Numerous affinity data are available for single Cys2His2‐type ZF motif peptides (with the focus on Zn(II) binding) (Hartwig et al., 2002; Quintal et al., 2011; Shi et al., 1993; Witkiewicz‐Kucharczyk & Bal, 2006), but much less quantitative results are published for full ZFPs (Bulathge et al., 2022; Guerrerio & Berg, 2004; Kluska et al., 2018; Zhao et al., 2012). Besides other aspects, such information is also important to understand the effect of other (potentially toxic) metal(loid) ions through their competition with Zn(II) for ZF metal binding sites (Hajdu, Hunyadi‐Gulyás, & Gyurcsik, 2023; Hartwig, 2001; Heinz et al., 2009; Huang et al., 2004; Makowski & Sunderman Jr., 1992; Sivo et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2003).

A Zn(II)‐bound Cys2His2 ZF subunit within a ZFP interacts with three subsequent nucleobases of a DNA molecule. High binding affinity and sequence specificity are achieved by combining several subunits (Dhanasekaran et al., 2007; Elrod‐Erickson et al., 1998). The quantitative evaluation of the DNA binding affinity of natural and artificial ZFPs may indicate whether a ZFP can function in vivo, but the available literature data show great variation (with apparent binding constant (logβ′) values between ca. 6 and 11) (Table S1). The logβ pH 7.4 values published for, for example, the Zif268–DNA interaction span a wide range from 7.5 to 11.1 in vitro (Hurt et al., 2003; Liu et al., 1997). This ambiguity may be related to variations in the measurement conditions and the techniques applied (Table S1). Previously, we have investigated an artificial ZFP (1MEY# protein (Belczyk‐Ciesielska et al., 2018)) consisting of three Cys2His2‐type ZFs based on the consensus peptide CP1 (Kim & Berg, 1996; Krizek et al., 1991). Thus, 1MEY# protein can be considered a general ZFP representative. An apparent stability constant logβ pH 7.4 = 8.20 ± 0.08 was determined for the specific DNA binding of Zn(II)‐saturated 1MEY#, while only a ~2 orders of magnitude lower value (logβ pH 7.4 = 6.27 ± 0.02) was found for the binding affinity to nonspecific DNA (Hajdu, Hunyadi‐Gulyás, Kato, et al., 2023). This would imply that no specific protein‐DNA interaction occurs below ~10 pM DNA concentration, taking into account the possible nonspecific interactions (Figures 1, S1). This raises the question of whether the specific DNA binding of a CP1‐based artificial ZFP, just as 1MEY#, is strong enough to fulfill its function inside the cells. Data about the DNA‐binding of ZFPs in cellular environments are scarce (Cornu et al., 2008; Durai et al., 2006; Hurt et al., 2003; Sander et al., 2009; Zhang et al., 2000). Several groups observed correlations between the affinity values determined by electrophoretic mobility shift assay and by bacterial or mammalian intracellular assays (Cornu et al., 2008; Durai et al., 2006; Hurt et al., 2003; Sander et al., 2009), but counterexamples are also found in the literature (Cornu et al., 2008; Zhang et al., 2000). Therefore, the relationship between the DNA‐binding affinity of a ZFP and its cellular function may have a much more general significance than the DNA binding affinity of 1MEY# alone.

ZFP‐s may also be converted into artificial zinc finger nucleases (ZFNs) assembled from a ZFP unit, responsible for the specific DNA recognition, and a nuclease domain for DNA cleavage (Katayama et al., 2024). Although CRISPR‐Cas9 is the most popular gene editing tool nowadays, recent researches on artificial ZFNs in gene modification experiments, or in cancer treatment related research (Cassandri et al., 2017; Gaj et al., 2014; Kim et al., 1996; Ousterout et al., 2015; Passananti et al., 2010; Petolino, 2015; Segal & Meckler, 2013; Urnov et al., 2005; Urnov et al., 2010) increased the interest in ZFNs again. This might be attributed to their compatibility with eukaryotic cells, for example, their small sizes allowing for easier delivery and a weaker chance to be recognized by the immune system. Furthermore, numerous ZFN patents expired around 2020, which opened up the possibility to employ ZFNs in academic research, as well (Malgieri et al., 2015). The precise DNA recognition in cells is a crucial aspect of artificial ZFNs. This inspired us to investigate the interaction of 1MEY# with DNA inside the cell with an aim to fill the gap between the in vitro affinity data and the in vivo functionality of ZFPs.

Several cellular assays have been tested so far. The popular CUT&Tag type assays, although performing well in regular recognition site identification experiments (Gosztyla et al., 2024), do not provide information on relative affinities. In addition, these assays may not detect weaker interactions and require a specific antibody that recognizes the protein under investigation (Fu et al., 2023). One‐ or two‐hybrid assays are also commonly used for such in‐cell studies (Najafabadi et al., 2015), but they require the fusion of additional protein domains to the proteins of interest, for example, VP16 (Zhang et al., 2000), NF‐kB p65 (Hurt et al., 2003; Morisaki et al., 2008), Gal11P (Cornu et al., 2008; Sander et al., 2009), or RpoA (RNA polymerase) (Durai et al., 2006). These protein tags are relatively large (~30–100 kDa) compared to a three‐subunit ZFP (~11 kDa) and may significantly influence the DNA binding affinity of the ZFP (Zhou et al., 2008).

To avoid these risks, we have utilized the novel Escherichia coli‐based I‐Block detection method (Szentes et al., 2020) for studying the DNA binding of 1MEY# ZFP without additional large fusion tags under various conditions, such as in the presence of competitive metal(loid) ions inside the cells. Numerous studies have already shown that the eukaryotic ZFPs can be expressed, bind Zn(II), fold into their native secondary structure (Isalan et al., 2001; Liang et al., 2025), and establish protein–DNA interactions (Joung et al., 2000; Thibodeau‐Beganny & Keith, 2007) inside E. coli, just like in eukaryotic cells. In the I‐Block assay, expression of β‐galactosidase and thus, the readout is regulated by the investigated ZF protein upon binding to its target DNA sequence (see Scheme S1). We examined the DNA binding ability of 1MEY# ZFP and the effect of Cd(II), As(III), Hg(II), Ag(I), and Pb(II) on this interaction in cells, as well as the ability to distinguish the ZFP–DNA binding from the cleavage of DNA by an 1MEY#‐based ZFN in the I‐Block assay.

2 RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

2.1 Analysis of ZFP–DNA interactions inside the cell

The I‐Block system requires two plasmids within the same cell, one carrying the gene of the investigated DNA binding protein and the other carrying its DNA recognition sequence in the promoter region of the operon including the gene of the lac repressor (a protein that inhibits β‐galactosidase expression).

1MEY# is a modified version of a three‐subunit ZFP protein with a published crystal structure deposited in the RCSB Protein Data Bank under the 1MEY PDB code (Kim & Berg, 1996). The 1MEY sequence was selected for this study because the DNA recognition features of the protein have already been well characterized in vitro. 1MEY interacts with 5′‐G(A/G)G(G/T)C(A/G)GAA‐3′ DNA sequences, but based on cocrystallization experiments the 5′‐GAGGCAGAA‐3′ DNA sequence was assigned as the main target. In the variant protein applied here (1MEY#), a His was replaced with Asn (H87N) and a Ser with Val (S89V) in the 3rd ZF subunit of the ZFP, which did not affect the DNA binding specificity, as described in our previous work (Belczyk‐Ciesielska et al., 2018). The genes of the 1MEY# protein with and without an N‐terminal decahistidine‐tag were incorporated into the L‐arabinose‐inducible pBAD24 expression vector, and the new DNA constructs were designated as pB‐H‐1MEY# and pB‐1MEY#, respectively (Table S3).

The 5′‐GAGGCAGAA‐3′ DNA recognition sequence of 1MEY# ZFP was incorporated into the promoter region of the pdUA‐LacI vector as described in the SI (S1.5). This procedure was optimized to avoid any effect of the inserted DNA sequence on the expression level of LacI and thus, indirectly of β‐galactosidase. Details of these steps leading to the final functional recognition site vector variant, designated as pdUA‐LacINE‐T3e, are also described in the SI (S2.1).

The 6ZA ZFP, consisting of 6 ZF subunits and showing a high sequence similarity to 1MEY# (Table S2), served as a positive control for DNA binding in our experiments, which had already been tested during the development of the I‐Block method (Szentes et al., 2020). The 6ZA ZFP provided an opportunity to monitor the effect of the decrease of the number of ZF subunits on the I‐Block readout applying a pdUA DNA with only one ZFP recognition site. As discussed in the SI Sections S2.2 and S2.3, the only efficient DNA binding and thereby, β‐galactosidase activity increase, was observed for the original 6ZA and its 5 membered N‐ or C‐ terminally truncated mutants. The 4 and 3 membered 6ZA derivatives could not be efficiently expressed, and their effect could not be detected in the I‐Block system.

In our work 6 mM L‐arabinose was used as an inducer, but the arabinose concentration dependence measurements revealed that β‐galactosidase activity increase was detectable even at 6 μM L‐arabinose level, so overexpression of the ZFPs is not necessary (Figure S12).

While the H‐1MEY# protein could be desirably expressed, it was observed that a single DNA target sequence did not allow for the detection of the ZFP–DNA interaction in the I‐Block assay. Therefore, we have designed various ZFP DNA recognition plasmids (SI Sections S2.1 and S2.4) changing the location, orientation and the number of 1MEY# target sequences. Altogether 25 different pdUA‐LacI recognition site plasmids were constructed (Figure 2), which resulted in significantly different I‐Block results. The measured relative β‐galactosidase activities for each construct are collected in Figure 2. Relative β‐galactosidase activity shows how much activity a given sample has compared to the positive control (only ZFP gene inside the cells) (SI, S1.8). Ideally, high values are expected for the cells transformed with both plasmids, including the target sequence and the ZFP gene, in the presence of 6 mM L‐arabinose inducer. Analysis of the data revealed that the pdUA‐LacI DNAs carrying a single 1MEY# recognition site did not function properly in the I‐Block assay, that is, the expression of LacI was not inhibited via interaction with 1MEY#. Among the DNA carriers with two copies of inserted 1MEY# recognition sites, relative β‐galactosidase activity was observed with pdUA‐LacINE‐R3a (~ 33%) and pdUA‐LacINE‐Z6a (~15%). Among the DNA carriers, pdUA‐LacINE‐T3e, containing three recognition sites positioned in all‐parallel orientation on the lagging strand, showed the optimal activity with the H‐1MEY# ZFP (~ 32%).

The above results clearly demonstrate that although 1MEY# is an artificial ZFP, it can recognize its DNA target sequence inside the cells similarly to natural ZFPs (Table S1). This is in agreement with the classification established by Sanders et al., which would classify 1MEY# as a protein with strong DNA binding affinity (Sander et al., 2009). Nevertheless, great care must be taken when comparing different ZFPs on the basis of in vitro DNA binding ability as these values are often underestimated and the ZFP expression level is also a key factor inside the cells (see SI S2.5).

2.2 Differentiation between DNA binding and DNA cleavage

Besides the characterization of the intracellular DNA binding of the ZFP using the I‐Block assay, the possibility of distinction between DNA binding and DNA cleavage was also investigated. The importance of such studies is that most of the ZFP‐related research focuses on the use of the ZFNs for gene modification or cancer treatment, but the screening can be challenging (Cassandri et al., 2017; Gaj et al., 2014; Kim et al., 1996; Ousterout et al., 2015; Passananti et al., 2010; Petolino, 2015; Segal & Meckler, 2013; Urnov et al., 2005; Urnov et al., 2010). In this study, the FokI nuclease domain (NFokI) was fused to the C‐terminus of the H‐1MEY# ZFP, as described in the SI (S1.4). NFokI is often used in ZFNs, and it is already well characterized in the literature (Kim et al., 1996; Szczepek et al., 2007). NFokI functions as a dimer (Smith et al., 1999), thus there was a need for a special ZFP recognition plasmid displaying the ZFP sites properly oriented on the opposite DNA strands at a 6 bp distance. The orientation of the recognition sites inside the pdUA‐LacINE‐Z6a plasmid (Figure 2) perfectly fulfilled this requirement. Furthermore, the interaction of H‐1MEY# ZFP with this plasmid resulted in a weak (~15%) relative β‐galactosidase activity, making it a good choice for I‐Block experiments with the specific H‐1MEY#‐NFokI artificial nuclease.

The DNA carriers of the ZFN gene and the ZFP‐recognition site provide ampicillin and kanamycin resistance, respectively, to the cells in the I‐Block assay. Thus, cleavage of the recognition site inside the pdUA‐LacI vector could result in the loss of the kanamycin resistance of the cells, leading to an additional monitoring option. The results of these experiments are collected in Figure 3. The measured relative β‐galactosidase activity in the pdUA‐LacINE‐Z6a–H‐1MEY# system was ~20%–25% independently of the presence or absence of kanamycin antibiotic. The pdUA‐LacINE‐Z6a–H‐1MEY#‐NFokI system also revealed a ~25% relative β‐galactosidase activity in the presence of kanamycin; however, in the absence of the antibiotic, the activity increased over 70% (see Figure S2 for the same experiments at various reaction conditions). This can be explained by the cleavage of the pdUA‐LacINE‐Z6a plasmid DNA by the H‐1MEY#‐NFokI ZFN resulting in a gradual loss of the kanamycin resistance of the E. coli cells. Therefore, in the presence of the antibiotic, cells containing the digested pdUA‐LacINE‐Z6a gradually died based on the OD600 values. In contrast, they remained viable in the absence of the kanamycin, while their β‐galactosidase production increased significantly in the absence of the LacI inhibitory protein, since its expression is also prevented by the cleavage of pdUA‐LacINE‐Z6a (Scheme S1D). Furthermore, the H‐1MEY#‐NFokI nuclease cleaved its target sequence in a specific manner as revealed by control experiments (see SI, S2.6). A remarkable finding is that the obtained data clearly demonstrated the specific effect of the H‐1MEY#‐NFokI nuclease inside the cells; thus, the I‐Block assay could distinguish DNA binding and cleavage events.

2.3 Monitoring the ZFP–Metal(loid) ion interactions inside the cells

ZFPs can only recognize the DNA target sequences in their Zn(II)‐bound form (Kluska et al., 2018). However, several toxic metal(loid) ions, with a presumably high affinity for the metal binding sites of ZFP, may enter living organisms from the environment, compete with Zn(II) and thus might even cease the DNA binding function of the ZFPs (Hartwig, 2001). A large number of publications discuss the interaction of ZFPs with various toxic metal(loid) ions in vitro but to the best of our knowledge, it has been investigated very rarely to what extent the in vitro reactions correlate with the in‐cell tendency of these ions to interact with ZFPs (Hajdu, Hunyadi‐Gulyás, & Gyurcsik, 2023; Malgieri et al., 2015; Vergara‐Gerónimo et al., 2021; Witkiewicz‐Kucharczyk & Bal, 2006).

Recently, we monitored competition reactions of the 1MEY# ZFP with various metal ions, such as Ag(I), Cd(II), and Hg(II) (Hajdu, Hunyadi‐Gulyás, & Gyurcsik, 2023; Kluska et al., 2022). Here, we extend this project for investigating the effect of these toxic metal ions on the DNA‐binding of H‐1MEY# as well as 6ZA ZFPs inside the ER1821ΔLacI E. coli cells, but including also As(III) and Pb(II). Both the pdUA‐LacINE‐T3e–H‐1MEY# and pdUA‐LacIN‐6ZA–6ZA systems were investigated in the presence of increasing amounts of Cd(II), As(III), Hg(II), Ag(I) (Figure 4), and Pb(II) (Figure S3).

FIGURE 4 Relative β‐galactosidase activities upon interaction of H‐1MEY# ZFP with pdUA‐LacINE‐T3e DNA in the presence of increasing amounts of (a) Cd(II); (b) As(III); (C) Hg(II); (D) Ag(I). In „ + Protein” cells, the protein expression was induced by the addition of 6 mM L‐arabinose, while „– Protein” cells contained only the pdUA‐LacINE‐T3e DNA recognition plasmid. Metal salts were added to the growth media at the beginning of the protein induction, followed by an 8 h incubation at 30°C. The results represent the average of 3–6 individual measurements (N) with their standard deviations (SD).

Cd(II) induced a gradual decrease of the relative β‐galactosidase activity both in the H‐1MEY# and 6ZA ZFP‐based assays, although a rather high Cd(II) concentration was necessary to observe this effect (Figures 4a, S4). While at 250 μM Cd(II) concentration the relative β‐galactosidase activity decreased below half of the starting level, measured in the absence of Cd(II), the cell viability was almost unchanged, as reflected by the recorded OD600 values (Figure S4). At 500 μM Cd(II) the observed OD600 values dropped by ~30% while the relative β‐galactosidase activity of the samples was barely higher than the baseline (see Figure 4a, and Figure S4). At 750 μM Cd(II) the cells died almost completely, thus no reliable β‐galactosidase activity could be determined.

A possible explanation for these observations could be that at high Cd(II) excess Zn(II) is replaced by Cd(II) in the ZFPs inside the cells and the formed Cd(II)‐ZFP complex is not functional. This assumption would be in line with the report by Malgieri et al., describing the expression and purification of Ros87 ZFP in its Cd(II)‐bound form from E. coli cells cultured in the presence of 600 μM Cd(II) (Malgieri et al., 2014). However, our former in vitro results reflected that the Cd(II)‐bound 1MEY# has a very similar secondary structure to that of the Zn(II)‐complex being capable to bind its specific DNA target, albeit with a ~0.6 log unit lower stability (logβCdII−1MEY#−DNApH7.4 = 7.62 ± 0.04) compared to that of the Zn(II)‐complex (logβZnII−1MEY#−DNApH7.4 = 8.20 ± 0.08) (Hajdu, Hunyadi‐Gulyás, & Gyurcsik, 2023). It is unclear how this ~0.6 logβ unit stability difference could be responsible for the different cellular and in vitro behavior of the Cd(II)‐complex, therefore other alternative scenarios must be considered in the kinetically constrained cellular environment. Cd(II) is known to exert its toxic effects in alternative ways within the cells, which may result in a decrease in the relative β‐galactosidase activity. The presence of Cd(II) itself had no significant effect on the proteins of the I‐Block system (LacI, β‐galactosidase) in the tested concentration range (Figure S4) in the positive (only ZFP gene) and negative (only recognition plasmid) control experiments, and it did not attenuate significantly the expression of the ZFPs based on the SDS‐PAGE analysis of the cell extracts (Figure S17). Thus, Cd(II) must have an effect on the ZFPs, yet it should be different from the in vitro mechanism. Based on a hypothesis put forward in our previous manuscript, Cd(II) might coordinate to the cysteine sidechains of multiple ZF subunits of the ZFP, thereby disrupting the cooperativity of the ZF subunits. This may result in the collapse of the secondary structure and thus the loss of the DNA‐binding function (Hajdu, Hunyadi‐Gulyás, & Gyurcsik, 2023). Such an event may be assisted by the efficient capture of the released Zn(II) by another cellular component.

Nevertheless, it cannot be excluded that thiol groups may be engaged in other interactions within the cell, non‐functional mixed complexes may be formed, and cysteines may even be oxidized in the absence of the efficiently coordinated Zn(II) (Berkmen, 2012). The effect of Cd(II) seems to be complex. As the readout of the I‐Block assay might be the composite of cellular physiology, and potential effects of toxic metal ions on protein stability or expression levels, in addition to the ZFP–DNA binding regulation of LacI and β‐galactosidase expression. Therefore further comprehensive experiments are needed in the future, with protein profiling, and establishment of the Cd(II) distribution within the cells.

Only a few studies were previously aimed at the As(III)‐ZFP interaction, but without focusing on the DNA‐binding ability of the ZFP (Zhao et al., 2012). The sequences of these investigated ZFPs also differed significantly from the classical Cys2His2 CP1 model sequence. The poly(ADP‐ribose) polymerase‐1 (PARP‐1), a protein containing Cys3His1 ZF domains, was almost completely inhibited in HeLa cells at a 10 nM As(III) concentration (Hartwig et al., 2003), while 200 nM and 5 μM As(III) concentrations were necessary in HaCaT cells (Ding et al., 2009) and in human Molt‐3T lymphoblast cell line (Yager & Wiencke, 1997), respectively, for a complete inhibition of the polymerase enzyme. In contrast, an increasing PARP‐1 activity was observed in CHO‐K1 cells at higher As(III) concentrations (Lynn et al., 1998).

When the pdUA DNA with the ZFP target sequence was present, As(III) reduced the relative β‐galactosidase activity both with the H‐1MEY# and the 6ZA ZFPs (Figures 4b and S5) in our I‐Block experiments. Since the relative β‐galactosidase activities of both the negative control (only recognition plasmid) and the positive control (only ZFP gene) was found to increase gradually by the increasing As(III) concentration, the decrease of the effect of ZFPs was even more remarkable. However, a significant decrease both in cell viabilities and in the relative β‐galactosidase activities was only observed above 100 μM As(III). The expression profile of H‐1MEY# was not affected noticeably based on SDS‐PAGE by the addition of the As(III) in this concentration range (Figure S17). The difference between the relative β‐galactosidase activities of the negative control (only 1MEY# binding site) and the actual sample (1MEY# binding site + H‐1MEY#) almost completely ceased around 750–1000 μM As(III) concentration (Figure 4b). 6ZA ZFP with the same As(III) concentration still displayed β‐galactosidase activity to some extent, but the relative activity dropped down to ca. half of the initial value. This might be attributed to the difference in the DNA‐binding of a three and six‐membered ZFPs (Figure S5), but this requires further clarification in the future. Above 1 mM As(III) concentration the cells could not proliferate, making the I‐Block readout uncertain. The inhibition of DNA binding of Cys2His2 ZFPs by As(III) may be surprising since arsenous acid (the solution form of As(III)) has a ca. 7 orders of magnitude lower affinity toward Cys2His2 ZFPs in vitro as compared to Zn(II) (logβAsIIIpH7.4 = 5.24 ± 0.04; logβZnIIpH7.4 = 12.2 ± 0.1) (Hajdu, Hunyadi‐Gulyás, Kato, et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2012). However, Zhao et al. found that a reduced pH (<6) and the presence of small thiol ligands, such as glutathione or cysteine may enhance the competition between As(III) and Zn(II) in their binding to Cys2His2 ZFPs in favor of As(III)‐binding (Zhao et al., 2014). Furthermore, under cellular conditions methylation of inorganic As(III) can also take place and the resulting monomethylarsonous acid (MMA(III)) might be more tightly bound by Cys2His2 ZFPs (Vergara‐Gerónimo et al., 2021).

The effect of other thiophilic metal ions on the relative β‐galactosidase activity was also tested. Pb(II) had no significant influence either on the cell viability or on the relative β‐galactosidase activity in the investigated 0–100 μM concentration range (SI Figure S3). The presence of Hg(II) up to a 5 μM metal ion concentration did not cause any noticeable change either; however, 10 μM Hg(II) prevented the growth of E. coli cells (Figures 4c and S6), and as a consequence, the relative β‐galactosidase activity of the negative control sample (cells transformed with the pdUA DNA carrying the ZFP target sequence) practically vanished at this Hg(II) concentration. These cells were cultured in the presence of kanamycin, while ampicillin could partially rescue the cells due to the formation of Hg(II)‐ampicillin complexes, as previously suggested (Nafaee et al., 2023). The cells also carrying the pB‐ZFP plasmids, cultured in the presence of kanamycin and ampicillin, showed a gradual decrease in their viabilities and in the observed relative β‐galactosidase activities between 10 and 25 μM Hg(II) concentration. At higher metal ion concentrations, the cells were no longer able to grow and the activity values became uncertain or unmeasurable at and above 30 μM Hg(II) (Figure S6). The results revealed that Hg(II) might also be able to influence the DNA recognition of ZFPs under cellular conditions, which is completely consistent with the in vitro results, where Hg(II) quantitatively displaced zinc(II) from the metalation sites (Hajdu, Hunyadi‐Gulyás, & Gyurcsik, 2023). The effect of Hg(II)‐coordination to ZFPs on the DNA recognition might depend on the strength of the interaction between ZFPs and DNA, as well as on the cellular competition reactions. This is demonstrated by the almost completely abolished relative β‐galactosidase activity of the 3‐membered 1MEY# protein in the presence of 25 μM Hg(II), in contrast to data obtained for the 6‐membered 6ZA ZFP showing only a ca ~50% activity decrease at the same metal ion concentration (Figure S6).

Surprisingly, no decrease in the relative β‐galactosidase activity was detected for either 1MEY# or 6ZA in the presence of Ag(I) until the point where the cells were killed. Cell viabilities were almost unchanged up to a 0.5 μM Ag(I) concentration, followed by a notable decrease in the OD600 values (Figure S7). At 1.0 μM Ag(I) concentration, the OD600 values were found to drop to ca. half of their initial level while, over a 1.0 μM Ag(I) concentration, the cells died. It is worth noting that the ZFP expression levels and the relative β‐galactosidase activities remained nearly unchanged even up to 1.0 μM Ag(I) concentration (Figures 4d, S7, and S17). This is an unexpected observation, since our previous in vitro experiments revealed that Ag(I) (just like Hg(II)) could quantitatively replace Zn(II) in 1MEY# Cys2His2 ZFP and thus, prevent the DNA‐binding of the protein (Kluska et al., 2022). While Hg(II) was found to impede DNA‐recognition by ZFPs inside the cells, the lack of a similar effect of Ag(I) under the applied experimental conditions requires a more detailed study.

3 CONCLUSION

The interaction of a ZFP with DNA in the presence and absence of a fusion nuclease domain and metal(loid) ions was studied inside E. coli cells. The main achievements are as follows:Despite the moderately strong in vitro DNA binding affinity of the 1MEY# three‐finger ZFP (logβ pH 7.4 = 8.20 ± 0.08), we have demonstrated that the protein was able to recognize its DNA target sequence inside the cells. This finding may open new research directions and facilitates the comparability of data from publications studying naturally occurring and synthetic ZFPs. It was also shown that the promoter region of the pdUA‐LacI vector was sensitive to modifications inside the I‐Block system. A small ZFP may not be able to sufficiently inhibit the transcription of the LacI gene upon interacting with its DNA target sequence, but the present study revealed that this can be overcome by incorporating more recognition sites next to each other in an optimized arrangement. Although establishing the proper sensing arrangement requires extra efforts, follow up studies on natural and artificial ZFP‐based effectors (e.g., ZFPs, ZFNs or ZF‐transcription factors (Németh et al., 2018)) may benefit from these results.

Our I‐Block reporter system using LacI carrier plasmids with DNA recognition sites, providing only an incomplete response toward DNA binding (e.g., pdUA‐LacINE‐Z6a), allowed for distinguishing between DNA binding and DNA cleavage. An increased relative β‐galactosidase activity was observed during the digestion of these plasmids by a specific ZFN, perfectly inhibiting the transcription of the lac repressor gene. While this could be an advantageous feature in studying DNA‐binding, similarly to classical one‐, or two‐hybrid assays, we also confirmed for the first time that a simple E. coli‐based assay is applicable for the detection of DNA cleavage by artificial nucleases utilized exactly in the form they were designed (i.e., without fusion tags).

The presence of various toxic metal(loid) ions, such as Cd(II), As(III), Pb(II), Hg(II), and Ag(I), and their intracellular interactions with the 1MEY# and 6ZA ZFPs resulted in rather diverse consequences with regard to the DNA‐recognition efficiency of the ZFPs in the studied I‐Block system and in terms of cell viabilities. Hg(II) inhibited the β‐galactosidase activity in a ~10–30 μM concentration range, while Cd(II) and As(III) displayed a similar effect only at ca. one order of magnitude higher concentrations. The presence of Pb(II) did not affect either cell viabilities or the relative β‐galactosidase activity in the investigated concentration range. Finally, Ag(I) reduced cell viabilities while having no significant influence on the β‐galactosidase activity. While the I‐Block assay can only indirectly monitor protein‐DNA interactions (via the production and function of the LacI inhibitor and the β‐galactosidase enzyme), a careful use of this biochemical tool, even in the presence of exogenous substances, such as toxic metal(loid) ions, may provide valuable information. The appropriate positive and negative controls are essential to reveal the interaction of a (toxic) metal(loid) ion with one of the proteins responsible for the assay performance (Lac inhibitor, β‐galactosidase). The obtained results also depend on the degree to which a protein can be expressed under certain conditions, and the applied antibiotic can also significantly affect the assay performance, as exemplified by the Hg(II)‐containing samples. With all findings considered, the I‐Block system possesses a great potential to investigate the binding of (toxic) metal(loid) ions to ZFPs via monitoring their influence on the proteins under cellular conditions. This method can be extended to other metal ion‐sensitive transcription factors that could not be investigated so far by the classical one‐ or two‐hybrid assays. The I‐Block test system may be coupled with other methods in the future, providing an even broader perspective for intracellular metal ion binding studies.

4 MATERIALS AND METHODS

4.1 Bacterial strains and plasmids

E. coli DH5α strain was used for plasmid DNA replication and the modified ER1821ΔlacI strain lacking the lacI gene was used in the I‐Block experiments. pdUA‐lacIN and pdUA‐lacINE (KanR) low copy cloning vectors were modified to carry the target DNA sequence of the ZFP, while the gene of the studied ZFP was inserted into a pBAD24 (AmpR) vector (Guzman et al., 1995). Both the I‐Block bacterial strain and the parent cloning vectors were constructed and kindly provided by the research group of Prof. Antal Kiss (Szentes et al., 2020). Details related to gene constructions can be found in SI (S1.2).

4.2 I‐block assay

In I‐Block assay, the binding of the 1MEY# ZFP to its target DNA sequence is expected to downregulate the expression of the LacI protein that would otherwise inhibit the expression of the β‐galactosidase reporter protein. The specific DNA binding event is thus detected by measuring the β‐galactosidase activity in the hydrolysis of galactose derivatives (Szentes et al., 2020). Experiments were performed based on (Scheme S2).

In solution experiments, I‐Block cells were grown in standard LB media (supplied with the appropriate antibiotics as described above) in test tubes or in 96 well, PS, F‐bottom, clear microplates (Greiner Bio‐One or Iwaki) at 37°C with 150–200 rpm shaking until they reached OD600~0.3 (Scheme S2). Then a portion of each cell suspension (typically between 2 and 20 μL) was transferred into a new microplate or test tube with fresh LB media, depending on the measured OD600, in order to obtain approximately equal numbers of cells. The protein expression was induced by 6 mM arabinose, while 6 mM glucose was added for the controls, then the cultures were incubated further for 8 h at 30°C. The OD600 value was recorded for each sample with a CLARIOstar® Plus (BMG Labtech) or an Infinite M1000 Pro (Tecan) microplate reader. Then 5–10 μL aliquots were transferred into a new 96 well clear microplate, and 45 μL permeabilization solution (100 mM Na2HPO4, 20 mM KCl, 2 mM MgSO4, 0.8 mg/mL CTAB, 0.4 mg/mL sodium deoxycholate and 5.4 μL/mL β‐mercaptoethanol) was added to each well. The plate was covered and incubated for 30 min at 30°C by shaking with 200 rpm. Determination of the β‐galactosidase activity was started by the addition of 300 μL substrate solution (1 mg/mL ONPG, 60 mM Na2HPO4, 40 mM NaH2PO4 and 2.7 μL/mL β‐mercaptoethanol). After homogenizing the samples, the absorbance was recorded at 420 and 550 nm, respectively, every 5 min during a 1 h incubation period at 30°C using a CLARIOstar® Plus (BMG Labtech) or an Infinite M1000 Pro (Tecan) microplate reader. 300 rpm double‐orbital shaking was applied for 10 s before each measurement cycle.

Further details about the experimental procedure can be found in SI, S1.6. Calculations were carried out as described in the SI in Sections S1.7 and S1.8. Additional details can also be found in the Supplementary Material.
