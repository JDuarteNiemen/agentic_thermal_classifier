The BASEL phage collection was developed to provide access to diverse bacteriophages, distinct from model phages. Escherichia phage JohannRWettstein (Bas63), a myophage in the collection, is a member of the subfamily Ounavirinae and the Felixounavirus genus. Using cryo–electron microscopy, we investigated Bas63’s structure to explore its evolutionary relationships and functional adaptations. Our structures reveal a series of gene products: (i) a capsid decorated with β-tulip proteins at three-fold symmetry axes and a Hoc-like protein at hexamer centers, (ii) a conserved connector with an additional 12-fold ring of collar proteins that extend unique whisker proteins that are structurally related to podophage GP4 tail fibers, and (iii) a baseplate with long tail fibers resembling a contracted form of T4’s long tail fibers. Sequence conservation analysis of Bas63 structural proteins across ICTV-recognized Felixounavirus’ supports its role as a structural model for Felixounavirus evolution. This study advances the mechanistic understanding of phage architecture and reinforces the structural mosaicism of bacteriophages.

Cryo-EM of phage Bas63 reveals unique capsid and tail features, offering insights into Felixounavirus evolution and architecture.

INTRODUCTION

Bacteriophages of the class Caudoviricetes represent the most abundant group of viruses on earth (1, 2). Their virions comprise icosahedral or prolate proteinaceous capsids packed to high pressure with double-stranded DNA (dsDNA) and capped by complex tail assemblies (3). The mechanical design of the tail machinery serves as a structural marker for segregation into the functional morphotypes: myophage, siphophage, or podophage (4, 5). Historically, these morphotypes represented the major caudovirus families, studied primarily using model phages from the T-odd and T-even phage collection described in the 1940s (6). In 2019, the International Committee on Taxonomy of Viruses (ICTV) dissolved these phage families as these major morphotype groups were found to be polyphyletic (7). As of March 2025, the class Caudoviricetes includes 7 orders, 74 families, and 121 subfamilies, with members of the former morphotype-based families redistributed among multiple taxa to better reflect their evolutionary relationships (8). Through this process, it has become clear that the T-odd and T-even collection, while highly valuable, offers only a partial view of the diversity within Caudoviricetes.

In recent years, steps have been taken to develop new phage catalogs that include a broader range of phage taxa. A notable example is the BASEL collection, composed of an original 68 newly isolated phages and more recently an additional 37 phages, characterized by receptor tropism, susceptibility to antiphage systems, and genomic makeup (9, 10). Among the most unique phages in the original collection is phage JohannRWettstein (Bas63), belonging to the subfamily Ounavirinae and the genus Felixounavirus based on similarity to the model Salmonella phage FelixO1 (7).

The Felixounavirus genus includes 17 lytic myophage species, excluding Bas63, that infect Salmonella or Escherichia hosts (11). The founding member of Felixounavirus, FelixO1, was described in 1943 and is considered one of the most important salmonella phages due to its ability to infect almost all Salmonella hosts (7, 12). A genetically modified version of FelixO1 has also been used as a biodetection tool for detecting Salmonella in food samples (13–18). Other felixounaviruses have also been investigated for their lytic potential or studied as therapeutic agents (19–21). The phage FelixO1 genome has been characterized, and several putative structural genes have been identified (13, 21).

At the onset of this project, members of Ounavirinae had not been described structurally, prompting us to perform an in-depth structural analysis of the Bas63 using single-particle cryo–electron microscopy (cryo-EM). Very recently, the ounavirus Sf14 was described by Subramanian et al. (22). Despite similarity between these viruses, our study provides an independent interpretation of ounavirus structure and reveals unique insights. We first describe a Hoc-like decoration protein that binds to hexamers at icosahedral facet edges. Second, we identified a myophage whisker and collar complex only previously observed in phage Sf14 and as part of the fiber network of a distantly related podophage. Third, we observe two types of tail fibers that adhere to the tail sheath, providing a unique opportunity to interpret these fiber structures that are often disordered due to mobility. Fourth, we observe a difference in the copy number of the tape measure protein (TMP) between the baseplate and tail regions. Last, we compare Bas63 to the entire structural module of all ICTV-recognized felixounaviruses to assess the validity of our virion structure as a model for the Felixounavirus genus.

RESULTS

Overall description of the Bas63 virion

The Bas63 complete virion was reconstructed from cryo-EM particle images as a series of contiguous reconstructions leveraging local symmetries: icosahedral symmetry in the capsid, C6 symmetry in the tail and connector, and C3 symmetry in the baseplate. The resolution of resulting reconstructions ranged between 3.0 and 4.0 Å.

The Bas63 virion comprises at least 23 gene products, 22 of which were partially modeled in this study (Fig. 1, A and B). The capsid shows T = 9 icosahedral symmetry and is formed by a major capsid protein (MCP) and two ordered decorations proteins (Fig. 1D). One of these decoration proteins is encoded by a gene found more than 10 kb outside of the Bas63 structural module (Fig. 1A). The capsid is connected to a contractile tail via a connector complex consisting of six gene products that feature a collar and a whisker network reminiscent of, but unrelated to, the collar and wac network found in phage T4 (23, 24). The Bas63 contractile tail comprises tube and sheath proteins organized into a helical array of 26 hexameric disks, extending to an intricate baseplate complex. The baseplate is formed by at least 10 gene products organized into hub, wedge, and fiber network substructures. The baseplate module encodes at least one additional gene product that forms a trimeric ring around the puncture apparatus (PA), extending past the PA tip. A comparison of the Bas63 structural module to that of phage FelixO1 shows almost 100% DNA sequence conservation, indicating that Bas63 also serves as a structural atlas of the FelixO1 virion (Fig. 1C).

Structural comparisons between Bas63 and Sf14

To interrogate the structural similarity of Sf14 and Bas63, we aligned all available models from both studies and interpreted the sequence conservation and RMSD between each structural protein. Structures directly comparable are summarized in table S3. Sequence conservation ranged from 51.99% identity in tail fibers to 99.32% identity in tail tube proteins. Structural alignments that structural proteins compared are very similar, with root mean square deviation (RMSD) ranging between 0.519 Å in the HexDec protein and 1.164 Å in the portal protein. Baseplate proteins could not be compared in terms of RMSD, as no Sf14 baseplate reconstruction is available. In addition to these comparisons, we used Dali to investigate other close structural homologs to Bas63, presenting the top two phage related Dali results for each Bas63 structural protein (table S4) (25). Where Sf14 models were available, Sf14 structural homologs represented the closest structural homolog in all cases. Other notably phages that were repeatedly observed include phages ɸTE, ɸ812, and GP4 (26–28). To avoid repetition, Bas63 structural components have been related primarily to phages other than Sf14 in this study to interrogate more distant evolutionary relationships between bacteriophages.

The Bas63 capsid features two decoration proteins.

The Bas63 capsid was resolved to 3.1 Å, with icosahedral symmetry imposed. It is composed of 535 MCP copies (gp8) that share close homology to the MCP of phages Sf14 and φTE, featuring the canonical HK97 N-terminal arm, A domain, P domain, E loop, and spine-helix features (29, 30). The MCP lattice is also encased in 540 copies of ꞵ-tulip decoration proteins (Dec and gp7) that bind to quasi-threefold axes of symmetry at the interface between MCP P-domains (Fig. 1F). The Dec protein also shows homology to phage Sf14 gp33 and φTE Dec (pTE212), featuring a C-terminal extension and an αꞵ domain with a topology divergent to ꞵ-tulip decorations found in other myophages (fig. S2) (22, 26, 31–38). An intriguing feature of the Bas63 capsid is the presence of an ordered monomeric gene product that binds to the centers of hexamers (Hex-Dec) (Fig. 1, E and G). Alpha-fold predictions of Hex-Dec suggested a protein fold that features an N-terminal capsid binding domain (CapBD), followed by four immunoglobulin (Ig)–like domains. Although the Ig-like domains of Hex-Dec were not resolvable, the CapBD domain binds tightly into hexamer centers and shows strong local density. Furthermore, Hex-Dec binds exclusively to hexamers at triangular facet edges, with no density observed for other hexamers; this is consistent with the distribution of the Sf14 homolog gp20. A closer inspection of the Hex-Dec CapBD domain revealed that its basal binding domain has pseudo C2 symmetry, with each lobe (L1 and L2) sharing a conserved fold linked together by a link-loop. The Ig-like repeats extend from the C-terminal end of the L2 lobe of CapBD.

The Bas63 connector features a collar-whisker assembly most closely related to podophage fibers.

The Bas63 connector was resolved as two local reconstructions in C6 and C12 symmetry at resolutions of 3.1 and 3.0 Å, respectively. It is composed of six gene products, four of which are ubiquitous among myophages and two that are not common in myophages (Fig. 2A). The central conserved core of the connector features a portal protein (gp3) dodecameric ring that adjoins a dodecameric adaptor protein (gp9) ring. Head-distal to the adaptor is a hexameric ring of head completion proteins (HC, gp11), followed by a hexameric ring of tail terminator proteins (TT, gp12) (Fig. 2, A and B). Subsequent head distal rings correspond to tube (gp14) and sheath (gp13) rings assembled as a six-start helix.

The portal protein features a canonical crown, wing, stem, and clip domains, with few substantial variations relative to other myophages (39). The adaptor protein shows close similarity to the adaptor protein of phage φTE. The N-terminal portion of the adaptor forms a globular domain, with a ꞵ-hairpin (adaptor hairpin) extending from residues 65 to 74. These hairpins are oriented in a straight head-distal direction and collectively form a 24-stranded ꞵ barrel that interfaces with the HC ring. The C-terminal arm of the adaptor forms a bouquet motif that wraps around portal stem helices, previously observed only in phages Sf14 and φTE (fig. S9). Unlike φTE, the Bas63 bouquet motif helices are linked by a continuous ring of putative disulfide bonds at residue positions Cys106 and Cys116 (fig. S10, A and B). The subsequent hexameric rings of HC protein and TT protein follow the standard fold found in most myophages (Fig. 2B).

In addition to this core stack of connector proteins, Bas63 also displays 12 whiskers that extend from a dodecameric ring of Ig-like collar proteins, with an alternating pattern of up and down-facing whiskers (Fig. 2, C to F). A Dali search of whisker monomers (gp5) revealed that the closest structural homologs belong to phage Sf14 and the podophage GP4 Fiber-1 protein, which also features an Ig-like collar attached to whisker homologs (25, 27). In the Bas63 connector, the dodecameric assembly of collar proteins is mounted to the periphery of the connector at the interface between adaptor proteins and HC proteins. Whiskers are then attached to collar monomers via a long N-terminal extension that emanates from the collar protein. These extensions appear homologous to the “rope” protein from phage GP4, which, in contrast, exists as an independent protein that interlocks with the GP4 Ig-like collar (Fig. 2C and fig. S4, A and C). The rope domains of Bas63 collar monomers alternate between up and down conformations, forming an alternating whisker pattern (Fig. 2F). The globular domains of collar proteins, such as adaptors, are also linked by a continuous ring of putative disulfide bonds that form between collar residues Cys52 and Cys90 (fig. S10D). Because of the C12-C6 symmetry mismatch between the collar and HC rings, only every second collar subunit contacts the HC via an external facing loop of the head completion protein (F9-N37). In contrast, every collar subunit interacts with the adaptor protein through a third putative disulfide bond between collar residue Cys69 and adaptor residue Cys2. Together, these two interfaces likely serve to bind the collar ring strongly to the connector core. Whiskers then form a series of dimers that wrap around collar rope domains such as beads on a string, with two dimers per collar protein (Fig. 2C). Whisker protein monomers feature an N-terminal helix, a small three-stranded ꞵ sheet (residues 45 to 48, 50 to 54, and 105 to 110) and an incomplete ꞵ sandwich (Fig. 2D and fig. S4B). Dimers of whisker proteins form an interface through the coiling of N-terminal helices and reciprocal augmentation of the short ꞵ sheet of the adjacent whisker with the first few N-terminal residues (residues 1 to 5). Whisker dimers are then positioned end-to-end, wrapped around collar rope domains, with the rope running through grooves in whisker monomers, completing the series of incomplete whisker ꞵ sandwiches (Fig. 2E). Outside of collar interactions, down-facing whisker proteins were observed to contact the first and second rings of head proximal sheath proteins and the TT. In the case of up-facing whiskers, only a single dimer could be confidently fitted into the C6 cryo-EM density that appeared to make no contact. We performed an asymmetric reconstruction of this region to establish if the density loss corresponding to a second up-whisker dimer was due to motion or asymmetric interactions. The asymmetric reconstruction yielded a resolution of 7.4 Å and revealed that the second dimer subunits do appear to contact the capsid asymmetrically, with four whiskers contacting Dec monomers closest to the portal, while one whisker contacts Dec and Hex-Dec via its Ig-like domains (fig. S3). These interactions could not be analyzed in detail due to the limited resolution of the asymmetric reconstruction.

The Bas63 baseplate and contractile tail

The contractile module of the Bas63 tail consists of a helical assembly of sheath and tube proteins, terminated by an intricate baseplate complex composed of at least 12 gene products (Fig. 3B). The helical tail assembly was reconstructed using C6 symmetry and helical symmetry, yielding a final helical rise and twist of 38.1° and 25.9°, respectively (Fig. 3D). The final resolution of the extended tail components was 3.2 Å. The baseplate wedge and hub substructures were reconstructed as several focused maps using C3 symmetry, yielding resolutions of 4.0 and 3.8 Å, respectively. Despite the predominant sixfold stoichiometry of the baseplate, we observed that wedge subunits adopt C3 symmetry. Thus, C6 symmetry was not used in these regions. Alternatively, fiber proteins were explored using C6 baseplate reconstructions. Because of the close association between the fiber network and the tail, fiber AlphaFold predictions could be flexibly fit to density using ISOLDE (40). To best use the resolution of fibers at full extension, we performed contiguous C3 and C6 reconstructions that extend the entire length of the fibers, which were merged using the ChimeraX “volume maximum” command (41). While the C6 contiguous reconstructions were used to interpret fiber densities, C3 contiguous reconstructions also permitted the interpretation of TMP stoichiometry along the length of the tail.

The helical portion of the Bas63 contractile tail comprises rings of sheath and tube proteins stacked according to the helical symmetry specified above. The tube protein (gp14) has a ꞵ sandwich fold without notable structural features. This appears to be common among myophage tube proteins, which tend to appear nearly identically among phages (42). The sheath protein (gp13) also features a typical four-domain architecture (D1: 1-80, D2: 81-184, D3: 185-341, and D4: 342-450) (Fig. 3A). Domains 1 and 4 are primarily required for linkage between sheath monomers, forming hinge sheets as previously described (fig. S6). Domain 3 is globular and nestles against domain 4. Domain 2 is not universally conserved among myophages but has been found in phage Sf14, φTE, Milano, E217, XM1, PA193, and A1-(L) sheath proteins (22, 26, 31, 35, 38, 43, 44). The function of this domain is not entirely clear. However, this domain makes extensive contacts with retracted fibers in Bas63.

The Bas63 baseplate comprises ~12 proteins, 11 of which could be modeled to varying extents. These proteins can be separated into three substructures: the hub, the wedge, and the fiber network.

The baseplate hub

The hub complex forms the central component of the Bas63 baseplate and is best characterized by C3 symmetry. It consists of four gene products: a central hub protein (gp20), a head distal PA protein (PA, gp21), a head proximal tube initiator protein (TI, gp18), and a fragment of the TMP (17) (Fig. 3G). The hub also features one additional C3 density of unknown identity that binds at the tip of the PA domain (Fig. 3E and fig. S11).

The central hub trimer forms the central component of the hub complex, with a large primary ꞵ sandwich structure and an additional head distal αꞵ domain (142 to 229) that interfaces with the trimeric PA. The PA monomers are each composed of an N-terminal α helix, a small ꞵ sandwich, and a ꞵ helix formed by all three PA monomers. At the tip of the PA, the C-termini contain ꞵ hairpins that augment each other to create a small pointed ꞵ sandwich. Using focused reconstruction and the deep EM enhancer tool, we were able to model the PA in its entirety, revealing that each monomer encodes a conserved di-histidine motif (HxH, H188, and H190) that orient toward the interior of the PA, likely coordinating iron ions as observed in other PA structures (45, 46).

The TI forms hexamers that adapt the C3 hub symmetry to the C6 symmetry of the tail. The TI protein shares a close structural homology with tube proteins, except for a ~100–amino acid C-terminal extension that spans much of the baseplate, primarily occupying space radially adjacent to spacer proteins. The C-terminal fragment of TMP could be modeled confidently in contact with the N-terminal helices of the PA and interacting with the Hub. Although only the 25 C-terminal residues of TMP could be modeled, a series of contiguous reconstructions allowed further analysis of the TMP copy number. Evidence of sixfold features was observed at multiple stages along the tail. This indicates that the TMP stoichiometry switches from threefold in the baseplate to sixfold at the interface with tube proteins (Fig. 3, B and C). We observe C6 symmetry as close as ~60 Å head-distal; at this point, the structure has C6 and C3 symmetry on either side of a 20-Å density gap that can be observed even at high contour level.

The baseplate wedge

Surrounding the hub complex are six wedge protomers that collectively form an aperture around the hub (Fig. 3F). The wedge is composed of four unique proteins: wedge 1 (gp24), wedge 2 (gp23), spacer (gp19), and long tail fiber (LTF)–platform (gp25). The baseplate wedge showed poor local resolution; accordingly, between 160 and 162 of 285 wedge 1 residues were modeled, and between 446 and 478 of 489 wedge 2 residues were modeled, in both cases depending on asymetric unit position. In wedge 1, missing residues predominantly correspond to a disordered C terminus. In wedge 2, missing residues primarily correspond to various disordered loops. Furthermore, the LTF platform, which showed the worst local resolution, was modeled as a Cα trace with all side chains truncated. In contrast, spacer subunits showed strong local density and could be confidently modeled.

The wedge is adapted to the C3 symmetry of the hub via spacer proteins, with an Ig-like fold capped at the C terminus with a short helix. The aperture component of the wedge is formed by an arrangement of two major wedge proteins: wedge 1 and wedge 2. wedge 2 is formed by four distinct domains, numbered linearly in sequence of their position (D1 to D4). The wedge 1 protein is composed of only two domains (D1 and D2), which are structurally similar to the domains D1 and D3 of wedge 2 (fig. S5A). In contrast to wedge 1, wedge 2 is found in two alternate conformations throughout the baseplate, referred to as wedge 2A and wedge 2B (fig. S5B). The domain architecture of both wedge 1 and 2 subunits is highly conserved across myophages, reliably forming the ring-like aperture. Key motifs such as the “core bundle” and “trifurcation unit” described before are observed in the Bas63 baseplate (47). Inspection of the aperture formed primarily by wedge 2 in C3 maps revealed that the D2 domain of every second wedge 2B monomer adopts a slightly different conformation (fig. S12B). This observation was corroborated by blurring in C6 wedge 2A D2 densities, likely resulting from merging these two conformational variants (fig. S12A). Accordingly, we consider the Bas63 baseplate to be entirely C3 regarding symmetry. At the periphery of the baseplate, the LTF platform binds to the wedge 1 D2 domain. This LTF platform becomes the attachment point for Bas63 long-tail fibers and is homologous to the T4 gp9 protein, which serves the same function (Fig. 4B and fig. S13, A and B).

The fiber network

Our reconstructions of the Bas63 baseplate reveal two fiber trimers, each appearing to be anchored to the sheath through contacts with the D2 domains along the tail. While densities for both fibers are unsuitable for modeling rotamers, the density was sufficient to assess the overall domain architecture confidently. As previously stated, the Bas63 baseplate binds both a 430-Å short tail fiber (STF; gp26) and a 630-Å LTF (gp27). Both the LTF and STF share several key structures. They are both bound to the baseplate via an N-terminal baseplate binding domain (BPBD) that is structurally conserved despite a lack of apparent sequence conservation (Fig. 4A). Both fibers feature a C-terminal receptor binding tip similar to the T4 receptor binding tip of the distal fiber subunit (T4-gp37) (Fig. 4C) (48). This structure includes a collar domain, a long-coiled β barrel needle domain with conserved HxH motifs, and a small head domain that directly contacts receptors in the case of T4 (Fig. 4E). The peptide chain of the entire tip region doubles back at the head domain, extending back to the collar domain; as a result, the needle β barrel is composed of six strands. In phage T4, the needle domain di-histidine motifs coordinate Fe ions along the length of the needle. T4 encodes seven di-histidine motifs in the gp37 needle domain. In Bas63, the STF also encodes seven di-histidine motifs. The LTF only appears to encode six di-histidine motifs; however, the head domain proximal histidine motif contains an additional histidine (HHxH: H692, H693, and H695). A comparison of all three tip domains shows that the LTF needle domain is the shortest; the number of di-histidine motifs required for correct receptor binding function may be proportional to the length of the needle domain.

The main point of distinction between LTF and STF is found in the N-terminal region, where LTF also encodes five β helix repeat domains. Regions between each β-helix domain contain “triple α helix and turn” motifs. Both features are very similar to the rod region of the T4 proximal fiber subunit (T4-gp34) (Fig. 4F) (49). Last, several triple α helix and turn motifs can also be observed in the STF without interspersed β helix domains. The STF is bound at its N terminus to D2 of wedge 2A monomers (Fig. 4D). The fiber then extends toward the capsid, making contact with the tenth baseplate distal sheath ring, where the STF head domain is positioned between the D2 and D3 domains of sheath monomers. The STF only appears to interact minimally with one other sheath ring (baseplate distal sheath ring 6). The density corresponding to the point of contact between the STF and wedge 2A is sufficient for backbone assessment but insufficient for confident analysis of interactions; regardless, it appears likely that the contact interface is small (620 Å2 as per PISA analysis of flexibly fitted AlphaFold predictions; fig. S7, A and B) (50). The interface between LTFs and LTF-platform trimers also appears small and cannot be interpreted.

There was a stark difference in the quality of density between the LTF and STF, with LTF density requiring a far lower map threshold for interpretation. We performed three-dimensional (3D) classification experiments on the Bas63 baseplate with focus masks placed on LTFs and STFs to investigate this. Classification revealed classes with zero, one, two, three, four, five, and six LTF fibers occupying the LTF-retracted position (Fig. 4G), suggesting that the poor density of LTF is likely caused by LTF dissociation or a lack of LTF occupancy.

Conservation and divergence in the Ounavirinae structural module

To test the robustness of our Bas63 virion structure as a model for the Felixounavirus genus, we performed sequence alignments of all felixounaviruses structural proteins. This kind of analysis is generally difficult due to poor annotation of structural proteins, preventing large-scale analysis of phage structural modules. However, because of the structural characterization performed here, we confidently identified all structural homologs among ICTV-recognized felixounaviruses (18 in total), permitting subsequent amino acid alignments. Through this process, we found that both the Hex-Dec and Dec proteins were incorrectly annotated as tail components in most Felixounavirus genomes. From these alignments, we collected identity information for every Felixounavirus structural protein relative to Bas63 (Fig. 5A). Broadly, Felixounavirus structural proteins diverge by little more than 5% in identity compared to Bas63, and in many cases, they are >99% identical. However, there were some important exceptions where sequence variability was substantial. Fiber proteins were found to be remarkably diverse among felixounaviruses. By mapping sequence conservation onto our fiber structures, we could easily visualize regions of sequence conservation and divergence (Fig. 5B). Both STF and LTF show high sequence variability in the receptor binding tip. Unexpectedly, there was also a lack of conservation in the LTF β helix repeat domains. The inspection of sequence alignments and AlphaFold 3 prediction of all Felixounavirus LTFs suggests that this is not due to large deletions or variable β helix repeat numbers but to a substantial sequence variation between species (figs. S8 and S16). This identity analysis also revealed a high diversity in Hex-Dec gene products. Unlike tail fibers, this variability appears to be caused by large missing sequences in Hex-Dec C-termini. To probe this further, we generated AlphaFold 3 predictions of all Felixounavirus Hex-Dec proteins (51). This revealed that the shorter Hex-Dec sequences are predicted to code for a different number of Ig-like domains extending from the N-terminal Hex-Dec CapBD, which itself appears strongly conserved (Fig. 5, C and D). Felixounavirus species other than Bas63 are predicted to encode between one and three Ig-like domains, while Bas63 is predicted to encode four. We note that some sequences in this analysis were likely incomplete due to large missing fragments (EC6 portal and LTF, Si3 LTF). These structures were omitted from identity-based comparisons.

DISCUSSION

The macromolecular structure of the Bas63 virion reveals a fascinating combination of structural features comparable to a range of structurally characterized bacteriophages. Bas63 capsid features MCPs and trimeric Dec proteins similar to phage φTE. While ꞵ-tulip decorations are found in many phages (λ, YSD1, XM1, TW1, Pam3, Pa193, E217, P24-76, and Sf14), Bas63 Dec, φTE Dec, and Sf14 gp33 adopt a αꞵ domain topology not observed in other ꞵ-tulip decorations and were found to group together in Dali all-against-all analysis (fig. S2) (22, 26, 31–38). In contrast, the Hex-Dec protein resembles the Hoc protein from phage T4 (52–54) more closely. Hoc and Hex-Dec both contain a string of Ig-like domains that extend from a CapBD, which in both cases consists of a small globular domain with two helices and four strands forming ꞵ sheets. Despite these similarities, the Bas63 CapBD is larger and topologically distinct (fig. S1A). Furthermore, Hex-Dec shows a pseudo C2 symmetry not observed in Hoc (fig. S1D). The domain orientation of each of these gene products is reversed and shares minimal sequence conservation (fig. S1B). The role and essentiality of each Bas63 decoration protein is unknown. Bas63 Dec proteins likely contribute to the structural stability, as is well documented among decorations that bind to threefold and quasi-threefold symmetry axes (55). Hex-Dec may function similarly to Hoc, which has been shown to have many roles, such as pH-dependent regulation of particle aggregation, attachment to bacterial surfaces, and attachment to gut mucosa (52, 53, 56). These functions mostly seem to aid in localizing virions to sites that increase the likelihood of host infection. The inspection of the electrostatic surface potential distribution of Hex-Dec shows a negative charge distribution similar to Hoc and a similar arrangement of Ig-like domains (fig. S1C). Hence, we propose that Hex-Dec and Hoc serve similar functions.

The Bas63 connector shares a core architecture of portal, adaptor, HC, and TT proteins similar to phage φTE, including forming a unique bouquet motif only observed in phage Bas63, Sf14, and φTE (Fig. 2 and fig. S9). However, unlike φTE, the Bas63 bouquet motif appears covalently linked via disulfide bonds (Cys106 and Cys116; fig. S10, A and B), strongly cinching the adaptor protein around the portal. Another unique feature of the ounavirus connector appears to be the collar and whisker complex. Whisker/collar assemblies are also found in phages T4 and XM1. In T4, whisker/collar complexes are formed by a single gene product (wac), which forms long trimeric coiled coils that alternate between up (collar) and down (whisker) conformations, making contacts with LTF’s in both cases (24, 57). The XM1 whisker/collar complex is formed by gp40 and has an N-terminal portion containing an Ig-like domain similar to Bas63 (31). However, XM1 gp40 also has long C-terminal α-helical extensions that form trimeric coiled coil whiskers similar to wac. It was speculated that the XM1 gp40 complex may reinforce the connection point between the preformed capsid and the tail structures. This hypothesis may also be applied to the Bas63 whisker/collar complex, as down-facing whiskers interface with TT and head distal sheath rings 1 and 2. In contrast, collar proteins firmly contact HC proteins and likely form disulfide bonds with adaptor proteins (adaptor Cys2 and collar Cys69; fig. S10D) and with adjacent collar subunits (Cys52-Cys90; fig. S10C). Up-facing whiskers also contact the capsid, creating a second adhesion point tying together the head and tail (fig. S3). Therefore, whiskers may provide an anchoring role at the interface of the head-tail attachment.

The most interesting observation regarding the whisker/collar complex is the apparent homology between the GP4 fiber-tail-adaptor/Fiber-1 complex and the Bas63 collar/whisker complex (27). Despite the strong conservation of quaternary structure, there are some meaningful differences. For instance, the GP4 Fiber-1 complex extends five fibers in an up-facing conformation toward the capsid, while Bas63 extends two sets of six whiskers in alternating up and down conformations (fig. S4D). Furthermore, the GP4 rope protein is substantially larger than the Bas63 collar rope domain, allowing the attachment of five Fiber-1 dimers instead of the two whisker dimers observed in Bas63. It has been speculated that GP4 Fiber-1 fibers may interact with lipopolysaccharide (LPS) to mediate host cell adsorption (27). As Bas63 is LPS dependent, whiskers may serve a similar role in transiently anchoring virions to the host cell surface via LPS.

The overall structure of the Bas63 baseplate is minimal and compact, consisting largely of conserved core components such as the tube and sheath initiators, wedges, hub, PA, TMP, and fiber. The trimeric density observed at the tip of the PA is unusual; a similar smeared ring of density was also observed in the Sf14 tail tip, likely corresponding to the same protein (22). Efforts were made without success to improve the local resolution of this protein. However, this density appears rich in helices (fig. S11).

Our observations of alternating C3 and C6 TMP copy numbers between the baseplate and tail corroborate findings in myophages φTE and A1- (L) and the stunning recent full-length TMP structure of the phage HFTV1 (26, 43, 58). In particular, the structure of HFTV1 showed a C6 TMP that extends the length of the tail as a coiled-coil until the C-terminal portion of the TMP, wherein even strands bend back toward the capsid, while odd strands interface with the tail tip. While this was observed in a distantly related archaeal siphophage, this model would well describe our observations in the Bas63 TMP. A recent study of a bacteriocin that targets Clostridium difficile reported a complete low-resolution TMP structure with threefold symmetry, which contradicts these findings (59). This suggests that different TMP copy numbers are observed in other systems depending on the TMP function. Channel formation (60–62), muralytic activity (63, 64), and the recently reported polymerase activity (65) are several functions observed in various phage TMPs. It is reasonable to suggest that variations in the functional outcome of contractile systems may govern TMP evolution toward variable functions and, correspondingly, alternate copy numbers. For instance, bacteriocins function to disrupt the target bacterium’s cell wall, while myophage tails must create a conduit for genomic passage without cell wall disruption. These differing functional outcomes may promote the adaptation of alternate TMP copy numbers if myophage TMP is required for channel-forming functions as speculated. Furthermore, different bacterial cell wall structures could also exert evolutionary pressure on the TMP due to variations in penetration requirements. This has been observed in other baseplate proteins, i.e., the C. difficile bacteriocin hub proteins feature transglycosylase and endopeptidase domains not observed in Gram-negative bacteria, where peptidoglycan layers are thinner and presumably more penetrable (59). Future studies of TMP structure and copy number in divergent contractile systems will show how these observations correlate to functional roles and host cell traits.

The fiber network of Bas63 offers a unique opportunity for structural characterization. Often, myophage tail fibers feature large, disordered regions that limit interpretation. While the local resolution of the fibers in Bas63 is insufficient to interpret individual residues, domain architecture, and secondary structural features can be confidently interpreted, allowing comparisons between other phage fibers. The Bas63 LTF shares several structural similarities to both the proximal and distal gene products of the T4 LTF (48, 49). Moreover, the LTF-platform trimer protein shows topological similarity to the T4 gp9 trimer, albeit on a smaller scale (47). Both the LTF and STF show identical domain organization to Sf14 gp53 and gp52, respectively; this may suggest this fiber organization is consistent within Ounavirinae. Last, the observed variation in retracted LTF fibers can be explained by transient dissociation of LTFs from the sheath for environmental sampling, similar to the functional role described for T4 LTFs (61). An alternative interepretation is that LTF positions are not saturated during virion assembly, resulting in variable occupancy. Irresepective, it seems that the Bas63 LTF-platform/LTF complex represents a simplified and compact version of the T4 LTF with functional similarity.

The fact that STFs retain occupancy in extended tail structure 3D classes suggests that Bas63 STFs may behave the same way as T4 STFs, retracting after LTF-receptor–induced baseplate reorganization (61). This is interesting, given the manner of Bas63 STF attachment, as the structure of Bas63 STF differs from T4 STFs, which more closely resembles a truncated version of the Bas63-LTF (fig. S13, C and D) (47).

Last, sequence alignments of Bas63 and Felixounavirus confirm that the vast majority of Bas63 structural proteins serve as near 1:1 correspondence to Felixounavirus gene products. A major benefit of this study is the identification of many structural proteins previously annotated as “hypothetical proteins” in Felixounavirus genomes. Several inaccurate annotations were also found; for instance, the Hex-Dec was annotated as a “tail protein” in most genomes analyzed, while Dec was annotated as a “tail protease.” The proteins displaying the lowest identity include Hex-Dec, LTF, and STF. The observed sequence variation in STF and LTF receptor binding tips was expected as this fiber region determines receptor tropism. However, the high degree of variability observed in the LTF β helix repeat regions 2,3, 4, and 5 was not expected. One of three possibilities could explain this: either the regions outside of β helices are more sensitive to mutation, resulting in higher conservation. In contrast, β helix regions are insensitive and can tolerate the accumulation of stable missense mutations. The second possibility is that these domains perform a variable host recognition function between host strains. The third possibility is that these domains serve as the target of an unknown antiphage defense system and are correspondingly prone to escape mutation. The Hex-Dec protein alternatively seems to strongly conserve CapBD and Ig-like repeat 1 with variable numbers of Ig-like repeats similar to Hoc proteins (53). It is unclear what the beneficial function may be in any given number of Ig-like domains. It should also be noted that β-tulip decorations, sheath proteins, and TMPs have slightly reduced identity relative to other gene products.

The Bas63 virion is unique and features structural elements related primarily to three distantly related phages (T4, φTE, and podophage GP4). Our model of phage Bas63 underlines the extraordinary evolutionary reach of tailed phages and serves as a model for interpreting felixounaviruses structural proteins. In addition, our analysis of the Felixounavirus structural module has identified many structural proteins that were previously unannotated or incorrectly annotated, including in the Bas63 genome itself. We hope this improved characterization of the Bas63 structural proteins will enable further understanding of the subfamily Ounavirinae.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

Sample purification

For lysate preparation, 2 ml of Escherichia coli K-12 MG1655 ΔRM overnight culture was added to 50 ml of LB supplemented with MgSO4 and CaCl2 [tryptone (10 g/ml), yeast extract (5 g/ml), NaCl (10 g/ml), 20 mM MgSO4, and 5 mM CaCl2]. Cultures were incubated for 1.5 hours at 37°C. A 100 μl of high titer Bas63 sample was added to the culture, followed by incubation at 37°C for 12 hours for phage growth and cell lysis. Crude lysates were centrifuged at 8000g for 20 min [Thermo Fisher Scientific (TFS) LYNX 6000, Fiberlite F12-6x500 LEX rotor] at 20°C; then, the supernatant was decanted into an autoclaved Schott bottle. The supernatant containing phage was filtered using a 0.22-μm filter. The sample was loaded into polypropylene thin-walled 16.5-ml ultracentrifuge tubes (Beckman, catalog no. 337986) underlaid with 3 ml of 20% (w/v) sucrose. Tubes were filled to the top and topped with phage buffer [10 mM tris-HCl (pH 7.4), 10 mM MgSO4, and ×0.01% w v21 gelatin). Tubes were loaded into SW32.1 swing buckets. The sucrose cushion was spun at 40,000g for 40 min at 20°C (TFS Sorvall WX+, Beckman SW32.1 TI rotor). The sample supernatant was decanted, and the residual buffer was tapped out of tubes. All pellets were resuspended in 100 μl of sterile RO water overnight at 4°C. Resuspended phage samples were pooled and dialyzed in sterile RO water (12 to 14 kDa MWCO Spectra/Por 2). Last, samples were concentrated to high titer using 100-kDa cutoff centrifugal columns (Amicon).

Cryo-EM data collection

Purified Bas63 samples were aliquoted (3.5 μl) onto Quantifoil R2/1 carbon grids that were previously plasma cleaned for 30 s with a 20% hydrogen and 80% oxygen plasma (Gatan Solarus, Gatan Inc., USA). Samples were then blotted at 4°C and 100% humidity with an FEI Vitrobot Mark IV (no waiting time, blotting time of 4 s, and blotting force of 0) before flash-freezing in liquid ethane/propane (50:50).

Two datasets were collected at different electron doses using a Titan Krios cryo-electron microscope (TFS) in TEM mode operating at an acceleration voltage of 300 kV. Images were recorded semi-automatically using EPU software (TFS) with a Falcon 3EC direct electron detector (TFS). Movies were captured in linear mode at a nominal magnification of ×59,000, resulting in a calibrated pixel size of 1.387 Å. A defocus range of −0.8 to −2.0 μm was applied. Dataset 1 consisted of 19,871 movies collected with a total dose of 50 e−/Å2 fractioned over 39 frames. Dataset 2 consisted of 1914 movies collected at a very high dose for future bubble gram imaging studies. The total dose of the second dataset was 537 e−/Å2 fractionated as 3 frames per fraction (4 e−/Å2 per fraction) in 107 fractions and 79 frames in fraction #108 (106 e−/Å2 in this final fraction).

Image processing

Bas63 reconstructions were performed using cryoSPARC V4.4.1 (66). Dataset 1 movies were subjected to patch-based motion correction using default parameters. For dataset 2 movies, only frames 1 to 12 were submitted to motion correction using default parameters. The dataset 2 resulting effective electron dose was thus 48 e−/Å2 (4 e−/Å2 per frame). Both datasets were then merged and submitted for patch-based contrast transfer function (CTF) correction. A workflow chart of all reconstruction methods and output reconstruction FSC curves can be found in figs. S14 and S15, respectively. Downstream workflows were performed as follows:

Capsid reconstruction

Micrographs were initially picked using the CryoSPARC auto-blob picking tool. The resulting picks were extracted using a box size of 960 pixels, Fourier-cropped to 240 pixels, and subsequently submitted to 2D classification. The best classes containing 31,735 particles were pooled and submitted to ab initio reconstruction without symmetry imposed. The ab initio model was refined using homogenous refinement with I2 symmetry imposed. Particles aligned to this volume were reextracted to the final box size of 756 pixels without Fourier cropping and were submitted to a final round of homogenous refinement, with local CTF refinement enabled and negative Ewald sphere correction enabled. The resulting capsid density resolution was calculated as 3.4 Å.

The WALC particle picking system: Picking baseplate and connector particles

The WALC (Walking Alignment and Classification) procedure was first described by Ayala et al. (67). The WALC procedure is used to identify major tail substructures from a single-particle pick job. To begin, the cryoSPARC filament tracer was used with default parameters to identify helical tail segments. Tail segments were extracted with a box size of 400 pixels (Fourier-cropped to 200 pixels), which were then submitted to helical reconstruction with C6 symmetry imposed. The particle stack was then submitted to 2D classification. Particles were then shifted along the z axis by 75 pixels (~208 Å) using the CryoSPARC Volume alignment tools utility. Particles were then reextracted with identical parameters. The helical reconstruction, 2D classification, particle box shifting, and particle extraction process were iteratively repeated seven times in a +Z direction and seven times in a −Z direction relative to the initial particles. As iterations proceed, boxes are shifted and realigned to symmetry axes along the length of the tail toward either the capsid or baseplate, depending on the direction of Z. Baseplate and connector particles were then pooled from 2D classification jobs from each iteration, followed by the removal of duplicate particles.

Native tail helical reconstruction

The Bas63 tail was reconstructed using the CryoSPARC helical reconstruction workflow. Particles picked in the initial iteration of WALC were reextracted with a box size of 400 pixels. Initial helical parameters were manually estimated from a manually picked low-resolution baseplate reconstruction that included several rings of sheath proteins. Helical parameters were then automatically refined and implemented using CryoSPARC helical reconstruction with C6 point group symmetry imposed and otherwise default parameters. Local CTF refinement was performed before the final helical reconstruction, which used 147,356 helical segments, yielding a resolution of 3.2 Å with helical parameters of 38.1-Å rise and 25.9° twist.

C6 connector and C12 portal/adaptor reconstructions

Particles used for the connector and portal reconstructions were pooled using two methods. The first method was the above-mentioned WALC picking scheme. The second method involved I2 symmetry expansion of capsid particles, followed by box recentering and reextraction (box size of 400 pixels) on an arbitrary vertex using the volume alignment tools job in cryoSPARC, followed by duplicate particle removal and 3D classification with a focus mask placed on the portal region of the vertex. During classification, a class emerged with strong density for the connector. Particles from this class, as well as pooled particles from WALC, were merged and submitted to another remove duplicates job. This approach yielded the largest particle stack size. These particles were then subjected to homogenous refinement with C6 symmetry imposed, using the corresponding class as an initial model. The corresponding model was then re-refined using nonuniform refinement with local CTF estimation enabled, resulting in a final map resolution of 3.1 Å. The final particle stack size was 20,388.

To reconstruct the portal and adaptor structures with C12 symmetry imposed, connector particles were submitted to a volume alignment tools job to shift particle images in Z by ~100 Å. We then performed homogenous reconstruction without alignment to regenerate the connector map with the portal/adaptor complex centered, allowing for mask placement. We then performed local CTF estimation and masked local refinement with C12 point group symmetry, yielding a map with a resolution of 3.0 Å. The portal map’s final particle stack size was also 20,388. Maps were combined using the ChimeraX “volume maximum” command for protein modeling (41).

Baseplate reconstructions

Baseplate particles were primarily picked using the abovementioned WALC method. Because of some issues with baseplate local resolution, baseplate particles were also manually picked. WALC and manually picked particle sets were merged, and duplicate particles were deleted. The corresponding stack was then submitted to 2D classification, resulting in a refined particle stack size of 14,390. Baseplate particles were extracted with a box size of 400 pixels and were used to generate an ab initio model with C6 symmetry imposed. The initial model was submitted to homogenous refinement with C6 symmetry, followed by nonuniform refinement with C6 symmetry and local CTF refinement enabled. We initially tried this reconstruction with C3 symmetry, but the output structure was still blurred in the central C3 region, indicating a misalignment of particles. To break the C6 symmetry, baseplate particles were symmetry expanded using C6 symmetry and were submitted to 3D classification with two classes and a focus mask placed on the C3 central region. Each output class represented the baseplate with the central C3 region rotated in two positions rotated by 120° relative to one another. The particles and output model from one class were selected and submitted to a remove duplicates job, followed by C3 local reconstruction. The corresponding C6 and C3 densities had resolutions of 3.7 Å from 11,071 particles and 3.8 Å from 14,390 particles, respectively. Several additional focused local reconstructions were generated from the C3 reconstruction. The first focused reconstructed used C3 symmetry and was centered on the baseplate wedge components; in addition, particle subtraction was performed on the upper sheath and tube region of micrographs. The corresponding focused refinement yielded a global resolution of 4.0 Å derived from 14,390 particles and was submitted to Deep-EM enhancer postprocessing. Particles from this density were then submitted to C3 symmetry expansion, followed by C1 focused local reconstruction centered on the PA and hub trimers. The corresponding density yielded a global resolution of 3.8 Å derived from 43,170 particles and was submitted to Deep-EM enhancer-based postprocessing (46). Last, the original C3 local reconstruction and both postprocessed focused refinements were merged using Phenix_combine_focussed_maps for deposition together with all component maps (68).

Contiguous tail reconstructions for TMP and fiber analysis

To analyze the TMP along the tail and to produce a high-resolution composite reconstruction of long and STFs, we performed contiguous local reconstructions along the length of the tail, starting from the baseplate, until density became contiguous with our C6 connector reconstruction. In total, three intermediary local reconstructions were performed along the tail. Local reconstructions were used to limit the degree of particle rotational parameters, preventing particle images from breaking C3 symmetry. Contiguous reconstruction began with the aligned particles used in our C3 baseplate reconstruction described above. Between each iteration, particles were shifted along the z axis by 199 pixels (~276 Å), followed by reextraction, reconstruction without alignment, and local reconstruction with C3 symmetry imposed. All C3 maps were then aligned to one another and were merged using the ChimeraX “volume maximum” command, generating a high-resolution reconstruction of the entire tail with C3 symmetry imposed. The same method was performed for C6 maps as well, primarily to enhance fiber resolution.

Asymmetric reconstruction of connector region

To reconstruct the connector region without symmetry, we took the 3D class used for the connector initial model (described above) and first performed focused local refinement to improve the alignment of particle images to the C5 capsid vertex. C5 symmetry expansion was performed, followed by 3D classification with a focus mask placed on the portal region. This yielded various 3D classes with C12 portals, and the C5 capsid regions were resolved. The C6 connector region was still blurred, as this portal of the tail is attached to the tail after independent assembly and can correspondingly be attached in two positions rotated 30° relative to one another. Accordingly, 3D classification was performed on an arbitrary class from the C12 focused 3D classification, now using a focus mask placed on the C6 connector region. Several classes emerged with C5, C12, and C6 features resolved, allowing us to interpret interactions between up-whiskers and the capsid. The best 3D class was selected and submitted to local refinement, yielding a final map resolution of 7.4 Å. The final asymmetric reconstruction particle set was truncated because the symmetry breakage between the capsid and tail creates rotameric species that cannot be integrated into a single asymmetric reconstruction (similar to observations made with the φM1 ejectosome (69). Correspondingly, 4770 particle images were used in the asymmetric reconstruction.

3D classification of long and STFs

To evaluate the occupancy of long and STFs, symmetry expansion was performed on C3 baseplate particles, which were then submitted to 3D classification with a focus mask placed on LTFs, STFs, and LTF platforms. Output volumes were then inspected for fiber occupancy in ChimeraX.

Near-atomic model building, refinement, and validation

Initial models were generated using AlphaFold 2 via the Google Colab platform (70) and, more recently, AlphaFold 3 via the AlphaFold 3 server (51). Models were rigid body fit into density using ChimeraX (v1.9) Fit-To-Map. Manual model refinement was performed in Coot (v0.9.5) and ISOLDE (v1.9) for all protein chains, followed by refinement in Phenix_real_space_refine (40, 71, 72). Phenix validation reports can be found in tables S6 to S10.

Felixounavirus homolog analysis and sequence conservation mapping

Felixounavirus species selected for interpretation were identified first through the ICTV classification of the Felixounavirus genus (11). Genome sequences of candidates were then identified through the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) taxonomy browser (GenBank accessions listed in tables S1 and S2). Homologs were identified for each candidate virus based on genome synteny, gene length, and short identical sequences (identified manually) when compared to our Bas63 structural module. All homologs of each gene product were then grouped and aligned via the Clustal Omega multisequence alignment web-based tool to validate putative homology (73). Sequence identity matrices output by Clustal Omega were then formatted and merged into a .csv table, which was then submitted to a custom Python script that converted the data into a NumPy array and plotted it as a heatmap using the Seaborn module in Python V3.9 (74–76). All homolog genes used in our heatmap are described by their GenBank accessions and individual gene locus tags in tables S1 and S2.

Alignment files generated through Clustal Omega were also used to map sequence conservation to LTF, STF, and Hex-Dec proteins (Fig. 5, B and C) using the ChimeraX commands “colour byattribute seq_conservation” and “worm seq_conservation.” This work did not involve human subject research nor animal research.
