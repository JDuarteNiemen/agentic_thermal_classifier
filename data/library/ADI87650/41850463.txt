NPmut- and M2e-decorated T4 phage nanoparticles induce mucosal immunity and cross-group protection against influenza A virus.

Inducing cross-group protection against diverse influenza A virus (IAV) subtypes remains a major
goal in next-generation influenza vaccine development. Nucleoprotein (NP) and the extracellular
domain of matrix protein 2 (M2e) are attractive conserved targets, but face distinct hurdles: NP co-
purified from Escherichia coli often contains pro-inflammatory bacterial RNA, raising safety
concerns, while its nucleic-acid-binding-deficient version (NPmut) improves safety but suffers from
reduced immunogenicity. In addition, M2e alone is weakly immunogenic. To address these limitations,
we developed a dual-antigen T4 phage-based nanoparticle vaccine by assembling an NPmut-3M2e fusion
protein, containing three tandem M2e segments (3M2e), onto T4 phage capsids. Adjuvant-free
intranasal immunization in mice elicited robust mucosal and systemic immunity, including high sIgA
and IgG titers and potent CD8+ T-cell responses. It also conferred significant cross-group
protection against lethal challenge with IAV strains from distinct hemagglutinin (HA) groups, as
evidenced by improved survival, reduced body weight loss, and attenuated lung pathology. These
findings support intranasal NPmut-3M2e-T4 VLP as a promising vaccine candidate that combines
conserved cross-group antigens with a T4-based delivery platform to achieve cross-group protection
against IAV.
