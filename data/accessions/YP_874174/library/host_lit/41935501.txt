Tracing the molecular evolution of essential GTPase Obg CgtA reveals a direct association of its C-terminal domain with the diversity of bacterial phyla.

The GTPase Obg CgtA is a multifunctional essential bacterial protein consisting of conserved
N-terminal Obg and GTPase domains and a variable C-Terminal Domain (CgtA-CTD). Using a large-scale
bioinformatic analysis of CgtA sequences from 737 bacterial reviewed strains from different species,
we undertook a phylogenetic study of this important protein. Our results reveal positive
correlations between the length of CgtA and phylogeny and evolution. Specifically, early-diverging
phyla predominantly retain an ordered CgtA-CTD structure with a unique fold identified from the
crystal structure of CgtA from Thermus thermophilus, called Obg C-Terminal fold or OCT-fold;
whereas, later-evolving phyla, containing primarily Gram-negative bacteria, exhibit a notable
absence of OCT-like fold and a significant overall loss of amino acid length at CTD. This loss is
also observed in endosymbiotic candidates Cyanobacteria and Alphaproteobacteria, linked to the
origin of eukaryotic organelles. Furthermore, the presence of OCT-like folds in chloroplastic CgtA-
homologs, but their absence in mitochondrial and cytoplasmic counterparts, highlights distinct
evolutionary paths. These observations collectively emphasize the evolutionary significance of CgtA,
particularly its CTD and its role in microbial adaptation, offering insights into bacterial
evolution and the origin of eukaryotic organelles. Thus, the study reveals that structural
variability at CgtA-CTD is non-random and likely linked to bacterial phylogeny, cellular
architecture, and evolutionary history suggesting CgtA as a potential molecular marker for exploring
protein evolution.
