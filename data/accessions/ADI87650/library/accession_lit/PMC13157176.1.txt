Background

Acute kidney injury (AKI) secondary to rhabdomyolysis is a life-threatening clinical condition characterized by oxidative stress, inflammation, and tubular cell apoptosis. Effective pharmacological treatments targeting these pathophysiological cascades remain limited.

Objective

This study aimed to investigate the nephroprotective effects of the natural monoterpene L-carvone in a murine model of glycerol-induced rhabdomyolysis-associated AKI.

Methods

Fifty male BALB/c mice were assigned to five groups (n = 10/group): control, rhabdomyolysis (50% glycerol, 10 mL/kg), and three groups receiving prophylactic oral L-carvone (25, 50, or 100 mg/kg) for five days prior to glycerol administration. Renal function was evaluated via blood urea nitrogen (BUN), creatinine, and myoglobin. Tubular injury (KIM-1, NGAL), inflammatory gene expression (IL-1β, TNF-α, NF-κB), apoptotic protein expression (BAX, BCL-2, caspase-8, cleaved caspase-3), and renal histopathology were analyzed.

Results

L-carvone pretreatment significantly lowered serum BUN, creatinine, and myoglobin levels compared to the untreated rhabdomyolysis group. Furthermore, L-carvone markedly reduced the expression of tubular injury biomarkers KIM-1 and NGAL. RT-qPCR analysis demonstrated that L-carvone substantially decreased mRNA levels of pro-inflammatory mediators IL-1β, TNF-α, and NF-κB. Western blot analysis revealed a strong anti-apoptotic shift, characterized by decreased BAX, caspase-8, and cleaved caspase-3, alongside significantly increased BCL-2. These molecular improvements strongly correlated with significantly preserved renal histopathological architecture.

Conclusion

L-carvone provides robust renal protection against glycerol-induced rhabdomyolysis by attenuating key inflammatory and apoptotic signaling pathways. These preclinical findings highlight its therapeutic potential as a promising candidate for the management of rhabdomyolysis-associated AKI.

1 Introduction

Acute kidney injury (AKI) is a severe and often life-threatening condition, commonly resulting from rhabdomyolysis (RM), a syndrome characterized by the breakdown of skeletal muscle fibers and the release of intracellular components, notably myoglobin, into the bloodstream [1], [2]. Myoglobin-induced nephrotoxicity plays a central role in the pathogenesis of RM-associated AKI, promoting renal tubular obstruction, oxidative stress, and direct cytotoxicity [3], [4]. The incidence of AKI in RM varies widely, affecting up to 50% of patients, depending on etiology and comorbidities [5].

The mechanisms underlying RM-induced AKI are multifactorial and include intratubular cast formation, renal vasoconstriction, and inflammatory responses. Myoglobin filtration leads to oxidative injury through free radical generation, particularly via iron-catalyzed Fenton reactions [6]. This oxidative burden is further compounded by inflammation, which involves upregulation of cytokines such as interleukin-1β (IL-1β), tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α), and activation of nuclear factor-kappa B (NF-κB) signaling pathways [7], [8].

Alongside oxidative stress and inflammation, programmed cell death plays a critical role in the pathophysiology of RM-associated AKI [9], [10]. The toxic accumulation of myoglobin directly triggers mitochondrial dysfunction and activates apoptotic cascades within the renal tubules [11]. This apoptotic process is highly regulated by the BCL-2 protein family, notably the pro-apoptotic BAX and anti-apoptotic BCL-2 proteins, culminating in the activation of executioner caspases [12]. Therefore, mitigating this tubular cell apoptosis is a vital therapeutic target for preventing irreversible renal damage [13].

Current management of RM-induced AKI relies mainly on supportive care, particularly aggressive intravenous hydration to maintain renal perfusion and facilitate myoglobin clearance. However, this approach does not target the key pathophysiological pathways—oxidative stress and inflammation—that drive renal damage [14]. Therefore, there is a pressing need for novel therapeutic strategies that address these mechanisms to limit renal injury and enhance recovery [15].

In this context, natural compounds have emerged as promising candidates for renal protection. Among them, carvone—a monoterpene ketone found in the essential oils of plants such as caraway and spearmint—has attracted interest due to its broad pharmacological activities, including anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial effects [16], [17]. Preclinical studies have demonstrated that carvone can inhibit inflammatory cytokine production, modulate redox balance, and protect against various forms of tissue injury [18], [19], [20].

The structural-activity rationale for selecting carvone is strongly supported by the broader landscape of natural product pharmacology [14]. Previous investigations have demonstrated the profound nephroprotective potential of structurally related monoterpenes—such as thymoquinone, eugenol [14], and notably citronellol [21], [22]—in identical murine models of glycerol-induced rhabdomyolysis. Furthermore, our prior work has established the pharmacological utility of L-carvone as a nephroprotective agent in a lipopolysaccharide-induced murine model of septic acute kidney injury, demonstrating highly analogous mechanisms of action involving the suppression of Toll-like receptor 4, NF-κB, and IL-1β [23]. Acknowledging that structurally related terpenoids share these mechanistic pathways [16], [17], evaluating carvone's specific efficacy in the setting of RM-induced AKI represents the next logical candidate for investigation.

Building on this pharmacological foundation, we hypothesize that L-carvone will exert a potent and direct nephroprotective effect against myoglobinuric kidney injury by targeting specific pathophysiological cascades triggered by rhabdomyolysis. Because intratubular myoglobin accumulation drives massive oxidative stress, subsequent NF-κB-mediated inflammation, and mitochondrial-dependent apoptosis in renal epithelial cells [10], [11], [4], we postulate that L-carvone will successfully mitigate acute tubular necrosis. Specifically, we hypothesize that L-carvone prevents irreversible renal damage by suppressing the transcription of pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1β, TNF-α) and shifting the cellular survival balance to prevent programmed cell death (modulating the BCL-2/BAX axis and inhibiting executioner caspases) [16], [23], [19].

To test this expanded hypothesis, the present study aims to investigate the nephroprotective potential of carvone in a murine model of glycerol-induced rhabdomyolysis. Specifically, we evaluated the effects of varying carvone doses on renal function, tubular injury biomarkers, inflammatory markers, apoptotic proteins, and histopathological changes. By demonstrating these protective mechanisms, we aim to position L-carvone as a novel and effective therapeutic candidate for the management of RM-associated AKI.

2 Methods

2.1 Materials

L-carvone (Car) was purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (St. Louis, USA). Glycerol (Gly) was obtained from Merck (Germany). Corn oil was acquired from Macklin (China). The biochemical kits for serum creatinine and urea were purchased from Elabscience (Wuhan, China). The ELISA kits for Creatine Kinase, Muscle / CK-MM (Catalog No: E-EL-M0358), Kidney Injury Molecule 1 / KIM-1 (Catalog No: E-EL-M3039), and Neutrophil Gelatinase-Associated Lipocalin / NGAL (Catalog No: E-EL-M0828) were manufactured by Elabscience (Wuhan, China). The ELISA kit for Myoglobin (Catalogue No.: EM1223) was produced by FineTest (Wuhan, China). The SV Total RNA Isolation System and GoTaq® 2-Step RT-qPCR System were purchased from Promega (Madison, WI, USA). PCR primers for GADPH, NF-κB, IL-1β, and TNF-α were synthesized and purchased from Eurofins Genomics (Montreal, Quebec, Canada). Antibodies against BAX (D-AB-10386L), BCL-2 (E-AB-93166), caspase-8 (E-AB-63533), cleaved caspase-3 (E-AB-93368), and beta-actin (E-AB-40338) were obtained from Elabscience (Wuhan, China).

2.2 Ethical statement

The Research Ethical Committee of the University of Baghdad, College of Pharmacy, approved the study; all tests were conducted with approval number “RECUBCP022025113A,” dated 7 March 2025. The study follows the principles and guidelines of the Office International des Épizooties on animal ethics. The methods used on the animals fully comply with regional and international regulations governing the ethical treatment and use of laboratory animals. ‎The authors complied with the ARRIVE 2.0 guidelines [24].

2.3 Animals and animal care

A total of 50 male BALB/c albino mice (weighing 25–32 g, aged 9–12 weeks) were obtained from the College of Pharmacy at the University of Baghdad and kept under standard conditions of temperature, humidity, and light/dark cycle. The mice had free access to water and a standard diet. A one-week acclimation period was allowed before initiating the treatments. All care and animal handling were in accordance with the ethical committee of the pharmacy college at the University of Baghdad, as mentioned above.

This study follows the AVMA 2020 Guidelines for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals [25]. Efforts were made to minimize animal suffering and distress. All procedures adhered to the principles of refinement, reduction, and replacement. Animals were monitored closely immediately after the injection of investigational drugs and throughout the following days (any bleeding was promptly controlled, and the injection site was disinfected with sterile gauze and water). If signs of infection appeared, a veterinarian was consulted to evaluate the animal's health and determine if it could continue in the study.

2.4 Experimental protocol

"The animals were randomly assigned into five groups, each consisting of 10 mice, utilizing a computer-generated randomization sequence: (I) A vehicle control group received 1 mL/kg of corn oil (the solvent vehicle used for L-carvone) by gavage for 5 consecutive days. To ensure baseline comparability, this group underwent the same 24-hour water deprivation on day 5, followed by an intramuscular injection of saline to an equivalent total systemic dose of 10 mL/kg. (II) Rhabdomyolysis was induced in the mice by 24 h of water deprivation (Rhabdomyolysis group), followed by an intramuscular injection of 50% glycerol to a total systemic dose of 10 mL/kg [21], [22]. (III) The treatment group received L-carvone at doses of 25 mg/kg orally via gavage for 5 consecutive days (Low-dose Car group), combined with water deprivation and an intramuscular injection of 50% glycerol at a total systemic dose of 10 mL/kg on day 5. (IV) The treatment group received L-carvone at 50 mg/kg for 5 consecutive days (Medium-dose Car group), combined with water deprivation and an intramuscular injection of 50% glycerol at a total systemic dose of 10 mL/kg on day 5. (V) The treatment group received L-carvone at 100 mg/kg for 5 consecutive days (High-dose Car group), combined with water deprivation and an intramuscular injection of 50% glycerol at a total systemic dose of 10 mL/kg on day 5. The specific dosage range of L-carvone (25, 50, and 100 mg/kg/day) was deliberately selected based on previous preclinical investigations demonstrating its optimal safety profile and profound anti-inflammatory and cytoprotective efficacy in analogous murine models of acute tissue injury [26], [27].

To adhere to strict veterinary welfare guidelines, capping murine intramuscular injections at 50 µL per site, the total systemic dose of 10 mL/kg for both glycerol and saline injections (approximately 300 µL per animal) was fractionated [28]. The total volume was equally divided into six separate 50 µL micro-injections administered across distinct, independent muscle compartments in both hindlimbs (e.g., bilateral quadriceps, gastrocnemius, and tibialis anterior). This multi-site fractionation protocol prevents gross hydrostatic tissue tearing, avoids acute compartment syndrome, and ensures the resulting rhabdomyolysis is primarily driven by glycerol-induced chemical myotoxicity rather than massive mechanical trauma.

Twenty-four hours after the glycerol injection, all mice were deeply anesthetized via an intraperitoneal injection of sodium pentobarbital (70 mg/kg) to reach a surgical plane of anesthesia while preserving cardiac output [29]. Once a deep surgical plane was confirmed (lack of pedal withdrawal reflex), blood was collected from the retro-orbital sinus and centrifuged to obtain serum. Immediately following successful blood collection, the animals were humanely euthanized via cervical dislocation to ensure death prior to tissue harvesting [30]. Both the right and left kidneys were carefully excised, rinsed with cold saline to remove blood residues, and processed for biochemical, molecular, and histological analyses after the animal was sacrificed.

Only healthy male BALB/c albino mice exhibiting normal baseline behavior and weighing within the specified narrow range of 25–32 g after acclimation were included in the study. Animals were prospectively excluded from the final analysis if they met any of the following criteria: (1) abnormal baseline weight fluctuations, defined as a loss of more than 10% of body weight during the acclimation period; (2) failure to adequately develop the rhabdomyolysis model after glycerol injection, as indicated by a lack of significant serum CK-MM or myoglobin elevation compared to control averages; (3) early mortality or morbidity unrelated to the primary experimental endpoints, such as anesthetic complications or gavage-induced trauma; or (4) obvious signs of unalleviated distress requiring early humane euthanasia, as determined by the attending veterinarian [24].

2.5 Biochemical tests

Biochemical tests were performed on serum samples from all groups to evaluate renal function. Serum creatinine and urea concentrations were measured spectrophotometrically according to the manufacturer’s protocols. The procedure for creatinine is based upon a modification of the original picrate reaction (Jaffe method) [31], while urea concentration was determined utilizing the Berthelot method [32]. To align with standardized clinical reporting metrics, all measured serum urea concentrations (mg/dL) were subsequently converted to Blood Urea Nitrogen (BUN) values (mg/dL) by dividing the raw urea values by a stoichiometric conversion factor of 2.14, representing the molecular weight ratio of the entire urea molecule to its dual nitrogen atoms.

2.6 Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA)

Following the assessment of standard renal clearance parameters, specific serum biomarkers indicating skeletal muscle breakdown and subsequent renal tubular injury were quantified utilizing commercially available, highly specific Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay (ELISA) kits.

To evaluate the extent of rhabdomyolysis, serum concentrations of Creatine Kinase, Muscle (CK-MM) were determined using a mouse-specific Sandwich-ELISA kit. The assay demonstrated an intra-assay coefficient of variation (CV) strictly maintained below 10%, a minimum detectable sensitivity of 0.94 ng/mL, and a dynamic detection range of 1.56–100 ng/mL. Similarly, serum myoglobin levels were quantified using a Sandwich-ELISA kit with a sensitivity of 0.938 ng/mL and a dynamic detection range of 1.563–100 ng/mL.

To directly assess the severity of acute tubular injury, two highly sensitive structural biomarkers were measured. Kidney Injury Molecule-1 (KIM-1) concentrations were determined using a dedicated Sandwich-ELISA kit with a proven sensitivity of 0.09 ng/mL and a detection range of 0.16–10 ng/mL. Neutrophil Gelatinase-Associated Lipocalin (NGAL) levels were concurrently quantified utilizing a Sandwich-ELISA kit exhibiting a sensitivity of 23.44 pg/mL and a detection range of 39.06–2500 pg/mL.

For all ELISA procedures, microplates pre-coated with strictly specific targeting antibodies were utilized. All experimental procedures, washing steps, and incubation times were carried out in strict accordance with the manufacturers' manuals. The optical density (OD) of the final reactions was measured spectrophotometrically at 450 nm, and the absolute biomarker concentrations in the experimental serum samples were mathematically derived by plotting their OD values against the concurrently generated standard curves [33], [34], [35].

2.7 Two-step quantitative real-time PCR (RT-qPCR)

Total RNA was extracted from kidney tissue using the SV Total RNA Isolation System (Promega, Madison, WI, USA). Briefly, tissue samples were homogenized in RNA Lysis Buffer containing beta-mercaptoethanol. The lysate was mixed with RNA Dilution Buffer, incubated at 70°C for 3 min, and centrifuged at 12,000–14,000 x g for 10 min. Following the addition of 95% ethanol, the cleared lysate was transferred to a Spin Column Assembly and centrifuged. To prevent genomic DNA contamination, a freshly prepared DNase incubation mix (40 µl Yellow Core Buffer, 5 µl 0.09 M MnCl2, and 5 µl DNase I) was applied directly to the silica membrane and incubated for 15 min at room temperature. The reaction was halted with DNase Stop Solution, followed by washes with RNA Wash Solution. Finally, the purified total RNA was eluted with 100 µL Nuclease-Free Water. RNA concentration and purity were assessed spectrophotometrically by measuring absorbance at 260 nm and 280 nm, and samples with an A260/A280 ratio between 1.8 and 2.0 were considered acceptable for further analysis.

First-strand complementary DNA (cDNA) was synthesized using the EasyScript® First-Strand cDNA Synthesis SuperMix (TransGen Biotech, China). For each sample, a 20 µL reaction mixture containing 1 µL random primer (N9), 10 µL 2 × ES reaction mix, 1 µL EasyScript® RT/RI enzyme mix, 3 µL RNase-free water, and 5 µL total RNA was prepared. Reverse transcription was performed at 25 °C for 11 min, followed by 42 °C for 35 min, and enzyme inactivation at 85 °C for 5 min. The synthesized cDNA was stored at −20 °C until quantitative PCR analysis. All reactions were performed in duplicate [36].

Quantitative real‑time PCR was carried out using GoTaq® qPCR Master Mix (Promega, USA) on a SaCycler‑96 Real‑Time PCR System (Sacace, Italy). Each PCR reaction was prepared in a final volume of 25 µL containing 10 µL of 2 × GoTaq® qPCR Master Mix, 1 µL of forward primer (10 pmol/µL), 1 µL of reverse primer (10 pmol/µL), 2 µL of cDNA template, and 11 µL of nuclease-free water. Gene-specific primers were used for nuclear factor kappa B (NF-κB), interleukin-1 beta (IL-1β), tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α), and glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase (GAPDH), which was selected as the reference gene (GAPDH), based on its stable expression across experimental groups. Primer sequences were designed based on mouse reference mRNA sequences obtained from the NCBI GenBank database (TNF-α: NM_013693.3; NF-κB: NM_008689.2; IL-1β: NM_008361.4; GAPDH: NM_008084.3) and validated in silico using NCBI BLAST to confirm gene‑specific amplification. The primer sequences are as follows: NF-κB (F: AAGACAAGGAGCAGGACATG, R: AGCAACATCTTCACATCCC), IL-1β (F: TGCCACCTTTTGACAGTGATG, R: TGATGTGCTGCTGCGAGATT), TNF-α (F: TAGCCCACGTCGTAGCAAAC, R: ACAAGGTACAACCCATCGGC), and GAPDH (F: CGGGTTCCTATAAATACGGACTG, R: CCAATACGGCCAAATCCGTTC). Primers were designed to span exon–exon junctions whenever possible to minimize amplification of genomic DNA [36].

PCR amplification was performed with an initial denaturation step at 95 °C for 2 min, followed by 40 cycles consisting of denaturation at 95 °C for 15 s and annealing/extension at 60 °C for 30 s. A melt‑curve analysis was conducted immediately after amplification by gradually increasing the temperature from 65 °C to 95 °C, with increments of 0.5 °C every 5 s, to confirm amplification specificity. The presence of a single sharp melt‑curve peak indicated specific amplification of the target genes. No‑template controls were included in each run to exclude reagent contamination. No‑reverse transcription controls were included to exclude genomic DNA contamination. Relative gene expression levels were calculated using the 2⁻ΔΔCt method. The ΔCt value was determined by subtracting the Ct value of the reference gene (GAPDH) from that of the target gene, while ΔΔCt was calculated by subtracting the mean ΔCt of the control group from the ΔCt of each experimental sample [37].

2.8 Western blot analysis

Kidney tissue samples were rinsed with cold PBS (pH 7.0), dried, and homogenized in RIPA lysis buffer supplemented with phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride (PMSF) and sodium orthovanadate (Na3VO4) in the following ratio: 0.3 g tissue to 1 mL RIPA lysis buffer and 10 µL of inhibitors. The homogenate was centrifuged at 14,000 x g for 10 min at 4°C, and the supernatant was collected. Total protein concentration was determined using a Bicinchoninic Acid (BCA) Protein Assay Kit (Elabscience, USA) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Protein samples were mixed with SDS loading buffer, denatured at 92°C for 10 min, and 10 μL of protein sample per lane was separated on a 10% SDS-polyacrylamide gel with a 5% stacking gel [38]. Electrophoresis was conducted using the Mini-Protean Tetra System (Bio-Rad) at 80 V for the stacking phase and 120 V for the separating phase. Proteins were then transferred onto methanol-activated polyvinylidene difluoride (PVDF) membranes using a semi-dry transfer system at 25 V for 30 min [39].

To prevent nonspecific binding, the membranes were blocked with 5% non-fat skim milk in TBST for 1.5 h at room temperature. Subsequently, the membranes were incubated overnight at 4°C with primary antibodies against Bax, Bcl-2, Caspase-8, cleaved Caspase-3, and beta-actin. After washing three times with TBST for 10 min each, the membranes were incubated with horseradish peroxidase (HRP)-conjugated secondary antibodies (Elabscience, 1:10,000) for 1 h at room temperature. Following three additional washes with TBST for 7 min each, protein expression signals were visualized using Enhanced Chemiluminescence (ECL) detection reagents (Elabscience, USA) and imaged using a ChemiDoc MP Imaging System (Bio-Rad Laboratories, USA). Densitometric scanning of band intensities was quantified using ImageJ software (NIH) and normalized to beta-actin levels, which served as the internal control for the analysis [40].

2.9 Determination of histopathology

Following euthanasia, the kidneys were immediately excised, rinsed with cold saline, and fixed in 10% neutral buffered formalin for 24–48 h. After fixation, tissues were dehydrated through a graded ethanol series, cleared in xylene, and embedded in paraffin wax. Sections of 4–5 µm thickness were cut using a microtome and mounted on glass slides. The sections were then stained with hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) for general histopathological evaluation.

Kidney sections were examined under a light microscope by a blinded histopathologist. The degree of renal injury was assessed semi-quantitatively based on morphological changes, including tubular epithelial cell necrosis, tubular dilation, cast formation, interstitial edema, and inflammatory cell infiltration. To obtain a semiquantitative measure of renal damage and the therapeutic efficacy of L-carvone, the Jablonski scoring system was used [41]. This scoring system ranges from 0 to 4, where 0 indicates normal renal architecture, and 4 represents extensive (>75%) tubular necrosis and cortical destruction. Intermediate grades correspond to necrosis involving < 10% (score 1), 10–25% (score 2), and 26–75% (score 3) of the cortical tubules.

2.10 Sample size calculation

In accordance with the published guidelines for experimental design and analysis, an a priori sample size calculation was performed using G*Power software (version 3.1.9.7) to ensure adequate statistical power while strictly adhering to the Reduction principle of the 3Rs [42]. The calculation was designed to prevent Type II errors when evaluating the primary continuous variables (e.g., serum creatinine, BUN, and KIM-1) across five independent groups utilizing a one-way ANOVA framework. Based on previous analogous studies investigating natural monoterpenes in murine AKI models, we anticipated a large effect size (Cohen's f ≥ 0.40) [43], [21], [44]. To detect a biologically significant difference of at least 30% in primary renal injury markers between the rhabdomyolysis induction group and the treatment groups, assuming a standard deviation of approximately 15–20% of the mean, an alpha (α) error probability of 0.05, and a target statistical power (1-β) of 0.80, the a priori power analysis power analysis determined that a minimum of 8 animals per group was required. To account for potential attrition and unexpected mortality inherent to the severe 50% glycerol-induced rhabdomyolysis model, the final sample size was conservatively set at n = 10 per group. This sample size provides robust capabilities for both parametric and nonparametric statistical testing without using an excessive number of animals.

2.11 Statistical analysis

Data were expressed as the mean ± standard error of the mean (SEM), except for the Jablonski histological scoring data, which were presented as the median and interquartile range (IQR). To minimize investigator bias, the researchers performing the biochemical assays, molecular quantification, and subsequent data analyses were blinded to the animal group assignments. Statistical analyses were conducted using GraphPad Prism version 11.0. The choice of statistical test was strictly based on data normality, as determined by the Anderson-Darling test. For normally distributed continuous data, multiple comparisons between groups were performed using an ordinary one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA). Post-hoc analyses following the ANOVA utilized Tukey's multiple comparisons test (for BUN, BAX, BCL-2, the BAX/BCL-2 ratio, caspase-8, and cleaved caspase-3) or Holm-Šídák's multiple comparisons test (for TNF-α and NF-κB) to identify specific group differences. For non-parametric data (including serum creatinine, myoglobin, CK-MM, KIM-1, NGAL, IL-1β, and Jablonski histological scores), the Kruskal-Wallis test was applied, followed by Dunn's multiple comparisons test for post-hoc analysis. A p-value of < 0.05 was deemed statistically significant across all tests.

3 Results

3.1 Impact of L-carvone on kidney function and muscle injury markers

There was a significant elevation in BUN, serum creatinine, myoglobin, and CK-MM levels in rhabdomyolysis-induced mice compared with normal controls. BUN levels rose sharply from 8.05 mg/dL in the control to 108.00 mg/dL in the rhabdomyolysis group (p < 0.001) (Fig. 1A). Treatment with low, medium, and high-dose L-carvone significantly lowered BUN levels to 41.64 mg/dL, 26.77 mg/dL, and 16.73 mg/dL, respectively. Specifically, the mean serum creatinine levels increased from 0.29 mg/dL in the control group to 1.41 mg/dL in the rhabdomyolysis group (p < 0.001) (Fig. 1B). Low-dose L-carvone treatment significantly reduced creatinine levels to 0.74 mg/dL compared to the rhabdomyolysis group (p < 0.001). Medium-dose L-carvone (50 mg/kg) treatment led to a substantial reduction in serum creatinine levels to 0.49 mg/dL, highlighting its protective efficacy. Regarding muscle injury, myoglobin concentrations were significantly elevated (1.32 vs 8.45 ng/mL) in the rhabdomyolysis group compared to controls, confirming severe muscular damage (Fig. 1C). Serum myoglobin levels decreased following L-carvone treatment; the low-dose group reduced myoglobin levels to 5.20 ng/mL, while the medium- and high-dose groups achieved more substantial, statistically significant decreases to 3.15 ng/mL and 1.66 ng/mL, respectively.

Furthermore, mice subjected to glycerol-induced rhabdomyolysis exhibited significantly elevated CK-MM levels (13.24 vs 28.53 ng/mL) (Fig. 1D). Administration of L-carvone dose-dependently ameliorated muscle injury. The low-dose L-carvone (25 mg/kg) group displayed a significant reduction in CK-MM levels to 15.96 ng/mL. The medium-dose (50 mg/kg) and high-dose (100 mg/kg) L-carvone groups showed more pronounced decreases in CK-MM levels (7.093 and 10.91 ng/mL, respectively) compared to the rhabdomyolysis group. This underscores its efficacy in preserving sarcolemmal integrity and reducing muscle protein leakage.

3.2 Effect of L-carvone on tubular injury biomarkers

Kidney injury molecule (KIM) levels spiked significantly in the rhabdomyolysis group (2.29 pg/mL) compared to the control group (0.841 pg/mL) (Fig. 2A). L-carvone administration curbed this increase, with the low, medium, and high-dose groups exhibiting mean KIM levels of 1.23 pg/mL, 0.76 pg/mL, and 1.21 pg/mL, respectively. NGAL concentrations also followed this pattern of tubular injury, increasing from 359.6 pg/mL in controls to 1184.0 pg/mL in the rhabdomyolysis group (p < 0.001) (Fig. 2B), while L-carvone treatment attenuated this rise (411.3 pg/mL, 240.7 pg/mL, and 347.6 pg/mL in the low, medium, and high-dose groups, respectively). 3.3 Effect of L-carvone on inflammation markers in kidney tissue

The induction of rhabdomyolysis triggered a profound inflammatory response in renal tissue, as evidenced by a marked spike in pro-inflammatory cytokines (Fig. 3). Specifically, IL-1β expression was significantly upregulated in the rhabdomyolysis group, reaching a mean of 6.62, compared with 1.02 in the normal control group (p = 0.0001). 4 Discussion

Acute kidney injury (AKI) is a critical clinical condition characterized by a rapid decline in renal function, leading to the accumulation of metabolic waste products, electrolyte disturbances, and fluid imbalance. The etiology of AKI is multifactorial and can be classified into prerenal (e.g., hypovolemia, hypotension), intrinsic renal (e.g., acute tubular necrosis, acute interstitial nephritis), and postrenal causes (e.g., urinary tract obstruction) [45].

Among the intrinsic causes, rhabdomyolysis is a well-recognized contributor to AKI, particularly in conditions involving trauma, prolonged immobilization, excessive physical exertion, infections, or exposure to nephrotoxic agents. Rhabdomyolysis involves the breakdown of skeletal muscle fibers and the subsequent release of intracellular components into the circulation, notably myoglobin, which plays a central role in the pathogenesis of renal injury [46], [45]. Myoglobin accumulation within the renal tubules contributes to obstruction, direct cytotoxicity, and the generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS), ultimately leading to acute tubular damage. The pathophysiological cascade also includes renal vasoconstriction, volume depletion due to fluid sequestration in damaged muscle, and the inflammatory response—all of which synergistically exacerbate renal dysfunction [2], [47]. Therefore, early recognition and management are essential to limit kidney damage.

The efficacy and severity of the glycerol-induced rhabdomyolysis model utilized in this study were robustly validated by the profound deterioration in systemic renal function markers. Specifically, the induction model successfully elicited massive, characteristic spikes in blood urea nitrogen (BUN) exceeding 100 mg/dL (mean 108.00 mg/dL), along with highly significant elevations in serum creatinine. These profound biochemical anomalies are entirely consistent with the severe clinical trajectory of established myoglobinuric acute kidney injury. The dose-dependent restoration of these functional baselines following L-carvone administration indicates potent preservation of overall renal clearance and glomerular filtration dynamics, an effect strictly secondary to protection of the downstream tubular architecture from toxic pigment accumulation.

There are different current treatment strategies for rhabdomyolysis-associated AKI; however, the evidence supporting these approaches remains controversial and inconsistent [48], [49]. Recent studies have explored adjunctive therapies that target the underlying oxidative and inflammatory pathways across various murine models of tissue injury [50], [51], [52], [53], [15]. Such findings offer promising directions for developing novel nephroprotective strategies beyond conventional fluid management. An increase in BUN and serum creatinine concentration is a primary diagnostic criterion for AKI, as defined by international guidelines [54]. While elevated BUN and creatinine indicate a severe decline in filtration capacity, myoglobin is particularly important in identifying rhabdomyolysis-induced AKI, offering both diagnostic and mechanistic information [55], [56]. In this study, L-carvone administration at all tested doses (25, 50, and 100 mg/kg) resulted in a highly significant decrease in BUN, creatinine, and myoglobin compared with the untreated glycerol-induced group.

KIM-1 and NGAL provide earlier and more direct evidence of tubular cell injury [57], [58]. In this study, glycerol-induced rhabdomyolysis significantly elevated BUN, creatinine, and myoglobin levels, as well as KIM-1 and NGAL in kidney tissue, confirming substantial tubular injury. Treatment with L-carvone at 25, 50, and 100 mg significantly reduced the expression of all markers, indicating a protective effect on renal tubular epithelium. This outcome is likely attributed to the antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties of L-carvone, which help attenuate oxidative stress and inflammatory cascades associated with rhabdomyolysis-induced AKI.

Comparable nephroprotective effects have been observed with other natural compounds. For instance, monoterpenes like thymol and eugenol have demonstrated protective effects in renal injury models by reducing KIM-1 and NGAL expression and modulating oxidative pathways [59], [14]. Therefore, the current findings suggest that L-carvone may offer a promising therapeutic strategy for rhabdomyolysis-associated AKI.

Inflammation is a central contributor to the pathogenesis of acute kidney injury (AKI), with cytokines such as tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α), interleukin-1 beta (IL-1β), and the transcription factor nuclear factor-kappa B (NF-κB) playing pivotal roles in initiating and amplifying renal injury [60]. These mediators are rapidly upregulated in response to cellular stress, ischemia, or nephrotoxic insults, and are directly involved in leukocyte recruitment, oxidative stress, and apoptosis in renal tissues [61], [62].

In the current study, mice subjected to glycerol-induced rhabdomyolysis exhibited a significant increase in the renal mRNA expression of TNF-α, IL-1β, and NF-κB, confirming an inflammatory response associated with muscle breakdown and renal injury. Treatment with L-carvone at all 25, 50, and 100 mg/kg significantly reduced the expression levels of all three markers, suggesting potent anti-inflammatory activity. These findings are consistent with previous studies demonstrating that carvone and related monoterpenes inhibit NF-κB activation and suppress cytokine production in models of inflammation and tissue injury [63], [17], [19], [20]. Thus, the anti-inflammatory effect of carvone likely contributes to its nephroprotective action by attenuating cytokine-driven damage, reducing immune cell infiltration, and preserving tubular structure and function.

Apoptosis plays a critical role in the pathophysiology of acute kidney injury (AKI), particularly in tubular epithelial cell loss and the progression of renal dysfunction [9], [12]. In the present study, glycerol-induced rhabdomyolysis significantly increased BAX expression and decreased BCL-2 expression in kidney tissue, resulting in a markedly elevated BAX/BCL-2 ratio—a molecular signature of apoptosis activation in addition to increased expression of casp-8 and cleaved-casp3. These findings align with prior research demonstrating that myoglobin and oxidative stress generated during rhabdomyolysis can directly trigger mitochondrial-mediated apoptosis in renal tubular cells [10], [11]. Interestingly, treatment with L-carvone at both doses (50 and 100 mg) effectively reduced BAX expression, restored BCL-2 levels, and consequently lowered the BAX/BCL-2 ratio, in addition to lower casp-8 and cleaved-casp3 expression, indicating a protective, anti-apoptotic effect. These outcomes are supported by previous studies showing that carvone can inhibit pro-apoptotic signaling and preserve mitochondrial integrity in models of oxidative injury [16], [64]. The anti-apoptotic action of carvone likely complements its antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects, contributing synergistically to renal protection in the context of AKI. Histopathological examination remains a cornerstone in confirming AKI, as it provides direct visual evidence of structural damage to renal tissues.

Central to the pathogenesis of rhabdomyolysis-associated AKI is myoglobin-induced Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN) [13], [65]. Upon massive skeletal muscle breakdown, circulating myoglobin freely filters through the glomerulus—leaving the glomerular tuft itself structurally intact—and rapidly concentrates within the downstream tubular network. Within the acidic environment of the proximal tubules, myoglobin dissociates to release free redox-active heme and iron, catalyzing highly destructive Fenton reactions [6], [66]. This unchecked oxidative burst drives severe lipid peroxidation of the tubular epithelial cell membranes, culminating in the widespread cellular desquamation, necrosis, loss of the brush border, and the formation of obstructive eosinophilic casts observed in our untreated induction group.

The profound histological protection afforded by L-carvone demonstrates that its primary therapeutic target is the proximal tubular epithelium. Renal tissues from L-carvone-treated mice showed remarkably preserved tubular architecture, with minimal cast formation and no widespread epithelial necrosis. By mitigating heme-induced oxidative stress and lipid peroxidation, L-carvone prevents the catastrophic stripping of the tubular basement membrane and maintains luminal patency.

Furthermore, as a natural monoterpene, carvone simultaneously suppresses the localized inflammatory activation and apoptotic signaling cascades triggered by this oxidative damage [67], [16], [68]. By halting this synergistic triad of lipid peroxidation, inflammation, and apoptosis at the tubular level, L-carvone effectively arrests the progression of pigment-induced ATN, validating its therapeutic potential as a multi-target nephroprotective agent.

These protective effects may be attributed to carvone’s ability to modulate multiple pathological pathways simultaneously. As a natural monoterpene, carvone has been shown to suppress inflammation activation, inhibit lipid peroxidation, and preserve mitochondrial integrity, mechanisms crucial in preventing tubular cell death and preserving renal histoarchitecture [26], [67], [16], [69], [68]. Overall, the histopathological evidence supports the conclusion that carvone effectively mitigates structural kidney damage in rhabdomyolysis-induced AKI, validating its therapeutic potential as a multi-target renal protective agent.

4.1 Limitations

Several limitations of the present study warrant consideration. First, the complex inflammatory response was evaluated exclusively by measuring mRNA expression levels using RT-qPCR for TNF-α, IL-1β, and NF-κB. While decreased mRNA levels clearly indicate transcriptional suppression, they do not necessarily reflect proportional changes in protein translation, post-translational modification, or active cellular secretion. Furthermore, although our Western blot analysis confirmed changes in the expression of specific apoptotic markers, we did not perform direct functional assays (e.g., caspase enzymatic activity assays or NF-κB DNA-binding assays). Future investigations should incorporate ELISA-based quantitative confirmation of these specific cytokine proteins within the renal parenchyma, alongside dedicated functional validation assays, to strengthen causal inference and fully map the compound's mechanistic impact.

Second, the experimental design used a prophylactic protocol, administering L-carvone by oral gavage for 5 consecutive days prior to rhabdomyolysis induction. While this effectively demonstrates the compound's intrinsic cytoprotective mechanisms by pre-loading antioxidant defenses, it possesses limited direct clinical relevance. Patients presenting with rhabdomyolysis strictly require rapid, post-insult therapeutic interventions. Consequently, this study does not evaluate whether L-carvone has measurable rescue efficacy when administered after the primary myoglobinuric insult. Therefore, subsequent investigations employing a strict therapeutic dosing protocol (initiating L-carvone administration only after the establishment of the myoglobinuric injury) are absolutely essential to ascertain the true clinical and translational viability of this compound.

Finally, the study lacks hemodynamic monitoring. While the data demonstrate a highly significant reduction in systemic BUN and creatinine, this functional improvement is currently attributed solely to direct cellular protection at the tubular level. However, extreme afferent arteriolar vasoconstriction—induced by myoglobin scavenging of nitric oxide—plays a determining role in the rapid reduction of glomerular filtration rate in myoglobinuric acute kidney injury. Because macroscopic renal blood flow, microscopic glomerular hemodynamics, and systemic mean arterial blood pressure were not evaluated, a gap remains in the comprehensive mechanistic understanding of L-carvone's in vivo efficacy.

Finally, and most crucially from a strict toxicological perspective, our investigation of the underlying molecular pathways remains primarily observational and correlative. While our multi-layered data demonstrate a strong, dose-dependent association between L-carvone treatment and the suppression of NF-κB transcription alongside the attenuation of caspase-mediated apoptotic cascades, this study did not employ causal experimental designs. The absence of specific pharmacological inhibitors (e.g., selective NF-κB or caspase inhibitors), receptor antagonists, or genetic knockout models limits our ability to definitively demonstrate that modulation of these pathways is the primary, causative driver of L-carvone’s nephroprotective effect rather than a secondary consequence of reduced upstream oxidative stress. Consequently, while these initial in vivo findings are robust, they must be interpreted as descriptive rather than mechanistically definitive. Future studies utilizing specific targeted interventions and in vitro proximal tubule cell models are necessary to advance these findings and firmly establish the exact causal mechanisms at play.

Furthermore, we acknowledge that this study did not include de novo pharmacokinetic profiling or a dedicated systemic toxicity assessment of L-carvone itself. While our dosing regimen was strictly guided by previously established safety margins in the murine literature ([26]; [27])The lack of direct pharmacokinetic tracking in this specific injury model limits our understanding of the compound's tissue distribution, half-life, and clearance kinetics in the context of severely impaired renal function.

Additionally, the current experimental design did not include a positive control group treated with a standard-of-care therapy or a known nephroprotective reference agent (such as N-acetylcysteine or a recognized clinical antioxidant). While our data clearly establish the dose-dependent efficacy of L-carvone relative to the untreated rhabdomyolysis model, the absence of a comparative benchmark limits our ability to definitively contextualize its protective effect size relative to established therapies. Future comparative studies utilizing well-characterized reference compounds are essential to accurately determine L-carvone’s relative clinical and pharmacological value.

Finally, the translational significance of these findings is inherently restricted by the constraints of preclinical modeling. The glycerol-induced injection model, while a standardized and robust tool for generating reproducible myoglobinuric injury, does not fully replicate the complex and heterogeneous etiology of clinical rhabdomyolysis (such as severe trauma or crush syndrome), which often involves profound systemic hemodynamic collapse, massive fluid shifts, and multi-organ failure. Additionally, fundamental species differences in renal hemodynamics, tubular architecture, and drug metabolism between mice and humans necessitate highly cautious interpretation. Furthermore, because L-carvone possesses well-established, broad-spectrum systemic antioxidant properties, it remains to be determined whether the observed nephroprotection is entirely driven by targeted cellular mechanisms within the renal tubules or is partially confounded by systemic redox buffering before myoglobin reaches the kidney. Consequently, the present findings should be strictly interpreted as a preliminary, descriptive proof-of-concept rather than a definitive demonstration of clinical therapeutic viability.

5 Conclusion

In summary, this preclinical study demonstrates the intrinsic cytoprotective effects of prophylactic L-carvone administration in a murine model of rhabdomyolysis-induced AKI, likely associated with the attenuation of inflammatory and apoptotic signaling pathways. While these findings establish a foundational mechanistic proof-of-concept, extensive future studies—specifically addressing post-injury therapeutic efficacy, comprehensive safety profiling, and dose-response pharmacodynamics—are strictly required before any translational clinical applicability can be established.
