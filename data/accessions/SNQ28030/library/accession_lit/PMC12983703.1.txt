Background

Cryo-electron microscopy (cryo-EM) has emerged as a powerful technique for high-resolution structural determination of macromolecules. However, accurately classifying single-particle cryo-EM images remains challenging, especially when dealing with deformed particles. In traditional 2D classification methods, clustering algorithms are used for classification. This assumption leads to some deformed particles being misclassified in 2D images, which adversely affects downstream tasks. To address this challenge, we propose a point cloud–based deformation measurement model that integrates a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) with a heuristic point cloud matching algorithm to calculate particle deformation values.

Results

This model enables the identification and removal of particles with large deformations. Our experiments on simulated and real cryo-EM datasets, including Tobacco Mosaic Virus (TMV) and mixed capsids of MS2 virions (MS2). The model achieves robust classification (F1: 0.85–0.88) while preserving 93–95% of structural details, and can effectively filter out deformed particles after 2D classification.

Conclusion

The model identifies and removes deformed or misclassified particles to improve classification quality. It serves as a data-filtering post-processing step following 2D classification. By improving the quality of particle datasets, it enhances the reliability of subsequent analysis in cryo-EM.

Background

Cryo-electron microscopy (cryo-EM) has become essential for biomolecular structure analysis, enabling direct visualization of molecules in their native state without crystallization [1]. Recent advances in detectors and image processing have made cryo-EM the method of choice for studying the structure of large protein complexes at atomic or near-atomic resolution [2]. In cryo-EM, 2D classification is a crucial step in 2D image processing, as the subsequent 3D reconstruction is performed based on the classification groups. Incorrectly classified particles can affect the accuracy of the reconstruction for that group [3]. Therefore, it is necessary to eliminate particles with poor quality through data filtering [4].

In practice, particles may deform during sample collection. This deformation can lead to classification errors [5]. Traditional classification methods struggle to recognize these deformed particles accurately. Existing classification algorithms typically rely on clustering techniques, but this method is limited when dealing with the deformation of particles. Deformed particles still retain certain characteristics that may be incorrectly classified by traditional 2D classification methods. Partial misclassification of these 2D particles can result in them being assigned to the wrong class, which affects the subsequent 3D particle selection and downstream 3D classification tasks. In contrast, our proposed method measures the deformation value of each particle relative to its class-average image, allowing users to manually remove highly deformed particles and achieve a data-filtering effect, thus solving this problem.

Our contributions are as follows: We propose a point cloud–based deformation measurement model. Initialize the point cloud coordinates of particles and templates, and predict the displacement of the particle point cloud in each round until it converges to the template point cloud. Through the process of moving the point cloud, we can obtain the deformation value of the particle. As a post-processing data-filtering step after 2D classification, our model removes highly deformed particles to improve data quality for downstream tasks.

We introduce the VAE model to obtain the particle displacement information by mapping the particle point cloud coordinates to the potential space in each round. The motion information learned in the latent space is crucial for estimating the final deformation value of each particle.

We propose the heuristic matching algorithm KMP, which efficiently completes matching in a short time while preserving a large portion of the template point cloud.

Related works

2D particle classification in cryo-EM has a long history and has played a crucial role in structural analysis. Over the years, classification methods have evolved from simple manual approaches to sophisticated computational techniques, improving the efficiency and accuracy of classification.

In the early stages of cryo-EM, 2D classification was limited by detector sensitivity, algorithms, and computing power [6, 7]. Manual particle picking was time-consuming and subjective [8]. Later, template matching methods were introduced [9], but suffered from template bias, low-SNR inaccuracies, and high computational cost. Several years laters, the introduction of K-means clustering advanced 2D classification [10, 11]. This unsupervised method grouped particle images without prior knowledge, distinguishing conformations via iterative centroid updates [8, 12]. Later, multireference refinement and correlation metrics further improved accuracy [13].

Modern implementations like RELION [14] and cryoSPARC [15] have since integrated advanced variants of these algorithms, incorporating Bayesian statistics [16] and stochastic gradient descent to handle increasingly large datasets. Recent benchmarks show that optimized K-means derivatives can achieve 90% classification accuracy on modern GPU-accelerated platforms, a dramatic improvement from the 60–70% accuracy typical of early manual methods.

Existing 2D particle classification methods are discrete classification methods, which work well for regular particles with distinct conformations but struggle with deformed particle deformation [17]. When applied to deformation particles, traditional classification methods produce meaningless “class“ boundaries, leading to inaccurate classifications [18]. They fail to distinguish deformed particles, leading to blurred class-mean images and reduced classification accuracy [19, 20], which affects downstream tasks.

Therefore, we propose a deformation value measurement model.This model effectively calculates the deformation of each particle relative to its class-average image. After manually removing particles with large deformation values, the remaining particles are reclassified in 2D, which effectively reduces classification artifacts and improves the clarity of class-mean images. After 2D classification, filtering out highly deformed particles in this way is beneficial for downstream tasks.

Mathematical connections and distinctions

From a mathematical perspective, our approach is similar to manifold learning and dimensionality reduction methods that describe continuous conformational variability, such as diffusion maps, spectral embedding, and principal component analysis (PCA). These methods aim to reconstruct continuous structural transitions in a global coordinate or latent space. However, they are primarily designed to model structural manifolds or motion trajectories rather than to quantify deformation strength at the particle level. In contrast, our method focuses on estimating the deformation value of each particle relative to its class-average template in a point cloud representation. The objective is not to reconstruct the deformation pathway but to obtain a stable and convergent deformation value for reliable data filtering. The proposed KMP matching algorithm improves the efficiency and robustness of point correspondence establishment, while the VAE-based latent space provides a nonlinear yet stable mapping that facilitates convergence and deformation quantification.

Methods

Our method involves three major steps—preprocessing, establishing point correspondences, and predicting deformation—to compute the deformation value of each particle. As shown in Figure  1a, the workflow consists of three phases: Phase 1 converts raw micrographs and the template into point clouds via denoising–binarization; Phase 2 establishes point-to-point correspondences between the two clouds using our KMP matcher; Phase 3 uses a VAE to predict deformation value based on the coordinates of the particles and the template.

Our heuristic model is named KMP, where K represents the use of the K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) approach to establish matching relationships, while M and P denote additional constraints. Compared to the nearest neighbor method, which may lose some point clouds, and the global optimal solution method, which can be time-consuming, our method is able to match point clouds efficiently in a shorter time while retaining a large number of point clouds.

Algorithm positioning

As our model functions as a post-processing data filtering step in the 2D classification process, the initial electron microscopy data needs to be processed through RELION software for particle picking and 2D classification. We select categories with blurry class averages, as blurriness indicates the presence of low-quality particle images, usually caused by deformation or excessive noise. We then export the particle images and angular information for those categories. These class average images, along with all the particle images and angular information for the selected category, serve as the input data for our model in the subsequent steps. Afterward, we obtain the deformation values of each particle relative to the class average image. The particles with large deformations are filtered out, and the refined dataset is used for downstream RELION tasks.

There are several failure cases encountered during optimization. When the number of points in the cloud is too small, the sampled points cannot adequately cover the entire particle shape, leading to inaccurate deformation estimation. In cases where the signal-to-noise ratio is excessively high, the coordinate initialization may fail to represent a valid particle shape. In addition, if the convergence threshold is set too low, the optimization process may struggle to reach convergence within a reasonable number of iterations.Fig. 1 Pipeline for deformation estimation of cryo-EM particles using point clouds

Obtain particle characteristics

In Phase 1, the particle images undergo a series of preprocessing steps to generate high-quality point cloud representations. First, raw particle images are denoised to enhance particle signal while suppressing background noise. After denoising, the dataset is imported into RELION, followed by data import, particle picking, and 2D classification. Then, the class average images for each category are obtained, along with the particle coordinates and angles for each category. Next, the class average image is used as a template. Both the class-average template and the corresponding particle images are binarized using an intensity threshold, retaining only the largest connected region to eliminate noise and irrelevant small fragments. To further standardize particle orientations, each particle is subjected to an affine transformation based on its known angle, ensuring that all particles are approximately aligned with the reference template. As shown in Figure 1b, the resulting clean and aligned binary images are then converted into 2D point clouds, which form the input to the subsequent deformation analysis stage.

Solving point cloud matching relationships

Some hyperparameters related to matching include the number of particles, which is typically set between 5000 and 15,000. A smaller number would lead to incomplete representation, while a larger number increases the time cost. The distance threshold for iteration termination is set to 0.1, which ensures near-convergence. Traditional global optimal matching methods, such as the Hungarian algorithm [22], provide accurate correspondences but suffer from high computational complexity. On the other hand, algorithms like nearest neighbor (KNN) matching [23–25] offer faster matching but risk losing significant portions of the point cloud structure.

The matching relationship between the point cloud of particles and templates is very important. If the optimal matching solution is pursued, it incurs extremely high computational cost [26]. However, if a simple nearest neighbor matching is applied, a large proportion of the point cloud structure is lost, making it difficult to preserve the original particle shape.

In Phase 2, as shown in Figure  1c. In order to accurately measure the particle deformation values in a short time, we propose KMP heuristic matching algorithm. By calculating the distance matrix between the particle point cloud and the template point cloud, and then using the nearest neighbor heuristic algorithm to find the matching relationship between the particle point cloud and the template point cloud. This strategy effectively reduces computational overhead while maximizing the preservation of particle point clouds to ensure a complete representation of object shapes.

Point cloud deformation prediction

In Phase 3, the point cloud deformation is estimated through an iterative deformation prediction process. The point cloud matching relationship module is responsible for determining where each point in the particle point cloud should converge within the template point cloud. Meanwhile, the point cloud deformation prediction module predicts the displacement of each point, gradually deforming the particle point cloud to align with the template. Figure 1d illustrates the deformation prediction process in Phase 2, aimed at quantifying particle deformation. Starting from class-mean images as templates, particle point clouds are initialized after noise reduction and feature extraction. A variational autoencoder (VAE [27]) is applied to encode shape features into latent space Z and decode them into a deformation field D, which updates the particle point cloud to match the template. The model is optimized by minimizing the point cloud matching loss, iteratively updating until convergence. This approach integrates deformation modeling and matching, enabling efficient analysis of flexible particle deformation.

This model is similar to 3DFlex [28] and Dynamight [29], both of which utilize VAE to obtain a deformation field. However, our method differs in that we focus on capturing the object’s deformation process, which requires additional matching to align the deformed particle with the template. Furthermore, we incorporate a squeeze-and-excitation (SE) attention module into the latent feature space, allowing the network to adaptively recalibrate channel-wise feature responses before decoding. This enhancement strengthens the representation of deformation-relevant features and suppresses irrelevant variations, leading to more accurate and robust displacement predictions.Fig. 4 Architecture of VAE, including encoder and decoder

To capture the deformation information of the point cloud, we adopt VAE, which encodes shape variations into a latent space. Figure 4 illustrates the network framework, where the encoder and decoder are symmetric, and the latent space dimension is set to 128. The batch size used in the experiments is 32. Results

To comprehensively evaluate our method, we conducted experiments on both simulated and real cryo-EM datasets. The datasets (simulated particles, MS2, TMV, and other medical imaging datasets) and experimental configurations are first introduced. Next, the deformation convergence process and prediction accuracy are analyzed using simulated data. Furthermore, classification performance on real datasets is validated through metrics such as Accuracy, Recall, and F1, while different point cloud matching strategies are compared. These evaluations collectively demonstrate the method’s capability in handling particle deformations.

There are several failure cases encountered during optimization. When the number of points in the cloud is too small, the sampled points cannot adequately cover the entire particle shape, leading to inaccurate deformation estimation. In cases where the signal-to-noise ratio is excessively high, the coordinate initialization may fail to represent a valid particle shape. In addition, if the convergence threshold is set too low, the optimization process may struggle to reach convergence within a reasonable number of iterations.

Dataset and experimental setup

To evaluate the performance of the proposed method. The evaluation methods included using accuracy and recall to assess the model’s ability to identify deformed particles. Additionally, RELION’s 2D classification was used to compare the class average images of deformed particles before and after filtering. To evaluate the performance of the proposed method, we used four datasets: (1) Two simulated datasets (I and II) generated based on EMPIAR-10,345 [30]; (2) The mixed capsids of MS2 virions dataset from EMPIAR-10,706 [31]; (3) The Tobacco mosaic virus (TMV) dataset from EMPIAR-10,555 [32]. Among them, EMPIAR-10,706 and EMPIAR-10,555 are real experimental datasets. These real datasets share several common characteristics: the particles are relatively large, with some particles exhibiting significant deformation. Additionally, their class average images typically display a pattern where one half is clear and the other half is blurred.

Description of the simulated datasets: First, a 2D projection from the EMPIAR-10,345 dataset with a relatively uniform conformation is selected as the reference image. Simulated dataset I involves global deformations applied to the reference image, ranging from weak to strong. Simulated dataset II involves local deformations applied to the four regions (top-left, bottom-left, top-right, bottom-right) of the image, generating four distinct images.

The simulated particle data can be used directly for model training and evaluation without preprocessing. In contrast, the MS2 and TMV datasets require denoising with Topaz, followed by manual particle selection and 2D classification in Relion. Low-quality class averages are then selected, and the class averages, particle images, rotation angles, and relative displacements (X/Y offsets) are saved for subsequent matching.

To evaluate the model’s performance in identifying deformed particles, we computed Accuracy, Recall and F1 score [33]. Additionally, we compared class average images before and after removing deformed particles.

The experiments are conducted on an NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU with Ubuntu 20.04 as the operating system, and the PyTorch version used was 2.0.1. RELION version 5.0.1 and Topaz version v0.2.5a were used. In the denoising step, we use a ratio of 1:2:1 for alpha, beta, and gamma. The number of particles is 15,000, and the termination threshold is 0.1.

Discussion

The existing 2D classification methods are difficult to distinguish deformation particles. This study introduces a point cloud–based deformation measurement model as a post-processing framework for particle selection following cryo-EM 2D classification.

Our method demonstrates consistent effectiveness across both simulated and real cryo-EM datasets. The deformation visualization results reveal diverse deformation patterns, while quantitative evaluation metrics confirm the model’s ability to accurately identify deformed particles. In addition, comparative analysis of different point cloud matching strategies shows that the proposed method outperforms commonly used matching methods in terms of time complexity and retention rate. From a practical perspective, the proposed method is designed as a lightweight post-processing module that can be seamlessly integrated into existing pipelines such as RELION.

Nevertheless, several limitations remain. The accuracy of deformation estimation depends on the quality and density of the point cloud representation, and extremely low signal-to-noise ratios or inaccurate initial alignment may affect convergence. In addition, highly irregular deformations may require more iterations to achieve stable results. Conclusions

In this paper, we propose a deformation measurement method based on point cloud deformation modeling to address the limitations of traditional discrete classification methods in the field of 2D classification. This method allows for the calculation of the deformation values of particles relative to the template, thereby removing particles with large deformations and allowing the remaining high-quality particles to proceed with the subsequent processes. The template selection is based on the class average images obtained from the upstream RELION 2D classification. We select slightly blurry categories, as these categories may include some deformed particles that cause the blurriness. After removing these particles, the image becomes clearer. We introduce the KMP heuristic matching algorithm, which retains a significant portion of the point cloud with minimal processing time. However, our method still has some limitations. For highly irregularly deformed particles, the convergence process may require additional iterations to achieve accurate deformation representation. Future improvements could focus on refining the matching strategy to further optimize computational efficiency and integrating deep learning-based enhancements for more accurate deformation predictions.

Future work

This model has certain applications in other medical datasets [34, 35]. In clinical medical imaging, dynamic processes such as cardiac contraction-relaxation in MRI cine sequences, respiratory motion in lung CT, or organ shifts in longitudinal scans exhibit similar non-rigid, time-varying deformation patterns. Accurate modeling of such changes can be valuable not only for tracking physiological motion but also for distinguishing pathological from normal states or assessing disease progression.
