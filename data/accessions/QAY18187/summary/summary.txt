Paper: PMC7077265.1.txt
Thermal Evidence:
- Optimal growth temperature: 60 °C (for substrate culture).
- Growth temperature range: 40–80 °C (for the phage's host).
- Survival temperature range: Maintained >90% activity after 30 min heat treatment between 40 and 70 °C.
- Incubation conditions: 37 °C (for antibacterial assays and in vivo model).
- Environmental source/habitat: Isolated from a hot spring.
- Thermophilic adaptation: Yes, the phage was isolated from an extremophilic source.
- Heat tolerance: High, demonstrated by maintaining activity at elevated temperatures.

Environmental Context:
- Habitat/Source: Hot spring (Tengchong hot spring).
- Classification: The phage is derived from a thermophilic organism, and the lysin exhibits thermostability.

Concise Summary:
The phage lysin was derived from a phage isolated from a hot spring, indicating an extremophilic origin. The host bacterium for the phage is characterized as a thermophile, thriving between 40–80 °C. The lysin itself exhibits significant thermostability, retaining over 90% activity after 30 minutes of heat treatment between 40 °C and 70 °C. While the lysin's optimal activity was tested at 60 °C against the host, its antibacterial efficacy was demonstrated at 37 °C, which is consistent with physiological conditions for human pathogens. The overall profile suggests a highly thermostable enzyme adapted to high-temperature environments, while retaining potent activity at mesophilic temperatures relevant for infection models.


Paper: PMC9629493.1.txt
Thermal Evidence:

- The enzyme's activity profile was tested across a broad range of temperatures (20 to 85°C) for polymerase activity, and the 3'–5' exonuclease activity was assessed at 65°C.

Environmental Context:

- The source material for the exonuclease component was a metagenome collected from several hot springs in Iceland.

Concise Summary:
The enzyme is derived from a bacteriophage infecting a bacterium isolated from coastal hot springs. The enzyme exhibits optimal polymerase activity around 70°C and shows limited activity below 40°C, indicating thermophilic characteristics. The source material for the associated exonuclease domain was obtained from a metagenome collected in hot springs. The authors classify the enzyme as belonging to a group of thermophilic bacteriophages, and the enzyme itself is characterized by its thermophilic adaptation.
