Efficient recognition of specific sites in DNA by repair proteins requires resolution of the fundamental “speed–stability paradox.” This paradox arises from the need to scan a vast genome rapidly while maintaining high accuracy in lesion detection. Classical models of protein–DNA interactions have shown that the association rate constants for site-specific complexes can approach or even exceed the diffusion limit, implying the existence of specialized mechanisms of facilitated, or accelerated, diffusion. Experimental and theoretical studies have identified several such mechanisms, including one-dimensional sliding along the DNA helix, two-dimensional diffusion over the DNA surface, intersegmental transfer, and dissociation–reassociation through hopping or jumping. These modes may operate in combination, and their relative contributions are determined by DNA conformation, structural features of the protein, and the cellular context. Particular attention has been paid to DNA glycosylases, which initiate base excision repair by locating and excising damaged bases with high efficiency and fidelity. Single-molecule studies of glycosylases such as human OGG1 and bacterial Fpg have shown that rapid sliding is often coupled with helical rotation, transient base interrogation, and conformational checkpoints that enable discrimination between damaged and undamaged bases. Disordered protein termini and specific amino acid residues may function as molecular wedges or anchors that promote lesion recognition, whereas cooperative protein–protein interactions may further facilitate target localization and catalytic turnover. Collectively, these findings indicate that DNA damage search and recognition are governed by a dynamic interplay of multidimensional diffusion, conformational probing, and interprotein cooperation, thereby ensuring both speed and precision in genome surveillance.

Highlights

• Efficient protein recognition of DNA sites must resolve the speed–stability paradox.

• Damaged base detection arises from rearrangements within biopolymer molecules.

• Protein interplay ensures both speed and precision in genome surveillance.

1 Introduction

DNA damage occurs frequently during normal cellular processes, and genome stability can be maintained only through the rapid and efficient action of cellular repair enzymes. The first step in enzymatic DNA repair is the precise positioning of a repair enzyme relative to the lesion, often with single-base resolution, because the damaged site must be identified among billions of undamaged bases in the genome. At the same time, lesions must be detected not only with high accuracy but also with high speed, since repair must occur before the next round of replication.

Numerous studies of lesion removal by DNA glycosylases have revealed multiple repair pathways and, in many cases, have established the detailed mechanisms underlying these processes. Nevertheless, despite extensive investigation, it remains unclear how damaged or mismatched bases, apurinic/apyrimidinic sites, and other DNA lesions are located and recognized with such high efficiency.

2 Mechanisms of searching for specific DNA recognition sites by DNA binding proteins

The earliest studies demonstrating that enzymes are capable of binding specific DNA sequences were published in the late 1960s [1,2]. Only three years later, the first kinetic study of this process was performed, and the kinetic features of Lac repressor binding to the lac operon were described [3]. For DNA containing 50,000 base pairs, the association rate constant for protein–nucleic acid complex formation was estimated to be 7 – 109 M−1s−1. This value became the starting point for subsequent studies of protein–nucleic acid interactions. Such processes include the search for DNA lesions by repair enzymes, the search for cleavage sites by restriction endonucleases, promoter binding by transcription factors, and other cases in which a protein must identify a unique sequence among a vast excess of alternative nucleotide sequences.

The maximum association rate for two molecules in solution is achieved when every collision results in complex formation and therefore corresponds to the collision frequency determined by thermal diffusion in three-dimensional space. This value is referred to as the diffusion limit and, for protein–ligand interactions, is usually estimated to be on the order of 108 M−1s−1 [4,5]. Thus, the measured association constant for lac repressor–operator binding is almost two orders of magnitude higher than the diffusion limit, implying that the lac repressor binds its target sequence faster than would be expected from free three-dimensional diffusion alone. Similarly high values for the rate constants of formation of specific protein–nucleic acid complexes, in some cases reaching 1010 M−1s−1, have also been reported [3,6]. These observations prompted intensive investigation of possible mechanisms of accelerated diffusion that enable DNA-binding proteins to locate their target sequences among an enormous number of alternative sites within a minimal time. Such mechanisms direct protein movement in space and reduce the time spent in futile exploration of regions where target sites are absent, thereby markedly increasing search efficiency. Typically, these processes involve an initial nonspecific association with a random DNA site, followed by intramolecular movement toward the specific target. At present, several mechanisms of accelerated diffusion have been identified [[7], [8], [9]].

2.1 Sliding along DNA or one-dimensional diffusion

One such mechanism is sliding along the DNA molecule, in which the protein remains in continuous contact with DNA while moving along the chain; this mode of motion is equivalent to one-dimensional diffusion. This mechanism of accelerated diffusion was first proposed by Riggs et al. [3], and was later described in more detail by Richter and Eigen [10]. The theory was subsequently developed further by Berg and colleagues [8,11].

In one-dimensional diffusion, a protein moves from its initial nonspecific binding site to its target sequence without dissociating from the same DNA molecule. The interactions between the protein and DNA are predominantly electrostatic. At each moment, the protein makes an elementary step to a neighboring nucleotide, and the probability of movement in either direction along the linear DNA sequence is equal; therefore, the mean position of the protein remains centered around the original nonspecific binding site. However, as time passes and the number of elementary steps increases, the distance over which the enzyme can deviate from its initial position also increases, and so does the probability of locating a specific DNA sequence [12]. Sliding therefore terminates either when the protein finds its target and forms a specific complex or when the protein–DNA complex dissociates. Sliding can occur when the energy barrier for movement to a neighboring nucleotide is substantially lower than the activation energy required for dissociation of the protein–nucleic acid complex.

In review [8], Otto Berg and colleagues, analyzing both theoretical and experimental data on protein sliding along DNA, including those for the lac repressor, proposed several reasons why the observed velocity of protein movement is lower than the theoretical maximum for one-dimensional diffusion. One of the principal explanations is that the protein must also rotate along the DNA double helix, following either the grooves or the phosphate backbone while maintaining a fixed orientation relative to the DNA surface. In this case, the protein completes approximately one full revolution for every 10 nucleotides traversed, and the rate of translocation is therefore strongly affected by hydrodynamic resistance to this rotational motion. In addition, sliding may be slowed by heterogeneity in the electrostatic potential along the DNA molecule and by local variations in counterion concentration. However, these factors appear to have only a limited effect, and sliding velocities may still approach the theoretical limit calculated with solvent resistance taken into account [8].

Protein movement along DNA by sliding has been demonstrated for enzymes of different classes using a variety of experimental approaches [7,13]. As early as the 1970s, the first theoretical model was proposed in which the protein was assumed to rotate helically around DNA during movement [14]. However, direct evidence for such rotational motion during sliding became possible only with the development of single-molecule methods capable of monitoring individual protein–DNA complexes. For example, enzyme rotation along the DNA groove was directly observed for RNA polymerase in using a single-molecule approach [15]. In Ref. [16], which addressed calculation of the diffusion coefficient for a protein nonspecifically bound to DNA while rotating around the helix, the authors proposed a theoretical model and compared the calculated values with experimentally determined diffusion coefficients for DNA-binding proteins. They showed that when rotation around the DNA helix is taken into account, the diffusion rate is inversely proportional to the cube of the effective radius of the enzyme, approximately ∼1/R3, whereas in simple linear diffusion it is inversely proportional to the effective radius, approximately ∼1/R. This prediction is in good agreement with experimental observations obtained under single-molecule conditions. The diffusion coefficients calculated in this way for the lac repressor and the repair enzyme hOGG1 were 2–4 times higher than the corresponding experimental values, but they were still much closer than estimates obtained without accounting for protein rotation along the DNA helix. Thus, the proposed theory is in reasonable agreement with experimental data, although it does not appear to incorporate all of the additional factors that arise during protein–DNA interactions and influence one-dimensional diffusion.

In article [17], which examines the mechanisms by which proteins search for specific DNA sequences directly within chromatin, the author concluded that one-dimensional diffusion alone, without the participation of other search modes, is an excessively slow and overly meticulous strategy for DNA sequence interrogation. However, it can greatly enhance overall target-search efficiency if the protein spends part of its time in one-dimensional diffusion before dissociating and relocating to another DNA site. In this sense, the addition of sliding may effectively increase the apparent size of the target sequence when combined with other accelerated diffusion mechanisms.

2.2 Two-dimensional diffusion along the DNA surface

The existence of a mechanism based on one-dimensional diffusion has been demonstrated in in vitro experiments for many proteins, including restriction endonucleases such as EcoRI, BssHII, HindIII, BamHI, and EcoRV, as well as E. coli RNA polymerase, the lac repressor LacI, methyltransferases, and many others [7]. However, in in vivo systems this mechanism does not permit proteins to travel long distances along the grooves of DNA, because stretches of free DNA are interrupted by regions occupied by other DNA-binding proteins that obstruct continued movement. To address this limitation, Martin Kampmann proposed a mechanism of two-dimensional diffusion across the DNA surface, which was later supported by experiments with the restriction endonuclease EcoRI [18]. In two-dimensional diffusion, as in one-dimensional diffusion, the protein moves from one nucleotide to another across the DNA surface. In contrast to one-dimensional sliding, however, the protein can move more freely over the DNA surface and can reach neighboring nonspecific binding sites that are not adjacent in the linear sequence but are close in three-dimensional space. In the case of naked DNA, strict distinction between one-dimensional and two-dimensional sliding may unnecessarily complicate the model of protein–DNA interaction. In the context of chromatin, however, the possibility of two-dimensional diffusion must be taken into account [17].

2.2.1 Inter-segment transfer

Another mechanism of accelerated diffusion is intersegmental transfer. In this process, a protein bound at one DNA site moves to another site through an intermediate state in which it simultaneously contacts two DNA segments, thereby forming a loop. After dissociation from one of the two sites, the protein either remains at its original position or relocates to a new site on the DNA molecule, and the cycle may then repeat. J.] Halford S.E., Szczelkun M.D. Eur. Biophys. J. 2002. V. 31, № 4. P. 257-267, copyright 2002).

This mechanism is realized for proteins containing more than one binding site for multidomain proteins. For example, it has been shown for various restriction endonucleases [19,20].

For a protein molecule to bind simultaneously to two DNA segments, those segments must be close to each other in three-dimensional space. The probability that two DNA segments will come into contact depends on the distance between them, and segments separated by approximately 300–400 base pairs in the primary structure approach each other most readily. This spacing is explained by the fact that thermally induced DNA bending can occur roughly every 150 base pairs [12,21]. Consequently, an elementary “step” achieved by intersegmental transfer is quite large, often exceeding 300 base pairs. As a result, intersegmental transfer provides a convenient means of moving over long distances on extended DNA molecules, although the range of proteins capable of using this mechanism is limited. Nevertheless, it may play a major role in target search by proteins possessing two DNA-binding sites. As an example, work demonstrated intersegmental transfer during the search for specific DNA sequences by the multidomain transcription factor Oct1 using NMR spectroscopy [22].

Partially disordered N-terminal regions are more common in DNA-binding proteins than in proteins that do not bind DNA, occurring in approximately 70% and 50% of proteins in these two groups, respectively [23]. Unlike globular DNA-binding domains, these terminal regions lack a strictly defined tertiary structure, are enriched in positively charged amino acid residues, contain relatively few hydrophobic residues, and are characterized by substantial conformational flexibility [23]. The functions of such disordered terminal segments are often associated with molecular recognition and regulation, and during association with DNA they may undergo a disorder-to-order transition, although in some cases considerable flexibility is retained even in the bound state [24]. A series of studies [[23], [24], [25]] modeled and analyzed the role of disordered N-terminal regions using homeodomain proteins and the transcription factor p53 as examples [ [[23], [24], [25]]]. These authors concluded that such regions may not only increase the efficiency of one-dimensional diffusion by enhancing the effective affinity of the globular protein for DNA through a positively charged mobile N-terminus, but may also enable a distinct form of intersegmental transfer [[23], [24], [25]].

In this proposed mechanism, the protein passes through a state in which it is simultaneously bound to two DNA sites during the search process. One binding site corresponds to the sequence-specific recognition domain located within the ordered globular structure, whereas the second is provided by the disordered N-terminal region. Thus, the disordered N-terminal segment, owing to its high affinity for DNA, can be regarded as a separate mobile DNA-binding domain that promotes nonspecific protein–DNA complex formation not through a rigid tertiary structure but through characteristic features of its amino acid sequence [[23], [24], [25]]. During transfer between DNA segments, the protein performs brachiation-like movements analogous to those of monkeys moving between branches; in the English-language literature this process is referred to as the “monkey-bar mechanism”. When the effect of N-terminal fragment structure on the efficiency of transfer between DNA sites was analyzed, the highest efficiency was predicted when the effective positive charge of the terminal domain was high but not maximal. In addition, translocation efficiency was greater when positive charges were clustered rather than evenly distributed and were positioned in the middle of the domain or closer to the N-terminus of the amino acid sequence [24]. A longer N-terminal region should further increase the number of such transfer events and thereby enhance the efficiency of target-sequence search [23]. Taken together with data from other studies of partially disordered proteins, these findings suggest that disordered N-terminal regions possess characteristic amino acid compositions and structural properties that may have been evolutionarily selected to optimize the efficiency of specific-sequence search on DNA [[23], [24], [25]].

2.3 Finding targets in DNA by sequential acts of dissociation/re-association

The accelerated diffusion mechanisms described above share a common feature: they do not require the protein to enter a completely free state, because during translocation the protein remains within either a nonspecific or a specific protein–DNA complex. However, target-site search may also proceed through a different mechanism, namely a sequence of dissociation and reassociation events of a nonspecific protein–nucleic acid complex until a specific binding site is located in DNA. In this case, the protein effectively “jumps” over several nucleotides during movement along the DNA molecule. Some studies distinguish between hopping, also referred to as microscopic reassociation, and jumping, also referred to as macroscopic reassociation. If a protein rebinds to DNA near the site from which it dissociated, the process is termed hopping; if reassociation occurs at a more distant position along the DNA contour, the process is termed jumping. In a single elementary step, the translocation distance is typically less than 10 base pairs in the case of hopping and approximately 100 nucleotides in the case of jumping, although this distinction is somewhat arbitrary (Fig. 4) [6,26].Fig. 4 Search for target sequences by the jumping mechanism. Protein movement through successive dissociation–reassociation events may play a critical role in the search for a target sequence on DNA that is coated with other DNA-binding proteins, which can prevent long-range sliding along the DNA molecule.

This process is predominantly intramolecular, because the effective volume occupied by a single DNA molecule is surrounded by DNA-free solution, and the distance between different DNA molecules in dilute solution is much greater than the distance between any two regions of the same molecule [12]. In addition, two macromolecules that are initially in close proximity tend to remain near one another for a certain period before diffusing apart [8]. Nevertheless, in less frequent cases, a protein may transfer from one DNA molecule to another [27].

In one of the earliest and most influential studies devoted to possible mechanisms of accelerated diffusion, Berg and colleagues classified jumping into three categories according to the scale of reassociation: microscopic reassociation, intradomain reassociation, and interdomain reassociation [8]. For each of these forms of movement on DNA, the authors proposed theoretical models and calculated the relevant kinetic parameters.

Whether microscopic hopping or macroscopic long-range jumping predominates depends on numerous conditions and system parameters. In Ref. [28], the authors calculated the distribution of jump lengths under different conditions by developing an analytical model for the distribution of distances traveled by a protein after dissociation from a DNA molecule. These relationships made it possible to estimate the fractions of microscopic and macroscopic jumping contributing to facilitated diffusion. The authors also found that molecular crowding favors microscopic jumping, or hopping, while reducing the probability of long-range jumps. This theoretical framework was subsequently used to interpret experimental data obtained for the EcoRV restriction enzyme [29].

2.4 Role and contribution of different mechanisms for efficient target sequence searching

All of the search mechanisms described above for the localization of specific DNA sequences by DNA-binding proteins can be divided into two broad classes: correlated and uncorrelated search. If, during the search process, the protein at time t + Δt remains close to its position at time t, the mechanism is considered correlated; otherwise, it is considered uncorrelated [8]. Sliding and microscopic jumping belong to correlated search mechanisms, whereas intersegmental transfer and long-range jumping are classified as uncorrelated mechanisms.

It would be incorrect to assign each DNA-binding protein rigidly to only one specific search mechanism. In general, protein behavior during accelerated diffusion can be modulated by a variety of factors. Potential regulatory influences include multimerization, ligand binding, and post-translational modifications, all of which may alter protein conformation and surface charge distribution and thereby affect protein–DNA interactions [17]. In addition, the search for recognition sites in DNA is a complex, multistep process whose pathway depends not only on protein structure but also on other factors, including DNA length, conformation, and degree of compaction, as well as environmental conditions such as component concentrations and ionic strength [4,6,17,30]. The extent to which DNA is occupied by other proteins also strongly affects the mode of protein translocation along DNA [4,28]. Taken together, these considerations make it more reasonable to assume that proteins use different combinations of mechanisms to locate their binding sites, a conclusion supported by many experimental studies [26,27,29,31,32].

Since the first reports showing that the association constants of protein–nucleic acid complexes can approach or even exceed the diffusion limit, estimated at approximately ∼108 M−1s−1, considerable effort has been devoted to determining the relative contributions of different mechanisms to the acceleration of target-site search on DNA [3,6].

Association constants on the order of 108 M−1s−1 have been reported for DNA-binding proteins of many classes, including restriction enzymes recognizing specific sites, repair enzymes recognizing damaged sites, RNA polymerases binding promoters, regulatory proteins, and others [21]. These observations indicate that the rate-limiting step in specific-site binding is diffusion of the protein to the DNA molecule itself, where it initially associates with a nonspecific site, whereas transfer from a nonspecific site to the specific site is rarely rate-determining. Indeed, if a specific recognition site occupies only 1/10,000 of the total DNA length, then the probability of a random collision with that site should be correspondingly low, and the association constant for formation of the specific complex should be 10,000-fold lower than the diffusion limit. This discrepancy inevitably raises the question of how intramolecular transfer from a nonspecific site to a specific site can occur rapidly enough not to limit the overall binding rate [6].

One possible explanation for substantial acceleration is sliding of the enzyme along DNA, which effectively reduces three-dimensional diffusion to one-dimensional diffusion and thus lowers the dimensionality of the search space by two. As discussed above, during such movement the probability that the protein moves one base pair closer to the target is equal to the probability that it moves one base pair away from it at any given instant. In Ref. [26] Halford provided a simple estimate of the probability of locating a specific binding site by one-dimensional diffusion alone. For this purpose, the rate constant for an elementary step per base pair can be denoted as ks, and the rate constant for dissociation of the nonspecific complex as koff. Because movement is directionally unbiased, the protein must take approximately n2 elementary steps in order to deviate by n positions from its starting point on the DNA. The probability that the protein can complete N steps before dissociation is then determined by relation (1):(1) PN=(ksks+koff)N

For quantitative evaluation, the authors suggest a ratio of 10000:1 for ks/koff. Even with such a large ratio of constants, 99.8% of protein molecules dissociate before they can move 250 nucleotide pairs. This is clearly insufficient for efficient search of the target sequence by one-dimensional sliding alone, which results in multiple repetitions of the protein "passing" the same DNA sites, but it becomes almost impossible to reach more remote binding sites without involving three-dimensional diffusion.

In their theoretical work [21], concerning possible ways of search by proteins for specific sequences in DNA as a result of both one-dimensional and three-dimensional diffusion, Halford et al. came to the conclusion that the speed of finding a specific sequence in DNA reaches such high values precisely as a result of combining in the search process both elements of three-dimensional movement (by the jumping mechanism) and elements of one-dimensional sliding. And in the article [33] Mirny and Slutsky present calculations according to which the optimal speed of protein movement in search of a binding site is achieved if the time that the protein spends in the process of sliding along DNA (τ1D) and the time the protein spends in the process of three-dimensional diffusion (τ3D) are equivalent (τ1D = τ3D).

Reducing three-dimensional diffusion to only one-dimensional diffusion may not only not decrease, but even increase the time required to find a specific sequence in DNA. In the work [21] the authors calculated that the rate of finding the target sequence gradually increases with increasing sliding length (the average distance between the start and end positions of the enzyme that the enzyme has time to "check" with one-dimensional diffusion before dissociating) to a maximum that corresponds to a sliding length equal to about ten times the size of the target sequence. For example, 60 base pairs for a restriction enzyme that recognizes a sequence 6 base pairs long. As a result of further increasing the sliding length, the rate of finding the binding site drops dramatically [21]. These calculations and conclusions are in good agreement with other theoretical and experimental work. Similar conclusions about increasing the search efficiency by combining one-dimensional and three-dimensional diffusion mechanisms were reached by the authors of many other both theoretical works (for example, [30,34,35]) as well as experimental ones for enzymes of various classes, for which sliding lengths of the order of 50 base pairs were obtained (e.g, Refs. [29,31,36]).

The kinetically optimal process of protein search for a recognition site is most likely to involve frequent protein dissociation, whereby the protein remains in the vicinity of the DNA rather than escaping into free solution. Such intradomain dissociation predisposes the protein to further association with the same DNA molecule in close proximity or at some distance from the previous binding site by microscopic or macroscopic jumping, respectively. In this case, each binding of the protein to a new site in DNA should be accompanied by a subsequent sliding along the DNA covering 50–100 nucleotides around the binding site. Implementation of such a mechanism allows us to avoid multiple repetition of analysis of the same sites in DNA, which occurs in the case of only one-dimensional diffusion, and at the same time leads to "scanning" of different DNA sites in the mode "from base pair to base pair", which is impossible to achieve using only jumping or intra-segmental transfer mechanisms [21].

Despite the efforts of a large number of researchers, the detailed mechanisms of search and recognition of specific DNA sites by proteins have not been established. The second part of this review is devoted to the mechanisms of searching for target sequences by DNA glycosylases, proteins that initiate the removal of small damages from DNA by excision base excision repair.

2.5 Search for DNA damage by DNA glycosylases using 8-oxoguanine-DNA glycosylases as an example

The most common types of DNA damage include apurinic/apyrimidinic sites and a wide variety of damaged bases, which are removed from DNA during base excision repair [37,38]. This process is initiated by DNA glycosylases, enzymes that hydrolyze the N-glycosidic bond between the damaged base and the deoxyribose residue. DNA glycosylases are found in all organisms [[39], [40], [41], [42]]. They are monomeric proteins of relatively small size, approximately 15–70 kDa. The vast majority of DNA glycosylases are specific for double-stranded DNA. According to their catalytic mechanism, DNA glycosylases are divided into monofunctional and bifunctional enzymes. Monofunctional DNA glycosylases cleave only the N-glycosidic bond through a hydrolytic mechanism, thereby generating an apurinic/apyrimidinic (AP) site as the reaction product. Bifunctional DNA glycosylases, in contrast, can additionally introduce a strand break on the 3'-side of the AP site formed after removal of the damaged base. Despite their diversity, the mechanisms of damaged-base excision by different DNA glycosylases represent variations of a nucleophilic substitution reaction. Different DNA glycosylases are responsible for the removal of different classes of damaged bases [[43], [44], [45]]. Some enzymes display very narrow substrate specificity and remove only a single type of lesion, such as uracil, whereas others excise a broader range of lesions that generally belong to the same chemical class, for example alkylated purines or pyrimidines [[43], [44], [45]]. The structures of a number of DNA glycosylases have been determined by X-ray crystallography and nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy. On the basis of their amino acid sequences and three-dimensional structures, these enzymes are classified into several unrelated families. Nevertheless, glycosylases belonging to different structural families may be responsible for removal of the same lesion in different organisms [39].

The excision of damaged bases by DNA glycosylases from different families proceeds through a broadly similar mechanism in which the damaged base is flipped out of the DNA helix and inserted into an extrahelical pocket within the enzyme active site. This mechanism has now been confirmed for almost all glycosylases, with the exception of phage T4 Endonuclease V, which removes pyrimidine dimers and instead flips out the adenine opposite the damaged thymidine on the 5'-side of the lesion [46]. To date, crystal structures have been obtained not only for the final protein–DNA complexes in which the damaged base is fully flipped into the enzyme active site, but also for intermediate complexes corresponding to different stages of base eversion in many DNA glycosylases [39,47]. It is also known that such binding is accompanied by bending of the DNA duplex. These data suggest that lesion recognition is a multistep process, in which the damaged base may first be accommodated in an exosite and only subsequently transferred into the catalytic pocke. However, there is still no consensus regarding the exact pathway by which the base is displaced from the DNA helix.

In particular, the question of what provides the driving force for base extrusion remains unresolved. One possibility is that the enzyme uses binding energy to bend the DNA duplex and promote eversion of the damaged base, with DNA bending itself introducing structural strain that destabilizes base stacking and facilitates extrusion. Alternatively, extrusion of the damaged base may be initiated by the intrinsic dynamic properties of noncanonical base pairs [48]. Despite substantial progress in understanding the catalytic mechanisms of damaged-base removal and the considerable interest in how these enzymes search for and recognize their cognate lesions, it remains unclear how DNA glycosylases achieve such rapid yet highly accurate detection of specific lesions among the overwhelming excess of undamaged bases [49]. Many damaged bases differ from canonical bases by only one or a few atoms, and the resulting distortion of DNA structure may be minimal; nevertheless, the enzyme must identify the lesion with exceptional specificity and speed. Moreover, a damaged base may closely resemble the natural substrate of a given glycosylase and yet still fail to be recognized as a substrate by that enzyme.

In Ref. [50], which addresses this central problem, Verdin and Bruner proposed two possible modes by which DNA glycosylases may search for damaged bases. The first applies to lesions that significantly destabilize DNA structure. In this case, the probability of spontaneous base eversion from the DNA duplex is increased, and the enzyme may bind the damaged base after it has already been partially or fully flipped out. The second mode applies to lesions that induce only minimal destabilization of DNA. Under these conditions, a more active search mechanism is required: the enzyme initially inserts an arbitrary base, located at some distance from the lesion, into its active site and then moves along the DNA duplex while sequentially interrogating individual bases until the damaged one is encountered, at which point catalytic excision begins.

Investigation of the mechanisms by which DNA glycosylases search for and select their substrates is of considerable current interest not only for advancing our fundamental understanding of DNA repair, but also for clarifying general principles of enzyme action and for enabling the design of protein catalysts with desired properties.

2.6 Human 8-oxoguanine-DNA glycosylase hOGG1

Human 8-Oxoguanine-DNA glycosylase (hOgg1) is one of the main cellular repair enzymes responsible for initiating repair from DNA of the endogenous mutagen, 8-oxoguanine. 8-Oxoguanine is the most common oxidative damage to the cell, its mutagenic effect is caused by the fact that the replacement of guanine by 8-oxoguanine when it is not removed in time leads to G:C→ A:T transversion [51]. 8-Oxoguanine-DNA glycosylase, is a bifunctional DNA glycosylase with both N-glycosylase and β-lyase activities. This enzyme belongs to the hHh-GPD family of DNA glycosylases, whose distinguishing structural feature is the presence of a highly conserved helix-loop-helix motif followed by a glycine- and proline-rich loop that ends with an aspartate-268 residue common to all enzymes in this family. For the bifunctional DNA glycosylases of this family, a lysine residue (in the case of hOgg1, Lys-249) acts as a nucleophile during catalytic cleavage by DNA [[52], [53], [54], [55]].

For human 8-oxoguanine-DNA-glycosylase, crystal structures for the free enzyme are known [56], its various complexes with damaged and intact and mutant forms carrying substitutions of amino acid residues in the active center of the enzyme [[57], [58], [59], [60]]. Like almost all other glycosylases, hOgg1 reverses the damaged base from the DNA helix and inserts it into the extrahelical pocket of the active center, which contains amino acid residues involved in both recognition and removal of the damage.

In the first study to investigate the search for oxidized guanosine among intact bases by human 8-oxoguanine-DNA-glycosylase, an atomic force microscopy technique was used to study protein-nucleic acid interactions in two-dimensional space on the surface of single-layer nanotubes [61]. This approach allowed us to determine the bending angles of individual DNA molecules upon interaction with the enzyme and to obtain the distribution of bending angles for DNA molecules containing no or one damaged base. In the case of DNA containing a single oxidized guanosine, the distribution of bending angles was well described by a Gaussian distribution with a maximum of 71°, which agreed well with previously known X-ray data [57]. Key results were obtained by studying the interaction of the enzyme with intact DNA. In this case, too, a large number of complexes with bent DNA were recorded, with 2/3 of all molecules being bent at the same angle as DNA containing ∼70° damage, the other maximum of the distribution corresponding to unbent DNA. That is, nonspecific interaction of the enzyme with DNA could induce the same changes in DNA structure as the formation of a specific complex. This distribution of bends with intact DNA with the existence of two maxima could be explained by the existence of two complexes of the enzyme with intact DNA. In the first step, the enzyme interacts with DNA through electrostatic interactions, not strongly distorting the DNA structure, and then the search for damage in three-dimensional space is reduced to a one-dimensional "active" search along the DNA molecule, during which DNA-helix bending and guanine eversion into the active center of the enzyme occur. These data were subsequently confirmed by X-ray diffraction data, which confirmed not only the bending but also the eversion of an intact base from the DNA helix during the interaction of hOgg1 with DNA [60]. It was further shown that the structure of complexes with intact and oxidized guanine located directly "deep" in the active center of the wild-type enzyme are almost identical [62]. At the same time, cleavage of the glycosidic bond with unoxidized guanine does not occur, although at neutral pH the N-glycosidic bond with guanine is more labile to hydrolysis than the bond with oxoguanine [63]. That is, the final identification and "verification" of the damage may already occur in the active center directly at the catalysis step. These data also support that during routine search for damage in DNA, human 8-oxoguanine-DNA glycosylase can sometimes evert and place guanine in the active center of the enzyme, without further cleavage of the glycosidic bond. The transition from one site to the next can take one of three pathways: unbending of DNA and nonspecific sliding to the next binding site; movement along the DNA while maintaining the bent complex, or release of DNA from the complex and subsequent binding to a neighboring site.

One-dimensional real-time diffusion of 8-oxoguanine-DNA-glycosylase during damage search has been reported in single-molecule experiments with good temporal and spatial resolution [64]. In this work, the authors studied the interaction of fluorescently labeled enzymes with intact DNA immobilized on a substrate in a flow cell. It was shown that hOgg1 moves in two directions along stretched DNA stretches, sliding along DNA molecules in close contact. Dependences for the average lifetimes of protein-nucleic acid complexes and on the pH and ionic strength of the solutions were obtained. The experimentally obtained diffusion coefficient at pH 7.8 turned out to be equal to 5 × 106 m2/s, which corresponds to 107 elementary steps per second. This value turned out to be less than the limiting value of the diffusion coefficient for hOgg1 – 1.2 × 107 m2/s, calculated on the basis of the hydrodynamic model [14,64]. Later, in Ref. [16] the calculation of the diffusion coefficient for hOgg1 sliding was proposed taking into account the helical rotation of the protein along the DNA helix; the obtained theoretical value (8.5 × 106 m2/s) turned out to be closer to the experimental one, but still exceeds it, which indicates the necessity to take into account additional factors affecting the sliding velocity.

According to the results obtained in Ref. [64], the value of the diffusion coefficient does not change with increasing ionic strength of the solution, while the average time of protein existence in the complex with DNA decreases significantly, which is evidence that the enzyme performs one-dimensional movement along DNA by the mechanism of sliding rather than microscopic jumping (hopping). Interestingly, the diffusion coefficient of hOgg1 along DNA increased by almost an order of magnitude as the pH increased from 6.6 to 7.8. The pKа of histidine may be in this range, with the conserved histidine-270 residue known from X-ray data to be in contact with DNA [57,60] and this may explain such dependence of the diffusion coefficient on pH. To test the hypothesis, we constructed a mutant form of the hOgg1 enzyme with a substitution of histidine-270 for alanine, for which the diffusion coefficient measured under similar conditions decreased to 0.8 × 106 m2/s and was independent of the pH of the medium. Thus, the role of His-270 is key at the damage-seeking stage, and interactions at the protein-nucleic acid contact surface have been evolutionarily selected to significantly reduce the activation barrier and promote hOgg1 sliding along DNA to efficiently seek and remove 8-oxoguanine from DNA.

The movement of hOgg1 along DNA at this diffusion coefficient allows 1000000 elementary movements per base pair in 0.1 s, such rapid sliding and low activation barriers preclude the possibility of eversion from the DNA helix and enzyme inspection of each base for 8-oxoguanine or finding all lesions during their spontaneous eversion, which occurs at a rather low frequency. These observations are a prerequisite that other mechanisms must exist for hOgg1 to facilitate the detection of intrahelical 8-oxoguanine and its subsequent eversion into the enzyme's active center [64].

In the following, using a similar experimental approach [65]experimentally confirmed the rotation of 8-oxoguanine-DNA-glycosylase during sliding of the enzyme along the DNA helix. The study was based on the fact that during simple sliding and during sliding accompanied by helical rotation of the protein around DNA, different dependence of diffusion coefficients on protein size 1/R and 1/R3, respectively, should be observed. This is due to the fact that during the motion accompanied by rotation, additional rotational friction components proportional to the solution viscosity associated with the rotation of the protein around the DNA axis and rotation relative to its axis arise [14,16,65]. Dividing the concepts of the protein radius R and the minimum distance from the center of the mass protein to the DNA axis Roc (Fig. 6), the authors obtained the dependences of the diffusion coefficient on protein size as 1/R, in the absence of rotation, and 1/[R3+ 3/4R(R)oc2 ], in the case of enzyme sliding along DNA accompanied by rotational motion [65].Fig. 6 Schematic representation of linear diffusion (A) and helical diffusion (B) during protein movement along DNA [65]. Having obtained diffusion coefficients for hOgg1 and its conjugate with streptavidin and analyzed the nature of the dependence of diffusion coefficients on the dimensional parameters of proteins, the authors concluded that the sliding of 8-oxoguanine-DNA glycosylase along the DNA helix is accompanied by a helical rotation around DNA, in which the contact of the protein binding site with the DNA surface is maintained during the entire movement. That is, for each revolution of the DNA helix, the protein makes a complete revolution around its axis. Analysis of diffusion coefficient values of the extended series of proteins obtained earlier (hOgg1 labeled Su3B [64] and E. coli LacI-YFP dimer [66]) and in this work (hOgg1 and its conjugate with streptavidin, MutY, MutM M74A, AVP-pVIc complex of adenovirus, BamHI restriction dimer, E. coli Klenow polymerase I fragment) including proteins of different sizes, families and classes confirmed that such a sliding-helical rotation mechanism may be a general mechanism of one-dimensional diffusion in the process of DNA-binding site search by DNA-binding proteins. In this case, different proteins can rotate around different DNA helical elements - small groove, large groove, etc. [65]. Another interesting conclusion of this work was that the calculated values for energy barriers when moving from one base pair to another turned out to be approximately equal for all proteins, regardless of their class and, of course, different number of contacts with the DNA surface, and small 1.1 ± 0.2 kBT, which allows DNA-binding proteins to glide quickly along DNA in the process of searching for the target sequence.

Thus, for human 8-oxoguanine-DNA glycosylase, the existence of rapid enzyme movement along DNA by a sliding mechanism accompanied by helical rotation around the DNA axis has been experimentally confirmed. The interaction of the enzyme with both specific and non-specific binding sites in DNA may result in bending of the DNA double helix and eversion of the damaged or undamaged base first into the exosite and then into the active center of the enzyme, with the "erroneous" eversion of guanine into the active center resulting in the final selection of damage at the catalysis stage.

2.6.1 Formamidopyrimidine-DNA glycosylase (Fpg, MutM)

The removal of oxidized guanosine in bacterial cells is the responsibility of the enzyme Fpg (MutM), which is a functional but not structural homolog of human 8-oxoguanine-DNA-glycosylase. This enzyme was first discovered In 1979 in E. coli as a DNA glycosylase that cleaves the methylated formamidopyrimidineguanine analog from DNA, and was therefore named formamidopyrimidine-DNA glycosylase (Fpg), its other name is MutM, and after it was shown that DNA glycosylase specific to 8-oxoGua bases was identical to Fpg, the enzyme was also referred to as bacterial 8-oxoguanine-DNA glycosylase [[67], [68], [69]]. Although this enzyme has broader substrate specificity than human hOgg1, it is its activity toward 8 -oxoguanine-containing substrates that has attracted particular attention because of its important biological role and the frequency of occurrence of this base damage [70,71]. Like hOgg1, Fpg is a bifunctional DNA glycosylase, but it is more active against AR sites and has three rather than two enzymatic activities. Removal of the damaged base during the N-glycosylase reaction (with the proline-1 terminal residue as the nucleophile) is followed by cleavage of the ribose phosphate backbone through sequential β- and δ-elimination reactions [[72], [73], [74]]. This enzyme belongs to the Fpg/Nei superfamily and contains highly conserved structural motifs: helix-two-turn-helix and zinc finger [71,75].

High-resolution structures have been obtained for Fpg enzymes from various organisms, both for the free enzyme and in complexes with damaged and undamaged DNA [[76], [77], [78]]. Some conclusions about the mechanism of damaged base retrieval in DNA can be drawn directly from the X-ray structural data. In particular, for Fpg from Bacillus stearotermophilus it was shown that the protein region responsible for the recognition of oxidized guanosine is a mobile loop, the structure of which is different for complexes with the helix-turned damaged and undamaged base, and it is the conformational rearrangements in the enzyme molecule that allow it to find and recognize the damaged base [76,77]. In 2006, for the same enzyme, the structures of complexes with intact DNA were obtained, and it was shown that the enzyme can "wedge" the amino acid residue of phenylalanine-114 into the DNA double helix next to the "checked" base, breaking the stacking interaction, in this case both bases from the pair are deflected, the nucleotide structure is bent, DNA is locally destabilized, but the nitrogenous base remains in the intrahelical position [79]. This interaction reflects the initial stages of enzyme-substrate complex (interrogation complex) formation and is a prerequisite for the fact that damage search occurs during active exploration of the DNA double helix by the protein without eversion of bases into the active center of the enzyme. In this case, the phenylalanine residue is a sensor for determining the ability to "deform" a base pair in DNA; its intercalation helps to distinguish oxoG:C pairs from canonical base pairs, although by itself it does not distort the structure of the double helix. And the destabilization of the DNA structure resulting from such intercalation may facilitate further eversion of the damaged base into the active center of the enzyme.

Further, in 2009, from the analysis of X-ray structure data and molecular dynamic modeling obtained for the complexes containing both normal guanine and ochOG, the assumption that the enzyme is able to distinguish the damaged base from the undamaged one at the very early stages of the enzyme's "collision" with the substrate and the formation of a pre-catalytic "collision" complex, in which all bases are in the DNA double helix, and there is no need to check each one by eversion. And the eversion of the target base from DNA is promoted by arginine-112 residue, which forms a hydrogen bond with it, "competing" with Watson-Crick interactions, and it is energetically more favorable to evert the damaged base specifically [78].

The results of the X-ray structural analysis agree well with the data obtained from various experiments to observe fluorescence of single molecules (single-molecule approach). As well as for hOgg1, it was possible to register the movement of the enzyme along DNA containing no specific lesions by the sliding mechanism. In this case, the activation energy of sliding is approximately equal to 3 kBT, and the value of the diffusion coefficient for Fpg from B. stearothermophilus was found to be (3.5 ± 0.6) × 105 m2/s [64]. This value is an order of magnitude lower than the value obtained in the same experiment for hOgg1, but at the same time the bacterial genome is a thousand times shorter; moreover, at the elevated temperature characteristic of the growth of these bacteria (up to 75 °C), the expected value of the diffusion coefficient will be much higher. Analysis of the dependence of the diffusion coefficient on the linear size of the protein also confirmed that for Fpg, one-dimensional diffusion along DNA is accompanied by rotation around the double helix [65].

Interesting results were observed in experiments with DNA-glycosylases of two families (HhH and Fpg/Nei) attached to quantum dots used as fluorophores. The use of conjugates of quantum dots with DNA-glycosylases and DNA intercalated with YOYO-1 dye for microscopy allowed us to obtain a "two-color" picture of the movement of DNA-glycosylases along DNA molecules in real time with good temporal and spatial resolution [80]. A similar search mechanism was observed for all three investigated DNA glycosylases from E. coli (Nth, Nei and Fpg), and the existence of a rotational component in the movement along DNA was also confirmed. DNA glycosylases moved along DNA with a wide range of possible velocities that differed up to two orders of magnitude: from slow – sub-diffusive movement, to faster - unrestricted diffusive movement (0.001 – 0.1 μm2/s). The authors concluded that the movement of DNA glycosylases, particularly Fpg, during damage search can be divided into two types: 'fast' and 'slow' search, and the 'switching' between these two types of movement and their relative contributions determine the value of the diffusion constant. A deeper understanding of the nature of the differences between these two types of searches was aided by experiments with a mutant form of Fpg from E. coli, in which the amino acid residue phenylalanine-111 (corresponding to phenylalanine-114 in the structure of Fpg from B. stearothermophilus) was replaced by alanine. A significant increase in the average diffusion coefficient was observed for the mutant form of Fpg F111A compared to the wild-type enzyme. At the same time, this increase was provided by selective disappearance from the set of observed DNA-glycosylases of those whose diffusion coefficient values were in the "slow" part of the enzyme molecule velocity distribution. That is, in the absence of a phenylalanine residue, Fpg was characterized only by rapid movement along DNA at rates limited by the one-dimensional diffusion rate. In combination with the data obtained for Fpg by X-ray diffraction analysis, kinetic experiments under steady-state conditions [81], data on the correlated cleavage of oligonucleotide substrates containing two oxidized guanosines [82], these results confirm that the phenylalanine residue plays a key role in the damage-finding stage. Its insertion into the DNA double helix "switches" the enzyme from the fast sliding phase in the form of a collision complex to the phase of active search for damage in the DNA double helix, with the phenylalanine residue playing the role of a sensor to help detect the oxidized base in the DNA double helix Fig. 7 [80,83]. In Ref. [84] using X-ray structure analysis in combination with molecular dynamics simulation calculations, it was shown that enzyme movement along intact DNA can take place unrelatedly along two complementary strands: the protein shifts by one base pair along one strand while remaining in an unchanged position relative to the complementary strand. This displacement causes a "skew" or tilt of the nitrogenous bases relative to the ribose-phosphate backbone of DNA, the nucleotides involved in protein-nucleic acid contact. The authors also showed the involvement of the amino acid residue arginine-112, which can induce this type of enzyme movement (strandwise translocation) along intact DNA. The registration of the "skewed" state of the enzyme-substrate complex opens new prerequisites for further investigation and understanding of the mechanisms of fast and efficient search for damaged bases by DNA-glycosylases. The authors have shown for the first time the possibility of the existence of a previously unpredicted mechanism of DNA-glycosylase movement along the DNA helix using the example of Fpg from B. stearothermophilus.

2.7 Current view

The current view on the mechanisms of damage detection in DNA emphasizes the ability of repair proteins to overcome the so-called “speed–stability paradox”: the necessity of rapidly scanning the vast genome while simultaneously ensuring high-affinity and stable binding at rare lesion sites [85,86]. To resolve this paradox, current research specifically focuses on flexible search strategies, conformational interrogation of the DNA helix, and cooperative interactions between repair factors.

Increasing experimental evidence suggests that many DNA repair proteins employ facilitated diffusion, switching dynamically between three-dimensional (3D) diffusion in solution and one-dimensional (1D) displacement along the DNA duplex via sliding or hopping. This dimensionality switching appears to be a general principle that substantially increases the efficiency of target site localization [[85], [86], [87]]. For example, UvrA proteins involved in bacterial nucleotide excision repair predominantly perform 3D search, transiently binding to DNA. However, upon forming a complex with UvrB, the system switches to a 1D sliding regime, thereby reducing the dimensionality of the search and extending its lifetime on DNA [87]. An analogous mechanism has been described for human base excision repair. Poly(ADP-ribose) polymerase 1 (PARP1) exhibits dynamic transitions between different diffusive states [88]. Initial DNA binding events are relatively immobile; however, auto-PARylation acts as a regulatory modification that transforms the enzyme into a more mobile, subdiffusive one-dimensional (1D) state, confining it to an area of several hundred base pairs around the lesion. This constrained local motion is believed to optimize target site recognition by balancing scanning efficiency with focused sampling [88]. A related principle is observed for DNA ligase 1: the non-catalytic N-terminal domain of the enzyme enables 1D diffusion along duplex DNA, facilitating accumulation at single-strand breaks and enhancing ligation accuracy [89].

Stable recognition of damaged sites in DNA frequently proceeds through direct interrogation of the helix structure, whereby repair proteins exploit intrinsic flexibility or actively impose local distortions. Such processes couple conformational sensing of the substrate with selective stabilization of complexes at lesions. An illustrative example is provided by the nucleotide excision repair protein XPA, which binds DNA as a monomer and induces a pronounced bend of approximately 60° in the double helix, independent of lesion presence [85,86,89,90]. This bending appears to serve as a mechanical test of local flexibility: at sites such as dG-C8-AAF adducts [90], where bending is energetically favorable, complex stabilization is enhanced. The dynamics of XPA are characterized by alternating phases of one-dimensional linear diffusion and stochastic pausing, the latter becoming more frequent and prolonged upon encountering damaged DNA. Importantly, the intrinsically disordered N- and C-terminal regions of the protein are essential for establishing this damage-specific paused state [85,90]. Another widely employed strategy is exemplified by DNA glycosylases such as Fpg, Nei, and Nth, which utilize a hydrophobic “wedge” residue during one-dimensional scanning [86,91]. This side chain intercalates between base pairs, inducing local disruption of stacking interactions and transient puckering of the double helix. Such probing corresponds to a slower diffusive mode, necessary for chemical verification of potential lesions. Mutational loss of the wedge residue abolishes this mode, resulting in faster scanning but significantly reduced capacity to detect and arrest at damaged sites [86,91]. In the case of MutY human homolog (MUTYH), recognition of 8-oxoG:A mispairs is governed by exquisite specificity toward the 2-amino substituent of 8-oxoG, uniquely presented in the major groove [92]. Within the C-terminal Domain β-hairpin loop, bearing the residues Phe-Ser-His, a conserved histidine residue acts as the critical lesion-sensing determinant; substitution of this residue abolishes lesion discrimination, functionally “blinding” the enzyme to its substrate [92].

Efficient repair requires not only the capacity of individual proteins to interrogate and recognize lesions but also coordinated interactions among different repair factors. Crosstalk between pathways such as NER and BER provides mechanisms to alleviate product inhibition, promote enzyme turnover, and ensure seamless progression through downstream repair steps [86,91,93]. For example, one of the critical contributions of UV-damaged DNA-binding (UV-DDB) protein lies in its ability to alleviate product inhibition of BER enzymes [85,86,93,94]. By forming transient ternary complexes with OGG1, MUTYH, or APE1 on DNA, UV-DDB facilitates dissociation of these enzymes from abasic site products, thereby enabling their turnover and multiple rounds of catalysis [85,86,94]. Beyond its canonical role in NER, UV-DDB exhibits exceptionally high affinity for intermediates of BER, particularly abasic site analogs such as tetrahydrofuran, for which binding is ∼290-fold stronger compared to undamaged DNA [93]. Similarly, UV-DDB binds with high affinity to 8-oxoG-containing base pairs, underscoring its role as a broad-spectrum lesion detector [86,93]. Live-cell studies have further demonstrated that UV-DDB is recruited to sites of 8-oxoG damage before OGG1, emphasizing its function as an early-acting sensor and coordinator of BER [85,86]. Efficient repair is further facilitated through direct cooperation among BER proteins. For example, the presence of APE1, even in catalytically inactive form, can induce dissociation of PARP1 from abasic sites and initiate one-dimensional diffusion of PARP1 along DNA. This “facilitated dissociation” mechanism arises because APE1 competes for overlapping binding surfaces, preventing rebinding of PARP1's multiple DNA-interacting domains and thereby releasing stalled complexes [86,88]. Whereas in the presence of the PARP inhibitor olaparib, auto-PARylation of PARP1 is blocked, but instead of stabilizing static binding, olaparib induces a conformational shift that increases the motile fraction of PARP1. The resulting motion is constrained and anomalous, with little effect on dissociation kinetics, suggesting a form of inhibitor-induced “trapping” that alters diffusion dynamics without directly preventing release [88].

Taken together, these findings demonstrate that DNA repair proteins integrate dimensionality switching, conformational interrogation of the helix, and cooperative protein–protein interactions to achieve rapid yet highly selective lesion detection. Such complementary strategies enable cells to resolve the inherent speed–stability paradox, ensuring both efficient genome surveillance and precise handoff between successive steps of repair.

3 Conclusions

Despite extensive research, the detailed mechanisms by which DNA-binding proteins and DNA glycosylases locate their specific targets in DNA have not yet been fully resolved. Nevertheless, the accumulated evidence strongly supports the view that efficient target search is achieved through a combination of complementary mechanisms rather than through any single mode of diffusion or recognition. These mechanisms include one-dimensional sliding, two-dimensional diffusion over the DNA surface, intersegmental transfer, hopping and jumping, local probing of DNA structure, and multistep conformational verification of candidate sites.

For DNA glycosylases in particular, the problem of lesion recognition is especially challenging because damaged bases are rare and often differ only subtly from normal nucleotides. The available data indicate that these enzymes solve the speed–specificity problem by integrating facilitated diffusion with local interrogation of DNA and progressive stabilization of productive enzyme–DNA complexes. Studies of hOGG1, Fpg, and other glycosylases have shown that lesion search is a dynamic and reversible process involving coordinated structural transitions of both the protein and the DNA substrate.

Further progress in this field will depend on the combination of single-molecule methods, structural biology, quantitative kinetics, molecular modeling, and studies performed under conditions that more closely reproduce the intracellular environment. Such approaches should clarify not only the molecular details of lesion search but also the principles by which repair enzymes function in crowded and highly organized genomic contexts. A deeper understanding of these processes is important both for fundamental insight into genome maintenance and for the rational design of enzymes and therapeutic strategies aimed at controlling DNA repair pathways.
