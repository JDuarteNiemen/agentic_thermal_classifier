Paper: PMC6963370.1.txt
Thermal Evidence:
- Thermophile endolysin catalytic domains were sourced from bacteria isolated from hot springs at Yellowstone National Park, USA, and grown at 70 °C in the laboratory.
- Another source bacterium was collected from an 88 °C water outflow from a hot spring.
- A third endolysin was isolated from a deep-sea hydrothermal vent, and its parent enzyme was previously shown to be active at temperatures up to 80 °C.
- The resulting chimeric proteins showed improved thermostability compared to native endolysins.
- The fusion proteins retained substantial activity after heat challenges at 60 °C, and some showed activity after 70 °C heat treatment.
- The best performing fusion protein retained >14% of its activity after incubation at 95 °C.
- The study compared thermostability to native lysins, noting that native lysins often lost activity between 50 °C and 60 °C.
- The optimal conditions for testing residual activity included incubation at 15 min at elevated temperatures, followed by cooling and assaying at 40 °C.

Environmental Context:
- Sources include hot springs (Yellowstone National Park, deep-sea hydrothermal vents) and high-temperature water outflows.

Concise Summary:
The study utilized endolysin catalytic domains derived from thermophilic bacteria, with sources including hot springs (Yellowstone National Park, 70 °C growth) and deep-sea hydrothermal vents. The enzymes exhibit inherent thermostability, with one source enzyme active up to 80 °C. The chimeric fusion proteins created showed enhanced thermostability compared to native enzymes, retaining activity after heat challenges up to 70 °C, and one candidate maintained activity after 95 °C incubation. The optimal conditions for assessing thermostability involved incubation at elevated temperatures followed by testing at 40 °C. The overall goal was to create enzymes stable enough for use in heat-treated animal feed pellets (steam conditioning up to 95 °C).
