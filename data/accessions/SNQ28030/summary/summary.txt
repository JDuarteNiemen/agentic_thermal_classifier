Concise Summary:
This study focuses on the molecular surveillance and diagnostic concordance of respiratory pathogens (bacterial, viral, and fungal) in critically ill ICU patients. The analysis utilized targeted next-generation sequencing (tNGS) comparing nasopharyngeal swabs (NPS) to bronchoalveolar lavage fluid (BALF). The pathogens detected include common nosocomial bacteria (e.g., *Acinetobacter baumannii*, *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*, *Staphylococcus aureus*), various viruses (e.g., SARS-CoV-2, Influenza), and fungi (e.g., *Candida* species, *Pneumocystis jirovecii*). The study provides high concordance data for bacterial detection in the context of severe pneumonia but notes variable and often low concordance for fungal and viral pathogens. No information regarding the optimal growth temperature, thermal adaptation, or environmental source beyond the human respiratory tract was provided for any of the identified microorganisms.

Paper: PMC13144091.1.txt
Thermal Evidence:
- Optimal stability was observed at a temperature range of 4°C–37°C.
- The phages demonstrated stability at 4°C, 25°C, and 37°C.
- Phage titers were completely inactivated at 70°C (for one phage) and 80°C (for the other phage).
- The phages were stable between pH 4.0 and 7.0, but were completely inactivated at pH 1.0 and 2.0.
- The phages were highly sensitive to strongly acidic and alkaline conditions.

Environmental Context:
- The phages were isolated from animal husbandry and hospital wastewater.

Concise Summary:
The bacteriophages exhibited optimal stability across a temperature range of 4°C to 37°C. They maintained viability at 4°C, 25°C, and 37°C, but showed significant inactivation at higher temperatures, with one phage losing activity at 70°C and the other at 80°C. Stability was also noted across a pH range of 4.0 to 7.0, indicating sensitivity to extreme pH levels. The phages were isolated from wastewater sources, suggesting environmental resilience. The overall profile suggests mesophilic characteristics with limited thermal tolerance above 50°C.
