The Kuril Islands are located in the Far-East of Russia and enriched with shallow and terrestrial hot springs. Prokaryotic diversity of Kuril geothermal environments has been studied fragmentarily and mainly by culture-dependent methods. We performed the first large-scale investigation of microbial communities, inhabited more than 30 terrestrial hot springs of Kunashir and Iturup Islands, analyzed by 16S rRNA gene fragment amplicon sequencing, together with chemical analysis of thermal waters and sediments. The Circumneutral Bacterial group containing springs with pH 5.7–8.5 and temperature 40–79 °C possessed the highest biodiversity and consisted almost entirely of Bacteria. Cyanobacteriota (the Leptolyngbyaceae and Oculatellaceae families) and phototrophic Chloroflexota dominated in the microbial mats in hot springs with temperatures up to 60 °C. The higher temperature ones were dominated by Aquificota (Sulfurihydrogenibium and Hydrogenobacter species). The Acidic Bacterial group (pH 2.2–3.6, 41–64 °C) inhabited by the genera Acidithiobacillus, Hydrogenobaculum and Thiomonas. Archaea of Acidianus, Metallosphaera, Thermoplasma and Caldisphaera spp. as well as uncultivated lineages (‘Ca. Marsarchaeales’, ‘Ca. Caldiarchaeum’, BSLdp215) were abundant in the Acidic Archaeal group (pH 1.5–2.9, 50–94 °C). The microbial composition of the Kuril hot springs strongly correlated with pH and moderately correlated with water chemistry, while degree of correlation between the communities’ compositions with temperature and location was low.

Background

The Kuril Islands, a chain of volcanic islands located between the Kamchatka Peninsula of Russia and Hokkaido Island in Japan are part of a global tectonic system called the Ring of Fire. They include 56 islands (4 of which are inhabited) with 39 active volcanoes and numerous thermal springs [1–3]. Despite the abundance of hot springs throughout the Kuril Islands, only Kunashir and Iturup are in the primary focus of scientific research in recent decades probably due to transport accessibility [4, 5]. Iturup is the largest island among the Kurils, and 9 volcanoes are active there. Kunashir is located in the south of the archipelago and has 4 active volcanoes. The physicochemical characteristics of thermal waters of Kunashir and Iturup are extremely diverse in mineralization, pH, temperature and elemental composition [3, 6–9]. Seventeen geothermal zones with different types of water composition, including landscape types, are identified on these islands [2]. Among dissolved salts the most common are halides, sulfates and hydrocarbonates. Dominated cations are Na+ and Ca2+. Temperature in the Kuril hot springs varies from 30 to 100 °C and pH – from 1 to 8.5. The main gas components of the hydrothermal fluids are also miscellaneous and could be CO2, N2, CH4, S-containing gasses or inert gasses, like Ar and Rn [2, 10–12].

Studies on microbial diversity of the Kuril hot springs have only been carried out on Kunashir. These works were focused on isolation and description of pure cultures of thermophilic microorganisms – about 15 species of Archaea and Bacteria were isolated so far, and they belong to the Bacteroidota, Calditrichaeota, Firmicutes (for now correct name is Bacillota according to the List of Prokaryotic names with Standing in Nomenclature (LPSN), https://lpsn.dsmz.de/, and the Genome Taxonomy Database (GTDB), https://gtdb.ecogenomic.org/), Planctomycetes (Planctomycetota), Proteobacteria (Pseudomonadota), Thermodesulfobacteriota, Thermotogota, Crenarchaeota (Thermoproteota), and Euryarchaeota (Methanobacteriota) [13]. In 2020, the first study on the microbial communities of two warm springs (18 and 38 °C) using 16S rRNA amplicon sequencing was reported [14]. Up to now, all the springs studied by next generation sequencing (NGS) are located only in the zone of Mendeleev volcano. It was shown that Faust Lake (pH 2.0, 48 °C) and Tretyakovsky thermal pool (pH 6.0, 52 °C) were dominated by Proteobacteria and Actinomycetota, whereas the Aqiuficota, Chloroflexota and Crenarchaeota were the most numerous in Stolbovskye springs with 17–81 °C and pH 6.0–7.5 [11, 15]. Despite the above-mentioned findings, the patterns and ecological drivers of microbial communities inhabiting the Kuril hot springs remain largely unknown.

Here we report for the first time the structure of the microbial communities of diverse Kuril hot springs, including more than 30 sampling sites obtained from solfatara fields, marine terraces, calderas, and coastal zones, and its correlation with environmental factors. The features of prokaryotes diversity in poorly studied geothermal environments, associated with activity of Baransky, Mendeleev and Golovnin volcanoes, were investigated. The aim of the work is to provide background information that can promote the potential ecological, industrial and anthropogenic value of microbiological resources, and can contribute to the protection and rational development of hot springs.

Methods

Sampling

For DNA isolation ~500 mg of sediments/biofilm/soil mixed with thermal water were collected in 2 mL Eppendorf tubes with 1 mL sterile fixation buffer (100 mM EDTA, 100 mM Tris HCl, 150 mM NaCl). For chemical and elemental analysis 150 mL of thermal water per each sample were placed into three sterile 50 mL Falcon tubes. For determination of iron compounds content 100 mL of sediments were collected into sterile 50 mL Falcon tubes, and the rest of air space was poured with 96% ethanol to get rid of oxygen. 30 µL of thermal water were fixed in 500 µL zinc acetate in sterile 2 mL Eppendorf tubes for H2S detection. All samples were stored at 4 °C during the expedition and transported to the laboratory in thermo-packs with refrigerants. The pH and temperature were measured in situ using SG2 SevenGo™ pH meter (Mettler-Toledo, Switzerland) with InLab® Expert Go sensor (Mettler-Toledo, Switzerland).

Chemical analysis of thermal water and sediments

Water chemistry analyses were carried out at Tomsk Polytechnic University (Russia). Potentiometric titration (ANION 7051, Russia) was used to HCO3-ion detection; the ICS-2000 ion chromatography system (Spectronica, USA) with conductometric detection was used to determine SO42−, NO3−, NO2−, PO43−, and Cl− ions; capillary electrophoresis (Capel-105 M, Russia) was used to determine Na+, K+, Ca2+, Mg2+, and NH4+ cations. Total dissolved solids (TDS) were calculated from the sum of major cations and anions (K+, Na+, Ca2+, Mg2+, Cl−, SO42−, HCO3−). Water hardness was counted from the sum of Ca2+ and Mg2+ concentration. Inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry analysis was carried out using a Perkin Elmer NexION 300D mass-spectrometer (USA) for the elemental analysis of waters. Dissolved H2S was measured colorimetrically [16] at Genesys 10Vis spectrophotometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA) at λ = 670 nm. For mineralogical studies, samples were analyzed using X-ray powder diffraction approach using an ULTIMA-IV X-ray diffractometer Rigaku (Japan) at Moscow State University (Russia) with the following operating mode: 40 kV-40 mA, copper radiation, nickel filter, a new generation semiconductor detector DTex/Ultra. Diagnostics of the mineral composition was carried out by comparing the experimental and reference spectra from the PDF-2 database in the Jade 6.5 software package, produced by MDI (https://www.icdd.com/mdi-jade/).

DNA extraction

Environmental DNA from water and sediment samples was isolated using FastDNA™ Spin Kit for Soil (MP Biomedicals, USA) according to the manufacturer’s instructions with mechanical cell disruption using a FastPrep-24™ 5G homogenizer (MP Biomedicals, USA). The DNA concentration was measured with Qubit 2.0 (Thermo Fisher Scientific) using Qubit dsDNA HS Assay Kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA).

16S rRNA gene amplicon sequencing

Amplicon libraries of V4 hypervariable region of 16S rRNA gene were prepared as described in [17] with the following universal primers 515F (5’- GTG BCA GCM GCC GCG GTA A-3’) and 806R (5′- GAC TAC NVG GGT MTC TAA TCC-3′) [18, 19] including Illumina technical sequences [20]. High-throughput sequencing was performed with MiSeq Reagent Micro Kit v2 (300-cycles) MS-103-1002 (Illumina, USA) on a MiSeq sequencer (Illumina, USA) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. The libraries were prepared and sequenced in two replicates for each sample.

Data analysis

Primary raw reads were processed as described at [21]. The raw reads were pre-processed as described earlier [20] and were demultiplexed using QIIME v.1.9.1. Further all reads were filtered by quality as well as bases affiliated to 16S rRNA primers were removed using CLC Genomics Workbench 10.0 (Qiagen, Germany). Operational taxonomic units (OTU) picking and taxonomy assignment were done using the SILVAngs service (https://ngs.arb-silva.de/silvangs/; SILVA r138.1 database). Taxonomic classification was performed using SILVA while taxa names according to GTDB nomenclature (https://gtdb.ecogenomic.org/) were additionally provided if it was possible. Mitochondrial and chloroplast sequences were deleted from initial datasets without recalculation of relative abundances for other taxonomic units. Data visualization was performed in Excel and R using the ggplot2 package [22]. Rarefaction curves were obtained using iNEXT package v.2.0.20 [23]. Prokaryotic phyla co-occurrence network was constructed using phylosmith package v.1.0.7 with rho threshold of 0.4 and p-value threshold of 0.05 (https://joss.theoj.org/papers/10.21105/joss.01442), and was visualized using ggrpah package v.2.2.1 (https://ggraph.data-imaginist.com). Mantel tests (Spearman method, 9999 permutations) were used to determine the impact of the environmental variables (Euclidean distance) on OTU compositions (Bray distance) in vegan v.2.6.4 package (https://CRAN.R-project.org/package=vegan). Pairwise Spearman’s rank correlations between relative abundance of basic taxa at order level (≥ 0.05% in at least one spring) and environmental variables were calculated in “microbiome” package v.1.8.0 (https://bioconductor.org/packages/release/bioc/html/microbiome.html). Alpha-diversity analysis based on Chao1, Shannon and inverse Simpson indexes was proven using phyloseq v.1.30.0 [24]. To estimate beta-diversity a non-metric multidimensional scaling (NMDS) ordination with Bray-Curtis dissimilarity matrix was performed using vegan v.2.6.4 package. Statistical analysis for T, pH and Archaea-Bacteria composition data was also performed in R stats broom package (https://www.rdocumentation.org/packages/broom/versions/1.0.4) and consisted of normality-proven Shapiro-Wilk test and Kruskall-Wallis or ANOVA tests for finding any statistical differences between NMDS-groups, and pair compilation which was provided via Tukey or Dunn tests. All sequencing data were deposited into the NCBI SRA database under BioProject number PRJNA1125859.

Results

Characteristics of the sampling sites

Thirty-four samples of water, sediments, microbial mats, and biofilms were collected from hot springs of 13 thermal groups during the expedition to Iturup and Kunashir islands in August 2023 (Fig. 1). On Iturup Island (samples 4402–4409), samples of water, sediments and solfatara soil were collected from two geothermal zones in the area of Reidovo village and the solfatara field of Baransky volcano. Temperature range in the sampling sites was 45–94 °C, pH range was 1.5–7.5. On Kunashir Island (4412–4450), samples of water, sediments and biofilms were collected from 11 geothermal zones located near Golovnin volcano (Kipyashchee lake, Northern and Southern Alekhin springs, Cherepakhovoe and Bezymyannoe solfatara fields) and Mendeleev volcano (Kislyi stream, Goryachyi Beach, Nizhnedoctorskye, Tretyakovskye and Stolbovskye springs, Northwest solfatara field). Temperature range in the sampling sites was 40–79 °C, pH range – 1.8–8.5. Detailed description and characteristics of the zones and samples are presented in Additional file 1 Supplementary Table 1. Water chemistry analysis revealed a low TDS variety (0.174–3.049 g L− 1), but a vast water hardness range (0.4–12.5 mEq L− 1), with domination of SO42− and Cl− anions and Na+ and Ca2+ cations. In some springs the Ca2+ ions were predominant. Full results of chemical and elemental analyses with H2S and iron total content are described further and also presented in Additional file 2 Supplementary Table 2A and Additional file 3 Supplementary Table 2B.

16S rRNA metabarcoding of microbial communities

Sequencing of V4 region of gene 16S rRNA resulted in 1688 bacterial OTUs and 80 archaeal OTUs. Rarefaction curves confirmed that sequencing depth was sufficient for all samples (Additional file 4 Supplementary Fig. 1). Bacteria predominated in 27 samples, ranging from 49.3 to 99.6% of the total microbial community (Fig. 2). Seven hot springs were predominated by Archaea (from 58.9 to 96.5%).

Among Bacteria (Fig. 2) sequences affiliated with the Proteobacteria (Pseudomonadota), Aquificota and Chloroflexi (Chloroflexota) were the most widespread. Sulfurihydrogenibium (3.5–72.6%), Hydrogenobaculum (4.5–66.1%), Roseiflexus (1.8–53%), Acidithiobacillus (4.7–44.5%), and Thiomonas (1.3–44.4%) represented the most numerous genera. Crenarchaeota (Thermoproteota) and Thermoplasmatota OTUs dominated among archaeal sequences (Fig. 2). The genera Thermoplasma (4.7–80.1%), Sulfolobus (2.5–69.5%) and Acidianus (2.13-28%) belonged to the most abundant and widespread archaeal taxa, as well as uncultured deep phylogenetic lineages, such as BSLdp215 (1.1–20.4%), ‘Ca. Caldiarchaeales’ (1.5–25%) and ‘Ca. Geoarchaeales’ (1.6–42.5%). According to our unpublished data, the last one affiliated with ‘Ca. Marsarchaeales’ and further we will use this taxonomic name.

Co-occurrence network (Fig. 3) analysis with statistically significant correlations on phyla-level showed 63 hub-taxa. The most abundant relationships derived from Desulfobacterota (24 edges), Chloroflexi (Chloroflexota) (22 edges), Calditrichota (20 edges), Nitrospirota (19 edges), Acidobacteriota (16 edges), and some uncultivated deep phylogenetic bacterial lineages, like Acetothermia (Bipolaricaulota) (17 edges), MBNT15 (20 edges) and Sva0485 groups (15 edges). Negative co-occurrences were observed for 9 phyla, and the most abundant negative edges were found for Thermoplasmatota (10 edges) and Crenarchaeota (Thermoproteota) (14 edges).

Fig. 3 Co-occurrence network of the Kuril Islands hot springs microbial communities on the phyla level

Correlations of environmental factors and microbial diversity

Mantel tests revealed that composition of Kuril hot springs microbial communities significantly associated with some environmental factors (Table 1). The microbial diversity had high degree of correlation with pH values (R = 0.66) as well as moderate degree of correlation with some water chemical parameters, such as TDS (R = 0.44), water element composition (R = 0.54) and ion water composition (R = 0.47). On the other hand, correlation with temperature and geographic location were relatively low, R = 0.24 and R = 0.19, respectively. Water hardness and Fe content in spring sediments did not significantly correlate with the community composition. Pairwise correlation analysis (Additional file 4 Supplementary Fig. 2) showed reliable correlations (either positive or negative) between the relative abundance of major prokaryotic orders with pH and TDS values, Na+, K+, Cl−, HCO3−, or NO2− ions concentrations, as well as concentrations of lanthanides and several elements, such as W, Cs, Y, Sr, Rb, Br, Se, Ge, Zn, Co, Fe, V, P, Si, Al, Li, U, and Th. Notably, some correlations could be dictated by the pH level of the springs (lanthanides, TDS level, soluble metal ions etc.) [25, 26], and their true impact on microbial composition must be experimentally evidenced.

Alpha and beta-diversity

Alpha-diversity analysis (Additional file 5 Supplementary Table 3) revealed a huge variety of Shannon (0.8–5.1), inverse Simpson (1.5–94.2) and Chao1 indices (13–358). In accordance with NMDS analysis of the microbial abundance profiles, all springs allocated to three groups linked with the pH range of them: the Circumneutral Bacterial group (CNB), the Acidic Archaeal group (AA) and the Acidic Bacterial group (AB) (Fig. 4). Statistical analysis revealed the significant differences between СNB group and AA group in Bacteria and Archaea representativity (p ≤ 0.001) and pH-range (p ≤ 0.001). AA group and AB group differed in Bacteria content (p = 0.048), Archaea content (p = 0.037) and T-range (p = 0.039). CNB group and AB group differed in pH-range (p = 0.005).

Fig. 4 Clustering of microbial communities inhabiting the Kuril hot springs via NMDS ordination and typical geothermal manifestations of each group. A – results of NMDS ordination based on Bray–Curtis distances (k = 3; stress value = 0.095), B – Circumneutral Bacterial group, C – Acidic Bacterial group, D – Acidic Archaeal group

CNB group included 20 hot springs (4402–4404, 4416–4418, 4423, 4431, 4432, 4438–4441, 4445–4450) from Kipyashchee lake, Goryachyi Beach, Northern and Southern Alekhin springs, Nizhnedoctorskye, Tretyakovskye and Stolbovskye springs (Kunashir) and a hot spring near Reidovo village (Iturup). Temperature and pH ranges varied there from 40 to 79 °C and from 5.7 to 8.5, respectively, with the only exception of the spring #4431 with рН 3.1, located in the marine intertidal zone, where pH range could vary significantly. Chemical analysis of 12 thermal water samples revealed a wide range of TDS (0.777–3.049 g L− 1), water hardness (1.5–12.5 mEq L− 1) and dominance of Na+ (91.4–900 mg L− 1), Ca2+ (27.5-235.7 mg L− 1), Cl− (35.8–1750 mg L− 1), and SO42− (19–567 mg L− 1) ions there (Additional file 2 Supplementary Table 2A). The total iron content in springs sediments was in a range of 0.14–9.21%, with the only exception of #4438 (40.1%), which was sampled from an old iron pipe. H2S was detected only in #4423 sample in an amount of 3.89 mmol L− 1. Elemental analysis showed that level of Al, V, Fe, Co, Zn, Y, Zr, In, and lanthanides in this group (except #4431 with low pH) was lower, while level of alkali and alkaline earth metals, as well as Si, Ge, As, Se, Br, I, Ru, Rh concentrations was higher than in AA and AB (Additional file 3 Supplementary Table 2B). The microbial communities of this group were the most diverse (Additional file 5 Supplementary Table 3) and dominated almost exclusively by Bacteria (85.7–99.6%) (Additional file 4 Supplementary Fig. 3). OTUs affiliated with Sulfurihydrogenibium (3.5–72.6%), Roseiflexus (1.8–53%), Thermus (1.7–64.6%), uncultivated representatives of Leptolyngbyaceae named JSC-12 (3.2–19.9%), Lysinibacillus (1.4–48%), Chloroflexus (1.8–21.1%), Serratia (5.3–29.2%), unclassified Acetothermia (Bipolaricaulota) (1.6–22%), Hydrogenobacter (1.3–8.3%), uncultured Anaerolineaceae (1.3–4.2%), Caldimicrobium (2-8.6%), uncultivated Anaerolineae named SBR1031 (1.1–3.3%), Thermodesulforhabdus (1.4–12.1%), ‘Ca. Alysiosphaera’ (1-6.5%), unclassified Kapabacteriales (1.1–4.9%), uncultured Thermodesulfovibrionia (1-7.9%), Bacillus (1.2–10.3%), Thiobacter (1-6.8%), and group RCP2-54 (Desulfobacterota) (1.1–11.1%) were the most abundant. Sequences affiliated with Geitlerinema PCC-8501 (18.7%), Pannonibacter (12.6–21.4%), Hydrogenobaculum (27.1%), ‘Ca. Chloroploca’ (2.4–16.3%), or Porticoccaceae C1-B045 (16.4%) were observed in single spring’s communities as most abundant.

Seven hot springs (4405, 4407, 4412–4414, 4443, 4444) with 50–94 °C and extremely low pH (1.5–2.9) from Kipyashchee Lake, Kislyi stream, Northwest solfatara field of Mendeleev volcano (Kunashir) and solfatara field of Baransky volcano (Iturup) fell into AA group. Chemical thermal water analysis was made only for #4444 sample and showed a low TDS value (0.633 g L− 1), 4.2 mEq L− 1 of water hardness and dominance of Ca2+ (55.1 mg L− 1) and SO42− (406 mg L− 1) ions. H2S in water (3.96 mmol L− 1) and 5.25% of total iron content in sediments were detected (Additional file 2 Supplementary Table 2A). In this group Archaea totally dominated, composing 58.9–96.5% of the total microbial communities. The most abundant genera were Thermoplasma (4.7–80.1%), Sulfolobus or Saccharolobus (2.5–69.5%), Caldisphaera (3.3–35.9%), Acidianus (2.1–28%), and Metallosphaera (2.1–29.2%). Large proportion of Mesoaciditoga spp. (35.5%) was observed in single spring #4444, which explains its close location to the AB group. Significant values of representativity were also detected for uncultivated deep lineages, such as ‘Ca. Marsarchaeales’ (1.6–42.5%) and ‘Ca. Caldiarchaeales’ (1.5–25%) (both from the Crenarchaeota), BSLdp215 (1.1–20.4%) and A10 (or ARK15) (6.25%) groups from the Thermoplasmatota (Additional file 4 Supplementary Fig. 4).

AB group consisted of 7 acidic (pH 2.2–3.6) springs (4409, 4421, 4425, 4428, 4430, 4433, 4436) with moderate high temperature of 41–64 °C from Cherepakhovoe and Bezymyannoe solfatara fields of Golovnin volcano, Northern and Southern Alekhin springs (Kunashir) and Kipyashchyi stream on solfatara field of Baransky volcano (Iturup). Chemical analysis of samples from the springs 4421, 4425, 4428, 4436 showed a low TDS value (0.174–0.747 g L− 1), soft and medium-hard water hardness level (0.4–4.1 mEq L− 1) with SO42− (136–449 mg L− 1), Na+(8.5–97.7 mg L− 1) and Ca2+ (5-72.7 mg L− 1) prevalence. Amounts of H2S in water were 0-0.95 mmol L− 1. Total iron content range in sediments was 2.22–14.8% (Additional file 2 Supplementary Table 2A). Bacteria were the dominant component there (49.3–98.3%) and the majority was affiliated with Hydrogenobaculum (6.3–66.1%), Acidithiobacillus (20.5–44.5%), Thiomonas (1.3–44.4%), Mesoaciditoga (5-7.6%), Acidiphilium (3.2–11.6%), and Desulfurella (1.5–5.7%). Serratia (21.5%) and Sulfuriferula (16.3%) OTUs were numerous in distinct springs. Only in the sample #4421 archaeal representatives of the genus Thermoplasma accounted for a significant part (27.6%) of the community (Additional file 4 Supplementary Fig. 5).

Discussion

Geothermal environments of active volcanic areas strongly vary in pH, chemical content, gas concentration, and so forth. The physicochemical conditions in springs dictate the diversity of microorganisms inhabiting them [27–29], and it turns out that hot springs of the Kuril Islands are not an exception. In our work, we conducted the first large-scale screening of the microbial communities of these hot springs using metabarcoding based on the V4 region of the 16S rRNA gene, concentrating our focus on two islands – Iturup and Kunashir, where a lot of geothermal sites are located. We analyzed 34 samples from thermal springs with temperature and pH ranges of 40–94 °C and 1.5–8.5, respectively, discharging in the areas of three active volcanoes. It was revealed that geothermal springs analyzed here have common patterns in the chemical composition of thermal waters (Additional file 4 Supplementary Fig. 6), similar to Japan and Kamchatka (Russia) [30–35], and the microbial diversity of the springs studied was highly dependent on pH values followed by water chemical parameters such as TDS, ionic and elemental composition of water (Table 1, Additional file 4 Supplementary Fig. 2).

Beta-diversity analysis based on NMDS ordination (Fig. 4A) revealed that all studied springs could be divided into three groups by community composition, but not the location: (1) CNB group with near-neutral pH (5.7–8.5) and temperatures of 40–79 °C; (2) AA group of acidic springs (pH 1.5–2.9) and temperatures from 50 to 94 °C; and (3) AB group of acidic springs (pH 2.2–3.6) and moderately high temperatures (from 41 to 64 °C). The samples were dominated mainly by Bacteria, mostly preferring springs with neutral pH (CNB group) and predominated over Archaea in moderately hot acidic springs (AB group). Archaea were absolutely dominant in springs with low pH and higher temperatures (AA) that match with classical views about these organisms as “major lovers” of the most extreme habitats [36, 37]. Notably, some of the springs (4418, 4431, 4432) were located in the tidal zone of the Sea of Okhotsk and the Pacific Ocean, which was reflected in their microbial content. Bacteria of the genera Thalassotalea [38], Thermomarinilinea [39] and Desulfomonile [40], as well as representatives of uncultured lineage C1-B045 of the Porticoccaceae family [41] were detected in those springs.

Geothermal springs of CNB group were characterized by the highest bacterial diversity (Additional file 5 Supplementary Table 3) due to moderately high temperatures and circumneutral pH, together with elevated TDS [36]. Most of CNB springs were distinguished by a huge number of thick sediments and microbial mats of brown-green-reddish colors (Fig. 4B). They were composed by thermophilic cyanobacteria of the Leptolyngbyaceae and Oculatellaceae families (the most numerous were the siderophilic uncultivated cyanobacteria JSC-12 [42], dominant in the spring #4438 with the highest iron content) and the thermotolerant Geitlerinema (Thermoleptolyngbya) PCC-8501 [43]. Also, the thermophilic cyanobacteria of the familiar genus Thermosynechococcus were detected in the mats [44]. In addition to Cyanobacteriota, the microbial mats of the Kuril springs of CNB group were dominated by phototrophic green non-sulfur bacteria affiliated with Roseiflexus, Chloroflexus and ‘Ca. Chloroploca’, giving the mats brown, red and pinkish shades [45–47]. Cyano-bacterial mats sampled in the Kuril hot springs were observed in environments with temperatures up to 60 °C. And this is similar to the nearby Japanese hot springs, where oxygenic phototrophs were found in mats at temperatures up to 63 °C [48, 49]. Contrary, in Kamchatka geothermal zones cyanobacteria were detected only in mesophilic conditions, and they concede to anoxygenic phototrophs or chemoautotrophs, being only a minor component of microbial communities in hot springs [13]. These differences may be related to the chemical composition of water and mainly by higher hydrogen sulfide concentrations, which could inhibit the growth of cyanobacteria [49].

In CNB springs with temperatures above 60 °C sequences affiliated with the potential chemolithoautotrophic bacteria within the Sulfurihydrogenibium and Hydrogenobacter (both the Aquificota) were the most numerous, forming up to 70% of the total community (Fig. 2). Representatives of these genera are the most abundant and widespread primary producers in most hydrothermal terrestrial sites worldwide [13, 50, 51] due to their high affinity to hydrogen and ability to utilize the huge range of inorganic electron donors and acceptors [52]. It should be noted that in CNB group a large number of mesophilic bacteria were also detected. The most abundant ones among them were representatives of the genera Serratia (up to 29%), Lysinibacillus (up to 48%) and Pannonibacter (up to 21%). They are known as common components of nature microbiomes responsible for decay of organic matter – necromass of microorganisms, plants, insects, and so on [53–55]. Growth temperatures of pure cultures of these genera are lower than in the hot springs; nevertheless, these mesophiles can probably tolerate higher temperatures in natural communities. Another characteristic feature of CNB springs sediments was the presence of various deep phylogenetic prokaryotic lineages that are difficult-to-cultivate or uncultivated until now. For example, archaea belonging to the class ‘Ca. Bathyarchaeia’ that can participate in decomposing of plants’ and animals’ debris [56] were detected up to 8.7% in community of #4403. Uncultivated lineages of presumably organotrophic bacteria such as ‘Ca. Alysiosphaera’ (up to 6%) [57], Anaerolineae SBR1031 (up to 3%) [58], together with bacteria of SVA0485 group (up to 9%) that can possibly impact the sulfur and iron cycles in aerobic and anaerobic conditions [59], and ‘Ca. Kapabacteriales’ (up to 4%) that could be sulfate reducers [60], were also detected in significant quantities. Notably, in CNB group OTUs affiliated with Asgardarchaeota (class ‘Ca. Odinarchaeia’) probably acting like organic destructors [61] were detected in a small amount (about 0.5%). Among all analyzed springs, prokaryotes commonly involved into the nitrogen cycle were dominant only in the spring #4441 (48 °C, pH 7.6) and belonged to the representatives of nitrifying ‘Ca. Nitrosotenuis’ (10.3%) and Nitrospira (8.5%) being the most numerous taxa there [62, 63]. The microbial community was fully coupled with the chemical composition of the thermal water in this spring as the highest concentrations of nitrite and nitrate, and the lowest concentrations of ammonium were detected there (Additional file 2 Supplementary Table 2A).

Group of springs, combined in AA, included part of the samples taken in areas closest to the active volcanoes (Baransky and Mendeleev). Their location determined their physicochemical parameters – rich sulfur deposits (Fig. 4D), high temperatures and acidity, and increased sulfate content (Additional file 2 Supplementary Table 2A, [2]). Microbial communities of such springs were mostly structured by thermoacidophilic archaea. NGS analysis showed that representatives of the genera Acidianus and Metallosphaera, facultative autotrophic archaea utilizing the energy of sulfur oxidation [64, 65], were the most frequently represented and numerous (up to 28%) in AA group springs. ‘Ca. Caldiarchaeum’, an uncultivated archaeon with a putative autotrophic type of metabolism [66], could also be involved in primary production in these springs, because it represented a significant part of the AA communities (up to 25%). Archaea of the well-known genera Thermoplasma (up to 80%) [67], Saccharolobus (up to 63%) [68], Sulfolobus (up to 69%) [69], and Caldisphaera (up to 35%) [70] are probably responsible of organic matter degradation. We suppose that representatives of uncultivated deep phylogenetic lineages, e.g. ‘Ca. Marsarchaeales’ (up to 42%) [71], belonging to the phylum Crenarchaeota (Thermoproteota), and the BSLdp215 group (up to 20%) [72] from the Thermoplasmatota, can participate in organic decomposition too [73].

AB group included acidic thermal streams with temperatures less than 64 °C (Fig. 4C), and based on comparisons with known physiological groups, we speculated that thermoacidophilic bacteria dominating them were potentially involved in sulfur cycling rather than archaea. Representatives of the genera Acidithiobacillus and Hydrogenobaculum, which can oxidize reduced sulfur compounds or hydrogen [74, 75], composed up to 45 and 66% of the total community, respectively, were probably responsible for primary production at these sites. In one spring (#4428) with a temperature 55 °C, there was a remarkable amount of sulfur-oxidizing autotrophic bacteria of the genus Sulfuriferula (16%), all isolated species of which are mesophilic [76]. Bacteria of the genera Desulfurella [77, 78], Mesoaciditoga [79], Acidiphilium [80], and Thiomonas [81], also detected in significant amounts (up to 5, 7, 11, and 44%, respectively), could be involved in degradation of organic matter in AB group springs, using sulfur compounds in redox reactions [73]. However, Archaea, despite the low pH in these springs, were not frequently detected and belonged only to the phylum Thermoplasmatota (Thermoplasma sp. and BSLdp215). This was probably due to the fact that the majority of springs in this group were thermal streams, and under such conditions (constant aeration and changing substrate availability, mobile lifestyle etc.) bacteria have an advantage over archaea [36].

The co-occurrence of microorganisms (Fig. 3) illustrated potential microbial relationships and responses to variations of environmental factors across all studied hot springs. For example, the Aquificota is characterized by a small number of edges with other taxa, since its representatives significantly predominated over the rest in terms of abundance. This is due to the fact that bacteria of this phylum are independent of other community components, being primary producers, as well as inhabiting extremely high temperatures environments where the level of microbial diversity is not high [52]. This group is linked only to bacterial phyla, consisting mainly of extremely thermophilic heterotrophic representatives, e.g. Thermotogota, Deinococcota, Dictyoglomota [82–84], and inhabiting the hottest springs with them. Large number of edges was founded for the “mat-living” bacteria, represented mainly by moderately thermophiles, and it suggests an active interaction in those environments, as it reflects for Chloroflexi (Chloroflexota) and Desulfobacterota representatives [85, 86]. Negative links reflected the very different physico-chemical characteristics of the hot springs that prokaryotes inhabit. For example, acidophilic archaea of the phyla Crenarchaeota (Thermoproteota) and Thermoplasmatota negatively correlated with mainly neutrophilic or alkaliphilic bacterial phyla, such as Thermotogota, Armatimonadota, Bacteroidota etc [87, 88]. Representatives of the symbiotic archaea Micrarchaeota [89] and presumably symbiotic bacteria (Patescibacteria) [90] had a large number of edges, probably with their potential hosts. Taxonomic analysis revealed a large number of uncultivated deep phylogenetic lineages of prokaryotes in the studied Kuril hot springs (Fig. 2) and the results of the co-occurrence network analysis may enhance metabolic reconstructions, made previously from metagenomes for these groups, and help in suggesting an ecological role for uncultivated taxa.

Conclusions

Thus, being a part of the global tectonic system hot springs of the Kuril Islands describe similar features with the Yellowstone National Park (USA), New Zealand, Kamchatka and Japan geothermal environments in microbial diversity [13, 29, 91–93]. At the same time, the communities of terrestrial Kuril hot springs are differed (1) by the presence of a marine component in some of them due to discharging at the seashore; (2) by the dominance of poorly studied Cyanobacteriota groups in moderately thermophilic springs [93], and (3) by a large number of uncultivated lineages of Archaea and Bacteria whose functions remain to be elucidated [29, 59, 60, 71, 72, 94]. Statistical analyses confirmed that the main drivers of the microbial diversity in the Kuril hot springs are pH and chemical characteristics of thermal waters. This study expands the current data on microbial diversity and community networks in geothermal environments, and may serve as a guide for further functional studies of the target microbial groups.
