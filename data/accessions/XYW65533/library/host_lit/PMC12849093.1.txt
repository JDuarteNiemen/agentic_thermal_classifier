Escherichia coli strains are widely utilized as cell factories for recombinant protein production. However, acetate overflow remains a significant challenge that negatively impacts both biomass yield and protein expression. Here, we evaluated a previously engineered E. coli K 12 BW25113 strain with pka and arcA deletions (RV04) for the expression of a single-chain variable fragments (scFv) derived from 4D5MOC-B, a monoclonal antibody that binds to epithelial cell adhesion molecule (EpCAM) as a biologically important marker for tumor immunotherapy. According to our results, RV04 strain demonstrated a significant growth advantage over both BW25113 and BL21 strains. In minimal M9 medium, RV04 exhibited a maximum cell density that was 44% higher than wild‑type and 11% higher than BL21. In enriched M9 medium, RV04 achieved a remarkable maximum specific growth rate (µ_max) of 0.775 ± 0.003 h⁻¹ and a maximum cell density of 2.1095 ± 0.0205, even under metabolic load. Regarding acetate accumulation, RV04 fully eliminated acetate accumulation within 24 h, whereas BW25113 accumulated acetate up to 0.521 g L− 1 under the same minimal medium conditions. Similarly, in enriched M9 medium, RV04 maintained significantly lower acetate levels (1.65 g L− 1 at 24 h) compared to BW25113 (3.99 g L− 1), despite increased biomass and protein production. These results confirm that RV04 can control acetate overflow more efficiently than the wild type under both minimal and enriched conditions. The combination of using the genetically modified strain and medium enrichment strategy resulted in significantly increased recombinant protein production. In LB medium, RV04 produced 5% more protein than BL21 and 44.8% more than the wild‑type, while in enriched synthetic M9 medium, it outperformed BL21 and BW25113 by 7.1% and 59.5%, respectively. Furthermore, RV04 demonstrated markedly enhanced protein expression compared to other commercial strains; it produced approximately 33.8% more protein than SHuffle, 145.7% more than Rosetta, and over sevenfold more than Origami B. Our findings demonstrate that RV04 effectively mitigates acetate overflow, enhances growth, and substantially increases the recombinant protein titer under both minimal and enriched culture conditions. These features make RV04 a strong candidate for large-scale industrial bioprocessing operations.

Introduction

Escherichia coli remains one of the most valuable and widely used bacterial strains in recombinant bioprocesses. High product yield, low production costs, rapid growth rate, and well-characterized genetics make it the preferred host for the recombinant production of many proteins. However, acetate overflow is a major challenge for obtaining high yields of recombinant proteins in this host, which occurs when the cells grow very rapidly. Rapid growth of cells under excess glucose results in high carbon flux via glycolysis. A slower rate of acetyl-CoA consumption in the TCA cycle than in the glycolytic cycle leads to accumulation of acetyl-CoA and lack of coenzyme A. Cells try to increase their pool of CoA in two ways: by converting acetyl-CoA to acetate via phosphotransacetylase-acetate kinase (PTA-ACKA) pathway, which results in the production of CoA, and by converting pyruvate to acetate via pyruvate oxidase (PoxB), resulting in a decrease in CoA conversion to acetyl-CoA. Although overflow metabolism is observed in all strains of E. coli, different strains produce very different amounts of acetate. One of the strains known to be a poor producer of acetate is E. coli BL21. Several approaches have been adopted to overcome this metabolic barrier, including reducing glucose uptake and redirecting carbon flux through the metabolic pathways of acetate production and consumption [1–4]. For example, disruption of the PTA-ACKA pathway reduces acetate excretion but leads to reduced biomass yields, decreased specific growth rate (µ) and increased excretion of formate and lactate [5, 6]. Abdel-Hamid et al. demonstrated loss of aerobic growth efficiency in cells with deletion in PoxB. These results show that deletion of the major pathways of acetate synthesis may result in some adverse side effects, despite diminishing acetate overflow [7].

Aerobic respiration control (arcA), the global transcriptional regulator, suppresses the TCA cycle and aerobic respiration. Acetyl-CoA synthetase (Acs) is an acetate-scavenging enzyme that reduces acetate accumulation by converting it to acetyl-CoA. Since acs is inactivated by phosphoenolpyruvate carboxykinase (pka), a higher concentration of active acs is increased via pka deletion, resulting in reduced acetate overflow. Peebo and colleagues previously showed that a coordinated activation of the TCA and PTA-ACS cycles via combined deletion of the pka (acs-inactivating gene) and arcA (the global regulator) not only remarkably reduced acetate overflow but also increased biomass yield in E. coli K12 BW25113 [8].

In this paper, we examine the effects of the combined deletion of pka and arcA on growth, glucose consumption, and acetate accumulation in a previously engineered E. coli K 12 BW25113 strain. Furthermore, for the evaluation of the functional results of this previously engineered strain on recombinant protein expression, anti-4D5MOC-B single-chain fragment variable (scFv) fragment is expressed and purified as a model target. Anti-4D5MOC-B scFv is a stable, highly selective antagonist of the extracellular domain of EpCAM that is a type I membrane protein detected in most human epithelial carcinomas and is an attractive target for immunotherapy [9–12]. Compared to whole monoclonal antibodies, recombinant antibody fragments, such as fragment antigen-binding (Fab) and scFv, are more effective and less adversely affected therapeutic agents. Moreover, they are simpler and cheaper to produce [13, 14]. Here, the recombinant BW25113 ΔpkaΔarcA (RV04) strain was also compared with commercially available hosts including BL21, SHuffle, Origami B, and Rosetta in terms of the amount of recombinant protein expressed.

Material & method

Reagents, growth media, and bacterial strains

The reagents for the preparation of the culture medium, including M9 medium (containing MgSO4 2 mM, KH2PO4 3 g L− 1, NaCl 0.5 g L− 1, Na2HPO4 6 g L− 1, CaCl2 0.1 mM, 0.1 mL 1000x trace element, 4 g L− 1 glucose, NH4Cl 1 g L− 1, FeCl3 0.01 mM) and Luria Bertani (LB) (tryptone (10 g L− 1), yeast extract (5 g L− 1), and NaCl (10 g L− 1)) were purchased from Teknova (USA) and Merck (Germany), respectively. Amino acids (glutamine, asparagine, and arginine (10 mg L− 1)) were procured from Sigma-Aldrich (Germany). The kits used in this work included the plasmid extraction kit (Vivantis), BCA Takara kit, gene extraction kit from the Intron PCR product gel, and acetate and glucose measurement kit (Megazyme). All the chemical compounds used have been obtained from commercial sources. The E. coli RV04 (K-12 BW25113 Δpka ΔarcA) strain kindly provided by Dr peebo (the Collection of Environmental and Laboratory Strains, Tartu University, Estonia) [8], Escherichia coli BL21 (DE3), Origami B (DE3), and Rosetta (DE3) (purchased from Pasteur Institute of Iran, Tehran, Iran), SHuffle (provided by Dr Nematollahi, Pasteur Institute of IRAN, Tehran, Iran), and BW25113 (DE3) (acquired from CGSC E.coli Genetic Stock Center United States of America) have been used as bacterial expression hosts. To enable T7 promoter–driven expression, the engineered strain (RV04) was lysogenized with λDE3 using the DE3 Lysogenization kit (Merck) according to the manufacturer’s instruction. Growth, glucose consumption, and acetate accumulation profiles

A UV-visible spectrophotometer was utilized to obtain optical density (OD) at 600 nm and to evaluate the growth profile of the cells (SHIMADZU, UV-2550). Maximum cell density was assessed by plotting OD600 over time. 1 mL of sample from the culture media was harvested to evaluate the concentration of glucose and acetate at one-hour intervals. After centrifugation of the samples (10,000 g over 10 min), the supernatant was collected. The concentrations of glucose and acetate in the supernatants were evaluated using megazyme glucose (K-GLUC 08/18) and acetate (K-ACETRM 06/18) kits, in accordance with the manufacturer’s instructions (Megazyme, Wicklow, Ireland). µmax was determined from the slope of the linear region of the semi-logarithmic growth curve over the exponential phase, where the curve is approximated by a straight line by at least three consecutive time points.\documentclass[12pt]{minimal} \usepackage{amsmath} \usepackage{wasysym} \usepackage{amsfonts} \usepackage{amssymb} \usepackage{amsbsy} \usepackage{mathrsfs} \usepackage{upgreek} \setlength{\oddsidemargin}{-69pt} \begin{document}$$\:\mu\:=\frac{ln\left({OD}_{2}\right)-ln\left({OD}_{1}\right)}{{t}_{2}-{t}_{1}}$$\end{document}

Expression and quantification of the ScFv against the extracellular domain of EpCAM (EpEX)

For protein expression, All shake-flask cultures were performed in 500 mL baffled flasks with 50 mL working volume (10% of the total volume), incubated at 37 degrees Celsius and shaken at 200 rpm, with an initial pH of ~ 7.2, for all media. A baffle flask with a working volume of less than 10% of its total capacity is generally considered adequate to maintain dissolved oxygen (DO) above the oxygen-limiting threshold of approximately 20% saturation in Escherichia coli cultures under comparable conditions [15].

For this purpose, an overnight cultures of one colony of expression host carrying the plasmid pET-22b-antiEpEX scFv [16, 17] were cultured in ampicillin-containing LB broth at 37 °C for 18 h. Then, 10% (v/v) of the original overnight culture was added to 50 mL of sterile LB broth. Isopropyl-b-D-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG) (0.8 mM) was added to the medium after reaching an OD600 of 0.8–1.8 at 37 °C. After 24 h, suspension of cells was collected and centrifuged at 10,000 g for 10 min and stored at −20 °C until further analysis. Total protein extraction was performed by ultrasonication (400 W for a total of 18 min with a 3-minute break between each 20-second period) in 10–15 mL lysis buffer (20 mM Tris HCl, 150 mM NaCl, and 50% (v/v) glycerol 100%). To assess the solubility of the protein, the resulting mixture was centrifuged (10,000 g for 25 min at 4 °C) to separate the soluble fraction from the insoluble fraction. Using SDS-PAGE, the soluble, insoluble, and total crude lysate fractions were run on the SDS-PAGE and the density of the bands was then checked by using TotalLab TL120 software (nonlinear Inc., Durham NC, USA). For the determination of the concentration of separated fractions of the crude extract, a standard curve was generated based on band intensities of different amounts of BSA (between 0 and 250 mg L− 1). The concentration of the purified protein was assessed by bicinchoninic acid (BCA) assay (Takara, Japan) (using bovine serum albumin as standard) [18].

Sodium dodecyl Sulfate-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS–PAGE) and western blotting

The MW-based protein electrophoretic separation was performed by SDS-PAGE (80 V for 5% (w/v) stacking gel and 150 V for 12% (w/v) running gel). The gel was stained with Coomassie brilliant blue G-250 after electrophoresis. Protein expression was confirmed by western blotting after electrophoretically transferring the separated bands on SDS-PAGE to a polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) membrane. Polyclonal anti-His tag antibody (dilution 1:10,000, Sigma, UK) and HRP-conjugated Goat Anti-mouse IgG (Thermo Fisher) (dilution 1:5000) were used as primary and secondary antibodies, respectively. The band visualization was performed with ECL reagent (RPN2232, Amersham, UK) and medical X-ray film. The actual protein ladder used in our SDS-PAGE and Western blot experiments is the Thermo Fisher Scientific Unstained Protein MW Marker (Reference # 26614) which spans the range of approximately 14.4 to 116 kDa.

Protein purification

In view of the adverse effect of acetate on the expression level of recombinant protein, the purified protein concentration was used as an indicator in this study to evaluate the expression efficiency of the engineered strain compared to the wild-type and common commercial strains. According to the manufacturer’s protocol (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany), recombinant scFv was applied to Ni–NTA column under denaturing conditions for purification. Briefly, the total extracted protein was resuspended in a denatured buffer containing 50 mM NaCl, 1% Triton X-100, 50 mM Tris, and 8 M urea, adjusted to pH 8, and subjected to chromatography column (Qiagen, Netherlands). The column was first washed with a buffer of 20 mM imidazole. Using 250 mM imidazole, the protein was then eluted from the column.

Statistical analysis

The data were analyzed (one-way and two-way ANOVA, Tukey post-hoc test) utilizing GraphPad Prism 10 for Windows (GraphPad Prism, USA). The numbers reported are the means and standard deviations (SD) of the independent experiments. P < 0.05 was considered to be the threshold indicating statistical significance.

Results

Glucose consumption, acetate accumulation and growth kinetics in E. coli strains under standard and amino acid-enriched media

The glucose consumption profiles were analyzed for BW25113, RV04 (BW25113 ΔpkaΔarcA), and BL21. The strain BW25113 exhibited a more efficient rate of glucose uptake. Compared to BW25113 (0.738 ± 0.0402 g L⁻¹ h⁻¹ in M9), the engineered strain RV04 (0.5515 ± 0.0457 g L⁻¹ h⁻¹) and BL21 (0.3237 ± 0.0081 g L⁻¹ h⁻¹) consumed glucose more slowly and completely over a longer period (24 h instead of 8 h in BW25113) in M9. As seen in the Fig. 1, in all three strains, some of the glucose consumed was used to produce acetate, resulting in acetate accumulation. However, in RV04 and BL21, the concentration of acetate in the culture medium began to decrease after 6 and 10 h respectively, which indicates the ability of these strains to consume acetate. The accumulation pattern of acetate in BW25113 was incremental, whereas the engineered strain was able to reduce the acetate content to zero within 24 h, similar to commercial strain BL21. However, the reduced accumulation of acetate in the engineered RV04 is likely to be due to both the reduction in acetate production (via arcA deletion enhancing TCA flux) and the increase in consumption (via pka deletion activating Acs). This reduction suggests that coordinated activation of metabolic pathways reduces acetate overflow.

The glucose consumption profiles were also assessed for BW25113, RV04, and BL21 in M9 enriched with three amino acids, glutamine, asparagine, and arginine (The use of these aminoacids at 10 mg/L in enriched M9 medium was based on results from a previous study [19]. Recombinant protein (scFv) expression and solubility across strains and media

We evaluated the expression levels of the recombinant scFv in strains BW25113, RV04 (BW25113 ΔpkaΔarcA), and BL21. Although no significant difference in protein content was observed between these strains in M9, the engineered strain showed a significant increase in solubility (61.894 ± 1.683%) compared to BL21 (46.271 ± 0.574%) and wild-type (42.045 ± 0.445%) strain (Fig. 3a and b). Enrichment of the culture medium did not result in a change in recombinant protein expression level in BW25113, whereas in RV04, this level increased significantly (462.302 ± 11.141 mg L− 1) compared to M9 (267.286 ± 14.007 mg L− 1) and reached a value more than that of the commercial BL21 strain cultured in the enriched medium (431.540 ± 9.674 mg L− 1) (Fig. 3a). Moreover, enrichment resulted in increase in protein solubility in BW25113 (42.045 ± 0.445% in M9 and 52.423 ± 1.1324% in enriched M9) and RV04 (61.894 ± 1.683% in M9 and 65.654 ± 1.011% in enriched M9) (BW25113 ΔpkaΔarcA) and a decrease in solubility in BL21 (46.271 ± 0.574% in M9 and 32.678 ± 0.052% in enriched M9) (Fig. 3b). The amino acids-enriched medium was also compared with LB in terms of recombinant protein expression in these three strains. In the engineered strain cultured in LB, the amount of protein (419.924 ± 14.672 mg L− 1) was more than that obtained with BL21 (400.314 ± 11.554 mg L− 1) and 44.87 ± 6.32% higher than that obtained in the same medium by wild-type BW25113 (289.874 ± 21.360 mg L− 1). No significant differences were observed between LB and enriched medium in BW25113 (p = 0.1393) and BL21 (p = 0.0917), while in RV04, a significant reduction of 9.17 ± 3.29% was observed in LB compared to the enriched medium (p = 0.0345) included in the supplementary materials (supplementary data Fig. S1) (Table 2). Effect of initial glucose concentration on metabolic behavior and protein expression

In the mutant strain, we examined the effect of initial glucose concentration on glucose consumption, acetate accumulation, growth profiles, and recombinant protein expression (Fig. 4) (Table 3). The results demonstrated that glucose at low concentrations (4 g L− 1 and 5 g L− 1) was depleted after ten hours, whereas a 3-fold increase in glucose concentration (15 g L− 1) did not lead to complete consumption after 24 h. According to Fig. 4, the initial increase in acetate concentration followed by a decrease in low-glucose medium reveals the ability of the RV04 strain to consume acetate completely, although increasing the glucose concentration leads to acetate accumulation. However, this accumulation is still much lower than that seen in the presence of amino acids and is comparable to that seen in wild-type cells cultured in standard M9 medium. One of the main differences is that BW25113 accumulates similar amounts of acetate several hours after glucose depletion and is unable to use it fully. In high glucose conditions, however, glucose is not fully depleted after 24 h— the amount corresponding to the initial glucose in standard M9 is detectable. This scenario demonstrates that RV04 can cope with high glucose concentrations without accumulation of acetate, and that there is a potential for acetate to be consumed after glucose depletion, which is the opposite of the behavior observed in BW25113. Moreover, as shown in Fig. 4, increasing the glucose concentration in the medium did not affect the growth pattern or biomass production of the engineered strain. In addition, the glucose level did not significantly affect the amount or solubility of anti-EpEX scFv produced in RV04 when comparing M9 + glucose to standard M9 conditions. However, when comparing RV04 grown in M9 + glucose to BW25113 grown in standard M9, RV04 exhibited statistically significant higher protein solubility (59.05 ± 1.55% versus 42.045 ± 0.445%) and slightly higher total recombinant protein content (277.484 ± 22.662 mg L− 1 versus 259.534 ± 23.751 mg L− 1). However, the increase in total protein was not statistically meaningful.

Comparative analysis of the performance of engineered E. coli compared to commercial hosts in protein expression

Expression of the protein was confirmed by western blotting in all strains (Fig. 5a) (Supplementary data Fig. S4). The engineered E. coli strain was compared with commercially available hosts for protein expression. According to the results, 400.314 ± 11.554 mg L− 1, 313.932 ± 12.400 mg L− 1, 58.993 ± 0.442 mg L− 1, and 170.893 ± 5.981 mg L− 1 of fragmented antibody were expressed in BL21, SHuffle, Origami B, and Rosetta respectively (Fig. 5b). The original maps of the protein expression of the different strains were included in the supplementary data (Supplementary data Fig. S3). After purification, these values reached 114.2195 ± 3.470 mg L− 1, 108.866 ± 2.712 mg L− 1, 36.933 ± 3.201 mg L− 1, and 71.394 ± 0.315 mg L− 1 in BL21, SHuffle, Origami B, and Rosetta, respectively (Fig. 5c) (Supplementary data Fig. S5). According to our results, the amount of scFv produced in the engineered strain (419.925 ± 14.672 mg L− 1) was comparable to that obtained in BL21 (400.314 ± 11.554 mg L− 1), while it was significantly higher than those obtained in the other three commercial strains. Moreover, Origami B produced the lowest amount of anti-EpEX scFv (58.993 ± 0.442 mg L− 1) (Table 4).

Discussion

In the case of acetate overflow, carbon equivalents that could otherwise be used for biomass production are consumed for accumulation of acetate leading to low biomass yield [5, 20, 21]. According to the results of our study, despite the BW25113, the previously engineered strain (RV04 (BW25113 ΔpkaΔarcA)) was able to simultaneously consume acetate and glucose and to spend the consumed carbon on biomass production. However, the reduction in acetate accumulation in this engineered strain seems to be due both to the reduction of acetate production (via deletion of arcA, which increases the TCA flux) and to the increase in acetate consumption (via activation of Acs by the removal of pka). RV04 showed a higher µmax and maximum cell density than wild-type strain in both culture media (M9 and enriched M9), which may be due to the negative effects of the incremental acetate in BW25113. When acetate accumulates, ATP synthesis is decreased and cellular functions are inhibited due to pH balance disruption and the dissipation of proton gradients across the membrane. This inhibition may result in a reduction of growth rate and biomass yield [22]. Similar to our results, improvement in biomass yield and growth rate were reported by Peebo et al. in RV04 due to reduced acetate accumulation indicating improved metabolic efficiency without negative effects on growth. According to their results, the deletion of pka gene leads to a higher accumulation of active Acs and a decrease in acetate overflow [8].

In our study, enrichment of culture medium resulted in increased in µmax and maximum cell density of the strains studied. As amino acids provide nitrogen essential for synthesis of vital nucleic acids, in the presence of amino acids, E. coli can grow more efficiently, leading to improved energy metabolism and biomass production [19]. In line with our findings, Maser et al. highlights the significant impact of amino acids on the growth rate of Escherichia coli strain BW25113. According to their results, amino acids supplied 72% of the carbon utilized for biomass growth in a defined nutrient-rich medium, while glucose provided 28%, suggesting that amino acids play a key role in supporting promoting high growth rates [23]. Contrary to the expectation that increased biomass due to amino acid addition to M9 would result in increased recombinant protein production, no significant change in antibody levels expressed by BW25113 was observed in the presence of amino acids. This may be caused by accumulation of acetate. Enrichment resulted in an 8-fold (800%) increase in acetate accumulation at t = 24 in BW25113, whereas RV04 was able to control the accumulation rate in enriched medium due to its ability to consume acetate and to produce less acetate. Accumulation of acetate may adversely affect the yield of recombinant proteins by inducing stress responses, shifting carbon flux from the production pathway and altering regulatory mechanisms. High levels of acetate may shift the metabolic flux from production pathways such as the TCA cycle towards pathways that produce acetate. Moreover, acetate can trigger various stress response mechanisms, including those that modify the expression of genes that prioritize survival over product formation and growth. Post-translational modifications such as acetylation may also be influenced by acetate, resulting in altered activity of enzymes involved in critical pathways of protein production [24, 25].

Based on the results of the present study, acetate accumulation was significantly higher in the enriched medium for all strains than in M9. This maybe because of the role that some amino acids play in the formation of acetate. Studies have demonstrated that while some amino acids may increase the availability of nitrogen and decrease the production of acetate, others may potentially increase acetate production by creating an imbalance in the availability of nutrient [19, 25]. The addition of amino acids may change the carbon-to-nitrogen ratio in the medium. The imbalance, in particular with excess carbon and limited nitrogen, encourages the production of acetate as a fermentation by-product. In addition, amino acids may affect the metabolism of acetate by activating or inhibiting relevant enzymes. Moreover, high concentrations of certain amino acids may trigger a stress response in the E. coli, resulting in an increase in the production of acetate. For example, the effect of amino acids on carbon metabolism, protein abundance and gene expression of E. coli B and K strains was investigated by Baez et al. They reported that amino acids suppressed glucose uptake, promoted the excretion of acetate, and reduced the TCA-related proteins, while no changes in upper or lower glycolysis pathways were observed in either E. coli K or E. coli B strains cultured in a medium devoid of amino acids [25].

According to our results, the double knockout strain BW25113 (RV04) shows a higher average scFv expression compared to BL21 and the wild type strain in LB and enriched medium. Consistently, Hendrik Waegeman et al. investigate the metabolic consequences of deleting the iclR and arcA genes in MG1655. Deletion of arcA leads to reduced repression of TCA cycle genes, while deletion of iclR activates the glyoxylate pathway. In batch conditions, the ΔiclRΔarcA double knockout strain showed increased biomass yield, reduced acetate yield, and improved protein synthesis [21, 26]. Moreover, our results not only showed that the engineered strain had a higher expression level than BL21, the strain currently utilized in many industrial processes, but also showed a higher recombinant expression level than other commercial strains studied. However, further studies are needed to evaluate the activity and degradation properties of the expressed recombinant scFv and to confirm its functional quality under the relevant industrial conditions.

Conclusion

While acetate accumulation was observed in wild-type BW25113, engineered E. coli strain RV04 (BW25113 ΔpkaΔarcA) effectively reduced acetate levels to zero similar to BL21 within 24 h in M9. A higher maximum growth rate and a higher maximum cell density were observed in the engineered host compared with both BL21 and wild-type BW25113. The amount of expressed scfv and its solubility increased in RV04 compared with BL21 and BW25113 in enriched medium. Moreover, in LB medium, RV04 expressed higher protein levels than BL21 and BW25113. The engineered RV04 also showed an increased level of protein expression compared to the commercial strains studied, including SHuffle, Origami B, and Rosetta. These promising characteristics may make RV04 a better cell factory for recombinant protein production at industrial scale than commercially available strains. However, it is suggested that future studies use a controlled bioreactor system to more closely examine the industrial performance of the developed engineered strain and evaluate the feasibility of replacing it with existing industrial strains.
