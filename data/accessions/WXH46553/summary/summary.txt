Concise Summary:
This paper focuses on the molecular mechanisms governing protein synthesis efficiency, specifically detailing how the dynamics of charged transfer RNAs (tRNAs) influence the optimal codon usage bias for maximal protein production. The core findings revolve around mathematical modeling and whole-cell simulation of bacteriophage T7 infection dynamics. Key physiological insights include:

1. **Codon Usage Optimization:** Protein expression rate is maximized when codon usage matches the available pool of charged tRNAs, particularly when tRNA recharging is rate-limiting. When recharging is fast, using 100% optimal codons is optimal.
2. **tRNA Dynamics:** The model explicitly incorporates tRNA charging/recharging kinetics, moving beyond simplified assumptions of fixed charged tRNA pools. This dynamic aspect is crucial for accurately predicting expression rates under stress or high demand.
3. **Phage Infection Model:** Application to bacteriophage T7 showed that structural gene expression rapidly depletes charged rare tRNAs, leading to a fitness penalty upon codon deoptimization. The fitness decline was observed in both dynamic and static tRNA models, though the dynamic model provided a more detailed mechanistic view.

The research is purely computational and physiological, focusing on the interplay between translation kinetics, tRNA availability, and genetic code usage, with no data provided regarding the organism's optimal growth temperature or thermal adaptation.
