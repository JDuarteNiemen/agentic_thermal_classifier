Summary

Intestinal oxygen gradients influence both gut homeostasis and pathogen colonization. Although oxygen release can promote infections like those caused by Citrobacter rodentium, it was unknown whether oxygen directly controls bacterial virulence systems. In this study, we demonstrate that enterohemorrhagic Escherichia coli (EHEC) senses oxygen via the metabolic two-component system ArcAB to regulate its type 3 secretion system (T3SS). Under low oxygen, phosphorylated ArcA binds the promoter of ler, which encodes the T3SS master regulator, to repress its expression. When oxygen increases, ArcB kinase activity is inhibited, leading to ArcA dephosphorylation and T3SS derepression. This oxygen-responsive ArcAB pathway is absent in C. rodentium, reflecting evolutionary adaptation. Additionally, ArcA suppresses Shiga toxin production. Our work reveals the ArcAB two-component system as a pivotal oxygen-sensing pathway that directly orchestrates locus of enterocyte effacement (LEE) gene expression and positions it as a promising target for antivirulence or metabolic interference strategies.

Microbial physiology; Molecular microbiology

Introduction

Bacterial pathogens have evolved sophisticated strategies to thrive within the host, including the integration of metabolic adaptation and virulence regulation. These processes are tightly coordinated to enable successful colonization of the complex intestinal microenvironment and to outcompete the resident microbiota.1,2,3 In response to diverse host-derived cues, metabolic and virulence programs are often synchronously and dynamically regulated to optimize pathogen fitness.4,5

Enterohemorrhagic Escherichia coli (EHEC) is a foodborne attaching and effacing (A/E) pathogen that causes hemorrhagic colitis and hemolytic uremic syndrome (HUS).6 Like other A/E pathogens—including enteropathogenic E. coli and Citrobacter rodentium—EHEC adheres to enterocytes by inducing actin-rich pedestals (attachment) and erasing microvilli (effacement) via a type 3 secretion system (T3SS).7 All structural T3SS components and many effectors encoded by the locus of enterocyte effacement (LEE) pathogenicity island, organized into five polycistronic operons (LEE1–5).8 LEE1 encodes the master regulator Ler,9 whose expression is fine-tuned by host/microbiota signals (sugars, amino acids, long-chain fatty acids and hormones) to synchronize pedestal formation with environmental conditions.3,5,6,10 In addition to T3SS-mediated lesion formation, EHEC produces Shiga toxins (Stx) that drive systemic complications such as HUS.11

Like many enteric pathogens, EHEC is a facultative anaerobe that encounters steep oxygen gradients in the gut. Under physiological conditions, the intestinal lumen is virtually anaerobic, while oxygen diffuses from the mucosal surface, establishing a microaerobic niche near the epithelium.12,13 Previous work has shown that C. rodentium induces epithelial damage and stimulates the proliferation of undifferentiated epithelial cells, thereby increasing mucosal oxygenation and facilitating aerobic expansion via cytochrome bd oxidase.14 These observations suggest that the ability to sense and respond to oxygen availability may be critical for the strategic deployment of virulence regulations during bacterial infection. While some studies have elucidated how EHEC senses oxygen fluctuations in the intestinal microenvironment to fine-tune its virulence arsenal,15,16,17 the precise molecular mechanisms remain incompletely understood.

In this study, we demonstrate that oxygen availability profoundly influences the T3SS-mediated colonization of EHEC in vivo. We further identify the ArcAB two-component system (TCS) as a key repressor of LEE expression under anaerobic conditions. ArcA directly binds the ler promoter to repress T3SS expression, thereby linking oxygen sensing to virulence regulation. These findings reveal that EHEC exploits ArcAB to coordinate respiratory metabolism with virulence deployment, optimizing its colonization and pathogenic potential in the oxygen-variable gut environment.

Results

Oxygen availability enhances the T3SS-dependent colonization of EHEC in vivo

To test whether reducing oxygen could limit colonization, we administered 5-aminosalicylic acid (5-ASA), which restores epithelial hypoxia via the activation of PPAR-γ.19 5-ASA treatment significantly decreased fecal bacterial loads in the DSS group (Figures 1B and 1C), but had little effect under normal conditions, except near the colon, where microaerobic niches exist (Figure 1C). These findings suggest that oxygen promotes EHEC colonization in a T3SS-dependent manner, and 5-ASA suppresses colonization by restoring epithelial hypoxia. Together, the results imply that oxygen functions both as an electron acceptor and a virulence regulator during EHEC infection.

Oxygen reprograms EHEC metabolism and upregulates LEE-encoded T3SS

To dissect how oxygen modulates EHEC virulence, we performed RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) of EHEC grown under aerobic versus anaerobic conditions. A total of 257 differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were identified (P-adjust ≤0.01, |Fold Change| ≥ 2), with 170 upregulated and 89 downregulated. The most significantly upregulated genes included those in the LEE island: espA (>170-fold), espD (>134-fold), espB (>78-fold), cesT (>67-fold), and eae (>60-fold). Genes linked to aerobic respiration (cyoABCDE operon), arginine metabolism (argT, astACD), and TCA cycle genes were also upregulated. Conversely, genes required for anaerobic metabolism (e.g., nrdD, pflB, hybO, and nirC) were repressed (Figure 1D). KEGG and GO enrichment analysis revealed that upregulated genes were associated with aerobic energy metabolism and virulence, while downregulated genes were enriched in nitrogen metabolism (Figures 1E, 1F, and S1). These results indicate that oxygen triggers a metabolic shift from anaerobic respiration to aerobic respiration and simultaneously induces LEE-encoded T3SS expression.

To further examine the impact of oxygen availability, Western blotting and Coomassie staining were used to confirm enhanced translocon secretion of EHEC under aerobic conditions (Figure 1G). Also, our quantitative real-time PCR (qRT-PCR) results validated that oxygen upregulated the transcription of the master regulator ler (Figure 1H), reinforcing the link between oxygen availability and LEE activation.

The ArcAB two-component system mediates the oxygen-dependent repression of T3SS

Based on our results above, we hypothesized the existence of an oxygen response factor/mechanism that regulates the genes involved in the central metabolism and expression of EHEC virulence simultaneously. Since RNA-seq data showed many DEGs potentially regulated by ArcA (Figure 1D, green labels), we speculated that the ArcAB TCS senses oxygen and regulates virulence in EHEC. It was further revealed in our study that Ler expression was significantly higher under aerobic conditions in wild-type EHEC using a GFP reporter plasmid (pLEE1-GFP), and ArcA depletion mimicked this high-level expression even under anaerobic conditions (Figure 2A). To exclude strain specificity, we also verified the role of ArcA in another EHEC strain (EDL933). arcA deletion led to increased icd transcription (an ArcA target), upregulated T3SS protein secretion, and enhanced ler transcription (Figures S2A and S2B) in EHEC. Notably, arcA deletion also increased stx1 and stx2a transcription in EHEC, likely via SOS response activation (elevated recA, Figure S2A), identifying ArcA as a repressor of both T3SS and Stx.

In our study, a ΔarcA mutant showed significantly increased adherence to HeLa and Caco-2 cells and more pedestal formation, as visualized by FAS staining (Figures 2D–2H). Our data therefore support ArcA as a key repressor of LEE expression and epithelial interaction.

ArcA is crucial for EHEC pathogenicity and survival in vivo

Consistent with previous findings that ArcA contributes to ROS resistance,20 our study confirmed that ΔarcA mutants are more sensitive to oxidative stress in the gut (Figure S3). Subsequently, to eliminate the confounding effects of oxidative stress, a short-term colonization model of mice was established under normal feeding conditions to determine whether arcA mutation could enhance EHEC colonization in the intestines of mice. The colonization ability of the ΔarcA strain was significantly increased in the ileum, cecum, and colon of mice (6 h post infection) (Figures 2I–2K). Following the complementation of the arcA gene, the intestinal colonization ability of EHEC reverted to levels comparable with those of the WT strain (Figures 2I–2K). These results indicated that arcA deletion could enhance EHEC colonization in vivo.

ArcB-dependent phosphorylation of ArcA is essential for T3SS repression

The ArcAB TCS is a crucial regulator of oxygen response that is composed of the sensor kinase ArcB and the response regulator ArcA. To examine the role of ArcA phosphorylation in EHEC T3SS activation, we generated isogenic ArcAD54A (unphosphorylated) and ΔarcB mutants. Both showed enhanced T3SS protein secretion (Figures 2L and 2M) and increased ler expression (Figure 2N) compared to the EHEC WT strain. Complementation with wild-type ArcB or ArcBCC (C180A/C241A, a hyperactive mutant) reversed this effect and further repressed ler and icd expression (Figures 2N and 2O), indicating that ArcB-dependent ArcA phosphorylation could repress LEE expression in EHEC.

The quinone pool modulates ArcA phosphorylation and T3SS expression

ArcAB was reported as a quinone-sensing TCS.21,22 Therefore, in our study, a ΔmenA mutant strain was generated to determine whether the quinone pool could regulate the expression of virulence genes in EHEC through ArcA-P. This mutation inhibits menaquinone (MK) and demethylmenaquinone (DMK) synthesis in microaerobic or anaerobic conditions, thereby affecting the redox state of the quinone pool in E. coli, which modulates T3SS via ArcAB (Figure S4D). The transcriptional level of icd was assessed to determine the phosphorylation state of ArcA. The ΔmenA mutant, which disrupts MK synthesis, exhibited increased icd and ler expression under microaerobic conditions, and this effect was reversed in the ΔmenAΔarcA double mutant (Figures S4A–S4C). Vitamin K3 (VK3) or MK4 supplementation restored T3SS repression in ΔmenA strains, correlating with icd and ler expression levels (Figures 2P and 2R). Thus, the redox state-dependent modulation of the quinone pool could influence T3SS via ArcA phosphorylation.

ArcA binds directly to the ler promoter to repress LEE expression

Nonradioactive labeled electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA) was performed to evaluate the binding of ArcA/ArcA-P to the ler promoter (LEE1 pro DNA) to determine whether ArcA directly regulates Ler expression and therefore influences T3SS. Slow-migrating bands were observed to indicate ArcA binding to the ler promoter and rpoS promoter (positive control), whereas ArcA did not bind the amplified DNA fragment of the suhB gene (negative control) (Figures 3A and 3B). Furthermore, ArcA-P demonstrated a higher binding affinity for both the ler and rpoS promoters. In contrast, the unphosphorylated ArcA protein (ArcAD54A) did not bind to the ler promoter (Figure 3B). These findings indicated that ArcA bound specifically to the ler promoter region in vitro, with ArcA-P exhibiting stronger affinity. The LEE1 operon comprises two promoters, designated P1 (distal) and P2 (proximal), in EHEC, whereas the ArcA DNA-binding consensus sequence is found in the P1 region (Figures 3A and 3C). To validate this interaction, the activities of two promoters were measured using a GFP reporter. Given the results above, EHEC ler promoter activity was further examined in Citrobacter rodentium (C. rodentium). It was apparent that ArcA depletion enhanced Ler (EHEC) expression in C. rodentium (Figure S5C), which is consistent with the phenotypes of EHEC. However, despite conserved protein sequence (Figure S5A), ArcA failed to inhibit T3SS in C. rodentium, possibly due to sequence divergence in the C. rodentium ler promoter (Figures S5B and S5D), as ArcA no longer binds to the EHEC ler promoter DNA with replacement according to the C. rodentium ler promoter DNA sequence (Figure S5E). Our results demonstrate that ArcA could repress T3SS via directly binding to the ler promoter in EHEC but not in CR.

The ArcAB two-component system coordinates EHEC metabolism and virulence expression

The ArcAB TCS is a crucial oxygen-sensing signal transduction system that regulates transitions among various bacterial lifestyles. Building on the aforementioned research findings, we hypothesized that the ArcA/B TCS significantly influences the coordination of metabolism and virulence expression in EHEC. To investigate our hypothesis, RNA-seq on the ΔarcB strain was conducted to determine whether the arcB mutation would replicate the same metabolic patterns and virulence expression that is observed in EHEC under aerobic conditions. The arcB mutation exhibited a similar metabolic pattern under aerobic conditions and was characterized by enhanced carbon oxidation pathways and increased virulence expression (Figure S6). Furthermore, the arcB mutation was found to significantly augment bacterial chemotaxis. This finding further substantiates the pivotal role of the ArcAB TCS in coordinating the amplification of EHEC and its pathogenic capabilities during infection.

Discussion

Oxygen availability in the gastrointestinal (GI) tract is spatially and temporally heterogeneous, shaping the composition of the intestinal microbiota and influencing pathogen behavior.23 This gradient becomes increasingly complex due to factors such as feeding-induced mucosal perfusion,22 inflammatory conditions, dietary interventions, and antibiotic use, all of which can alter local oxygen tension.24,25 As a result, oxygen serves not only as a metabolic substrate but also as a dynamic environmental signal that enteric pathogens must rapidly sense and respond to for successful colonization. Our findings demonstrate that the ArcAB TCS acts as a key oxygen-responsive hub, directly repressing the LEE-encoded T3SS in EHEC (Figure S7). This mechanism positions oxygen not merely as a metabolic substrate but as a critical spatial cue that fine-tunes virulence expression during intestinal colonization. We propose a model whereby low oxygen in the intestinal lumen maintains ArcA in its phosphorylated, active state (ArcA-P), repressing the T3SS and conserving energy. As EHEC migrates toward the colonic epithelium—a niche with relatively higher oxygen tension (∼20 μM) due to diffusion from the host vasculature12,26,27—the ArcB kinase is inhibited, leading to ArcA dephosphorylation and derepression of the T3SS. This ensures that the energetically costly secretion system is deployed precisely at the site of action, enhancing attachment and colonization. This strategy of oxygen-dependent virulence regulation aligns EHEC with other enteric pathogens, though the specific mechanisms and outcomes can vary significantly.

The role of oxygen as a master regulator of virulence is a recurring theme in enteric pathogenesis. In Salmonella Typhimurium, microaerobicity enhances the expression and activity of the SPI-1 T3SS-1, which is crucial for epithelial invasion.28 Interestingly, this regulation involves the precise, oxygen-dependent incapacitation of the SPI-1 repressor HilE, ensuring maximal T3SS-1 activity at the epithelial surface. Similarly, Shigella flexneri exploits oxygen gradients to control its T3SS; it remains in a “primed” state with extended T3SS needles under lumenal anaerobiosis but activates effector secretion only upon encountering the oxygenated mucosal niche.28,29 While the end result—T3SS activation near the epithelium—is similar to our findings in EHEC, the molecular circuitry differs. EHEC employs the metabolic regulator ArcA for direct repression, whereas Salmonella and Shigella utilize distinct mechanisms (HilE incapacitation and Fnr-mediated repression of T3SS inhibitors, respectively), highlighting evolutionary convergence on oxygen sensing for niche adaptation. Furthermore, in Enteroaggregative E. coli (EAEC), oxygen and direct host cell contact independently stimulate the expression of virulence genes, including aggregative adherence fimbriae and toxins, through both AggR-dependent and independent pathways.30 This suggests that coordination between metabolic sensing (oxygen) and host interaction (contact) is a sophisticated strategy shared among diarrheagenic E. coli pathotypes.

The mechanism of oxygen regulation of EHEC virulence expression has been increasingly recognized.15,16,17,31 Previous studies have established that transcriptional regulators, such as Cra, KdpE, and FusR, are involved in carbon metabolism and oxygen-dependent regulation of the LEE pathogenicity island.15 Under aerobic conditions, Cra and KdpE activate LEE expression, while FusR acts as a repressor, with their interplay finely tuned by metabolites such as pyruvate. Furthermore, post-transcriptional mechanisms have emerged as vital for rapid adaptation. The sRNA DicF was shown to be stabilized under low oxygen and promotes LEE expression by base-pairing within the coding sequence of pchA mRNA, relieving translational repression and enhancing the activity of the Ler-transcriptional activator PchA.16 Another sRNA, EvrS, was recently reported to be downregulated under low oxygen, repressing LEE expression by destabilizing the mRNA of a novel transcriptional regulator Z2269, which indirectly activates ler.17 Our findings position the ArcAB system within this established framework. While Cra/KdpE/FusR and sRNAs such as DicF and EvrS respond to oxygen availability, ArcAB represents a more direct and canonical oxygen-sensing pathway, widely conserved in bacteria for coordinating metabolic shifts in response to redox state. In conclusion, EHEC employs a multi-layered strategy to sense oxygen and coordinate virulence. The ArcAB system, the Cra/KdpE/FusR network, and sRNAs such as DicF and EvrS likely function in a coordinated, and potentially hierarchical, manner to ensure precise spatiotemporal control of LEE expression. This redundancy and integration contribute to the remarkable adaptability and low infectious dose of EHEC.

Our study also suggests a potential link between ArcA and Stx regulation. The deletion of arcA led to increased Stx expression, likely through indirect activation of the SOS response. Given that ArcA is known to regulate uvrA,32 a key component of the nucleotide excision repair pathway and SOS signaling, it is plausible that ArcA modulates Stx levels via repression of the SOS system under normoxic conditions. An important perspective is offered by Shimizu et al., who demonstrated that higher oxygen concentrations foster the production of Stx1 in EHEC.33 This regulation is mediated by the ferric uptake regulator (Fur), which derepresses the stx1 promoter under conditions of lower intracellular Fe2+ availability, a consequence of higher oxygen tension. Therefore, this phenotype is consistent with our findings. Oxygen may not only regulate Stx1 expression through Fur, but also modulate Stx expression via the ArcAB system. These findings illustrate the complexity of EHEC virulence regulation, where oxygen simultaneously modulates distinct virulence modules through parallel regulatory circuits (ArcAB vs. Fur), possibly to coordinate different phases of the infectious cycle. Notably, phage-encoded regulators such as Cro have been shown to modulate EHEC virulence gene expression during lysogeny, suggesting a complex regulatory interplay between phage biology and bacterial metabolic sensors such as ArcAB.34 Unraveling how ArcA integrates with the phage lytic cycle and other phage-derived regulatory elements will be crucial for a comprehensive understanding of EHEC pathogenesis and may inform the development of antivirulence strategies that avoid inadvertently triggering phage-mediated toxin release.

In conclusion, our findings establish ArcAB as a key integrator of oxygen sensing and virulence regulation in EHEC. By directly coupling environmental oxygen availability to T3SS expression, the ArcAB system enables EHEC to fine-tune its pathogenic strategy according to local redox cues. These insights not only deepen our understanding of EHEC adaptation during gut colonization but also suggest that oxygen limitation may represent a promising therapeutic avenue to attenuate both bacterial growth and virulence.

Limitations of the study

Notably, our study focused exclusively on in vitro models under controlled anaerobic conditions, which may not fully recapitulate the dynamic oxygen gradients encountered across intestinal niches in vivo. Additionally, ArcAB’s crosstalk with other oxygen-responsive regulons remains incompletely characterized. Whether ArcAB is a key target of oxygen response still requires further investigation.

Experimental model and study participant details

Bacterial strains and cultivation

All bacterial strains, plasmids, and oligonucleotides used in this study are listed in the key resources table and Tables S1 and S2. Gene deletions and chromosomal complementation were generated using the CRISPR/λ Red recombination system and confirmed by polymerase chain reaction (PCR) and DNA sequencing. For plasmid-based complementation, the open reading frame of the target gene was cloned into the low-copy-number plasmid pWSK29 and introduced into the corresponding strains by electroporation.

For routine cultivation, bacteria were grown overnight in Luria–Bertani (LB) broth, diluted 1:100 into Minimum Essential Medium (MEM; Sigma), and cultured at 37 °C under aerobic (200 rpm shaking), microaerobic (static culture in 5% CO2), or anaerobic conditions in a glove box as indicated. Cultures were harvested at mid-log phase (OD600 = 0.6–0.8) unless otherwise specified. When required, antibiotics were added at the following concentrations: nalidixic acid (25 μg/mL), kanamycin (25 μg/mL), spectinomycin (100 μg/mL), and ampicillin (50 μg/mL).

Cell lines and cell culture

All cell lines used in this study were authenticated by short tandem repeat (STR) profiling (Mycoplasma contamination free) (See details in the key resources table). HeLa and Caco-2 cells were maintained in Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium (DMEM) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) at 37 °C in a humidified incubator with 5% CO2.

Animal experiments

All animal experiments were approved by the Animal Ethics Committee of Xiamen University (approval number: XMULAC20220280). Six-week-old C57BL/6J mice were obtained from the Experimental Animal Center of Xiamen University and housed in individually ventilated cages with free access to food and water. At experimental endpoints, mice were anesthetized with 1–2% isoflurane and euthanized by cervical dislocation.

DSS-induced colitis and 5-ASA treatment

Male mice were administered 2.5% dextran sodium sulfate (DSS) in drinking water for 9 consecutive days. Therapeutic treatment with 5-aminosalicylic acid (5-ASA; 2.5 g/kg/day) was initiated 3 days prior to DSS exposure and continued throughout the experiment. On day 10, mice were orally gavaged with 1 × 109 CFU of EHEC wild-type (WT) or ΔescN strains in a total volume of 100 μL. Fecal samples were collected 24 h post-infection, weighed, homogenized in sterile phosphate-buffered saline (PBS), and serially diluted for enumeration of bacterial colony-forming units (CFUs) on selective agar plates. In this study, the DSS treatment model utilized male C57BL/6J mice, with no female individuals included. The experimental design was based on prior literature and pre-experiments confirming that males of this strain exhibit stable sensitivity to DSS-induced colitis.

Short-term infection model

Female C57BL/6J mice (6 weeks old) were randomly assigned to WT, ΔarcA, or ΔarcA-complemented (ΔarcA-Compl) groups. Each mouse was orally gavaged with 1 × 109 CFU of the indicated strain. At 6 h post-infection, ileum, cecum, and colon tissues were aseptically collected, weighed, homogenized in sterile PBS, and plated on LB agar containing appropriate antibiotics for CFU determination. However, the impact of sex differences on disease phenotypes and therapeutic responses remains unknown, which might represent one of the limitations of this research.

Method details

Analysis of secreted proteins

Overnight bacterial cultures were subcultured into fresh MEM and grown under aerobic, microaerobic, or anaerobic conditions to an optical density at 600 nm (OD600) of approximately 0.8. Cultures were centrifuged at 4,500 × g for 30 min, and supernatants were collected and filtered through 0.45-μm low-protein-binding PVDF membranes (Millipore) to remove residual cells. Bovine serum albumin (BSA; 10 μg) was added to each supernatant sample as an external loading control. Proteins were precipitated by the addition of trichloroacetic acid (TCA) to a final concentration of 10%, followed by centrifugation. Protein pellets were resuspended in SDS sample buffer and resolved by SDS–PAGE.

Proteins were visualized by Coomassie Brilliant Blue staining or analyzed by immunoblotting. For Western blotting, proteins were transferred onto nitrocellulose membranes by wet transfer. Membranes were blocked with 5% (w/v) non-fat milk in Tris-buffered saline (TBS) containing 0.1% Tween 20 (TBST) for 1 h at room temperature, and then incubated with primary antibodies at 4 °C overnight. After washing with Tris-buffered saline (TBS), membranes were incubated with horseradish peroxidase (HRP)-conjugated secondary antibodies (Beyotime) for 1 h at room temperature. Following washes with Tris-buffered saline containing 0.1% Tween 20 (TBST), signals were detected using enhanced chemiluminescence (ECL; Meilunbio). Primary antibodies included polyclonal anti-EspA and monoclonal anti-EspD antibodies.

RNA sequencing and data analysis

The EHEC wild-type strains cultured to the late logarithmic phase under aerobic and anaerobic conditions, as well as the ΔarcB strains, were immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen after collection and then transported on dry ice to Shanghai Majorbio Bio-pharm Technology Co.,Ltd.(China) for transcriptome sequencing. The specific steps for extracting total bacterial RNA include: removing rRNA to enrich mRNA, randomly cutting the obtained mRNA into fragments of approximately 200 base pairs using fragmentation buffer, reverse transcription with random primers under the action of reverse transcriptase, followed by synthesis of cDNA to form double-stranded cDNA. The synthesized double-stranded DNA is subjected to blunt end treatment and 3′ end addition of an A base. The resulting product is amplified by PCR using specific primers; the PCR product is denatured to single strands; then, single-stranded DNA is circularized into a single-stranded circular DNA library using bridging primers. Finally, Perform RNA-seq double-end sequencing using Illumina sequencers.

Gene expression levels were quantitatively analyzed using RSEM software. The quantitative metric was transcripts per million reads (TPM), which represents the number of reads for a specific transcript per million reads. TPM was first normalized by gene length and then corrected based on sequencing depth. This normalization process ensures consistency in total expression levels across different samples, thereby facilitating more intuitive comparison of gene expression differences. After obtaining the Read Counts of genes, differential expression analysis between samples can be performed to identify differentially expressed genes, thereby investigating the functions of these genes.

Quantitative real-time PCR

Total RNA was extracted using TRIpure RNA Isolation Reagent (TransGen Biotech) and reverse-transcribed using the 5× All-in-One RT Master Mix (ABM) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Quantitative real-time PCR (qRT–PCR) was performed on a CFX96 Touch Real-Time PCR Detection System (Bio-Rad) using SYBR Green PCR Master Mix (ABclonal). Each reaction was performed in technical triplicates. Relative gene expression levels were normalized to 16S rRNA and calculated using the 2−ΔΔCT method. Primer sequences are provided in Table S2.

Fluorescence assays

The LEE1-GFP reporter plasmid (pDWLEE1) was introduced into the indicated EHEC strains by electroporation. Bacteria were cultured under anaerobic conditions to an optical density at 600 nm (OD600) of approximately 0.3, after which cultures were split equally. One-half was shifted to aerobic conditions, while the other half was maintained under anaerobic conditions. Cultivation was subsequently continued until the mid-log phase. Bacterial fluorescence was detected using a microplate reader (BMG FLUOstar Omega).

For ler promoter truncation analyses, GFP reporter plasmids containing different lengths of the ler promoter (pDWLEE1, pFL-1, and pFL-2) were introduced into wild-type (WT), ΔarcA, and ΔarcA-complemented (ΔarcA-Compl) strains. Cultures were harvested at an OD600 of 0.6–0.8, and fluorescence was measured using a microplate reader. Relative fluorescence units (RFUs) were calculated by normalizing fluorescence values to the corresponding OD600. All assays were performed with technical triplicates and repeated using at least three independent biological replicates.

Bacterial adhesion assay

HeLa and Caco-2 cells were maintained in DMEM supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum, respectively, at 37 °C in a 5% CO2 atmosphere and grown to 70–80% confluence. Prior to infection, cells were washed with pre-warmed PBS and incubated in fresh DMEM.

Bacterial strains were grown overnight at 37 °C, diluted into MEM, and cultured to mid-log phase (OD600 ≈ 0.6). Host cells were infected at a multiplicity of infection (MOI) of 100 (bacteria:cell) and incubated for 3 h. After infection, cells were washed three times with PBS to remove non-adherent bacteria. Adherent bacteria were recovered by lysing host cells with sterile ultrapure water at 37 °C for 5 min.Cell lysates were serially diluted and plated on LB agar, and colony-forming units (CFUs) were enumerated to quantify bacterial adhesion.

Fluorescence staining assays

Cells grown overnight on confocal imaging dishes were infected with bacteria at an MOI of 100 (bacteria:cell) for 3 h. After infection, samples were washed with phosphate-buffered saline (PBS), fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde, and permeabilized with 0.1% Triton X-100. Samples were then blocked with 3% bovine serum albumin (BSA) in PBS for 30 min at room temperature to reduce non-specific binding. Bacteria were stained with an O157-specific monoclonal primary antibody, followed by an Alexa Fluor 568-conjugated secondary antibody (red). Actin filaments were visualized using fluorescein isothiocyanate (FITC)-conjugated phalloidin (green), and nuclei were counterstained with DAPI (blue). Fluorescence images were acquired with an Olympus FV1200-IX83 laser scanning confocal microscope (100×).

Nonradioactive electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA)

ArcAWT-His6 and ArcAD54A-His6 proteins were purified by nickel affinity chromatography. DNA probes corresponding to the ler operon promoter and the rpoS promoter (positive control) were amplified by PCR using primer pairs LEE1 pro DNA-5’/LEE1 pro DNA-3′ and rpoS pro DNA-5’/rpoS pro DNA-3′, respectively, and purified using a DNA purification kit (Vazyme Biotech). A nonspecific DNA fragment containing an internal suhB sequence was amplified with primers suhB pro DNA-5’/suhB pro DNA-3′ as a negative control.

Binding reactions were performed by incubating DNA probes with the indicated concentrations of purified proteins in EMSA buffer (10 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.4; 50 mM KCl; 0.1 mM EDTA; 0.2 mM DTT) at 37 °C for 30 min. Samples were resolved on 6% (w/v) native polyacrylamide gels at 100 V for 1 h in 0.5× Tris-glycine-EDTA buffer (12.5 mM Tris, 125 mM glycine, 1 mM EDTA, pH 8.0). Gels were stained with ethidium bromide and imaged under UV light.

For phosphorylation assays, 20 μg of protein was pre-incubated with 20 mM carbamoyl phosphate in phosphorylation buffer (50 mM Tris-Cl, pH 7.5; 50 mM KCl; 20 mM MgCl2; 20 mM DTT; 10 μg/mL BSA) at 37 °C for 1 h prior to EMSA.

Quantitative and statistical analysis

Data were analyzed using GraphPad Prism 8. Two-group comparisons were performed using unpaired t-tests, while multiple-group comparisons were analyzed by one-way or two-way ANOVA, as appropriate. For nonparametric data, the Mann–Whitney U test was applied. Statistical significance was defined as p < 0.05. Specific statistical tests for each experiment are detailed in the figure legends.
