Abstract

Temperature stress is a fundamental challenge for all organisms. While two-component systems (TCSs) are known to transduce environmental signals in microbes, their role in thermal sensing remains underexplored. Here, we unveil a novel thermosensitive TCS, DhqSR, in the thermophile Thermus thermophilus HB27. We demonstrate that the histidine kinase DhqS perceives thermal cues and autophosphorylates at His327. Its cognate response regulator, DhqR, is activated through a unique tyrosine-phosphorylation mechanism: phosphorylation at a unique Tyr84 residue, rather than the canonical aspartate. This atypical DhqSR system orchestrates cellular thermoadaptation by directly regulating a key enzyme in the shikimate pathway, type II 3-dehydroquinate dehydratase (DHQase). Our findings reveal a novel molecular mechanism of temperature sensing and adaptation, providing a new paradigm for microbial environmental adaptation and offering a unique toolbox for engineering thermotolerance.

Introduction

Temperature stress—ranging from freezing cold to scorching heat—poses a fundamental and perpetual challenge to all forms of life [1–3]. For microorganisms, these extremes disrupt vital metabolic processes, impair growth, and threaten survival, making the maintenance of intracellular homeostasis a critical priority [4, 5]. To sense and adapt to such environmental fluctuations, prokaryotes have evolved a sophisticated signaling apparatus: the two-component system (TCS) [6]. Typically composed of a membrane-anchored histidine kinase (HK) and its cognate response regulator (RR), this system allows cells to translate external cues into coordinated genetic responses.

The activation of a TCS begins with signal perception. When an environmental stimulus, such as temperature shift, is detected by the sensor domain of the HK, it triggers a cascade of molecular events [7]. Adenosine triphosphate (ATP) binds and is hydrolyzed by the HATPase_c subdomain, liberating a phosphate group that is subsequently transferred to a conserved histidine residue within the HisKA domain [8]. This autophosphorylation event turns the HK into an activated hub, ready to relay the signal [9, 10]. The phosphate is then transferred to a specific aspartate residue in the receiver (REC) domain of the RR, inducing a conformational change that enables the RR to bind DNA and modulate the expression of target genes [11–14]. This elegant phosphorelay lies at the heart of bacterial environmental sensing and adaptation.

In the context of escalating global climate change, understanding temperature-responsive TCSs is particularly pressing. Psychrotolerant organisms such as Methanococcoides burtonii (LtrK/LtrR) [15], as well as mesophiles like Vibrio alginolyticus (VarS/VarR) [16], Bacillus cereus (CasK/CasR) [17], and Pseudomonas aeruginosa PG1480 (CorS/CorR) [18], utilize TCSs where HKs act as thermometers, fine-tuning bacterial proliferation and protease synthesis [16] within narrow thermal windows. Yet, it is in thermophiles—life forms thriving at high temperatures—where TCSs reveal their most remarkable adaptations. For instance, the pH-gated HK853/RR468 system in Thermotoga maritima maintains robust ATP-binding and phosphotransfer activity even at 80°C, functioning as a dual sensor for osmoregulation and thermal stress [12]. Similarly, Streptococcus thermophilus employs an intricate TCS network to coordinate thermoadaptation with oxidative stress responses [19].

Among these extremophiles, Thermus thermophilus strain HB27 has emerged as a model organism [20, 21]. Intriguingly, it possesses fewer predicted TCSs than many mesophilic bacteria, yet those it retains appear indispensable for survival under extreme heat. This paradox suggests that the TCSs in T. thermophilus are not redundant relics but highly specialized, efficient regulatory modules. A central and unresolved question thus remains: what are the specific identities and unique operational mechanisms of these essential thermostable TCSs, and which critical cellular processes do they commandeer to ensure survival under thermal stress?

Guided by these unresolved questions, our study set out to identify and mechanistically characterize a novel TCS in T. thermophilus HB27 dedicated to thermal sensing. We report the discovery of the DhqSR system, demonstrating that DhqS functions as a highly thermostable HK. We elucidate a unique phosphotransfer mechanism to its cognate regulator, DhqR, and establish that this system is essential for thermal adaptation by directly regulating a key component of central metabolism. Our findings decode a fundamental survival strategy in a thermophile and provide a new paradigm for engineering environmental resilience.

Materials and methods

Bacterial strains and culture conditions

Thermus thermophilus HB27 and the engineered strain (Supplementary Table 1) were cultured in modified Terrific broth (TB) medium (8 g/l tryptophan, 4 g/l yeast extract, 3 g/l NaCl, 367 mg/l NaHCO3, 150.44 mg/l MgCl2·6H2O, 36.75 mg/l CaCl2·2H2O, 31.34 mg/l MgSO4, and 0.95 mg/l KCl, pH 7.5). Antibiotics were used at the following concentrations: kanamycin (20 µg/ml) and ampicillin (100 µg/ml). The normal culture temperature was 65°C, with cold stress at 50°C, and heat stress at 80°C.

RNA-seq analysis

Single colony of HB27 was inoculated in 100 ml TB medium at 65°C, 50°C, and 80°C until logarithmic phase (OD600 = 1.0). One milliliter bacterial cultures were collected by centrifugation (9000 rpm, 4°C). Cells were washed twice with 800 µl 1× PBS buffer (Thermo Fisher Scientific) and immediately flash-frozen in liquid nitrogen. The transcriptome was sequenced using the Illumina Hiseq 2000 by Sangon Biotech (Shanghai, China). The RNA-seq read length was 2 × 150 bp, and complementary DNA (cDNA) libraries were constructed for three groups. Differentially expressed genes (DEGs) with a threshold of |log2FoldChange| > 1 and P < .05 were screened, and the ggplot2 software [22] was used to map volcano plots of the DEGs. TopGO [23] and clusterProfiler [24] were used to perform Gene Ontology and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) analyses of differential genes. The degree of enrichment was measured by rich factor, false discovery rate, and number of genes enriched in the KEGG pathway analysis.

Cloning construction

All the T. thermophilus HB27 mutant strains were constructed using the methods previously described [25]. To clone T. thermophilus TtS (DhqS) and TtR (DhqR) into vectors, the target genes or gene fragments were amplified from genomic DNA (gDNA). Polymerase chain reaction (PCR) products were digested with DpnI (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA) and then assembled with recombinase (ABclonal Technology, Wuhan, China). Point mutations based on these constructs were generated by site-directed mutant primers (Supplementary Table 2). TtS, TtSH327A, TtS∆HisKA, TtRY84A, and TtR∆REC were cloned into the vector pET28a; TtR was cloned into both pET28a and pGEX-6p-1, and expressed in Escherichia coli Rosetta (DE3). The TtS protein and its engineered variants utilized in this study were truncated constructs with transmembrane domains removed. The knockout plasmid with selective removal of the clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats (CRISPR) array was used as the complementary plasmid. It was transformed into the corresponding mutant strains to serve as complementary strains and into wild-type strains to serve as overexpression strains. All editing plasmids are listed in Supplementary Table 1, and primers are listed in Supplementary Table 2.

Protein expression and purification

All clones were induced with 0.5 mM isopropyl-β-D-thiogalactopyranoside at 16°C for 20 h. Cells were resuspended in lysis buffer [50 mM Tris–HCl (pH 7.5), 200 mM NaCl, 5 mM MgCl2, 1 mM Tris (2-chloroethyl) phosphate (TCEP), and 1 mM phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride (PMSF)] and lysed by high pressure homogenization. Cell lysate was collected by centrifugation (13 000 rpm, 4°C). Target proteins were captured from the lysate supernatant using Ni-charged resin FF columns (GenScript, Nanjing, China) and GST-sepharose resin 4FF columns (Sangon Biotech, Shanghai, China), and then eluted with elution buffer 1 (50 mM Tris–HCl, pH 7.5, 200 mM NaCl, and 50–500 mM imidazole) and elution buffer 2 (50 mM Tris–HCl, pH 7.5, 200 mM NaCl, and 1–10 mM reduced glutathione), respectively. Finally, the proteins were stored in storage buffer (50 mM Tris–HCl, pH 7.5, 200 mM NaCl, 1 mM TCEP, and 10% glycerol).

In vitro phosphorylation assay

The autophosphorylation activity and phosphorylation sites of TtS were analyzed by Pro-Q Diamond phosphoprotein gel staining (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA). In brief, each reaction contained 2.5 mM ATP and 1 mM dithiothreitol (DTT). The reaction was initiated by adding 100 μg protein with 1 mM DTT, and conducted at 65°C for 30 min to determine the optimal ATP concentration (0.25, 0.50, 1.00, 1.50, 2.00, and 2.50 mM). Next, each reaction system was configured with 2.5 mM ATP and 1 mM DTT to determine the optimal incubation time (3, 5, 10, 20, 30, 45, and 60 min) and reaction temperature (40, 50, 60, 65, 70, 80, and 90°C). Reactions were subsequently stopped by addition of sodium dodecyl sulphate (SDS) sample buffer, followed by 15% SDS-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (PAGE) analysis and phosphoprotein gel staining. In addition, histidine phosphorylation antibodies were also used to verify the activity of TtS and its mutants. Samples were separated for SDS–PAGE gel and detected by immunoblot analysis with anti-His antibody (Abclonal, Wuhan, China; 1:4000 dilution) and anti-pHis antibody (Abcam Plc, Cambridge, UK; 1:4000 dilution), respectively.

To show phospho-transfer between TtS and TtR in vitro, a three-fold molar excess of autophosphorylated TtS was mixed with TtR and incubated for 0 s, 5 s, 15 s, 30 s, 1 min, 2 min, and 3 min at 65°C. In addition, the same protocol as phosphorylated TtS (pTtS) staining was performed to detect the phosphorylation site of TtR with an action time for 15 s. In addition, an linear trap quadrupole (LTQ) ion trap mass spectrometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA) coupled with an Easy-nLC 1000 solvent delivery system (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA) was used to detect the phosphorylated amino acids in TtR. Peptides were pressure-loaded onto the column, then eluted, and analyzed by microcapillary liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LC-MS)/MS. The collision energy for the liquid chromatography quadrupole (LCQ) was set at 35%. Proteome Discoverer was used for searching peptide sequence and identification of site(s) of phosphorylation. Search parameters included differential mass modification to aspartic acid and tyrosine due to phosphorylation. Differential mass modification means both possibilities of modified or unmodified amino acid residues were used in database search.

Pull-down

A fusion protein as 5 mg His-tagged (His-TtS or His-TtSH327A) was mixed with another fusion protein for 5 mg GST-tagged (GST-TtR). Ni-charged resin beads (4 ml) were added and incubated at 4°C for 2 h with rocking. Beads were washed 10 times with wash buffer (50 mM Tris–HCl, pH 7.5, 300 mM NaCl, 10% glycerol, 1 mM PMSF, and 1 mM TECP). The proteins were further eluted with buffer (50 mM Tris–HCl, pH 7.5, 200 mM NaCl, and 500 mM imidazole). Isolates were separated for SDS–PAGE gel and detected by immunoblot analysis with anti-His antibody (Abclonal, Wuhan, China; 1:1000 dilution) and anti-GST antibody (Abclonal, Wuhan, China; 1:1000 dilution), respectively.

Fluorescence colocalization

To observe the positional relationship of TtS and TtR in cells, the TtS and TtR genes were cloned into the pACYCDuet-sGFP-mCherry plasmid and transformed into E. coli BL21 cells. Construct strains expressing sGFP-tagged TtS and mCherry-tagged TtR. Cells were induced with 0.1 mM isopropyl-β-D-thiogalactopyranoside at 16°C for 20 h. Colocalization of sGFP-tagged TtS and mCherry-tagged TtR in E. coli BL21 was assessed by fluorescence microscope (Olympus BX63, Japan) equipped with a 100 W mercury lamp and appropriate filters. Images were captured and processed with OlyVIA software.

DNA immunoprecipitation sequencing analysis

The DNA fragments that bind to the TtR protein were detected using the in vitro binding method. The HB27 genome was extracted using the DNA extraction kit (Sangon Biotech, Shanghai, China). DNA fragments (100–500 bp) were obtained by using MNase (Beyotime, Shanghai, China) to lyse the genome at 37°C for 10 min. The genomic fragments and TtR protein were incubated in binding buffer (50 mM Tris–HCl, pH 7.5, 100 mM KCl, 2.5 mM MgCl2, and 1 mM DTT) at 65°C for 30 min. After that, samples were added to the Anti-Flag affinity gel (Yeasen, Shanghai, China), incubated at 4°C for 6 h. Twenty microliters of pre-treated protein A/G agarose beads were added, followed by continued incubation at 4°C for 2 h, washed of impurities with 5 ml of wash buffer (0.15 M NaCl, 20 mM Na2HPO4, pH 7.5), and eluted with 100 μl of elution buffer (1% SDS, 50 mM Tris–HCl, pH 7.5). The eluate was collected as the binding samples. Libraries were sequenced on an Illumina HiSeq 2000, enriched peaks identified with model-based analysis of ChIP-Seq (MACS) [26], and visualized in Integrative Genome Viewer [27].

Electrophoretic mobility shift assay

The DNA-binding affinity of TtR to the promoters was analyzed by electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA). The promoter regions, extending from −200 to 0, were amplified from T. thermophilus HB27 gDNA. Binding reactions (20 μl) were prepared for 0, 0.5, 1, and 1.5 μmol protein and 100 μg DNA was incubated in the binding buffer (50 mM Tris–HCl, pH 7.5, 100 mM KCl, 2.5 mM MgCl2, 1 mM DTT, and 10% glycerol) at 65°C for 30 min. After, the reaction mixtures were mixed with 10× EMSA/gel-shift loading dye (Beyotime, Shanghai, China) and separated on 12% native polyacrylamide gels. Finally, the gels were stained with SYBR Green I Nucleic Acid Gel Stain (Invitrogen, Waltham, USA) and visualized by fluorescence imaging using a Typhoon FLA 7000 laser scanner (GE Healthcare).

RT-qPCR

To determine whether the TtSR system regulates the HB27c_C1022–1023 gene cluster and investigate the functional relationship between TtS and TtR in HB27, we measured the expression levels of TtS in ΔTtR and WT/p-TtR; TtR in ΔTtS and WT/p-TtS; HB27c_C1022 in ΔTtR and ΔTtS; and HB27c_C1023 in ΔTtR, ΔTtS, ΔHB27c_C1022, WT/p-TtR, WT/p-TtS, and WT/p-HB27c_C1022. Total RNA was extracted from the wild-type, ΔTtR, ΔTtS, and Δ1022 strains using the U-Fast Bacterial RNA Extraction Kit (Zoman Biotech, Beijing, China). Subsequently, cDNA synthesis with concurrent gDNA removal was performed using the ABScript Ⅲ RT Master Mix (Abclonal, Wuhan, China). Reverse transcription quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR) analysis was then conducted with the PerfectStart® Green qPCR SuperMix (Zoman Biotech, Beijing, China), strictly following the manufacturer’s recommended protocols. Each reaction was performed in 10 µl reaction volumes with 1 μg cDNA and Eno [28] as the internal control. The fold change represents the relative expression level of mRNA, calculated using the threshold cycle (Ct) method with the formula 2 (−∆∆Ct).

Sequence analysis

To analyze sequence conservation in TtSR regulatory pathway, we conducted a BLASTP search for the TtS, TtR, HB27c_C1022, and HB27c_C1023 against the nonredundant protein sequence database. Phylogenetic reconstruction was performed with the neighbor-joining algorithm in MEGA-X software to delineate evolutionary relationships among species possessing the four target genes, with branch support evaluated by bootstrap analysis (1000 replicates). Visualization was refined using the iTOL website.

Statistics and reproducibility

All biochemical experiments were performed in biological triplicate. All replicates were successful, and no data were excluded from the analyses. In figures, bars represent the mean and error bars the standard deviation from triplicate measurements. No statistical method was used to predetermine sample size. The experiments were not randomized, and the investigators were not blinded to allocation during experiments and outcome assessment.

Results

TtS is essential for cold adaptation in T. thermophilus

To systematically identify thermosensory TCSs in T. thermophilus HB27, we performed transcriptomic analysis under thermal stress. Comparison of the transcriptome at the optimal growth temperature (65°C) with those under hypothermal (50°C) and hyperthermal (80°C) conditions revealed nine HKs with significantly altered expression (Fig. 1A and Supplementary Table S3). We constructed knockout strains for all nine candidates using the endogenous CRISPR system [25] and assessed their growth. Strikingly, only the ΔTtS9 strain exhibited a pronounced cold-sensitive growth defect at 50°C (Fig. 1B), identifying it as a critical mediator of cold adaptation. Consequently, we renamed this gene TtS for further characterization.

Bioinformatic analysis using the TMHMM Server [29] predicted that TtS is a transmembrane protein with two transmembrane α-helices (Supplementary Fig. S1A), an architecture typical for sensor kinases. Further examination of its conserved HisKA domain revealed a surface with distinct electrostatic potentials (Supplementary Fig. S1B), which may facilitate specific interactions with its cognate RR.

To delineate the domains essential for its thermosensory function, we engineered a series of TtS truncation variants: NS (sensor domain deletion), NTM (transmembrane domain deletion), NGAF (GAF domain deletion), and NSTG (deletion of all three aforementioned domains) (Fig. 1C). Complementation of the ΔTtS strain with these variants showed that none could fully restore the wild-type growth phenotype at 50°C. While the NS, NTM, and NGAF variants partially rescued the growth defect, the NSTG variant completely failed to do so, phenocopying the ΔTtS mutant (Fig. 1D). These results demonstrate that the sensor, transmembrane, and GAF domains collectively constitute an integrated thermosensing module, with the sensor-transmembrane unit likely acting as the extracellular sensor and the GAF domain serving as an intracellular signal processor (Fig. 1E).

TtS is a thermostable histidine kinase autophosphorylating at His327

To establish TtS as a bona fide HK, we combined structural prediction with biochemical assays. Molecular docking analysis identified key residues (Asn437, Lys440, His441, Asp465, Leu483, Thr507) within the HATPase_c subdomain that are critical for ATP binding (Supplementary Fig. S2A), confirming a conserved kinase architecture. However, to confirm the ATP-binding sites of TtS, we constructed a mutant in which all six residues were simultaneously substituted with alanine. This mutant lost ATP–induced activation (Fig. 2A), confirming that these six residues constitute the ATP–binding sites of TtS. We next evaluated the enzymatic activity of purified TtS in vitro. Phosphorylation assays demonstrated ATP-dependent autophosphorylation, with a threshold ATP concentration of 0.25 mM and a maximum activity (2.3-fold increase over baseline) achieved at 2.5 mM ATP (Fig. 2B). A time-course experiment revealed rapid activation, with TtS activity doubling within 3 min and reaching peak levels by 30 min (Fig. 2C and Supplementary Fig. S2B). Remarkably, TtS exhibited considerable thermostability, maintaining substantial phosphorylation activity even at 90°C and showing optimal activity (2.5-fold increase) at 70°C (Fig. 2D), a temperature profile that distinguishes it from many typical bacterial kinases.

We next sought to identify the specific histidine residue responsible for autophosphorylation. Sequence alignment pinpointed His327 within the HisKA domain as the putative phospho-accepting site (Fig. 2E). To test its functional necessity, we generated a point mutant (TtSH327A) and a mutant, TtS∆HisKA, with a deletion of the HisKA domain (residues 318–385). Both mutants completely lost ATP-induced phosphorylation activity in vitro (Fig. 2F). Accordingly, the TtSH327A mutant failed to rescue the cold-sensitive growth defect of the ΔTtS strain (Fig. 2G). These results unequivocally identify His327 as the catalytic residue essential for TtS autophosphorylation and confirm TtS as a thermophilic HK.

TtR is the cognate response regulator of TtS, forming a temperature-responsive two-component system

Having identified TtS as a thermosensory HK, we sought to identify its cognate RR. Search Tool for the Retrieval of Interacting Genes/Proteins (STRING) analysis predicted two potential interaction partners, TtR1 and TtR2 (Supplementary Fig. S3A). Phenotypic screening of the corresponding knockout strains revealed that only ΔTtR1 exhibited a clear growth defect at 50°C (Fig. 3A), mirroring the cold-sensitive phenotype of ΔTtS. This genetic evidence suggested that TtR1 (hereafter TtR) and TtS constitute a functional TCS pair.

We next validated this interaction biochemically and cellularly. A pull-down assay confirmed a direct physical interaction between the soluble portion of TtS (lacking its transmembrane domain) and TtR (Fig. 3B). Notably, the kinase-inactive mutant TtSH327A retained the ability to bind TtR, indicating that complex formation is independent of the kinase’s catalytic activity. Furthermore, fluorescence microscopy in E. coli showed clear colocalization of sGFP-tagged TtS and mCherry-tagged TtR at the cell membrane (Fig. 3C and Supplementary Fig. S4), supporting their interaction in a physiological cellular context.

To establish functional phosphotransfer within the TtSR system, we performed in vitro phosphorylation assays. Pre-phosphorylated TtS rapidly transferred its phosphate group to TtR, with maximum phosphorylation observed within 15 s, followed by a decrease, indicative of a regulated phosphatase activity inherent to TtS (Fig. 3D). The functional hierarchy was confirmed in vivo: introducing TtR into the ΔTtS strain (ΔTtS/p-TtR) fully restored growth at 50°C, whereas expressing TtS in the ΔTtR background (ΔTtR/p-TtS) did not (Fig. 3E and Supplementary Fig. S3B). This demonstrates that TtR is the essential downstream effector of TtS. Collectively, our genetic, biochemical, and cellular evidence establishes TtS and TtR as a cognate, temperature-responsive TCS.

TtS phosphorylates TtR at a conserved tyrosine residue

Having established TtS–TtR phosphotransfer, we sought to define the molecular mechanism of TtR activation. In canonical TCSs, the phosphoryl group is transferred from the HK’s histidine to a conserved aspartate in the RR’s REC domain [30]. To explore this in the TtSR system, we performed molecular docking analysis. Surprisingly, the predicted interaction interface highlighted Tyr84 in the REC domain of TtR as the primary residue interacting with the phosphorylatable His327 of TtS, with no dominant His–Asp pairing observed (Fig. 4A and B and Supplementary Fig. S5). This suggested a potential deviation from the canonical phosphorelay mechanism.

To test whether Tyr84 is the genuine phosphoacceptor site, MS analysis revealed that TtS specifically transferred the phosphate group to Tyr84 of TtR, but not to Asp54 (Supplementary Fig. S6). Additionally, we purified the TtRY84A point mutant, TtRD54A point mutant, and the REC domain (residues 3–119) deletion mutant TtR∆REC. In vitro phosphorylation assays demonstrated that TtS could phosphorylate wild-type TtR. However, under identical conditions (in the presence of TtS and ATP), the TtRY84A and TtR∆REC mutants were completely incapable of being phosphorylated. In contrast, phosphorylation of the TtRD54A mutant occurred at levels comparable to the wild-type protein (Fig. 4C). The essential role of Tyr84 was confirmed in vivo, as the ΔTtR strain expressing TtRY84A (ΔTtR/p-TtRY84A) failed to rescue the cold-sensitive growth phenotype (Fig. 4D). Taken together, these results demonstrate that Tyr84, rather than a canonical aspartate, is the critical phosphorylation site for TtR activation, defining a noncanonical signaling mechanism within the TtSR system.

The TtSR system governs thermal adaptation by directly regulating the shikimate pathway enzyme DHQase

To elucidate how the TtSR system confers cold adaptation, we aimed to identify its direct transcriptional targets. DNA immunoprecipitation sequencing revealed that TtR specifically binds to three genomic regions: the promoter of TtS (pTtS), its own promoter (pTtR), and the promoter upstream of the HB27c_C1022 gene (p1022) (Fig. 5A–C). EMSAs confirmed TtR’s direct binding to these promoters in vitro (Fig. 5D). These findings establish TtR as a transcriptional regulator that employs a negative feedback loop to autoregulate its own expression and repress TtS, while potentially activating the HB27c_C1022 locus.

Notably, HB27c_C1022 and the downstream gene HB27c_C1023, which encodes a type II 3-dehydroquinate dehydratase (DHQase), are organized in a potential operon (Supplementary Fig. S7A and B). To delineate the TtR-mediated regulatory network, we systematically profiled gene expression across relevant genetic backgrounds. RT-qPCR analysis revealed a complex regulatory circuit: deletion of TtR led to a two-fold upregulation of TtS expression (Fig. 5E), confirming that TtR acts as a transcriptional repressor of its cognate kinase. Conversely, TtR transcript levels were reduced by half in the ΔTtS strain (Fig. 5F), indicating that the TtS-mediated signal is required for full TtR expression. This suggests that phosphorylated TtR positively autoregulates its own promoter, creating a positive feedback loop. Concurrently, both HB27c_C1022 and HB27c_C1023 exhibited significantly decreased mRNA levels in ΔTtR and ΔTtS mutants (Fig. 5G and H), confirming their positive regulation by the TtSR system. Crucially, the expression of HB27c_C1023 was unaffected in the ΔHB27c_C1022 strain, and it was robustly upregulated in TtR-, TtS-, or HB27c_C1022-overexpression backgrounds (Fig. 5H). This collective evidence establishes that TtR functions as the central hub of a complex circuit, repressing its kinase while potentially activating its own expression and positively driving the HB27c_C1022-1023 operon. Furthermore, RT-PCR confirmed that HB27c_C1022 and HB27c_C1023 are cotranscribed as a single operon (Supplementary Fig. S7B), solidifying their genetic linkage within the TtSR regulon (genomic context shown in Supplementary Fig. S7A). The integrity of HB27c_C1023 transcription despite the upstream gene deletion confirms that RNA polymerase processivity is maintained, positioning the HB27c_C1022-1023 cluster as the downstream effector unit for TtSR-mediated temperature adaptation.

To definitively pinpoint the effector responsible for the cold adaptation, we performed a series of genetic complementation assays. Introducing the HB27c_C1022-1023 cluster into the ΔTtSR mutant restored growth at 50°C, whereas complementation with another TtR-adjacent cluster (HB27c_C1019-1020) did not (Fig. 5I). Importantly, this same operon also rescued the cold-sensitive growth of both ΔTtR and ΔTtS single mutants (Supplementary Fig. S7C), confirming it acts downstream of the entire TCS. Subsequent analysis revealed that the single-gene deletion ΔHB27c_C1023 (ΔDHQase) recapitulated the cold-sensitive growth defect of the ΔTtSR mutant, while ΔHB27c_C1022 exhibited no obvious phenotype (Fig. 5J). The ultimate functional proof was achieved by showing that supplementing HB27c_C1023 (DHQase) alone is sufficient to restore growth in the ΔTtSR background (Supplementary Fig. S7D). These results collectively and unequivocally identify DHQase, a key enzyme in the shikimate pathway for aromatic amino acid biosynthesis, as the critical downstream effector through which the TtSR system mediates cold adaptation. Given this definitive functional linkage, we hereafter refer to this regulatory pathway as the DhqSR system.

The DhqSR system is conserved within the Thermus genus

To evaluate the conservation and ecological distribution of the DhqSR system, we performed phylogenomic analysis. We first investigated its presence across a broad spectrum of prokaryotes and found that homologs of DhqS, DhqR, and DHQase co-occur in genomes from 14 bacterial families (Fig. 6A), many of which include species adapted to extreme environments such as high temperature or radiation. Notably, the system forms a distinct phylogenetic clade encompassing the thermophiles Thermus, Marinithermus, Meiothermus, and Vulcanithermus (all belonging to Thermaceae) as well as the radiation-resistant Deinococcus (Deinococcaceae). This phylogenetic association suggests the DhqSR system may represent a versatile adaptive module for abiotic stress response, potentially contributing to thermotolerance in the former and radiotolerance in the latter.

We next focused on the Thermus genus to determine the regulatory conservation of this pathway. Genomic analysis confirmed that the specific genetic architecture—the DhqSR TCS coupled with the downstream rRNA methyltransferase (HB27c_C1022)-DHQase operon—is a unique and conserved feature of Thermus (Fig. 6B). Furthermore, analysis of promoter regions across Thermus strains revealed highly conserved binding motifs for DhqR upstream of its target genes (Supplementary Fig. S8). This indicates that the DhqSR-mediated regulatory circuit for thermal adaptation is not merely present but functionally maintained across this genus. Together, these findings establish the DhqSR system as a conserved, temperature-responsive regulatory paradigm in Thermus, with homologs of the system distributed among other extremophiles, suggesting a broader role in environmental adaptation.

Discussion

Our study elucidates a complete signaling pathway through which the thermophile T. thermophilus HB27 perceives and adapts to temperature downshift. We identify and characterize the DhqSR TCS. Here, DhqS is a temperature-sensitive HK, and the DhqSR system integrates a noncanonical phosphotransfer mechanism with direct transcriptional control of a key metabolic enzyme, thereby bridging environmental sensing with metabolic reprogramming. This model of thermal adaptation is summarized in Fig. 7.

Figure 7. Regulation strategies of the DhqSR system. In T. thermophilus HB27, the DhqSR TCS modulates transcriptional responses to low-temperature stress. Upon cold exposure, the HK DhqS autophosphorylates at His327 within its HisKA domain and subsequently transfers the phosphate group to Tyr84 in the REC domain of its cognate RR DhqR, triggering its activation. This phosphorylation cascade functions as a negative feedback inhibitor, concurrently upregulates DHQase, and enhances shikimate pathway flux to confer cold-adaptive metabolic remodeling.

The most striking mechanistic feature of the DhqSR system is its deviation from the universally conserved aspartate-based phosphorelay in TCSs. We provide compelling evidence that the HK DhqS specifically phosphorylates Tyr84, rather than a canonical aspartate, in the REC domain of its cognate RR DhqR (Fig. 4C and D). This His-to-Tyr phosphotransfer is rare in bacterial signaling [13, 31]. The evolutionary driver for this innovation may be linked to the thermophilic lifestyle of HB27. Its compact, streamlined genome [21] may have favored the evolution of highly specific protein–protein interactions, enabling such a precise regulatory deviation. Furthermore, the bifunctional nature of DhqS, exhibiting both kinase and phosphatase activities toward DhqR–Tyr84–P (Fig. 3D), allows for rapid signal response and termination, a crucial feature for adapting to fluctuating environments. While our data firmly establish Tyr84 as the phosphoacceptor, the structural basis for this specificity and whether DhqS possesses intrinsic tyrosine phosphatase activity warrant future investigation.

A key finding of our work is the identification of the molecular effector downstream of the DhqSR signaling cascade. Since DHQase expression decreases in both ∆DhqS and ∆DhqR strains, we propose that DhqR directly activates the transcription of a gene encoding type II 3-dehydroquinate dehydratase (DHQase), a critical enzyme in the shikimate pathway (Figs 5 and 7). This pathway is essential for the biosynthesis of aromatic amino acids—phenylalanine, tyrosine, and tryptophan [32] —which are fundamental building blocks for proteins and precursors for diverse secondary metabolites [33, 34]. The regulatory logic is elegant: under cold stress, which inherently slows enzyme kinetics, the cell compensates by upregulating the expression of a rate-limiting metabolic enzyme (DHQase) [35, 36] to maintain metabolic flux. This ensures the continued supply of aromatic amino acids, thereby sustaining protein synthesis and the production of stress-resilience molecules, such as phenolics derived from phenylalanine [34, 37]. Our discovery that DhqSR regulation of DHQase is conserved within the Thermus genus (Fig. 6B) underscores the evolutionary importance of this metabolic control strategy for thermophiles confronting thermal fluctuations. Nevertheless, while genetic evidence positions DhqR as a positive regulator and biochemical data confirm its direct DNA binding, its precise mechanistic mode remains undefined. It could function either by recruiting RNA polymerase, akin to classic activators like OmpR in E. coli [38], or by acting as an antirepressor, similar to DegU counteracting the repressor Rok in Bacillus subtilis [39]. Distinguishing between these classic activation and antirepression mechanisms is therefore a crucial future direction.

The DhqSR system exhibits several genomic and regulatory characteristics that reflect adaptive evolution. First, the nonadjacent genomic localization of dhqS and dhqR departs from the typical operon organization of many TCSs [6, 18], resembling orphan kinases and regulators found in other microbes [40]. This arrangement may allow for independent regulation or integration into larger regulatory networks. Second, the system incorporates a tight negative feedback loop, with DhqR repressing its own expression and that of dhqS (Fig. 5E and F). This autoregulatory architecture is a hallmark of robust signaling systems [41–43], preventing signal overload and maintaining cellular homeostasis. Finally, the coregulation of the essential DHQase with an rRNA methyltransferase (HB27c_C1022) in a single operon, despite their functional independence in cold adaptation (Figs. 5H and J), represents an efficient strategy for extremophiles to coregulate multiple adaptive traits under a single environmental cue. This genetic arrangement suggests a potential coordinated adaptation mechanism, where the coupled expression of a metabolic enzyme and an RNA-modifying enzyme may work synergistically to fine-tune cellular responses under stress conditions. Similar coordinated regulatory strategies, where multiple components are integrated to enhance environmental adaptation, have been observed in other extremophiles [44]. This minimizes regulatory complexity and allows for synchronized transcriptional and post-transcriptional adjustments, reflecting an evolutionary optimization for efficient stress responses.

In conclusion, we have decoded a sophisticated thermal adaptation strategy in T. thermophilus HB27. The DhqSR system functions as a dedicated thermosensor that employs a unique histidine-to-tyrosine phosphorelay mechanism to directly control the shikimate pathway, a central metabolic route. No conserved Asp phosphorylation site is present in the REC domain of DhqR. This work expands the known diversity of two-component signaling mechanisms and provides a clear example of how microbes intimately couple signal perception to core metabolism for survival. Several intriguing questions remain for future research: How does the sensor domain of DhqS physically perceive temperature changes? What is the precise structural mechanism enabling Tyr84 phosphorylation? Is DhqR a protein that acts as an RNA polymerase activator or a repressor? And does the homologous DhqSR system in other extremophiles, such as Deinococcus, facilitate adaptation to radiation or desiccation? Answering these questions will further illuminate the principles of extremophile biology and may provide novel components for engineering stress tolerance in biotechnology.
